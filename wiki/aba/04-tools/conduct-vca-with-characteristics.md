<a id="purpose"></a>
## Purpose

A VCA "should be wide-ranging," but in practice "so much information is collected... that
it is difficult to shape a coherent analysis" (Twigg 2009, p. 22). This tool uses the
Characteristics framework as the **organising and interpreting lens** for that data.
Field-testing users reported it let them "**sharpen**" their analysis "by identifying areas
of inquiry before a VCA is carried out and in providing a framework with which the data can
be organized and interpreted" (p. 22) — giving field workers an idea of "the range and types
of vulnerability/resilience that the VCA might reveal" while keeping the assessment itself
open (p. 23). The source is explicit that it "does not go into detail about how to carry out
VCAs" (p. 23); this tool is therefore grounded in its framing-and-analysis guidance, not in
VCA field method.

<a id="decision-questions"></a>
## Decision Questions

- *"Our VCA produces too much messy data — how do I structure analysis without biasing it?"* (p. 22)
- Before fieldwork, which Thematic Areas and Components define the **range of vulnerability and capacity** we should be alert to (p. 23)?
- After collection, which Components are **under-represented** in what the community raised, signalling where to widen the enquiry (p. 23)?
- Have we disaggregated across the different "vulnerable groups" — the poor, women, children, older people, ethnic and religious groups, people with disabilities — who are vulnerable in different ways (p. 23)?

<a id="method"></a>
## Method

The source does not prescribe a VCA procedure, so this method applies the Characteristics
as a lens around an existing participatory VCA (pp. 22–23, Box 9).

1. **Frame the enquiry, don't script it.** "Usually only the broad areas of enquiry are laid
   down in advance. Indicators are not pre-defined because this could lead to incomplete or
   distorted findings" (p. 23). Use the Characteristics only to orient field workers to the
   likely range of issues — as ActionAid did in Northern Afghanistan, applying them in
   "Participatory Vulnerability Analysis orientation and training, to identify different types
   of ever-present vulnerability" (p. 23).
2. **Anchor on what the framework requires.** Thematic Area 2 sets the standard: VCAs should
   provide a "comprehensive picture" of vulnerabilities and capacities and be a "participatory
   process including representatives of all vulnerable groups" (Characteristics 2.1, 2.2; p. 23).
3. **Run the VCA openly, then map findings to the framework.** After collection, use the
   Characteristics "as an organizing and interpreting framework" to group findings by Thematic
   Area and Component — Box 9 is the worked example (p. 23). The same lens can be applied
   retrospectively to assessments carried out previously (p. 23).
4. **Read the map for gaps.** Use the mapping to "focus or widen the scope of the enquiry, as
   appropriate" (p. 23); sparse areas of the framework flag where the assessment may have
   under-investigated.
5. **Treat the hazard/vulnerability split as analytical, not operational.** The framework
   separates hazard/risk (Component 1) and vulnerability/capacity assessment (Component 2),
   but field testers called this "an artificial distinction"; in practice they overlap and are
   often combined (pp. 13, 22–23). The separation exists "to ensure that all relevant aspects
   of hazard, risk and human vulnerability are considered together" (p. 23), not to run them
   as separate exercises.

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

- **Pre-defining indicators distorts collection.** Laying indicators down in advance "could
  lead to incomplete or distorted findings" (p. 23); the Characteristics must frame the
  *range* of enquiry, never become a pre-set questionnaire.
- **The checklist trap.** Avoid "the 'checklist' approach" — use the resource "as a starting
  point for identifying and discussing relevant issues" (p. 23).
- **Aggregating away vulnerable groups.** "Vulnerable groups" are not one social group; good
  work must disaggregate, and individual Characteristics "may need to be rewritten to reflect
  the situation and needs of those groups" (p. 23). Gender in particular "is still often
  downplayed or overlooked in VCAs" and is only implicit in the framework (p. 23).
- **Under-investigating broad Thematic Area 4.** Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction
  bundles seven loosely related components, risking "some important questions being
  overlooked"; users should "take particular care" to investigate each thoroughly (pp. 13–14).
- **Power structures distorting the process.** "The influence of local power structures on the
  VCA process could distort their results, with serious implications for the direction of
  project planning and the accountability of project management" (p. 26).

> **Evidence note.** Single-source (Twigg 2009), so this tool sits at `limited` trust. The
> source states plainly that it "does not go into detail about how to carry out VCAs" (p. 23),
> so the method is grounded in its framing-and-analysis guidance (pp. 22–23, Box 9) and the
> Characteristics it does detail, not in independent VCA field method. Corroboration from a
> dedicated VCA source would be needed to promote to `usable`.
