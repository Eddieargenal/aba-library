<a id="purpose"></a>
## Purpose

This tool turns the Young Foundation's **local public realm** — "the ensemble of
neighbourhood spaces open to people" (Young Foundation 2010, p. 18) — into a
diagnostic a field team can carry out on the ground. The realm is the frame for
"our lived experiences of neighbourhood … where neighbours meet and gather, and
… where neighbourhood challenges are confronted" (p. 18), so mapping it surfaces
the observable infrastructure that anchors a neighbourhood rather than relying on
administrative lines alone. The source treats the realm explicitly as both a
**diagnostic and prescriptive framework**: "where that environment is derelict or
lacking" it shows what to build (p. 14).

The purpose is therefore twofold. First, it **anchors neighbourhood definition in
shared facilities** — the source notes that residents' mental maps "are often
based around these common landmarks: public buildings, public spaces, public
services and community dynamics," so the realm "may help us build a bridge between
the lenses of administrative geography and personal identifications" (p. 14).
Second, it **identifies anchors and gaps for intervention**: some neighbourhoods
"centre primarily around civic buildings or parks, others around third spaces;
some may be characterised by the near-total absence of a local public realm, or
blighted by negative centres (e.g. a fortress-like benefits office)" (p. 18).
Knowing which case applies tells a team where the existing community life is and
where it must be enriched.

<a id="decision-questions"></a>
## Decision Questions

A team uses the map to answer:

- **What holds this neighbourhood together?** Does it centre on civic buildings,
  parks, third spaces, or a mix — or is the realm largely absent (p. 18)?
- **Where are the gaps and "negative centres"?** Which spaces are derelict,
  unsafe, "dirty and poorly maintained, dominated by traffic" (p. 19), or
  actively repelling (the "fortress-like benefits office," p. 18)?
- **Which assets are under-used?** The source flags that civic centres are
  "often substantially closed to the community" — branch libraries open "for a
  fraction of the week," schools "closed … even outside teaching hours" (p. 20).
- **Do people even know what is here?** "People often don't even know about the
  existence or location of key local centres," an argument "for more public
  neighbourhood maps" in transient populations (p. 20).
- **Can residents communicate locally?** Is there any local public sphere —
  newsletters, noticeboards, neighbourhood maps, community radio, email lists
  (p. 21) — through which neighbourhood action could be triggered?

<a id="method"></a>
## Method

Map the four components the source names for the realm (p. 18):

1. **Local public spaces** — the network of open spaces: "streets, parks,
   squares, estate areas" (p. 18). Note the quality of the street scene, parks
   and green spaces, and play spaces, since derelict or unsafe spaces stop people
   "using" the neighbourhood for social relations (p. 19).
2. **Local public services and civic centres** — "primary schools, community
   centres, public transport facilities, churches and mosques, doctors'
   surgeries, leisure centres" (p. 18); the source singles out schools — and
   primary schools in particular — as "key neighbourhood centres" (p. 20).
   Record not just their location but their connectedness and how open they are
   to the community (p. 20).
3. **Third spaces** — "cafés, pubs, and shopping centres" (p. 18); informal
   settings for public life, noting that "in some cases, particularly in rural
   areas, post offices and pubs are all there is of the local public realm"
   (p. 20).
4. **The local public sphere (civic media)** — "community radio, civic websites
   and email lists, neighbourhood newsletters or magazines, noticeboards"
   (pp. 18, 21), plus public maps of the neighbourhood and "other signs of
   identity" (p. 21).

For each, the source directs attention to **centres and dynamics**, not just an
inventory: "if the local public realm is understood as a grounding for
neighbourhood, it has more to do with centres and dynamics" (p. 17). Read the
realm as a **personal** as well as physical realm — "organically connected to
local people's identity and practices" (p. 18) — so the map should capture how
spaces are actually used, by whom, and the interests residents share around them
(safety, leisure, cleanliness, sociability, p. 19). Because mapping presupposes a
defined unit, it follows the neighbourhood-definition step rather than preceding
it. Finally, mark each component as an **anchor** (a working centre) or a **gap**
(absent, derelict, or negative), since "enterprise, intervention and collective
action can enrich and transform it, healing scars and filling gaps" (p. 18).

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

- **Treating it as a static inventory.** "The ensemble of the local public realm
  is not static" (p. 18); a one-off list of facilities misses dynamics, use, and
  change, which is where the source locates neighbourhood life.
- **Counting facilities without checking they function.** A civic centre on the
  map may be "substantially closed to the community" or barely open (p. 20); a
  third space's "publicness … depends considerably on its proprietor" (p. 20).
  Presence is not the same as a working anchor.
- **Mapping the physical and missing the personal.** The realm is "also a
  personal public realm" (p. 18); a purely "physical and anonymous" map loses the
  identity, practices and shared interests that make a space matter.
- **Mapping without a defined neighbourhood unit.** The source frames the realm
  as a bridge between administrative geography and personal identifications
  (p. 14); mapping before the unit is settled produces an unanchored inventory.
- **Evidence is provisional.** The source's own definition is offered as a
  working "hypothesis" (p. 17), and it is UK-focused (e.g. parishes, OFCOM
  community radio); its categories need adaptation where housing, civic
  infrastructure or local media differ from the British context assumed
  throughout.

> **Evidence note.** Single-source page (Young Foundation 2010), so this sits at
> `limited` trust. Every claim is grounded in the source's local-public-realm
> chapter (pp. 14, 17–21); the four-component typology and the diagnostic framing
> are the source's own. The source presents these as hypotheses requiring
> testing, and provides no validated field instrument or scoring method;
> corroboration from a second source would be needed to promote to `usable`.
