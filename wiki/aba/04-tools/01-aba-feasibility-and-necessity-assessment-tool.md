# Tool 01: ABA Feasibility and Necessity Assessment Tool

## Purpose

This tool helps program managers and technical teams determine whether an area-based approach (ABA) is needed, feasible, and justified for a specific urban crisis context. It produces a structured evidence base and a scored recommendation across five categories: Full ABA / Conditional ABA / Hybrid / Sector-only / Do not use ABA.

The tool is the entry gate to the ABA lifecycle. Teams should complete it before committing resources to area-based program design. Its output is a decision memo grounded in field evidence collected across nine assessment modules.

## Decision questions

1. Is the affected area clearly defined and geographically bounded?
2. Are needs, hazards, and vulnerabilities geographically concentrated?
3. Are there multiple interdependent sector needs requiring cross-sector response?
4. Are urban systems driving the problem in ways requiring systems-level response?
5. Is the actor environment complex enough to require structured area coordination?
6. Are there local capacities, actors, or resources to leverage?
7. Is there a meaningful DRR or future risk dimension?
8. Is meaningful community participation feasible?
9. Is the ABA operationally feasible with adequate safeguards?

## Scoring model

| Domain | Weight |
|---|---:|
| Geographic concentration | 15% |
| Multi-sector need | 15% |
| Urban systems interdependence | 15% |
| Stakeholder/coordination complexity | 15% |
| Local capacity/resource leverage | 10% |
| DRR/future risk relevance | 15% |
| Participation feasibility | 10% |
| Operational feasibility/safeguards | 5% |

Each domain is scored 0–4. Weighted score = (raw score / 4) × weight × 100. Total score = sum of weighted scores (max 100).

## Decision categories

| Score | Recommendation |
|---:|---|
| 80–100 | Full area-based approach justified |
| 65–79 | Area-based approach justified with conditions |
| 50–64 | Hybrid model recommended |
| 35–49 | Sector/institution/household approach preferred |
| 0–34 | Do not use area-based approach |

## Minimum evidence rule

Each domain score must be supported by at least 2 independent source types:
1. Direct observation (transect walk or rapid area observation)
2. Key informant interviews (disaggregated by actor type)
3. Community consultation
4. Household mini-survey
5. Service provider data
6. Secondary data and maps
7. Existing 4W/5W coordination data

Flag any domain score based on only 1 source type as **weak evidence**. Weak-evidence domain scores should be discounted in the final recommendation.

## Module 1: Crisis and Area Definition

### Decision question
Is the crisis affecting a clearly definable geographic area, and is that area bounded enough to be operationally meaningful for a multi-sector area-based program?

### Evidence needed
- Description of the crisis type (sudden onset, slow onset, protracted, compounding)
- Geographic extent of impact
- Whether the affected population is concentrated in identifiable neighborhoods
- Whether administrative, social, or service boundaries define coherent operational areas
- Whether the area is large enough to warrant area-based investment but not so large as to be unmanageable

### Field data points
- Crisis event type and onset date
- Primary affected area name, administrative unit, and approximate area in km²
- Estimated affected population within the proposed area
- Boundary description: administrative, community-defined, hazard-based, or service catchment
- Evidence of geographic concentration vs. city-wide or dispersed impact

### Collection methods
- Secondary data review: government situation reports, cluster assessments, satellite imagery, OCHA situation reports
- Rapid area observation: walking or windshield survey to observe impact footprint
- Key informant interviews: municipal officials and local NGOs with area knowledge

### Respondents / data sources
- Municipal emergency management office or local government
- Cluster/sector lead focal points
- Local NGO and CBO representatives with pre-crisis area knowledge
- Secondary: OCHA SitReps, 4W datasets, government damage reports

### Field instruments
- [[../05-field-instruments/rapid-area-observation-form]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-municipality]]

### Minimum evidence standard
At minimum: 1 secondary data source confirming crisis type and geographic scope + 1 KII with a municipal official or area-knowledgeable actor.

### Analysis method
Review secondary data and KII notes. Map the affected area on a sketch map or satellite image. Assess whether a coherent operational boundary can be drawn that: includes the core affected population; is not so large as to exceed operational capacity; corresponds to recognizable community or service boundaries.

### Scoring rule
- **4 points**: Crisis is clearly defined; area is geographically bounded and operationally coherent; boundary can be drawn with confidence; area size is appropriate
- **3 points**: Crisis is defined but area boundaries are somewhat unclear; coherent boundary is possible with further delimitation
- **2 points**: Area is partially defined; significant uncertainty about extent or boundaries remains
- **1 point**: Crisis impact is diffuse or boundaries cannot be meaningfully drawn; area is too large or fragmented
- **0 points**: No coherent operational area can be defined

### Decision rule
Score 0 = do not proceed with ABA assessment. Area definition is a prerequisite gate. All other modules depend on having a definable area.

### Risk flags
- Boundaries defined by administrative unit only (may not reflect community identity or service catchment)
- Extremely large area exceeding organizational operational capacity
- Multiple disconnected zones that would require separate programs with different designs

### Source foundation
Sanderson & Sitko (2017) define geographically targeted response as a core ABA characteristic and discuss boundary definition challenges in post-disaster urban contexts. Parker & Maynard (2015) emphasize that area selection must be based on need and feasibility, not administrative convenience.

## Module 2: Geographic Concentration Test

### Decision question
Are the needs, hazards, and vulnerabilities in this crisis geographically concentrated enough to justify an area-targeted response rather than a dispersed or household-only approach?

### Evidence needed
- Distribution of affected households across the city or district
- Concentration of high-severity needs within the proposed area vs. elsewhere in the city
- Spatial distribution of hazard exposure
- Presence of informal settlements or high-density neighborhoods with distinct vulnerability profiles
- Proportion of most-affected population residing within the proposed boundary

### Field data points
- Estimated percentage of severely affected households within proposed area vs. total affected city-wide
- Number of distinct high-vulnerability neighborhoods identifiable within the area
- Hazard intensity indicators within the area (flood zones, landslide slopes, fire-prone density)
- Relative informality and housing density compared to rest of city
- Service access gaps concentrated in the area vs. city-wide distribution

### Collection methods
- Secondary data: cluster assessment maps, multi-sector needs analysis outputs, hazard maps, satellite imagery, census data
- Rapid area observation: observe housing density, visible damage, service infrastructure concentration
- Household mini-survey: sample affected households in the proposed area and comparison areas
- Key informant interviews: community leaders and local NGOs on where needs are most acute

### Respondents / data sources
- Community leaders and local population with knowledge of city-wide distribution
- Local NGOs and CBOs with city-wide program footprint
- Cluster coordinators with 4W/sector assessment data
- Secondary: UNHCR/OCHA assessments, government damage reports, remote sensing outputs

### Field instruments
- [[../05-field-instruments/rapid-area-observation-form]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/household-mini-survey]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-community-leaders]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/hazard-exposure-vulnerability-capacity-matrix]]

### Minimum evidence standard
At minimum: 1 spatial dataset or map showing need/damage distribution + 1 KII confirming geographic concentration. Ideally: severity map from cluster assessment + rapid observation + at least 5 household mini-surveys.

### Analysis method
1. Map available need/severity data onto the proposed area boundary
2. Estimate proportion of most-affected households within boundary vs. total city-affected
3. Rate concentration: >60% of most-affected within area = high; 40–60% = medium; <40% = low
4. Check whether concentration reflects hazard geography, settlement type, or specific population characteristics

### Scoring rule
- **4 points**: >60% of most-affected population concentrated in area; clear geographic clustering of high-severity needs; hazard exposure concentrated
- **3 points**: 40–60% concentration; clustering visible but not dominant; moderate geographic patterning
- **2 points**: 20–40% concentration; needs spread across multiple areas of city
- **1 point**: <20% concentration; needs highly dispersed city-wide
- **0 points**: No geographic concentration identifiable; needs are random or city-wide

### Decision rule
Score 1 or below strongly suggests a dispersed or sector-based response is more appropriate than a geographically targeted ABA.

### Risk flags
- Using administrative boundary concentration rather than need-based concentration
- Conflating large affected population with high geographic concentration
- Cherry-picking the area boundary to artificially inflate concentration percentages
- Ignoring peri-urban areas that may have higher concentration outside the proposed urban core

### Source foundation
Sanderson & Sitko (2017) identify geographic concentration as a primary ABA suitability criterion. REACH/UNHCR (2019) provide practical guidance on concentration analysis using key informant sampling and spatial disaggregation methods.

## Module 3: Multi-Sector Needs Test

### Decision question
Are there multiple interdependent sector needs in the area that cannot be effectively addressed through single-sector programs, and would a multi-sector integrated response produce substantially better outcomes?

### Evidence needed
- Identification of all major sector needs (shelter, WASH, food, health, livelihoods, protection, education)
- Evidence of sector interdependencies: causal links showing how one sector's failure drives another's
- Assessment of whether single-sector response would leave critical gaps undermining overall outcomes
- Community perspective on which needs are most pressing and how they are connected

### Field data points
- Number of distinct sectors with significant unmet needs in the area
- Identified causal chains between sectors (e.g., flooding → shelter damage → WASH contamination → health impacts → lost workdays → income loss)
- Community-reported priority sequence and interconnections between needs
- Existing single-sector programs and evidence of their limitations due to multi-sector gaps

### Collection methods
- Multi-sector rapid assessment (MSNA/MIRA if available; or rapid sector checklist using service functionality mapping)
- Community consultation: ask communities what would happen if only one sector was addressed
- Key informant interviews with service providers across different sectors
- Review of existing sector assessment outputs

### Respondents / data sources
- Community representatives (balanced for gender, age, displacement status)
- Municipal service department staff (water, electricity, health, transport)
- Cluster/sector focal points
- Households (mini-survey)

### Field instruments
- [[../05-field-instruments/rapid-area-observation-form]] (sector infrastructure status)
- [[../05-field-instruments/household-mini-survey]] (household multi-sector need profile)
- [[../05-field-instruments/service-functionality-mapping-sheet]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-community-leaders]]

### Minimum evidence standard
At minimum: service functionality mapping covering ≥3 sectors + household mini-survey on multi-sector needs from ≥5 households.

### Analysis method
1. List all sectors with significant unmet needs
2. For each pair of sectors with needs, assess causal linkage (does addressing one enable or require the other?)
3. Rate interdependence: high = 3+ sectors causally linked; medium = 2 sectors linked; low = sectors essentially independent
4. Verify community priorities reflect multi-sector logic vs. single dominant priority

### Scoring rule
- **4 points**: 4+ sectors with significant needs; strong causal interdependencies documented; community confirms multi-sector priority; single-sector response demonstrably insufficient
- **3 points**: 3 sectors with needs; moderate interdependencies; multi-sector response adds clear value
- **2 points**: 2 sectors with needs; limited documented interdependency
- **1 point**: 2+ sectors with needs but they operate independently; sector-by-sector response is adequate
- **0 points**: Single sector dominates; multi-sector response not warranted by evidence

### Decision rule
Score 0–1 = consider sector-focused response rather than full ABA. Multi-sector needs are necessary but not sufficient for ABA — they must also be interdependent.

### Risk flags
- Listing sectors without documenting evidence of interdependency
- Confusing the presence of multi-sector needs with ABA suitability (multi-sector needs do not automatically require ABA)
- Ignoring protection and social cohesion as sectors with causal links to others
- Overweighting a single dominant sector need while treating other sectors as minor add-ons

### Source foundation
Parker & Maynard (2015) define multi-sectorality as a core ABA characteristic, distinguishing between programs that are geographically placed and programs that are genuinely integrated. ALNAP GPR12 (2019) documents cases where single-sector programs failed because sector interdependencies were not addressed. URAF (Mohiddin, Smith & Phelps, 2017) provides a structured multi-sector response analysis framework.

## Module 4: Urban Systems Interdependence Test

### Decision question
Are the problems facing the affected area driven by failures or disruptions in interconnected urban systems — infrastructure, services, governance, markets, land, mobility, and social networks — in ways that require a systems-level rather than a sector-level response?

### Evidence needed
- Functional status of key urban systems (water, sanitation, electricity, transport, markets, land governance, emergency services, social networks)
- Evidence of cascading failures: one system failure causing another
- Whether restoration of one system depends on functioning of another
- Degree of urban system degradation relative to pre-crisis baseline

### Field data points
- Functionality status of each urban system: functioning / degraded / disrupted / destroyed
- Identified causal chains: system A failure → system B failure → household impact sequence
- Community description of what systems were functioning before the crisis and what changed
- Market price changes and supply chain disruptions as proxy for economic system status
- Governance gaps (which authorities are absent, dysfunctional, or overwhelmed)

### Collection methods
- Transect walk: observe infrastructure, market areas, public services, transport routes
- Service functionality mapping: structured interview with service providers
- Community focus group on urban system changes since crisis onset
- Key informant interviews with municipal infrastructure departments

### Respondents / data sources
- Municipal engineers and infrastructure department staff
- Market traders, transport operators, and service providers
- Community members (focus group or household survey)
- Water/electricity/transport utility staff

### Field instruments
- [[../05-field-instruments/transect-walk-observation-form]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/service-functionality-mapping-sheet]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-service-provider]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-municipality]]

### Minimum evidence standard
At minimum: service functionality mapping for ≥4 urban systems + transect walk observation covering key infrastructure corridors.

### Analysis method
1. Map the status of each urban system using the service functionality mapping sheet
2. Draw causal chains: which system failures are driving other system failures and household impacts?
3. Assess depth of interdependency: are multiple systems failing together due to a common cause, or are they failing independently?
4. Compare to pre-crisis baseline: is this a crisis-induced systems failure or pre-existing chronic degradation?

### Scoring rule
- **4 points**: Multiple urban systems in failure; strong documented causal chains; response requires addressing system interdependencies to achieve outcomes; systems lens is essential
- **3 points**: 2–3 systems failing with moderate causal interdependency; systems lens adds significant design value
- **2 points**: 1–2 systems affected; some interdependency but mostly independent failures
- **1 point**: Urban systems largely intact; problem primarily at household level; systems lens adds limited value
- **0 points**: No significant urban systems failure; problem does not require systems-level response

### Decision rule
Score 1 or below suggests a household or sector response is sufficient; a systems-level ABA adds coordination complexity without proportionate value.

### Risk flags
- Treating pre-existing chronic urban under-development as crisis-induced systems failure
- Confusing urban systems analysis (focused on system interdependencies) with standard household needs assessment
- Analyzing only formal systems and ignoring informal economy, informal governance, and community social networks
- Mapping systems without drawing implications for program design

### Source foundation
Campbell (2016) provides the foundational framework for urban systems thinking applied to humanitarian response. IRC Urban Context Analysis Toolkit (2017) operationalizes systems mapping for field use. ALNAP GPR12 (2019) documents cases where systems interdependencies shaped response design and where their absence led to program failure.

## Module 5: Stakeholder and Coordination Complexity Test

### Decision question
Is the actor environment in this area complex enough — in terms of number of actors, existing duplications and gaps, and coordination architecture — to require structured area-based coordination as a distinct layer of the response?

### Evidence needed
- Number and type of actors already present or planned in the area (government, UN, INGO, NGO, CBO, private sector)
- Existing coordination mechanisms and their effectiveness
- Evidence of active or likely duplication (same activity, same area, same beneficiary group)
- Evidence of coordination gaps (needs with no actor coverage)
- Municipal government's role, capacity, and engagement
- Community-based organizations and local resource holders

### Field data points
- 4W/5W data: who is doing what, where, when, and for whom
- Identified duplications: overlapping activities by different actors
- Identified gaps: unaddressed needs or populations
- Local actor capacity: CBOs, community associations, local businesses with relevant resources
- Existence and functioning of any area-level coordination platform

### Collection methods
- 4W/5W data collection from key actors in the area
- Key informant interviews with NGO/CBO coordination focal points
- Review of cluster/sector 4W datasets from OCHA or coordination body
- Municipal coordination meeting observation or briefing

### Respondents / data sources
- OCHA or cluster coordination staff
- NGO program managers with presence in the area
- Municipal coordination or emergency management office
- CBO and community association leaders

### Field instruments
- [[../05-field-instruments/stakeholder-5w-mapping-form]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-ngos-cbos]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-municipality]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/local-resource-inventory]]

### Minimum evidence standard
At minimum: 5W mapping covering ≥5 distinct actors in or near the area + at least 2 KIIs with actors (NGO + municipality or equivalent).

### Analysis method
1. Compile actor list from 5W data and KIIs
2. Map activities by sector, geographic zone, and target population
3. Identify: (a) overlaps — same sector + area + population group covered by multiple actors; (b) gaps — identified needs with no actor coverage
4. Assess local actor capacity and whether CBOs or community resources are underutilized
5. Rate coordination complexity: high = many actors, significant gaps and duplications, no functioning coordination; low = few actors, already aligned

### Scoring rule
- **4 points**: ≥6 actors; documented duplication and gaps; no functioning area coordination mechanism; significant local resources underutilized; ABA coordination platform would add clear value
- **3 points**: 4–5 actors; some duplication/gaps; coordination mechanism exists but weak; local resources partially leveraged
- **2 points**: 3–4 actors; limited coordination needed; some alignment exists; local resources partially engaged
- **1 point**: 1–2 actors; simple bilateral coordination is sufficient
- **0 points**: Single actor or actors already fully coordinated with no duplication or gaps

### Decision rule
Low scores suggest that sector-by-sector coordination is sufficient and the coordination overhead of ABA is not justified.

### Risk flags
- Counting international actors who have not yet deployed (intention ≠ presence)
- Assuming coordination complexity exists without 5W evidence
- Ignoring local and municipal actors in the actor mapping
- Conflating many actors with high coordination complexity (a few coordinated actors is not complex)

### Source foundation
Parker & Maynard (2015) identify multi-actor coordination as a primary ABA benefit in complex urban contexts. IASC Standard Terms of Reference for Area-Based Coordination (2026) defines conditions under which area coordination platforms are warranted. URAF (Mohiddin, Smith & Phelps, 2017) provides structured coordination mapping guidance as part of response analysis.

## Module 6: Local Capacity and Resource Leverage Test

### Decision question
Are there existing local capacities, actors, institutions, markets, or community resources that an ABA program can identify, engage, and systematically leverage to improve outcomes, sustainability, and community ownership?

### Evidence needed
- List of local organizations (CBOs, community associations, local NGOs) with relevant capacity and reach
- Status of local markets (functioning, partially functioning, disrupted) and key market actors
- Municipal or government services with remaining capacity that can be aligned with or strengthened
- Community leaders and traditional institutions with credibility, legitimacy, and reach to vulnerable groups
- Local technical capacity (construction workers, health community workers, water operators, teachers)

### Field data points
- Number and types of local organizations with demonstrated presence and activity in the area
- Market functionality status, key market actors, and supply chain resilience
- Municipal department staffing and capacity levels (social services, public works, water utility)
- Community leadership structures: formal (elected), traditional, women's, youth, religious
- Identified local labor, materials, or service capacities that could be contracted or supported

### Collection methods
- Key informant interviews with local CBOs, community leaders, and market actors
- Completion of local resource inventory
- Service functionality mapping (includes local service provider mapping)
- Community consultation to identify trusted local actors and their roles

### Respondents / data sources
- Community association and CBO leaders
- Local market traders and business associations
- Municipal staff from social services, public works, water utilities
- Local health workers, community volunteers, school staff

### Field instruments
- [[../05-field-instruments/local-resource-inventory]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-community-leaders]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-market-actors]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/stakeholder-5w-mapping-form]]

### Minimum evidence standard
At minimum: local resource inventory completed + at least 3 KIIs with local actors (at least one CBO, one market actor, one municipal representative).

### Analysis method
1. List all local organizations, institutions, and market actors identified
2. Assess each for: (a) capacity to contribute to response; (b) credibility and trust in the area; (c) reach to vulnerable and marginalized populations
3. Identify specific local resources that could be leveraged: labor, materials, spaces, services, knowledge, relationships
4. Assess risk of elite capture or marginalization in local actor engagement

### Scoring rule
- **4 points**: Multiple functional local organizations; active and accessible markets; municipal capacity present; clear leverage opportunities for at least 3 resource types; local resources can be integrated into program design
- **3 points**: Some local actors; markets partially functional; some municipal alignment possible; leverage is feasible with investment
- **2 points**: Limited local actors; markets disrupted; weak municipal capacity; leverage opportunities exist but require substantial relationship building
- **1 point**: Minimal local capacity; most resources must be externally supplied; limited leverage opportunities
- **0 points**: No meaningful local actors or resources identifiable; full external delivery required

### Decision rule
Low scores do not automatically disqualify ABA, but flag that sustainability and local ownership will require substantial upfront capacity investment, which should be reflected in program design and budget.

### Risk flags
- Confusing local presence with local capacity (organizational presence alone is not enough)
- Engaging only formal or visible community leaders and ignoring informal leaders and women's networks
- Risk of elite capture: dominant community leaders may use ABA engagement to consolidate power or exclude marginalized groups
- Over-estimating market functionality when traders are present but supply chains are broken

### Source foundation
Sanderson & Sitko (2017) identify local resource leverage as a distinctive ABA advantage over sector-only approaches. IRC Urban Context Analysis Toolkit (2017) provides structured methods for local actor and market mapping. GSC/USWG Compendium (2019) case studies document both successful local resource leverage and cases where local actor engagement reproduced exclusion.

## Module 7: DRR and Future Risk Relevance Test

### Decision question
Is there a meaningful disaster risk reduction opportunity or future risk dimension in this context that should shape the response design toward reducing risk and building resilience, rather than simply meeting immediate needs?

### Evidence needed
- Identification of primary hazards affecting the area (flood, earthquake, landslide, fire, cyclone, storm surge, drought)
- Exposure: which populations and physical assets are in harm's way and to what degree
- Vulnerability: what physical, social, economic, and institutional factors make exposed populations unable to cope or recover
- Capacity: what protective capacities exist at community and municipal level (early warning, evacuation infrastructure, local preparedness groups)
- Whether the crisis was driven or exacerbated by pre-existing and unaddressed risk factors
- Alignment opportunities with Sendai Framework priorities and existing municipal DRR plans

### Field data points
- Hazard type, historical frequency, and observed severity in the area
- Proportion of affected population living in demonstrably high-hazard zones (mapped flood plains, unstable slopes, fire-dense informal settlements)
- Physical vulnerability indicators: informal tenure, overcrowded housing, poor construction quality, inadequate drainage
- Existing protective capacity: community hazard awareness, early warning systems, evacuation routes, preparedness committee activity
- Municipal DRR plan existence, coverage of the area, and implementation status
- Build Back Better opportunities: are reconstruction decisions being made now that could reduce future risk?

### Collection methods
- Secondary data: hazard maps, national DRR databases, disaster history records, satellite land cover analysis
- Community consultation: historical hazard experience, community coping strategies, local knowledge of risk
- Key informant interviews with municipal DRR office or national disaster management authority
- Direct observation: visible hazard exposure indicators (proximity to flood plains, slope stability, drainage infrastructure)
- HEVC matrix completion

### Respondents / data sources
- Municipal DRR office or city disaster management authority
- Community members with long-term hazard knowledge (elderly residents, community emergency volunteers)
- Local engineers or technical staff with infrastructure knowledge
- Secondary: UNDRR PreventionWeb, national hazard databases, GFDRR risk profiles

### Field instruments
- [[../05-field-instruments/hazard-exposure-vulnerability-capacity-matrix]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-municipality]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/rapid-area-observation-form]] (physical hazard indicators section)

### Minimum evidence standard
At minimum: HEVC matrix completed for at least 2 hazard types using 2 source types + 1 KII with municipal DRR staff or equivalent.

### Analysis method
1. Complete HEVC matrix: rate hazard severity, exposure level, vulnerability level, and capacity level for each identified hazard
2. Assess whether and how the response can reduce future risk (BBB dimension — what design choices now affect long-term risk?)
3. Check alignment with Sendai Framework's four priorities: understanding risk, strengthening governance, investing in resilience, preparedness and BBB
4. Identify specific DRR actions that should be integrated into the area strategy design

### Scoring rule
- **4 points**: Clear major hazards with high exposure and vulnerability; low existing capacity; strong BBB opportunity exists; municipal DRR alignment is feasible; DRR integration is essential to program value
- **3 points**: Moderate hazards; medium exposure and vulnerability; some DRR integration opportunity; BBB dimension present
- **2 points**: Minor hazards or low exposure; limited DRR dimension; minimal BBB opportunity
- **1 point**: Hazards present but minimal exposure, low vulnerability, or no meaningful BBB opportunity
- **0 points**: No meaningful DRR dimension; immediate response only; no future risk reduction opportunity

### Decision rule
Even low DRR scores do not automatically disqualify ABA, but should trigger a design recommendation to incorporate minimum DRR standards and BBB principles as a baseline. A program that ignores DRR in a high-risk area is a missed opportunity.

### Risk flags
- Using generic global or national risk data rather than locally validated hazard information
- Ignoring community knowledge of local hazard history and micro-geographic risk variation
- Treating DRR as an optional add-on rather than as integrated into response design choices
- Failing to identify and engage municipal DRR offices as key stakeholders

### Source foundation
UNDRR Sendai Framework (2015) defines the four priorities for DRR and the BBB recovery principle. World Bank/GFDRR (2013) provides urban risk screening tools and investment frameworks. Twigg (2007) provides community resilience characteristics for DRR capacity assessment. UNDRR How to Make Cities More Resilient handbook (2017) operationalizes the Ten Essentials for municipal DRR planning.

## Module 8: Community Participation Feasibility Test

### Decision question
Is it feasible to design and implement meaningful community participation mechanisms that are genuinely inclusive of women, youth, and marginalized groups, given the operational context, timelines, and safety constraints of this program?

### Evidence needed
- Existing community leadership structures and their degree of inclusivity and representativeness
- Evidence of meaningful participation by women, youth, elderly, disabled persons, and other marginalized groups
- Security and protection constraints that may limit community meetings or consultations
- Time and resource requirements for genuine participation given program timelines
- Community trust level in humanitarian actors and local authorities
- History of community engagement with humanitarian programs in the area

### Field data points
- Types of community leadership structures present: formal elected, traditional, women's groups, youth associations, religious bodies, tenant/resident associations
- Specific barriers to participation for each marginalized group: security, mobility constraints, language, social norms, trauma, time poverty
- History of community-humanitarian engagement: has community been consulted before? Trust level?
- Physical safe spaces available for community meetings, including women-only spaces
- Timeline to program launch vs. time needed for genuine consultation rounds

### Collection methods
- Key informant interviews with disaggregated community leaders: minimum one male leader, one female leader, one youth representative
- Community group consultations (separate by gender/age if cultural norms require)
- Direct observation of community spaces available for meetings
- Protection risk assessment for proposed engagement modalities

### Respondents / data sources
- Women's group leaders
- Youth representatives
- Traditional or religious leaders
- Persons with disabilities or their representatives
- Protection actors with knowledge of the area's social dynamics

### Field instruments
- [[../05-field-instruments/key-informant-interview-guide-community-leaders]]
- [[../05-field-instruments/participation-feasibility-checklist]]

### Minimum evidence standard
At minimum: participation feasibility checklist completed + at least 2 KIIs with different community leader types, and at minimum one must represent women.

### Analysis method
1. Complete participation feasibility checklist
2. Identify specific barriers for each marginalized group category
3. Assess whether genuine participation is achievable or whether proposed consultation would be largely tokenistic
4. Identify safe and feasible participation mechanisms adapted to context constraints

### Scoring rule
- **4 points**: Active, inclusive community structures including women's and youth groups; no significant safety barriers; safe spaces exist; community trust in actors is present; timeline allows ≥2 genuine consultation rounds
- **3 points**: Community structures exist; some barriers but manageable with adaptation; inclusive participation is feasible with effort
- **2 points**: Limited community structures; significant barriers for some groups; participation possible but constrained; risk of tokenism
- **1 point**: Major barriers to inclusive participation; participation would be largely tokenistic or unsafe for vulnerable groups; significant mitigation needed
- **0 points**: Genuine participation is not feasible in the current context; engagement would create risks or false legitimacy

### Decision rule
Score 0–1 does not automatically disqualify ABA, but requires: (a) explicit risk mitigation plan for the participation component; (b) possible shift to lighter-touch engagement mechanisms during acute phase with fuller participation planned for subsequent phases.

### Risk flags
- Consulting only male community leaders and presenting this as "community consultation"
- Assuming participation feasibility without verifying security and protection risks
- Planning unrealistic consultation timelines for acute emergency phases
- Conflating beneficiary feedback mechanisms (e.g., hotlines) with genuine participatory design
- Not planning separate engagement sessions for groups that cannot participate in mixed settings

### Source foundation
Sanderson & Sitko (2017) identify participation as a definitional ABA characteristic and discuss practical methods for inclusive community engagement. Parker & Maynard (2015) document the inherent tension between genuine participation and emergency timelines in urban ABA programs. ALNAP GPR12 (2019) provides case documentation of both strong participatory design and tokenistic consultation in urban ABA programs.

## Module 9: Operational Feasibility and Safeguards Test

### Decision question
Does the implementing team have — or can it put in place — the access, staffing, timeline, data systems, protection safeguards, accountability mechanisms, and exit plan needed to implement an ABA responsibly?

### Evidence needed
- Physical access conditions for staff and for beneficiaries across the full proposed area
- Team capacity: staffing levels, skills for ABA coordination, and deployment timeline
- Data availability and quality for targeting, monitoring, and program design
- Protection and safeguard systems currently in place or feasibly buildable
- Exit and handover plan viability: are there realistic local or municipal partners to hand over to?
- Budget adequacy for the full ABA lifecycle including coordination, MEAL, and handover
- Organizational mandates and any required approvals

### Field data points
- Current access constraints: security incidents, physical barriers, bureaucratic restrictions
- Estimated staff deployment timeline vs. urgency of acute needs
- Availability of data for targeting: population figures, displacement data, vulnerability profiling
- Existing PSEA policy and implementation status; complaints and feedback mechanism status
- Realistic handover partners identified: municipality, local NGOs, CBOs
- Budget ceiling vs. estimated ABA program cost

### Collection methods
- Internal organizational assessment by program management and logistics team
- Key informant interviews with access/security focal point and potential handover partners
- Review of existing data quality and availability
- Completion of operational feasibility checklist by program management team

### Respondents / data sources
- Program management and operations team
- Security and access focal point
- MEAL and data management team
- Finance team for budget feasibility
- Potential handover partners for exit plan validation

### Field instruments
- [[../05-field-instruments/operational-feasibility-checklist]]

### Minimum evidence standard
At minimum: operational feasibility checklist completed by program management team + at least 1 potential handover partner identified (even tentatively).

### Analysis method
1. Complete operational feasibility checklist
2. Classify each constraint as: (a) hard blocker — requires resolution before ABA can proceed; (b) manageable challenge — can be addressed in program design; (c) acceptable risk
3. Confirm protection safeguards are in place or have a credible plan
4. Confirm exit/handover is conceptually viable even if details are TBD

### Scoring rule
- **4 points**: Full access across area; adequate staffing and feasible timeline; good data availability; safeguards in place; handover partner identified; budget adequate
- **3 points**: Access feasible; some staffing or data gaps that can be managed; safeguards mostly in place; handover is possible with planning
- **2 points**: Access constrained in some zones; significant gaps in staffing or data; safeguards require building; handover uncertain
- **1 point**: Major operational constraints; weak safeguards; no realistic handover pathway; significant risk of program failure
- **0 points**: ABA is not operationally feasible in the current context; prerequisite conditions for responsible implementation are absent

### Decision rule
Score 0 = do not proceed. Operational feasibility is a hard prerequisite. Red flags override even high weighted scores from other domains.

### Risk flags
- Planning ABA in areas with active conflict without robust access and security protocols
- No PSEA policy or mechanism for sexual exploitation and abuse prevention
- No realistic handover plan (program will create dependency and fail to exit responsibly)
- Timeline so compressed that participation and safeguards cannot be implemented properly
- Data quality so poor that targeting will be arbitrary and may cause harm

### Source foundation
Sanderson & Sitko (2017) discuss operational feasibility conditions for ABA in post-disaster contexts, including access, data, and exit plan requirements. ALNAP GPR12 (2019) documents operational constraints that undermined urban ABA programs. IASC Standard TOR for Area-Based Coordination (2026) specifies minimum coordination infrastructure requirements that shape operational feasibility.

## 12 Required Outputs

Completing this tool produces the following 12 deliverables:

1. **Field data collection plan** — schedule of all data collection activities, responsible staff, estimated timeline, areas and zones to cover, logistics requirements

2. **Required tools/forms list** — list of all field instruments to be prepared and used, with copies printed or loaded onto devices

3. **Sampling guidance** — household sampling approach for the mini-survey (random, purposive, or cluster), sample size target, and KII respondent selection criteria by informant type

4. **KII respondent list** — list of all key informants to interview organized by module and respondent type, with contact information and interview scheduling plan

5. **Community consultation plan** — schedule of consultation sessions, venues, facilitation methods, inclusion plan for women, youth, elderly, disabled and marginalized groups, safety protocols

6. **Observation/transect plan** — transect routes mapped on area sketch or satellite image, observation stops identified, timing, team composition, safety protocols

7. **Service mapping template** — service functionality mapping sheet pre-populated with all relevant service types for the context

8. **Actor/4W mapping template** — 5W mapping form pre-populated with known actors from secondary data, ready for field completion and verification

9. **Local resource inventory** — template with local actor categories pre-listed, ready for completion during KIIs and community consultations

10. **Risk and safeguard checklist** — protection risks identified for this specific context, mitigation measures assigned to responsible team members

11. **Evidence scoring matrix** — scoring worksheet with all 9 domains, evidence sources used, raw scores, weighted scores, confidence flags (weak evidence), and decision thresholds

12. **Decision memo** — summary of evidence by module, total weighted score, recommendation, conditions and caveats, next steps, evidence gaps, and required approvals — use [[../05-field-instruments/decision-memo-template]]
