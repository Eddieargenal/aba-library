<a id="purpose"></a>
## Purpose

The full set of Characteristics represents a **goal** — "the highest level of
resilience that is realistically attainable" — not a project indicator set, so a
community will almost always sit some distance below it (Twigg 2009, p. 19). This
tool provides a **milestones model** for measuring that distance, tracking
progress from current state toward the ideal so improvement shows incrementally
over time rather than as a binary resilient / not-resilient judgement. The source
frames the need directly: the Characteristics "would benefit from a simple
weighting process to account for different country/community starting positions
and to recognize 'distance travelled' in some way" (p. 19, field-test feedback).
The milestones between baseline and goal are what make that distance visible.

<a id="decision-questions"></a>
## Decision Questions

- "How do we show **gradual improvement** in resilience, not just yes/no?" (the
  source's field-query trigger for this tool).
- Where, on a stage scale from "little awareness" to a "culture of safety," does
  each Thematic Area, Component, or selected Characteristic sit today (p. 20)?
- Should progress be reviewed across all five Thematic Areas, within individual
  ones, or for selected Components — the scale "may not necessarily" fit all (p. 20)?
- Which few **key** Characteristics will we measure against, rather than all 167
  (p. 20)?
- How often should we re-assess, given that achieved features of resilience can be
  lost in rapidly changing societies (p. 20)?

<a id="method"></a>
## Method

The source suggests a **five-level attainment scale**, each level marking a
distinct stage in the development of DRR (p. 20):

1. **Level 1** — Little awareness or motivation; action limited to crisis response.
2. **Level 2** — Awareness and willingness to act, but limited capacity; one-off,
   piecemeal, short-term interventions.
3. **Level 3** — Solutions developed and implemented; capacity substantially
   improved; interventions more numerous and long-term.
4. **Level 4** — Coherence and integration; interventions cover all main aspects
   within a coherent long-term strategy.
5. **Level 5** — A "culture of safety" embedded in policy, planning, practice,
   attitudes and behaviour; this approximates the ideal disaster-resilient
   community (p. 20).

To use it: select the level of resolution (Thematic Areas, individual ones, or a
few key Characteristics), assess current position on the scale, and **repeat the
assessment** over time — the first reading is the baseline and later readings
"indicate the extent of progress in DRR" (p. 20). Assessment weighs the range of
issues addressed, the number and type of Characteristics achieved or worked
towards, and — importantly — the coherence and co-ordination of effort (p. 20).
The source recommends a **multi-stakeholder exercise** spanning all groups working
on DRR locally, working out a path from current to end state for each chosen
Characteristic and agreeing indicators for the stages along the way (p. 19). At
baseline this connects to the simpler scoring scale for baseline studies — marking
each Characteristic (1) met, (2) partly met, (3) not met — to generate crude
scores and visual displays, though "any scoring system can be devised" and scores
must be debated and agreed on qualitative data and value judgements (pp. 22, 24).
Where Characteristics serve as conventional indicators, first identify whether
each is an impact/outcome, output, or process indicator, since the relationship is
not fixed (p. 24).

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

The source is explicit that this is experimental territory: "there is no common
agreement on different stages of progress in DRR; work on milestones and
benchmarking is still quite experimental" (p. 20). Specific cautions:

- **Indicators are proxies, not the goal.** The Characteristics describe an ideal
  state "in quite general terms," not project indicators; they are "signposts for
  indicator development" that must be translated into specific measures, and the
  scale gives only "a broad view" of resilience (pp. 19, 20, 24).
- **Do not over-quantify or over-engineer.** Assessments "could be rapid or more
  intensive but complex analytical processes should be avoided"; trying to build a
  complete picture from all 167 Characteristics is "unrealistic for field
  projects." A more practical approach makes general qualitative judgements at the
  higher levels, drawing on a few key Characteristics (p. 20).
- **Progress is not linear.** Users warned against assuming a simple linear model:
  in societies under rapid environmental, socio-economic or political change,
  features of resilience previously achieved may be lost — so regular review of the
  overall state of resilience is needed (p. 20).
- **It is slow and participatory, not a standalone scorecard.** Many changes come
  only in the long term; assessments must be participatory and aim at consensus,
  and any numerical scores must be debated and agreed rather than treated as
  objective (pp. 20, 24).

> **Evidence note.** This page rests on a single source (Twigg 2009,
> *Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community*, pp. 19–20, 22, 24), so it
> sits at `limited` trust. The five-level scale, baseline scoring, and cautions are
> all drawn directly from that guidance note; a validated weighting scheme is
> explicitly absent in the source, and corroboration from a second source would be
> needed to promote beyond `limited`.
