<a id="purpose"></a>
## Purpose

This tool establishes where a community sits **today** relative to the ideal of a
disaster-resilient community, using Twigg's Characteristics framework as the
reference. The framework "set[s] the current situation against the ideal state
set out in the Characteristics and identif[ies] areas for future work" (Twigg
2009, p. 22). The aim is a structured, current-state read — a baseline that
"identif[ies] the extent of resilience that already exists in a community" (p.
22) — so that gaps become visible and a starting point for measuring progress
(T-measure-resilience-progress) is established. Resilience here is treated as a
*relative* and ideal state: one must always ask "what individuals, communities
and systems are vulnerable or resilient to, and to what extent" (p. 8), and "no
community can ever be completely safe" (p. 7), so the assessment locates a
community on a spectrum rather than declaring it resilient or not.

<a id="decision-questions"></a>
## Decision Questions

This tool helps a team judge:

- Against each relevant Component of Resilience and Characteristic, is the
  feature **met, partly met, or not met** in the community now (p. 22)?
- Which Thematic Areas and Components are comparatively strong, and where do the
  largest gaps against the ideal lie?
- What baseline does this current-state read establish, against which later
  progress will be tracked (p. 22)?
- How much of the enabling environment (external policy, institutional and
  socio-economic support) is in place, since community resilience depends on it
  (p. 12)?

<a id="method"></a>
## Method

Assess against the Characteristics at the **level appropriate to the setting**.
Working through all 167 Characteristics is "unrealistic for field projects"; a
more practical approach is to "make general assessments based on qualitative
judgements at the higher levels (Thematic Areas, Components of Resilience),
perhaps drawing on a few key Characteristics... if appropriate" (p. 20).
Assessments "would have to be participatory" and "the aim should be to achieve a
consensus" (p. 20); "complex analytical processes should be avoided" (p. 20).

Match collected community data against the framework's elements to read existing
resilience (p. 22), typically structured through a Characteristics-framed VCA
(T-conduct-vca-with-characteristics), which lets users "sharpen" their analysis
by identifying areas of inquiry beforehand and organising the data afterward (p.
22). Where a team finds it helpful, individual Characteristics may be marked on a
"simple scale: (1) met, (2) partly met, (3) not met" to "generate crude scores
that would help in deciding a project's priorities," with results presentable as
spider diagrams (p. 22). Any such scores "would have to be debated and agreed,
often on the basis of qualitative data and value judgements" (p. 22). **Gaps:**
the source offers no validated weighting and no scoring system tested across
contexts; urban and conflict adaptation are absent (see R-resilience-conflict-gap).

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

The source cautions against several failure modes:

- **Do not reduce the assessment to a numeric score.** Any scoring is explicitly
  "crude" and must rest on debated, agreed qualitative judgements (p. 22); the
  Characteristics are "a resource, not a checklist to be ticked off" (p. 18).
- **The characteristics are interdependent, not a flat list.** Components "are
  not strictly independent," so a met/not-met read of isolated items can miss
  the connections between them; the assessment should "stimulate and facilitate
  discussion" rather than tally items (p. 18).
- **Resilience is not a simple linear state.** Users have "warned against
  assuming a simple linear model of progress towards resilience: in societies
  experiencing rapid environmental, socio-economic or political change, features
  of resilience that were previously achieved may be lost," which "suggests the
  need for regular review of the overall state of resilience" (p. 20). A single
  assessment can therefore go stale.
- **It is context-specific.** The framework assumes a spatially-defined community
  with some consensus and an accessible enabling environment (pp. 9, 12); it has
  no field-tested guidance for conflict settings, where these assumptions break
  down (p. 15, R-resilience-conflict-gap), and presenting a partial, qualitative
  read as a definitive verdict over-claims what the tool supports.
