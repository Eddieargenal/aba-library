<a id="purpose"></a>
## Purpose

Turn the generic Characteristics into a **locally valid** set — fitted to the area's hazards, culture, and capacities. *"The global characteristics don't fit our village — how do we rewrite them?"*

The source is explicit that this adaptation is not optional polish but the intended way to use the resource. The guidance note is "a resource, not a manual… not a checklist to be ticked off" (Twigg 2009, pp. 5, 18); the "disaster-resilient community" it describes is "an ideal… not project indicators in the conventional sense" (p. 7). Because the 167 Characteristics "are general ones for all contexts, whereas every project, location and community is unique" (p. 7), customising is what makes them "more relevant to the particular needs and capacities of communities, the hazard (and other) threats those communities face, the type of DRR work implementing organizations are expert in… and the wider operational and policy environment" (p. 18). The output is a locally owned version of the framework, not a completed audit.

<a id="decision-questions"></a>
## Decision Questions

- Which Components and Characteristics are relevant here, and which should be dropped, reworded, added, or rearranged (Twigg 2009, p. 18)?
- Do the local hazards, capacities, and the type of DRR work require a Characteristic the generic tables do not contain — even a whole new Thematic Area (p. 18; Box 6)?
- Who selects and validates the adapted set? The source insists the Characteristics are "most useful (and most used) when they are selected by, or at least with, those who need to use them" (p. 18).
- Is a short list of "key" / "core" / "minimum" Characteristics actually necessary — and if so, has it been agreed deliberatively rather than imposed (pp. 18–19)?

<a id="method"></a>
## Method

Twigg sets out customisation as a participatory, deliberative process rather than a fixed protocol (Section 4.3, pp. 18–19):

1. **Engage with the resource, do not adopt it.** Debate each Characteristic's "accuracy and relevance to a given situation" before keeping it; nobody is expected to use every one, and even all 167 "cannot cover every dimension of resilience, every sector or every vulnerable group" (p. 18).
2. **Select, adapt, add, or rearrange.** Customising "might involve selecting particular Components of Resilience and Characteristics… adapting and rewriting them, adding new ones, or even arranging the framework differently" (p. 18), including translating into simpler local language or re-packaging it (p. 18).
3. **Validate locally.** Run selection and rewriting through "participatory processes of discussion and validation at local level" with the community and the organisations working with them (pp. 5, 18).
4. **Follow the worked example.** Box 5 (Philippines) shows MACEC translating generic community and Enabling Environment Characteristics into versions matched to its local Governance context (Barangay development and investment plans); Plan's child-centred work added a draft new Thematic Area (p. 18; Box 6; Case Study 4).
5. **Re-question over time.** "In rapidly-changing contexts, this questioning should be repeated"; any "key" list "must not be a one-off — there should be regular reviews," because resilience "is not static" (pp. 18–19).

**Gaps:** the source gives no step-by-step contextual-analysis protocol, and its worked examples are rural/generic — meaningful adaptation also depends first on local hazard and vulnerability/capacity assessment (p. 18; cf. DEP-001).

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

- **Checklist capture.** Skipping adaptation and ticking Characteristics off mechanically is the failure mode the source warns against most directly — "a resource, not a checklist to be ticked off" (p. 18). It produces superficial compliance and ignores local context.
- **Frozen "key" lists.** If a selected short list "remains fixed, project managers may simply forget about other aspects of resilience that might be significant" (p. 19); selection of key Characteristics "should not be assumed" to be necessary at all (p. 19).
- **Top-down imposition.** Customisation "should be participatory and deliberative, not imposed from the top" (p. 19); adaptation captured by facilitators or DRR specialists rather than the community defeats the purpose of local ownership.
- **Cost and consensus.** The process needs time, skilled facilitation, and stakeholder agreement, and "it might be difficult to reach consensus" (p. 19).
- **Conflict settings.** The Characteristics assume community consensus and a functioning enabling environment; in conflict-affected or fragmented communities the source reports no field experience and expects heavy modification — see R-resilience-conflict-gap before customising (p. 15).

> **Evidence note.** Single source (Twigg 2009), so this tool sits at `limited` trust. Every claim is grounded in the guidance note's Section 4.3 ("Selecting, modifying, 'customizing'", pp. 18–19) and its introductory framing (pp. 5, 7), with worked examples from Box 5 (Philippines) and Box 6 (Plan). The source provides no step-by-step contextual-analysis protocol and no non-rural examples; corroboration from a second source would be needed to promote to `usable`.
