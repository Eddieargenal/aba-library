# 04 — Tools

17 numbered operational tools for field evidence collection, analysis, and decision-making across the ABA/DRR lifecycle. Tool 01 is fully populated and serves as the quality reference; tools 02–17 have skeletal structures with TODO markers and should not be used for field guidance yet.

## Documents

| Document | Description |
|---|---|
| [[01-aba-feasibility-and-necessity-assessment-tool]] | **What:** A 9-module structured assessment tool that scores whether an Area-Based Approach is needed, feasible, and justified, producing a recommendation across 5 categories (Full ABA to Do Not Use). **Why:** The entry gate to the ABA lifecycle — prevents committing resources to ABA design in contexts where sector or household approaches would serve better. **When:** Stage 0 (appropriateness decision), before any ABA program design begins. **How:** Complete all 9 modules, score each domain (0–4), apply weights to reach a total score, then generate a decision memo using the decision-memo-template. |
| [[02-area-selection-matrix]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A comparative matrix that scores candidate neighborhoods against hazard exposure, vulnerability, access, service gaps, and strategic value. Why: Guides objective area selection by making trade-offs between need severity and operational feasibility explicit. How: Score each candidate area against defined criteria, apply weights, and produce a ranked comparison to inform the final area selection decision. |
| [[03-settlement-neighborhood-boundary-definition-tool]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A tool that defines the operational boundary of the selected area using community identity, service catchments, administrative units, and hazard zones. Why: Establishes a formally agreed boundary that aligns with community perception and operational manageability for consistent programming. How: Use participatory mapping, GIS tools, and administrative boundary review to reconcile conflicting definitions and produce an agreed boundary map. |
| [[04-urban-systems-diagnosis-tool]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A structured tool for mapping and diagnosing interconnected urban systems (water, sanitation, transport, markets, governance) to identify cascading failures and systemic vulnerability drivers. Why: Reveals which systems must be restored first for others to function, preventing siloed responses that ignore systemic interdependencies. How: Conduct transect walks and service provider KIIs, map causal chains between failing systems, and categorize each system by functionality status. |
| [[05-hevc-risk-mapping-tool]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A systematic HEVC (Hazard, Exposure, Vulnerability, Capacity) mapping tool that produces a risk profile to inform DRR integration into response design. Why: Links humanitarian needs to underlying risk drivers and identifies Build Back Better opportunities before construction begins. How: Populate a HEVC matrix per hazard using community consultation, hazard maps, and municipal data to generate combined risk ratings and DRR integration recommendations. |
| [[06-stakeholder-coordination-mapping-tool]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A tool mapping all actors in the target area — their activities, coverage, target populations, and coordination relationships — to identify duplications, gaps, and local resource leverage opportunities. Why: Prevents duplicated efforts and exposes coverage gaps that could leave vulnerable populations unserved. How: Collect 5W data through KIIs and cluster datasets, perform overlap/gap analysis, and document local actor capacity. |
| [[07-community-engagement-platform-tool]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A framework for designing a community engagement platform that enables meaningful participation of affected communities, including women, youth, and marginalized groups, in assessment and program design. Why: Ensures response design is informed by affected populations and that inclusion safeguards are built into ABA processes. How: Select appropriate participation mechanisms, establish safe feedback spaces, and define inclusion protocols for marginalized groups. |
| [[08-joint-risk-needs-prioritization-matrix]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A joint prioritization matrix that ranks needs and risks by severity, vulnerability, feasibility, and DRR relevance through a combined community and technical team process. Why: Produces an evidence-backed, collectively agreed priority list that aligns humanitarian urgency with long-term risk reduction. How: Score each identified need across multiple criteria in a participatory workshop and aggregate into a ranked priority list. |
| [[09-response-option-comparison-matrix]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A matrix comparing response modalities (cash, in-kind, direct works, referral, advocacy, technical assistance) across sectors to identify the optimal combination for each need. Why: Ensures the selected modality matches market functionality, local capacity, and outcome probability rather than defaulting to a single approach. How: Assess market conditions, local supply chain capacity, and modality-specific risks for each sector need and score options accordingly. |
| [[10-integrated-area-strategy-builder]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A structured framework for building a coherent area strategy that combines sector interventions, DRR actions, local actor roles, coordination mechanisms, and sequencing logic into a unified program. Why: Ensures sector interventions reinforce each other strategically rather than operating in silos, maximizing collective impact. How: Synthesize outputs from all prior tools (area profile, risk map, prioritization, modalities) into a single integrated strategy document with cross-sector synergy analysis. |
| [[11-sector-technical-design-checklist]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A quality checklist verifying that each sector intervention design meets Sphere standards, integrates DRR principles, includes local actor roles, and specifies handover arrangements. Why: Catches design deficiencies before implementation begins, ensuring minimum quality and Build Back Better compliance across all sectors. How: Review each sector design document against the checklist criteria, flag non-compliant elements, and require remediation before approval. |
| [[12-implementation-sequencing-dependency-map]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A dependency mapping tool that sequences all program activities, identifies predecessors, external dependencies (permits, seasons), and parallel workstreams. Why: Prevents implementation bottlenecks and delays by revealing what must happen before what, enabling realistic scheduling. How: Map each activity's technical and external dependencies, sequence critical path items, and identify opportunities for parallel execution. |
| [[13-area-coordination-dashboard]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A live dashboard tracking coordination status during implementation — actor activities, geographic coverage, emerging gaps, duplications, referral completions, and coordination meeting decisions. Why: Provides real-time visibility into the coordination landscape so gaps and duplications are detected and addressed before they compound. How: Consolidate 5W data, referral logs, and meeting decisions into a single dashboard updated at regular coordination intervals. |
| [[14-accountability-feedback-tracker]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A tracker managing community feedback, complaints, and accountability responses during ABA implementation, with pattern detection for systemic issues. Why: Ensures community concerns are documented, resolved, and escalated, maintaining program legitimacy and enabling evidence-based adaptation. How: Log each complaint with originator demographics, assign response owners, track resolution status, and flag recurring patterns for program-level review. |
| [[15-referral-pathway-tracker]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A tracker for managing cross-sector and cross-actor referrals, ensuring beneficiaries needing multiple services are identified, referred, and followed through to completion. Why: Prevents beneficiaries from falling through the cracks in multi-sector responses by making referral handoffs visible and accountable. How: Record each referral with beneficiary ID, receiving actor, service needed, and resolution date; analyze completion rates and bottlenecks. |
| [[16-area-based-mel-adaptation-framework]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A MEL framework tracking area-level outcomes, systems changes, DRR progress, and community resilience indicators across all sectors with adaptive management triggers. Why: Measures collective area-wide impact rather than siloed outputs, and defines evidence thresholds for course correction. How: Define area-level indicators and baselines per sector, establish monitoring schedules, and set trigger thresholds for adaptive management decisions. |
| [[17-handover-scale-up-checklist]] | **[STUB — draft status]** What: A checklist for planning and executing a structured handover of ABA responsibilities to local authorities, service providers, and community organizations, with scale-up criteria for replication. Why: Prevents service collapse when the humanitarian actors exit by ensuring local systems are ready and resourced to sustain outcomes. How: Assess local capacity and readiness per sector, document handover agreements, set sustainability monitoring indicators, and define conditions for scaling the approach to adjacent areas. |
