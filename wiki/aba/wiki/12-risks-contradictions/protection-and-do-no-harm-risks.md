# Protection and Do-No-Harm Risks in Urban ABA

## Purpose

Catalogues protection risks specific to area-based approaches in urban settings. These risks arise from the intersection of geographic targeting, community mapping, coordination data sharing, and engagement with local power structures. Field teams must screen against these risks before and during programme implementation.

---

## Risk Categories

### 1. Cross-Neighbourhood Inequality and Tension

**How it arises:** Geographic targeting concentrates resources in one area while adjacent areas with similar needs receive nothing. Residents outside the target area perceive the programme as unfair.

**Evidence:** Parker & Maynard (2015, p.15) — the Katye Neighbourhood Improvement programme in Ravine Pintade, Port-au-Prince reported "tension with surrounding neighbourhoods not included in the project." Smith (1999, cited) notes this as a systemic risk of area targeting: "there can be significant challenges and pressure associated with targeting some areas and not others."

**Populations most exposed:** Residents in adjacent informal settlements with similar need profiles; areas that narrowly missed inclusion criteria.

**Warning signs:** Community complaints from outside the target area; local media coverage of programme as unfair; hostility toward beneficiaries from non-beneficiary neighbours.

**Mitigation:**
- "Blur the line" by extending some low-cost projects (paths, drainage, lighting) just beyond the formal programme boundary
- Locate larger shared facilities (health centres, community spaces) on or near the boundary to encourage cross-usage
- Communicate selection criteria transparently to adjacent communities
- Engage non-target area leaders in coordination meetings

**When to halt:** If tension escalates to physical conflict or programme staff face security incidents from non-target communities.

---

### 2. Elite Capture in Area Selection and Targeting

**How it arises:** Local leaders (community committees, informal power brokers, local government officials) influence area selection or within-area beneficiary targeting to benefit politically-connected households or exclude rivals.

**Evidence:** Parker & Maynard (2015) — community engagement structures can reproduce existing power hierarchies if not designed to be inclusive. The KASS Kabul programme mitigated this by supporting elections for Community Council membership rather than working only with existing appointed leaders.

**Populations most exposed:** Women, renters, IDPs, ethnic minorities, persons without social connections to dominant community leaders.

**Warning signs:** Beneficiary list skews toward community leaders' social networks; marginalized groups absent from community meetings; community feedback mechanism unused or untrusted.

**Mitigation:**
- Conduct separate consultations with women, IDPs, and marginalised groups outside formal leader structures
- Verify beneficiary lists against independent vulnerability data (household survey)
- Establish anonymous feedback and complaints mechanisms
- Use elected (not appointed) community representatives where feasible

---

### 3. Data-Related Exposure Risks

**How it arises:** Participatory mapping and area profiling creates detailed data on the location, vulnerability, and identity of households and individuals. In conflict, displacement, or politically sensitive contexts, this data can be misused.

**Specific risks:**
- Community mapping reveals locations of IDPs, minority groups, or LGBTQ+ individuals to hostile actors
- Beneficiary lists repurposed by local authorities for discriminatory targeting
- Coordination data sharing exposes sensitive population data to actors without adequate data protection protocols
- Area designation as "crisis zone" affects property values or exposes residents to landlord eviction

**Evidence:** Parker & Maynard (2015) — NRC Baghdad specifically noted that their inclusive approach (targeting all residents, not just IDPs) was partly a protection strategy to reduce IDP visibility and associated risk.

**Mitigation:**
- Apply data minimisation — collect only what is needed for the programme
- Establish data sharing protocols before entering any multi-actor coordination
- Store beneficiary data locally; do not share disaggregated lists with government actors in politicised contexts
- Use aggregated data in coordination meetings; share individual data only with confirmed protection actors

---

### 4. Responsibility Displacement onto Local Actors

**How it arises:** ABA focuses programme responsibility at local level, engaging communities and local government. When the underlying problem is caused by structural factors (national policy, macro-economic conditions), local actors bear responsibility without having control or influence.

**Evidence:** Parker & Maynard (2015, p.15): "the problems of localised poverty and unemployment are often caused by wider economic and social processes that have relatively little to do with the characteristics of the areas themselves. Thus local actors may have responsibility, but little control and limited influence." NRC Baghdad experienced this when district councils were legally prohibited from extending services to informal settlements despite willingness to do so.

**Populations most exposed:** Local government partners who are set up to fail; community structures that cannot sustain programme outputs after exit.

**Mitigation:**
- Conduct political economy analysis before assigning responsibilities to local actors
- Pair area-level programming with national-level advocacy where structural barriers exist (NRC Baghdad model: community-level + national policy advocacy)
- Build exit criteria around structural change, not just capacity metrics

---

### 5. Gender-Based and Intra-Community Harm from Participation

**How it arises:** Participation structures that increase women's visibility in community decision-making can expose them to backlash, including intimate partner violence. Community engagement that brings rival groups together can resurface pre-existing tensions.

**Populations most exposed:** Women in conservative community contexts; members of politically or ethnically divided communities.

**Warning signs:** Women absent from or silent in community meetings; community tension during joint planning sessions; reports of household-level friction after women attend public meetings.

**Mitigation:**
- Conduct gender analysis before designing participation structures
- Hold separate women-only sessions in contexts where mixed meetings exclude women
- Consult with protection actors before launching community engagement in post-conflict settings
- Monitor for intra-community tension during and after participation activities

---

### 6. Programme Creates Distraction from Structural Problems

**How it arises:** Area-based programmes addressing visible localised symptoms can give the impression that action is being taken while deflecting attention from structural economic, social, or governance causes that require national-level or systemic change.

**Evidence:** Parker & Maynard (2015, p.15): "Area-based approaches have been criticised as high profile palliatives for the visible manifestations of deep-rooted problems, giving the impression that something is being done but deflecting attention from more fundamental economic, social and institutional changes."

**Mitigation:**
- Explicitly document what the programme can and cannot address in the decision memo
- Pair programme with advocacy on structural issues where feasible
- Avoid communications framing that implies the programme resolves the underlying problem

---

## Minimum Protection Standards Before Programme Launch

- [ ] Cross-neighbourhood impact assessed; adjacent community engagement plan exists
- [ ] Elite capture risk assessed; independent vulnerability verification method identified
- [ ] Data protection protocol in place before any community mapping begins
- [ ] Political economy analysis conducted; local actor responsibilities matched to actual authority
- [ ] Gender analysis conducted; participation design accounts for context-specific risks
- [ ] Feedback and complaints mechanism established and communicated to all target residents
