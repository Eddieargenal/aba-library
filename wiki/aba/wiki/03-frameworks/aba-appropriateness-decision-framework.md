# ABA Appropriateness Decision Framework

## Purpose
Provide a structured evidence-based framework for deciding whether an area-based approach is needed, feasible, and justified for a given crisis, area, and time. This framework defines the criteria, domains, and logic that underpin Tool #01.

## Core decision
**Is an area-based approach needed, feasible, and justified for this crisis, in this area, at this time?**

A program is NOT area-based simply because it is located in a neighborhood. The three core ABA conditions — geographic targeting, multi-sectorality, and participation — must all be present.

## Evidence-based foundations

1. Area-based approaches are geographically targeted, multi-sectoral, and participatory — all three characteristics must be present and designed into the program logic.
2. Urban crises require systems thinking across infrastructure, governance, services, markets, land, mobility, and social systems — isolated sector interventions often fail in urban contexts.
3. DRR requires risk-informed decision-making based on hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and capacity analysis — not generic vulnerability targeting.
4. Coordination must prevent duplication and leverage local resources — urban contexts with many actors require explicit coordination architecture.
5. Feasibility must include access, staffing, time, data quality, participation mechanisms, protection safeguards, and a realistic handover plan.
6. Not all urban programs benefit from ABA design — a poorly designed ABA creates coordination complexity without added value. When in doubt, a simpler approach is preferable.

## Decision domains

1. **Crisis and Area Definition** — Is the crisis and affected area clearly defined? Can a coherent operational boundary be drawn?
2. **Geographic Concentration** — Are needs, hazards, and vulnerabilities geographically concentrated enough to justify area targeting rather than dispersed or household-only response?
3. **Multi-Sector Needs** — Are there multiple interdependent sector needs that cannot be effectively addressed through single-sector programs?
4. **Urban Systems Interdependence** — Are urban systems (infrastructure, services, markets, governance) driving the problem in ways that require systems-level response?
5. **Stakeholder and Coordination Complexity** — Is there a complex actor environment requiring structured area coordination to prevent duplication and fill gaps?
6. **Local Capacity and Resource Leverage** — Are there local actors, institutions, markets, or resources that can be identified, engaged, and leveraged?
7. **DRR and Future Risk Relevance** — Is there a meaningful DRR opportunity or future risk dimension that should shape the response design?
8. **Community Participation Feasibility** — Is meaningful community participation feasible within the operational context, timelines, and safety constraints?
9. **Operational Feasibility and Safeguards** — Does the team have the access, staffing, time, data, systems, and protection safeguards to implement responsibly?

## Scoring model

| Domain | Weight |
|---|---:|
| Geographic concentration | 15% |
| Multi-sector need | 15% |
| Urban systems interdependence | 15% |
| Stakeholder/coordination complexity | 15% |
| Local capacity/resource leverage | 10% |
| DRR/future risk relevance | 15% |
| Participation feasibility | 10% |
| Operational feasibility/safeguards | 5% |

Note: Crisis and Area Definition (Domain 1) is a prerequisite gate — if the area cannot be defined, scoring does not proceed.

## Decision categories

| Score | Recommendation |
|---:|---|
| 80–100 | Full area-based approach justified |
| 65–79 | Area-based approach justified with conditions |
| 50–64 | Hybrid model recommended |
| 35–49 | Sector/institution/household approach preferred |
| 0–34 | Do not use area-based approach |

## Red flags (override score)

Even a high weighted score does NOT justify proceeding with ABA if any of the following hard constraints apply:
- Operational access is insufficient to cover the full area safely
- No viable community consultation mechanism exists (participation will be tokenistic)
- Protection risks from area-based targeting are not mitigated
- No exit or handover plan is feasible within the program timeframe
- Data quality is too weak to support defensible area selection
- Key authorities refuse to engage or actively obstruct the program

## Minimum evidence standard

Each domain score must be supported by at least 2 independent source types:
- Direct observation
- Key informant interviews (disaggregated by actor type)
- Community consultation
- Household mini-survey
- Service provider data
- Secondary data / maps
- 4W / 5W coordination data

Flag any domain score based on only 1 source type as **weak evidence**.
