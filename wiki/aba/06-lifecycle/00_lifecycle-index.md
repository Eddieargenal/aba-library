# 06 — Lifecycle

11 sequential programme phase pages numbered 00–10, from appropriateness decision through transition and handover. Stage 00 (appropriateness decision) is fully populated; stages 01–10 have structural templates with TODO markers and are not yet ready for field use.

## Documents

| Document | Description |
|---|---|
| [[00-appropriateness-decision]] | **What:** The Stage 0 gateway page defining the five key decisions required before any ABA program design: whether ABA is warranted, at what scale, what boundary type, whether participation is feasible, and whether coordination overhead is justified. **Why:** Teams that skip this stage risk committing to ABA design that doesn't fit the context, wasting coordination resources on contexts where sector or household approaches would be more effective. **When:** Before any ABA program design begins; this decision gates entry to all subsequent lifecycle stages. **How:** Work through the 5 key decisions using evidence from field instruments; complete Tool 01 scoring and the decision-memo-template before advancing to Stage 1. |
|| [[01-area-selection-boundary-definition]] | **What:** Selection and delineation of target neighborhoods using evidence-based criteria combining humanitarian severity, DRR risk, operational feasibility, and strategic value to establish a formally agreed operational boundary. **Why:** Transparent, defensible area selection prevents arbitrary geographic targeting and ensures resources reach the most appropriate neighborhoods for ABA programming. **When/How:** Execute immediately after the appropriateness decision gates entry; use Tool #02 (Area Selection Matrix) and Tool #03 (Boundary Definition) to produce an agreed boundary that anchors all subsequent work. |
|| [[02-area-profile-systems-diagnosis]] | **What:** Comprehensive area profiling across urban systems status, HEVC risk, service functionality, stakeholder landscape, and local resource inventory to establish the evidence base for strategy design. **Why:** Without a deep systemic understanding of the area, interventions risk targeting symptoms rather than root causes and miss leveraging existing local capacities. **When/How:** Conduct after boundaries are defined and before strategy development; use Tools #04 (Systems Diagnosis), #05 (HEVC Mapping), and #06 (Coordination Mapping) to produce a unified baseline diagnosis. |
|| [[03-community-stakeholder-engagement]] | **What:** Establishment of a structured platform for ongoing meaningful community participation and stakeholder engagement that shapes program design, implementation, monitoring, and accountability throughout the lifecycle. **Why:** Programs that fail to engage communities and stakeholders from the outset risk designing interventions that do not reflect local priorities, missing critical local knowledge, and undermining long-term ownership and sustainability. **When/How:** Initiate after the systems diagnosis identifies key actors; use Tool #07 (Community Engagement Platform) alongside KII guides (Tools 01–05) to establish inclusive, representative engagement mechanisms before strategy and design stages. |
|| [[04-joint-risk-needs-prioritization]] | **What:** Joint multi-stakeholder process integrating humanitarian needs with DRR priorities to produce a consensus-prioritized list of risks and needs across sectors, balancing severity, vulnerability, feasibility, and protection considerations. **Why:** Without joint prioritization, sectoral actors compete for resources and siloed programming fails to address cross-cutting risks at the area level. **When/How:** Conduct after the area diagnosis is complete; use Tool #08 (Joint Prioritization Matrix) with communities, technical teams, and local actors to produce an agreed priority list that feeds the integrated strategy. |
|| [[05-integrated-area-strategy]] | **What:** Development of a coherent area-level response strategy combining sector interventions, DRR actions, local actor roles, and coordination mechanisms into a unified program framework grounded in area evidence and a clear theory of change. **Why:** Without a unifying strategy, sectoral interventions proceed in isolation, creating duplication, gaps, and missed opportunities for synergistic impact across the area. **When/How:** Synthesize diagnosis findings and prioritized needs into an integrated strategy using Tool #10 (Strategy Builder); use the result to guide all subsequent program design and coordination decisions. |
|| [[06-program-design-modality-selection]] | **What:** Evidence-based selection of the most appropriate response modalities for each sector and population group, assessing market functionality, local capacity, and outcome probability to determine the optimal mix of cash, in-kind, direct works, referral, advocacy, and technical assistance. **Why:** Selecting the wrong modality wastes resources and fails to achieve intended outcomes — market-based approaches require functioning markets, and direct works require local capacity. **When/How:** Apply after the strategy is defined; use Tool #09 (Response Option Comparison) to evaluate and justify modality choices before committing to detailed technical design. |
|| [[07-detailed-technical-design]] | **What:** Development of detailed sector-specific technical designs integrating DRR standards, Build Back Better principles, local actor roles, and targeting criteria into implementation-ready sector design documents. **Why:** Without rigorous technical design, sectoral interventions may fail to incorporate DRR standards, miss local capacity opportunities, or produce designs that are technically sound but contextually inappropriate. **When/How:** Execute after modalities are selected; use Tool #11 (Sector Technical Design Checklist) to produce implementation-ready technical specifications for each sector intervention. |
|| [[08-area-based-implementation]] | **What:** Coordinated delivery of the area strategy through multi-sector programming, active coordination platform management, real-time gap and duplication tracking, referral pathway management, and community accountability mechanisms. **Why:** Implementation without active coordination leads to duplication, gaps, sequencing failures, and community frustration — the very risks ABA is designed to prevent. **When/How:** Execute after technical designs are complete; use Tools #12 (Sequencing & Dependency Map), #13 (Coordination Dashboard), #14 (Accountability Feedback Tracker), and #15 (Referral Pathway Tracker) to manage delivery across all actors in the area. |
|| [[09-monitoring-learning-adaptation]] | **What:** Continuous monitoring of area-level outcomes, systematic learning generation, and structured adaptive management triggered by evidence thresholds and community feedback to adjust program direction based on real-time performance data. **Why:** Rigid adherence to pre-set plans in complex urban contexts leads to wasted resources and missed opportunities — programs must adapt to changing conditions while maintaining accountability. **When/How:** Operate throughout implementation and transition; use Tool #16 (M&E Adaptation Framework) alongside the Implementation Adaptation Framework to define indicators, set adaptive triggers, and conduct periodic learning reviews. |
|| [[10-transition-handover-scaling]] | **What:** Systematic transfer of program ownership, capacity, and resources to local and municipal actors while documenting lessons learned to enable scale-up, replication in other areas, and alignment with Build Back Better recovery objectives. **Why:** Without planned transition, humanitarian gains are lost when external actors exit — systematic handover to local structures is essential for sustaining risk reduction outcomes. **When/How:** Initiate when adaptive management signals transition readiness; use Tool #17 (Handover & Scale-Up Checklist) with the Transition Handover Framework to structure exit conditions, identify receiving actors, and document replication learning. |
