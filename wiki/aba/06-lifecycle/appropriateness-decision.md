# Stage 0: Appropriateness Decision

## Stage Purpose

Determine whether an area-based approach is appropriate, necessary, and feasible for this crisis. This is the gateway decision that must precede any ABA program design. Teams that skip this stage risk committing to an ABA design that does not fit the context, wasting coordination overhead for contexts where a sector or household approach would be more effective.

> "Area-based approaches are not a 'quick fix solution to difficult places with deep-rooted social and economic problems.' They are 'not a panacea and cannot hope to solve everything.'" — Parker & Maynard (2015, p.17), citing Turok (2004) and Smith (1999)

---

## Key Decisions

1. **Is an ABA warranted at all?** — Compare ABA against market-based, systems-based, institution-based, and household approaches. ABA is justified only when all three criteria (geographic, multi-sector, participatory) are both necessary and feasible.
2. **What is the appropriate scale?** — Neighbourhood, district, or city-wide? Scale should be defined by the problem, not by operational convenience.
3. **What boundary type is appropriate?** — Administrative, physical, or social? Boundary logic must be documented.
4. **Is participation genuinely feasible?** — Given timeline, access, and community context, can meaningful participation be achieved? If not, ABA should not be attempted.
5. **What is the coordination overhead worth?** — ABA creates significant coordination costs. Are 4+ actors present with uncoordinated activities that justify the overhead?

## Tools to Use

- [[../04-tools/01-aba-feasibility-and-necessity-assessment-tool]] — 8-domain scoring model; produces scored recommendation

**Five-question pre-screen** (from Parker & Maynard 2015 and Tool #01):
1. Are needs geographically concentrated (>40% of affected population in one area)?
2. Are 3+ sectors failing in interdependent ways?
3. Are urban systems driving the problem?
4. Are 4+ actors present with uncoordinated activities?
5. Is meaningful community participation feasible in this timeline?

If ≤1 yes → do not proceed to full Tool #01. Document rationale and apply sector/household approach.

---

## Minimum Outputs

Before moving to Stage 1 (Area Selection), the following must exist:

1. Completed Tool #01 scoring worksheet (or documented rationale for not using ABA)
2. Signed decision memo → [[../05-field-instruments/decision-memo-template]]
3. Documented comparison of ABA vs. alternative approaches
4. Identified boundary hypothesis (administrative or physical) for validation in Stage 1
5. Initial stakeholder mapping → [[../05-field-instruments/stakeholder-5w-mapping-form]]

---

## Quality Review Checks

- Decision memo is signed by programme lead and MEAL/technical lead
- Alternative approaches were explicitly considered (not just ABA evaluated in isolation)
- Participation feasibility was assessed (not assumed)
- Score reflects actual evidence collected, not desk estimates
- If score is 65–79 (conditional), specific conditions are documented with owner and timeline

---

## Common Failure Modes

From Parker & Maynard (2015):

1. **Skipping the decision entirely** — Teams assume ABA is appropriate because they are working in a city, and proceed directly to design
2. **Confusing geographic placement with appropriateness** — Being located in a neighbourhood does not make ABA appropriate
3. **Not comparing alternatives** — ABA is evaluated in isolation without considering whether a market, systems, or household approach would be more efficient
4. **Underestimating time and cost** — ABA requires significantly more coordination overhead than single-sector programmes; teams underestimate this and run out of resources mid-programme
5. **Assuming participation is always feasible** — In acute emergency phases with restricted access and short timelines, genuine participation may be impossible; this should disqualify ABA at this stage rather than be discovered mid-implementation
