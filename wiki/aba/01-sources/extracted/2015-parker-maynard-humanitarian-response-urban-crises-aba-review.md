# Summary
This IIED working paper reviews available humanitarian and development literature on area-based approaches (ABAs) in urban crises and supplements it with practitioner interviews and a workshop session (pp. 4-5). Its purpose is to inform practice/policy and identify knowledge gaps for further research (pp. 4-5).

The paper argues that in urban humanitarian contexts ABAs are best understood by three combined characteristics: geographically targeted design, participatory processes, and multi-sectoral delivery (pp. 8, 21-22). It also notes persistent terminology fragmentation across "area-based," "neighbourhood," "settlement," and "integrated" labels (pp. 4, 11, 21).

Using case material from Kabul, Port-au-Prince, Baghdad, and Tacloban, the review highlights why agencies are adopting ABAs in cities: complexity of beneficiary targeting, need for stronger inter-sector coordination, and need to work with municipal/non-humanitarian actors in system-dependent urban settings (pp. 12-13, 21-22).

Its central conclusion is conditional: ABAs are not intrinsically good or bad; outcomes depend on context, design quality, implementation practice, and whether ABA is the right strategy for the specific problem (pp. 3, 22). The paper closes with practical improvement lessons and a research agenda focused on when/how to adopt ABAs and how they compare with alternative targeting approaches (pp. 22-23).

# Key concepts
- Area-based approach (urban humanitarian context): defined in this review as an approach that is geographically targeted and uses participatory, multi-sectoral delivery; all three elements must be present (pp. 8, 21).
- Geographical targeting: intervention area is spatially bounded (administrative/physical/social) and selected as the entry point for programming (pp. 8-10).
- Participatory approach: residents and local stakeholders are engaged in assessment, planning, implementation, and sometimes monitoring, with community processes central to delivery (pp. 10, 19).
- Multi-sectoral approach: responses combine multiple sectors/services to match interconnected urban needs rather than single-sector delivery (pp. 10-11).
- Inclusive area logic: ABA can reduce tension where host and displaced populations are both included in support within one target area (pp. 9, 14).
- Multi-scale linkage challenge: neighbourhood interventions risk weak linkage to city/regional systems unless explicit vertical integration is built in (pp. 18, 22).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Rapid evidence-review method: desk literature review plus expert interviews/workshop verification to synthesize emerging ABA evidence (p. 5).
- Two-pronged source identification: broad database scoping plus direct practitioner outreach (including Urban Response Community of Practice) to find case material (p. 5).
- Three-characteristic ABA test (Figure 1 logic): check whether a programme is geographically targeted, participatory, and multi-sectoral before classifying as ABA (pp. 8, 21).
- Targeting-options comparison lens (Box 3): compare ABA with market-, systems-, institution-, and household-based approaches before strategy choice (p. 13).
- District Council Coordination Meetings example: used in Baghdad as a platform to connect settlement residents and district councils (p. 14).
- Participatory planning tool example (PASSA): cited in the Haiti case as a structured community hazard/vulnerability planning process (p. 10).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Context/strategy selection: emphasizes that ABA should be adopted selectively after comparing alternatives and organizational capacity (pp. 17, 22).
- Design phase: requires clear scope, intended outcomes, and realistic expectations matched to area boundaries and timeline (pp. 17-18, 22).
- Set-up phase: early stakeholder engagement (municipal, civil society, residents) is a core precondition for trust and implementation feasibility (pp. 18-19, 22).
- Implementation phase: calls for flexible management, adaptable resources, and iterative adjustment as context evolves (pp. 19-20, 22).
- Transition/scale phase: stresses linkage of neighborhood plans to wider city/regional plans and policy pathways for sustainability (pp. 18, 22).

# Field data collection implications
- Collect evidence on both area-level social dynamics and service/system conditions, since needs are interconnected in urban settings (pp. 10-13).
- Build participatory data processes with residents and local structures, not only external agency-led data extraction (pp. 19-20).
- Capture mobility and tenure realities because high population movement complicates baseline/follow-up interpretation (p. 16).
- Track outcomes beyond single-sector outputs; multi-factor urban change makes attribution difficult and should be anticipated in MEL design (p. 16).
- Document early decision-making rationale (why ABA vs alternatives), which the review identifies as poorly captured in current literature (p. 23).

# Coordination implications
- ABAs can function as a multi-actor coordination mechanism in dense urban contexts where sector silos underperform (pp. 12-13).
- Effective ABA requires coordination beyond humanitarian actors, including municipalities, civil society, and private-sector/service actors (pp. 13, 18).
- Without explicit integration, area plans may become isolated from wider city systems and policies (pp. 18, 22).
- Coordination and funding architecture constraints are identified as practical barriers requiring further sector-level analysis (pp. 17, 23).

# DRR implications
- The review links urban ABA adoption to the challenge of "urbanisation of disaster risk" and concentration of vulnerability in informal settlements (p. 4).
- Case examples include risk-reduction related activities (eg drainage, hazard mitigation training, safer settlement planning) within multi-sector packages (pp. 7, 11).
- DRR benefit is conditional on scale-linkage: local risk reduction gains may be undermined if disconnected from city-wide systems/plans (pp. 18, 22).

# Operational implications
- Do not default to ABA; assess suitability case-by-case against other approaches and implementation capacity (pp. 17, 22).
- Define scope tightly and communicate limits early to manage expectations and reduce implementation friction (pp. 17-18, 22).
- Plan longer timelines or phased pathways where participation and institutional linkage are required; short humanitarian windows create trade-offs (pp. 16-18).
- Budget for coordination/management overhead in complex multi-sector delivery and for adaptive management during execution (pp. 16, 19-20).
- Build quick-win interventions to sustain trust and participation while longer planning processes mature (p. 20).

# Claims worth citing
- "Three defining characteristics" of ABAs in urban contexts are geographic targeting, participatory process, and multi-sectoral delivery (pp. 3, 8, 21).
- ABA outcomes are context-dependent: effects depend on context, design, delivery, and appropriateness of strategy choice (pp. 3, 22).
- Urban response challenges include beneficiary identification complexity, coordination gaps, and dependence on partnerships with non-humanitarian actors (pp. 12-13, 22).
- Poorly designed ABAs can increase inequality between target and surrounding areas and weaken system linkage across scales (pp. 15, 22).
- Improvement priorities include selective adoption, clear scoping, timeline realism, city-linkage, sustained stakeholder engagement, flexibility, and early visible results (pp. 17-20, 22).

# Limitations / cautions
- The paper is an initial rapid review and explicitly identifies itself as a foundation rather than definitive evidence (p. 5).
- Case coverage is constrained by available, documented, English-language material and has geographic gaps noted by the authors (p. 5).
- The authors report limited well-documented humanitarian cases and call for further primary research, especially on decision-making, funding/coordination constraints, and local perspectives (pp. 17, 23).
- Monitoring and evaluation attribution is difficult in urban ABA contexts because outcomes are affected by multiple concurrent factors and high mobility (p. 16).
