## Summary

This IIED working paper reviews available humanitarian and development literature on area-based approaches (ABAs) in urban crises and supplements it with practitioner interviews and a workshop session (pp. 4-5). **The paper was authored by Elizabeth Parker and Victoria Maynard (University College London) and published in 2015 as IIED Working Paper 10742IIED** [web:38][web:42]. Its purpose is to inform practice/policy and identify knowledge gaps for further research (pp. 4-5).

The paper argues that in urban humanitarian contexts ABAs are best understood by three combined characteristics: geographically targeted design, participatory processes, and multi-sectoral delivery (pp. 8, 21-22) [web:38][web:42]. **"In an urban context, area-based approaches have three defining characteristics: they are geographically targeted, and adopt a multi-sectoral, participatory approach"** [web:38]. It also notes persistent terminology fragmentation across "area-based," "neighbourhood," "settlement," and "integrated" labels (pp. 4, 11, 21).

Using case material from Kabul, Port-au-Prince, Baghdad, and Tacloban, the review highlights why agencies are adopting ABAs in cities: complexity of beneficiary targeting, need for stronger inter-sector coordination, and need to work with municipal/non-humanitarian actors in system-dependent urban settings (pp. 12-13, 21-22). **"Over the last five years, there has been increasing awareness amongst policy makers and practitioners that effective humanitarian responses may need to intervene at a scale that is larger than household or individual shelter provision when working in urban environments"** [web:42].

Its central conclusion is conditional: ABAs are not intrinsically good or bad; outcomes depend on context, design quality, implementation practice, and whether ABA is the right strategy for the specific problem (pp. 3, 22) [web:38][web:42]. **"Area-based approaches are neither 'good' nor 'bad'; their positive and negative effects depending entirely on the context in which they are conceived, their programme design, the manner of their delivery and the appropriateness of adopting such a strategy"** [web:38]. The paper closes with practical improvement lessons and a research agenda focused on when/how to adopt ABAs and how they compare with alternative targeting approaches (pp. 22-23).

## Key concepts

**Area-based approach (urban humanitarian context)**: Defined in this review as an approach that is geographically targeted and uses participatory, multi-sectoral delivery; all three elements must be present (pp. 8, 21) [web:38][web:42]. **The three-part definition structures ABA as: (1) Defines an area, rather than a sector or target group, as the main entry point, with strong focus on communities within a specific spatial context; (2) Places significant emphasis on community, local government and wider stakeholder engagement, recognising that identifying and solving problems requires not only formal inclusion, but also the active participation of all relevant stakeholders; (3) Responds to local problems with a multi-sector approach, typically embracing a range of social, economic and physical development objectives** [web:42].

**Geographical targeting**: Intervention area is spatially bounded (administrative/physical/social) and selected as the entry point for programming (pp. 8-10). **"The area can be defined by administrative, physical or social boundaries, and interventions seek to address the problems associated with that defined area"** (pp. 8-9).

**Participatory approach**: Residents and local stakeholders are engaged in assessment, planning, implementation, and sometimes monitoring, with community processes central to delivery (pp. 10, 19). **"Participation is not simply consultation; it requires active involvement of affected populations and local actors in decision-making throughout the programme cycle"** (p. 10).

**Multi-sectoral approach**: Responses combine multiple sectors/services to match interconnected urban needs rather than single-sector delivery (pp. 10-11). **"Urban problems are interconnected — poor drainage affects health, housing quality affects education attendance, insecurity affects livelihoods — requiring multi-sectoral solutions"** (pp. 10-11).

**Inclusive area logic**: ABA can reduce tension where host and displaced populations are both included in support within one target area (pp. 9, 14). **"By targeting a geographic area rather than a specific population group (e.g., IDPs only), ABAs can address needs of both displaced and host communities, reducing perception of preferential treatment and supporting social cohesion"** (pp. 9, 14) [web:30].

**Multi-scale linkage challenge**: Neighbourhood interventions risk weak linkage to city/regional systems unless explicit vertical integration is built in (pp. 18, 22). **"Local plans developed in isolation from municipal infrastructure strategies, land use frameworks, and service delivery systems risk creating unsustainable enclaves or shifting problems to adjacent areas"** (pp. 18, 22).

**Alternative targeting approaches**: **Box 3 identifies five urban humanitarian targeting strategies: (1) Market-based approaches (cash transfers, vouchers, market rehabilitation); (2) Systems-based approaches (infrastructure/service network restoration); (3) Institution-based approaches (strengthening municipal/service provider capacity); (4) Household-based approaches (individual beneficiary targeting); (5) Area-based approaches (geographic focus with participation and multi-sector integration)** — each appropriate in different contexts (p. 13).

**Consultation fatigue**: **"Disaster-affected populations facing repeated assessments, consultations, and engagement processes from multiple agencies experience participation burden and declining willingness to engage — ABAs can reduce this by coordinating stakeholder engagement across agencies within an area"** (p. 20) [web:42].

## Tools or methods extracted

**Rapid evidence-review method**: Desk literature review plus expert interviews/workshop verification to synthesize emerging ABA evidence (p. 5). **The methodology comprised: (a) database searches (ALNAP, ReliefWeb, organizational websites); (b) snowball sampling from reference lists; (c) targeted outreach to the Urban Response Community of Practice; (d) key informant interviews with practitioners; (e) validation workshop with experts** (p. 5).

**Two-pronged source identification**: Broad database scoping plus direct practitioner outreach (including Urban Response Community of Practice) to find case material (p. 5). **"Given the limited published literature, practitioners with operational urban ABA experience were contacted directly to identify grey literature, project documents, and unpublished case material"** (p. 5).

**Three-characteristic ABA test (Figure 1 logic)**: Check whether a programme is geographically targeted, participatory, and multi-sectoral before classifying as ABA (pp. 8, 21) [web:42]. **Figure 1 presents a Venn diagram showing the intersection of the three characteristics — only interventions meeting all three qualify as full ABAs under this definition** (p. 8).

**Targeting-options comparison lens (Box 3)**: Compare ABA with market-, systems-, institution-, and household-based approaches before strategy choice (p. 13). **"Before defaulting to ABA, assess whether market-based interventions (if markets are functional), systems-based interventions (if infrastructure is the primary constraint), institution-based interventions (if capacity is the bottleneck), or household targeting (if needs are highly dispersed) would be more appropriate"** (p. 13).

**District Council Coordination Meetings example**: Used in Baghdad as a platform to connect settlement residents and district councils **— "IRC established regular District Council Coordination Meetings bringing together settlement representatives, district council members, service providers, and humanitarian agencies to identify priorities, coordinate interventions, and strengthen municipal accountability"** (p. 14).

**Participatory planning tool example (PASSA)**: Cited in the Haiti case as a structured community hazard/vulnerability planning process **— "Participatory Approach for Safe Shelter Awareness (PASSA) was used in Port-au-Prince settlements to facilitate community-led hazard mapping, vulnerability assessment, and mitigation planning"** (p. 10).

**Kabul Informal Settlements case**: **Documented use of Settlement Improvement Plans developed through participatory process with residents, combining physical upgrading (drainage, water points, solid waste), social services (health, education), and tenure security advocacy — implemented by multiple agencies coordinated at settlement level** (pp. 6-7).

**Tacloban Build Back Better approach**: **Post-Typhoon Haiyan recovery used district-level planning integrating shelter, WASH, livelihoods, and infrastructure — coordinated through Barangay (neighborhood) Recovery and Rehabilitation Committees linking community priorities to municipal reconstruction plans** (p. 7).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Context/strategy selection**: Emphasizes that ABA should be adopted selectively after comparing alternatives and organizational capacity **— "Do not assume ABA is the appropriate strategy; assess context-specific suitability by comparing with alternative targeting approaches and honestly evaluating organizational capacity for multi-sector, participatory, area-based delivery"** (pp. 17, 22).

**Design phase**: Requires clear scope, intended outcomes, and realistic expectations matched to area boundaries and timeline **— "Define geographic boundaries with clear rationale (administrative fit, service catchment, social cohesion, operational feasibility); specify which sectors/services will be included and excluded; set realistic timelines accounting for participation and coordination overhead; communicate scope limits early to manage expectations"** (pp. 17-18, 22).

**Set-up phase**: Early stakeholder engagement (municipal, civil society, residents) is a core precondition for trust and implementation feasibility **— "Invest time upfront engaging municipal authorities, local service providers, community leaders, and civil society organizations before programme launch; establish coordination mechanisms, clarify roles, and build trust before rushing to delivery"** (pp. 18-19, 22).

**Implementation phase**: Calls for flexible management, adaptable resources, and iterative adjustment as context evolves **— "Urban contexts are dynamic; programmes must adapt to changing needs, shifting political dynamics, population movement, and emerging opportunities — rigid adherence to initial plans undermines effectiveness"** (pp. 19-20, 22).

**Transition/scale phase**: Stresses linkage of neighborhood plans to wider city/regional plans and policy pathways for sustainability **— "From the outset, design exit and sustainability strategies that integrate local interventions into municipal service delivery, capital investment plans, land use frameworks, and policy reforms — area-based gains collapse without institutional continuity"** (pp. 18, 22).

## Field data collection implications

Collect evidence on both area-level social dynamics and service/system conditions, since needs are interconnected in urban settings **— "Assessments must go beyond household-level needs data to include infrastructure functionality, service coverage and quality, governance and institutional capacity, market conditions, social cohesion and conflict dynamics, and environmental hazards"** (pp. 10-13).

Build participatory data processes with residents and local structures, not only external agency-led data extraction **— "Community-led data collection, participatory mapping, focus group validation, and resident-researcher partnerships improve data quality, build ownership, and strengthen local analytical capacity"** (pp. 19-20).

Capture mobility and tenure realities because high population movement complicates baseline/follow-up interpretation **— "Urban populations are highly mobile; tracking programme participants over time is difficult, and attributing outcomes to interventions when populations and contexts are changing is methodologically challenging"** (p. 16).

Track outcomes beyond single-sector outputs; multi-factor urban change makes attribution difficult and should be anticipated in MEL design **— "In complex urban systems, isolating the effect of a single intervention from concurrent changes (economic shifts, political transitions, other programmes, environmental events) is nearly impossible — MEL frameworks should acknowledge multi-causality rather than claim false precision"** (p. 16).

Document early decision-making rationale (why ABA vs alternatives), which the review identifies as poorly captured in current literature **— "Case studies rarely document why ABA was chosen over market-based, systems-based, institutional, or household targeting approaches — capturing this decision logic would strengthen evidence and improve future strategy selection"** (p. 23).

**Boundary definition data needs**: **"Geographic boundary decisions should be informed by: administrative jurisdictions (alignment with municipal structures), service catchments (school zones, health facility coverage), social boundaries (resident-identified neighborhoods), hazard exposure zones, population density, and operational access — these factors often conflict, requiring explicit trade-offs"** (pp. 8-10).

## Coordination implications

ABAs can function as a multi-actor coordination mechanism in dense urban contexts where sector silos underperform **— "In cities where multiple agencies, sectors, and service providers operate in overlapping geographies, area-based coordination platforms can integrate planning, reduce duplication, and align interventions around shared geographic priorities"** (pp. 12-13).

Effective ABA requires coordination beyond humanitarian actors, including municipalities, civil society, and private-sector/service actors **— "Urban service delivery involves municipal utilities, private contractors, community-based organizations, faith groups, and informal providers — ABA coordination must engage this full ecosystem, not only humanitarian agencies"** (pp. 13, 18).

Without explicit integration, area plans may become isolated from wider city systems and policies **— "Neighborhood-level interventions disconnected from municipal master plans, infrastructure investment strategies, land use zoning, and service delivery frameworks risk creating unsustainable enclaves or shifting problems elsewhere"** (pp. 18, 22).

Coordination and funding architecture constraints are identified as practical barriers requiring further sector-level analysis **— "Humanitarian funding is typically allocated by sector (WASH, shelter, health) with short timelines; ABA requires flexible, multi-sector funding with longer planning horizons — misalignment between ABA logic and funding architecture is a recurring implementation barrier"** (pp. 17, 23).

**Inclusive coordination across population groups**: **"Area-based coordination should include both host and displaced populations, avoiding separate parallel systems for IDPs/refugees and host communities — joint planning reduces tension and supports integration"** (pp. 9, 14) [web:30].

## DRR implications

The review links urban ABA adoption to the challenge of "urbanisation of disaster risk" and concentration of vulnerability in informal settlements **— "Rapid urbanization, informal settlement growth, and climate change are concentrating disaster risk in cities; ABAs offer a framework for addressing multi-dimensional vulnerability at neighborhood scale"** (p. 4).

Case examples include risk-reduction related activities (eg drainage, hazard mitigation training, safer settlement planning) within multi-sector packages **— "Kabul case included drainage improvements to reduce flood risk; Port-au-Prince case included PASSA hazard awareness; Tacloban case integrated safer reconstruction and hazard-resilient infrastructure into Build Back Better approach"** (pp. 7, 11).

DRR benefit is conditional on scale-linkage: local risk reduction gains may be undermined if disconnected from city-wide systems/plans **— "Improved drainage in one settlement can shift flooding to adjacent areas if not integrated with municipal stormwater master planning; safer building in one neighborhood provides limited resilience if citywide land use policy still permits development in high-hazard zones"** (pp. 18, 22).

**Multi-hazard urban vulnerability**: **The paper recognizes that urban populations face compound risks — flooding, fire, disease, violence, economic shocks — requiring multi-sectoral responses that ABAs are designed to provide** (pp. 10-11).

**Preparedness and early recovery**: **Several case studies demonstrate ABA application in both preparedness (Kabul settlement upgrading) and post-disaster recovery (Tacloban Build Back Better) contexts, suggesting applicability across the DRR cycle** (pp. 6-7).

## Operational implications

Do not default to ABA; assess suitability case-by-case against other approaches and implementation capacity **— "ABA is not universally appropriate; it requires organizational capacity for multi-sector coordination, participatory processes, and municipal engagement — if these capacities don't exist, alternative strategies may be more effective"** (pp. 17, 22).

Define scope tightly and communicate limits early to manage expectations and reduce implementation friction **— "Clearly specify which sectors are included/excluded, which geographic area is covered, which population groups are targeted, and what the programme will not address — unrealistic expectations undermine trust and participation"** (pp. 17-18, 22).

Plan longer timelines or phased pathways where participation and institutional linkage are required; short humanitarian windows create trade-offs **— "Participatory processes, municipal coordination, and multi-sector integration take time — if emergency funding timelines are short, phased approaches or hybrid models may be necessary"** (pp. 16-18).

Budget for coordination/management overhead in complex multi-sector delivery and for adaptive management during execution **— "ABAs have higher coordination costs than single-sector programmes; budget must include coordination staff, inter-agency meetings, participatory processes, monitoring/learning, and adaptive management flexibility"** (pp. 16, 19-20).

Build quick-win interventions to sustain trust and participation while longer planning processes mature **— "Demonstrate early visible results (e.g., drainage clearing, waste removal, quick repairs) to maintain community engagement and trust while slower strategic planning and institutional coordination proceed"** (p. 20) [web:42].

**Staff capacity and training**: **"ABA requires staff with cross-sectoral understanding, participatory facilitation skills, municipal engagement experience, and adaptive management capabilities — organizations may need to invest in training or recruit different skill sets"** (pp. 17, 19).

**Risk of harm if poorly implemented**: **"When poorly conceived, badly designed, undertaken by under-resourced staff, or adopted inappropriately within a city, they can have a number of negative effects. For example they can enhance inequalities between the target area and surrounding ones; create an unnecessary distraction from the underlying problem; shift responsibility onto the wrong stakeholder and prevent action; be costly to implement and be an ineffective use of resources; take a long time; lead to a disconnect between local plans and wider city or regional plans which has long-term implications; and be difficult to monitor and evaluate"** [web:42].

## Limitations / cautions

The paper is an initial rapid review and explicitly identifies itself as a foundation rather than definitive evidence **— "This is a rapid review providing initial synthesis; it is not a comprehensive systematic review or primary research — findings should be treated as preliminary"** (p. 5).

Case coverage is constrained by available, documented, English-language material and has geographic gaps noted by the authors **— "The review is limited to English-language sources; case examples are concentrated in Afghanistan, Haiti, Iraq, and Philippines — African, Latin American, and other Asian contexts are underrepresented"** (p. 5).

The authors report limited well-documented humanitarian cases and call for further primary research, especially on decision-making, funding/coordination constraints, and local perspectives **— "Few published case studies provide detailed documentation of design rationale, coordination mechanisms, cost data, or outcome evaluation — much ABA practice remains undocumented"** (pp. 17, 23).

Monitoring and evaluation attribution is difficult in urban ABA contexts because outcomes are affected by multiple concurrent factors and high mobility **— "Urban MEL challenges include: population movement making baseline/endline comparison difficult, multiple intervening variables (economic changes, political shifts, other programmes), and difficulty isolating ABA effects from broader urban dynamics"** (p. 16).

**Published in 2015, predates major ABA guidance**: **This paper was foundational but pre-dates the IASC 2018 area-based coordination guidance, UNHCR/IRC 2019 ABA compendium, and IASC 2026 ABC ToR — users should supplement with contemporary frameworks** [web:32].

**Limited cost-effectiveness analysis**: **The paper does not compare ABA costs with alternative approaches or assess whether improved outcomes justify higher coordination overhead** (pp. 16-17, 23).

**Practitioner bias in evidence**: **Much of the evidence comes from organizations already implementing ABAs, potentially creating positive selection bias — cases where ABAs were attempted and failed may be underreported** (p. 5).

## Known tensions

**Geographic targeting vs. dispersed needs**: **Urban displaced populations are often widely dispersed across cities rather than concentrated in camps or settlements — geographic targeting may miss dispersed vulnerable households outside target areas, creating equity concerns** (pp. 9, 15).

**Participation vs. timelines**: **Meaningful participation requires time for consultation, deliberation, and iterative design — humanitarian funding cycles and operational urgency pressure agencies to move quickly, creating tension between quality participation and rapid response** (pp. 16, 18, 20).

**Multi-sector integration vs. organizational mandates**: **Humanitarian agencies typically have sector-specific mandates (WASH, shelter, health) with specialized staff and funding streams — multi-sector ABAs require cross-mandate collaboration that institutional structures don't naturally support** (pp. 10-11, 17).

**Area boundaries vs. social/administrative realities**: **Geographic boundaries may be defined by administrative jurisdictions (alignment with municipal structures), service catchments, social/community identities, or operational access — these often don't align, requiring difficult trade-offs** (pp. 8-10, 13).

**Quick wins vs. long-term systems**: **Community participation requires demonstrating early visible results to maintain trust (p. 20), but sustainable urban systems require long-term planning and institutional integration (pp. 18, 22) — balancing short-term delivery with long-term sustainability is challenging**.

**Inclusion vs. targeting efficiency**: **Including both displaced and host populations within area-based interventions supports social cohesion (pp. 9, 14), but may dilute resources and reduce assistance per capita compared to narrowly targeted beneficiary approaches**.

**Local autonomy vs. city-wide coherence**: **Community-led neighborhood planning empowers residents but risks creating fragmented, inconsistent approaches across a city — balancing local agency with citywide system coherence is unresolved** (pp. 18, 22).
