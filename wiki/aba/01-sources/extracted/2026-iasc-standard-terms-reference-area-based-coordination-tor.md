# Summary
This document provides IASC-endorsed standard Terms of Reference (TOR) for area-based coordination (ABC), intended as a minimum common reference for humanitarian operations adopting ABC under IASC-supported coordination arrangements (pp. 1-2).

It situates ABC within broader coordination reform and flexibility trends, noting ABC and clusters are not mutually exclusive and should generally co-exist as complementary tools according to context and operational needs (pp. 1, 8).

The TOR defines ABC as a light, subnational, operational, multi-sectoral and participatory coordination mechanism, chaired by an actor present in the area and linked to national IASC structures for alignment and escalation (p. 2).

The document then specifies establishment criteria, chair selection and setup steps, core functions, actor roles, membership composition, and dissolution/transition triggers, while explicitly acknowledging unresolved inter-agency questions and expected future updates (pp. 2-8).

# Key concepts
- Area-based coordination (ABC): a light operational coordination structure focused on a defined subnational geography, linked to wider IASC structures (p. 2).
- Complementarity with clusters: ABC does not replace sector coordination; clusters retain sectoral standards/analysis while ABC coordinates integrated area delivery (pp. 1, 5, 8).
- Localized and participatory coordination: ABC emphasizes local actor engagement, community participation, and accountability to affected people (pp. 2, 6).
- Centrality of Protection at area level: ABC supports protection risk understanding and area-level protection outcomes aligned with national CoP efforts (p. 5).
- Time-bound modality: ABC is not intended to be indefinite and should transition/disband when criteria are met (pp. 7-8).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Establishment criteria checklist (multi-sector needs concentration, coordination gap, chair capacity, area prioritization, and use/adaptation of existing mechanisms) (p. 3).
- Chair/co-chair selection process with expression-of-interest and participant voting, then HC/HCT endorsement (pp. 3-4).
- Setup steps for HC/HCT/ICCG (scope definition, accessibility barriers, channels, milestones, concept note/TOR, local outreach) (p. 4).
- Core function framework for ABC groups (operational coordination, integrated assessment/planning, service disruption tracking, protection, access, advocacy, AAP, PSEA, surge) (pp. 4-6).
- Dissolution/transition criteria and governance path (proposal by chair/HCT/HC; HC final decision; lessons learned requirement) (pp. 7-8).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Activation/scoping: supports decision on whether ABC is needed and where it should be geographically bounded (pp. 3-4).
- Setup phase: provides concrete sequence for launching governance, participation channels, and co-chairing arrangements (p. 4).
- Delivery phase: defines recurring operational coordination functions for multi-sector response quality and access/problem-solving (pp. 4-6).
- Adaptation phase: requires regular adjustment of geography/duration and escalation pathways as context shifts (p. 4).
- Transition/close-out: gives formal conditions and authority process for dissolving or transitioning ABC modalities (pp. 7-8).

# Field data collection implications
- ABC should facilitate context-sensitive joint needs assessments with integrated analysis while avoiding duplication with ongoing assessments (pp. 4-5).
- Data produced through ABC remains inside the wider humanitarian information ecosystem and feeds clusters/ICCG systems (p. 5).
- Sector-specific severity/gap analysis remains with clusters/sectors; ABC adds contextual multisector operational insight (p. 5).
- Community preference/concern channels should be systematic and transmitted upward to ICCG/HCT levels (p. 6).

# Coordination implications
- HC/HCT retain authority to determine coordination architecture and endorse ABC leadership arrangements (pp. 3-4).
- OCHA and ICCG roles are explicitly defined for facilitation, information flow, and national-subnational coherence (p. 6).
- Clear role delineation between ABC and clusters is required to avoid overlap and preserve technical quality standards (pp. 5-6, 8).
- Membership is context-specific but should prioritize operational actors physically present in the area and keep mechanisms nimble (p. 7).

# DRR implications
- The TOR is primarily humanitarian coordination guidance; DRR is not framed as a standalone pillar but appears through risk-sensitive planning, contingency planning, and transition linkages (pp. 5-6).
- ABC’s linkages with development, durable solutions, and peacebuilding actors can support risk-informed transition pathways where feasible (p. 5).
- Area-level service disruption identification and surge coordination can strengthen readiness and response to localized escalations (pp. 5-6).

# Operational implications
- ABC should operate as a problem-solving platform at local level and escalate only when needed, improving response agility (p. 4).
- Accessibility barriers to participation (language, connectivity, mobility, security) must be actively mitigated to make coordination functional and inclusive (p. 4).
- Coordination should avoid becoming fundraising-oriented; core participation should reflect real operational capacity/presence (p. 7).
- Succession planning and capacity support for national/local leadership handover are expected where chairs are not local actors (pp. 4, 6).

# Claims worth citing
- ABC is defined as a light, subnational, operational coordination mechanism that is multi-sectoral and participatory (p. 2).
- ABC and clusters are not mutually exclusive; ABC should generally co-exist with cluster coordination rather than replace it (pp. 1, 8).
- Establishment should be based on concentrated multi-sector needs/protection risks and coordination gaps not covered by other mechanisms (p. 3).
- Sectoral standards and sector-specific analysis remain cluster responsibilities; ABC contributes contextual and operational multisector insights (p. 5).
- ABC is not intended to exist indefinitely and should be dissolved or transitioned when predefined conditions are met (pp. 7-8).

# Limitations / cautions
- The TOR explicitly states several core policy/architecture questions are unresolved and pending inter-agency agreement, with planned updates to the document (pp. 1-2).
- It is a standard reference and working draft to adapt by operation, not a one-size-fits-all implementation manual (p. 1).
- Existing operations already using ABC are not required to realign TORs to this document, which may lead to variation across contexts (p. 1).
