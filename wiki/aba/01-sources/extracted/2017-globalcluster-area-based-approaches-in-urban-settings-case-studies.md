## Summary

This compendium (May 2019 edition) consolidates case studies on area-based approaches (ABAs) in urban settings, coordinated by the Global Shelter Cluster Urban Settlements Working Group, with examples spanning humanitarian and development contexts from 2010-2019 (pp. 1, 6). **The compendium includes over 30 case studies contributed by more than 60 organizations including ACT Alliance, CRS, IFRC, Impact Initiatives, InterAction, IRC, NRC, UN-Habitat, UNHCR, and many others** (pp. 1, 6) [web:32][web:37][web:60]. It presents cases across city, district/borough, and neighborhood scales and synthesizes common characteristics, achievements, challenges, and improvement priorities (pp. 5-6, 11-13).

The document defines ABAs as geographically targeted, multi-sectoral, multi-stakeholder approaches that consider the whole population in an affected area, especially where needs are complex and interrelated (pp. 4, 9). **"Area-based approaches provide support to the whole population in a specific geographic area with high levels of need. The approach is multi-sectoral and engages different stakeholders, including humanitarian and development partners, government, civil society, private sector and affected populations"** (p. 9) [web:30][web:32]. It emphasizes that this is one approach among several (alongside individual/household, systems-, market-, institution-, and advocacy-based approaches), and that suitability depends on context, mandate, and capacity (pp. 10-11).

A major operational contribution is a practical framing of scales, process stages, and coordination options. It describes common process stages (initiation, assessment/data collection, analysis/planning, plan approval, implementation/monitoring), and documents varied coordination arrangements (city working groups, sub-city taskforces, agency-led local coordination, community committees) (pp. 11-12). **Cases span 19 countries including Afghanistan, Bangladesh, Colombia, DRC, Ecuador, Haiti, Iraq, Jordan, Lebanon, Libya, Nepal, Nigeria, Pakistan, Philippines, Somalia, South Sudan, Syria, Ukraine, and Yemen** (pp. 6-8) [web:32].

The synthesis highlights that well-designed ABAs can improve coherence, stakeholder alignment, and integrated response quality, but poorly designed ABAs can produce exclusions, tensions, political distortion, weak city-linkage, high costs, and monitoring/evaluation difficulties (pp. 10, 12-13). **"When poorly conceived, badly designed, undertaken by under-resourced staff, or adopted inappropriately within a city, area-based approaches can enhance inequalities between the target area and surrounding ones; create an unnecessary distraction from the underlying problem; shift responsibility onto the wrong stakeholder and prevent action; be costly to implement and be an ineffective use of resources; take a long time; lead to a disconnect between local plans and wider city or regional plans; and be difficult to monitor and evaluate"** (p. 10). The paper closes with practical strengthening measures and a research agenda on enabling conditions, impacts over time, and comparative efficiency/effectiveness versus alternatives (p. 13).

## Key concepts

**Area-based approach (ABA)**: Support to the whole population in a defined geographic area with high needs through multi-sectoral and multi-stakeholder action (pp. 9, 13). **"Area-based approaches are characterized by four core elements: (1) Geographic targeting of a defined area based on physical, social, or administrative boundaries; (2) Multi-sectoral interventions addressing interconnected needs across sectors; (3) Multi-stakeholder engagement including government, civil society, private sector, affected populations, and humanitarian/development actors; (4) Inclusive whole-population focus considering all groups in the area regardless of legal status or category"** (p. 9) [web:30][web:62].

**Inclusive whole-population lens**: Includes all affected groups in target areas regardless of legal status or category, while also recognizing long-term residents **— "The approach considers the needs of the whole population in the area, including host communities, internally displaced persons (IDPs), refugees, returnees, and other vulnerable groups, rather than targeting specific population categories. This reduces the risk of creating tensions between groups and supports social cohesion"** (p. 9) [web:30].

**Geographic targeting**: Boundaries may be physical, social, administrative, or combined; scale can range from neighborhood to city **— "Geographic boundaries can be defined by: administrative divisions (municipalities, districts, wards); physical features (rivers, roads, infrastructure catchments); social boundaries (neighborhoods as locally understood); or combined criteria reflecting operational realities"** (pp. 9, 11).

**Multi-sectoral support**: Combines interventions across sectors (eg shelter, WASH, health, livelihoods, social protection, etc.) to address interconnected needs **— "Urban needs are interconnected — shelter depends on tenure security and HLP, WASH depends on energy and infrastructure access, livelihoods depend on transport and markets. Multi-sectoral approaches address these interdependencies rather than treating sectors in isolation"** (p. 9).

**Multi-stakeholder participation**: Requires active engagement of government, civil society, affected communities, private sector, and external actors as context requires **— "Effective area-based approaches require participation from: municipal and national government (service delivery, regulation, planning); civil society organizations (local knowledge, trust, service provision); affected populations (needs identification, priority setting, implementation); private sector (markets, employment, service providers); and humanitarian/development agencies (technical expertise, funding, coordination)"** (pp. 9-10, 13) [web:60].

**Multi-scale linkage**: ABA plans at local scales should connect with wider city/regional plans and policies **— "Neighborhood-level area-based interventions must link to district and city-wide infrastructure, service delivery systems, land use planning, and policy frameworks. Without these vertical linkages, local gains can be unsustainable or create unintended consequences elsewhere"** (pp. 10, 13).

**Settlement-based approach**: **An earlier term for area-based approach emphasizing the spatial unit of intervention; the compendium uses both terms interchangeably, with "area-based" becoming the more common terminology by 2019** (pp. 4, 9) [web:60][web:62].

## Tools or methods extracted

**ABA characteristics framework (Figure 1)**: Practical definitional model combining multi-sectoral, multi-stakeholder, whole-population, and geographic-focus dimensions **— Figure 1 presents a Venn diagram showing the intersection of these four characteristics, with full ABAs meeting all four criteria** (p. 9).

**Approach comparison frame (Figure 2)**: Positions ABA relative to individual/household, systems-, market-, institution-, and advocacy-based approaches **— "Different urban response strategies are appropriate in different contexts: (1) Individual/household approaches target specific beneficiaries; (2) Systems-based approaches strengthen infrastructure/service networks; (3) Market-based approaches use cash and vouchers; (4) Institution-based approaches build government/provider capacity; (5) Area-based approaches focus on geographic zones with multi-sectoral, multi-stakeholder integration. These approaches can be complementary rather than mutually exclusive"** (p. 10).

**Scale typology (Figure 3)**: City, district/borough, and ward/neighborhood operational scales with corresponding coordination options **— City scale typically involves citywide working groups with municipal government, UN agencies, and major NGOs; District/borough scale uses sub-city taskforces or tri-cluster coordination; Ward/neighborhood scale employs agency-led local coordination or community committees** (pp. 11-12).

**Five-stage process model**: Initiation, assessment/data collection, analysis/planning, approval, implementation/monitoring **— "(1) Initiation: Define geographic area, establish coordination structure, engage stakeholders; (2) Assessment/data collection: Multi-sector, multi-agency data collection with participatory methods; (3) Analysis/planning: Develop area-based plan integrating sector priorities and community input; (4) Approval: Secure government endorsement and multi-stakeholder agreement; (5) Implementation/monitoring: Adaptive delivery with regular coordination, monitoring, and adjustment"** (p. 12).

**Stakeholder participation package**: Mapping stakeholders and relationships, participatory assessment/planning tools, multi-sector working groups, and linked decision mechanisms **— "Effective stakeholder engagement requires: initial power and relationship mapping; use of participatory tools (community mapping, focus groups, settlement walks); establishment of representative community structures; creation of multi-sector coordination platforms; and clear decision-making processes linking community input to planning and resource allocation"** (p. 13) [web:60].

**Multi-agency M&E framing**: Track collective contribution to change rather than only single-agency attribution **— "In area-based approaches where multiple agencies work in the same geographic space, traditional attribution-focused M&E is inappropriate. M&E frameworks should track collective contribution to area-level outcomes (e.g., improved water access, reduced housing vulnerability, strengthened social cohesion) rather than isolating individual agency impacts"** (pp. 10, 13).

**Tri-cluster coordination model**: **"In Mogadishu, a tri-cluster system (initially shelter, WASH, and health, later expanded to education and protection) was adapted to reflect area-based approach, improving settlement planning and integrated service provision across multiple sectors"** [web:30].

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Initiation**: Define scale, boundaries, and coordination architecture suited to context and actors **— "Early decisions about geographic scale (city, district, neighborhood), boundary definition (administrative, social, physical), and coordination model (who leads, what structure, what decision authority) shape the entire programme trajectory. These decisions should reflect context analysis, stakeholder mapping, and organizational capacity assessment"** (pp. 11-12).

**Assessment**: Use multi-sector, multi-agency assessments with participatory methods to identify concentrated, interrelated needs **— "Area-based assessments should go beyond household surveys to include: infrastructure and service functionality mapping; stakeholder and governance analysis; social cohesion and tension dynamics; market and livelihood systems; and spatial/environmental hazards. Participatory methods (community mapping, settlement walks, focus groups) strengthen data quality and build trust"** (pp. 10, 12-13) [web:60].

**Planning**: Produce area-level plans while aligning with broader city/regional policies and sector systems **— "Area-based plans should specify: priority interventions across sectors; roles and responsibilities of different stakeholders; sequencing and phasing; budget and resource mobilization; linkages to municipal infrastructure and service delivery plans; and mechanisms for ongoing participation and adaptation"** (pp. 10-11, 13).

**Implementation**: Use flexible, iterative management and coordination to adapt to context shifts and evolving priorities **— "Urban contexts are volatile — populations move, political dynamics shift, economic conditions change, security situations evolve. Implementation requires: regular coordination meetings; adaptive resource allocation; monitoring of context changes; responsiveness to emerging needs and opportunities; and willingness to adjust plans rather than rigid adherence to initial designs"** (pp. 10, 13) [web:60].

**Monitoring/transition**: Evaluate multi-agency contribution, plan role transfer/exit pathways, and connect short-term action to longer-term service/governance outcomes **— "Area-based programmes should plan for transition and sustainability from the outset: building local capacity; integrating interventions into municipal service delivery; advocating for policy and budget allocation; strengthening community organization; and phasing external support rather than abrupt exit"** (pp. 10, 13).

## Field data collection implications

Data collection should be multi-sector and multi-agency, not single-sector, to capture interrelated needs in target areas **— "Joint multi-sector assessments reduce consultation fatigue, enable cross-sector analysis of interdependencies, improve data sharing, and support integrated planning. Case studies demonstrate value of coordinated assessment protocols and shared data platforms"** (pp. 10, 12-13) [web:60].

Participatory tools for community-based assessment, mapping, and planning are repeatedly cited as useful for trust and ownership **— "High levels of community engagement, supported through the use of maps, spatial data and participatory mapping exercises were important vehicles in achieving a nuanced understanding of the community/settlement"** [web:60]. **"Participatory tools include: community-led mapping; transect walks; focus group discussions; key informant interviews; community committees; and participatory planning workshops"** (pp. 12-13).

Assessment and planning should incorporate whole-population coverage logic (including hosts and long-term residents) to reduce exclusion risks **— "Data collection should capture needs, capacities, and vulnerabilities of all population groups in the target area — host communities, IDPs, refugees, returnees, migrants, and other vulnerable groups — to inform inclusive programming and avoid visible inequities that fuel tension"** (p. 9).

Localized area data should be linked upward to city/regional planning and policy data structures where possible **— "Assessment data should be structured to feed into municipal information systems, national databases, and sector monitoring frameworks. Using compatible formats, indicators, and spatial units facilitates integration and advocacy"** (pp. 12-13).

**Spatial data and GIS capacity**: **"Several case studies stressed the importance of investing in strong information management capacity to ensure effective monitoring of settlement-based programming, especially when undertaken by multiple actors. GIS, spatial data, and visualization tools support stakeholder communication and decision-making"** [web:60].

## Coordination implications

Effective ABAs rely on deliberate multi-sector coordination bodies at appropriate scales (city, sub-city, neighborhood) (pp. 11-12). **"Area-based coordination requires structures that match the geographic scale of intervention: citywide platforms for strategic alignment and resource mobilization; district/borough taskforces for operational coordination; neighborhood committees for community engagement and local implementation"** (pp. 11-12).

Coordination models vary by context and may include city-level working groups, tri-sector taskforces, agency-led local area coordination, and community committees **— "No single coordination model fits all contexts. Case studies demonstrate variety: in some contexts, municipal government leads; in others, UN agencies or international NGOs convene; in still others, community-based organizations drive coordination. Model selection depends on government capacity, security context, funding architecture, and actor relationships"** (p. 12) [web:30][web:60].

High stakeholder complexity can create coordination friction (role confusion, overlap, changing influence, sequencing problems), requiring strong facilitation **— "Engaging with multiple actors brought significant challenges in terms of resource investment, ensuring a thorough understanding of the environment, and often fluid relationships with actors to manage... Challenges can arise when balancing the needs and priorities of affected populations within the specific settlement, in relation to neighboring settlements and overall city level planning"** [web:60]. (pp. 12-13).

Sustainable coordination is more likely where donor backing, existing coordination culture, and supportive local government are present **— "Advocating for donors to support settlement-based approaches is critical to further improve and upscale the approach... Proactive engagement with different levels of government, acknowledging the challenges of balancing priorities at settlement/neighbourhood, city and national humanitarian and development priorities"** [web:60]. (p. 13).

**Cluster-ABA integration challenges**: **"Several case studies highlighted challenges associated with engaging with numerous coordination mechanisms, i.e. national and international coordination mechanisms operating during a humanitarian response, with mechanisms often structured sectorally, as opposed to geographically, which can bring confusion to coordination efforts"** [web:60].

## DRR implications

The compendium includes multiple cases with DRR components and stresses integrating risk reduction into area-based urban recovery and planning **— "Case studies from Nepal (post-earthquake), Philippines (post-Typhoon Haiyan), Haiti (post-earthquake), and other disaster contexts demonstrate integration of DRR into area-based approaches through: safer reconstruction with building standards; drainage and flood mitigation; slope stabilization; hazard mapping and community preparedness; land use planning to reduce exposure"** (pp. 5, 7, 23-24).

ABAs can contribute to social cohesion and reduced tension when they are inclusive and context-sensitive **— "Area-based approaches have been used to try to reduce tensions and inequalities and to improve social cohesion, e.g. an area-based program implemented by CARE International Lebanon in Tripoli, and an area-based program implemented by UN-HABITAT in Afghanistan"** [web:30]. (p. 10).

DRR/rehabilitation effectiveness depends on linking local area interventions to broader city systems and plans, rather than isolated enclaves **— "Isolated neighborhood improvements can shift risk to adjacent areas or create unsustainable enclaves. DRR effectiveness requires integration with citywide drainage master plans, municipal building codes, land use zoning, and infrastructure investment strategies"** (pp. 10-11, 13).

**Build Back Better integration**: **Multiple post-disaster case studies (Nepal, Philippines, Haiti) demonstrate application of Build Back Better principles through area-based approaches that combine physical reconstruction with social, economic, and governance strengthening** (pp. 6-8, 23-24).

## Operational implications

ABAs should be used selectively for areas with concentrated, complex multi-sector needs, not as a default for all settings **— "Area-based approaches are one tool among several. They are most appropriate when: needs are geographically concentrated; problems are multi-sectoral and interconnected; whole-population inclusion is important for social cohesion; municipal engagement is feasible; and sufficient time, funding, and capacity exist for multi-stakeholder coordination"** (pp. 10, 13).

Programmes need sufficient time, funding, and urban-specific expertise (community engagement, settlement planning, land issues, engineering, etc.) **— "Area-based approaches require longer timelines than standard humanitarian projects, flexible multi-sector funding, and staff with urban-specific skills: participatory planning facilitation, municipal engagement, HLP expertise, settlement planning, infrastructure engineering, and adaptive management"** (p. 13).

Working with and strengthening existing local governance/service systems is emphasized over parallel replacement structures **— "Capacity strengthening of local actors: Several case studies prioritized direct engagement with and capacity strengthening of local municipal authorities. Engagement with authorities was considered vital to ensure projects: remain participatory and inclusive; increase legitimacy and local support; and contribute to more macro-level government priorities"** [web:60]. (pp. 10, 13).

Flexible and adaptive coordination/program management is required in rapidly changing urban contexts **— "Urban risk and volatility: Whilst not exclusive to urban contexts, risks and impact of urban instability and violence can be compounded in urban environments with poor socio-economic indicators and diverse population groups... Anticipate a fluid urban context: A strong focus on planning and actor mapping is vital, as is the need to remain flexible in an often fluid urban environment"** [web:60]. (pp. 10, 13).

**HLP complexity**: **"Complex Housing, Land and Property contexts: Settlement based programming including a Housing, Land and Property (HLP) focus was relatively common. However, in numerous case studies, proof of ownership was non-existent (for various reasons) in urban contexts. Close collaboration with numerous actors on sensitive HLP issues required significant time-investment and could become a source of tension"** [web:60].

**Resource realism**: **"Be realistic about coverage: Projects following a settlement based approach can have a catchment area incorporating hundreds of thousands of people. Agencies need to be realistic about what can be undertaken in large catchment areas and promote strong coordination and partnerships wherever possible"** [web:60].

## Claims worth citing

ABAs are defined as support to whole populations in specific geographic areas through multi-sectoral and multi-stakeholder action (pp. 4, 9) [web:30][web:32].

ABAs are one approach among several and should be selected based on context, mandate, feasibility, and complementarities with other approaches (pp. 10-11).

Strong ABAs commonly rely on multi-sector, multi-agency assessment and coordinated planning/implementation mechanisms (pp. 10, 12) [web:60].

Poorly designed ABAs can increase inequalities/tensions and disconnect local interventions from wider city or regional plans (pp. 10, 12-13).

Priorities for strengthening include inclusivity, stakeholder participation, city-linkage, adaptive management, and multi-agency contribution-focused M&E (p. 13).

**"Understanding the community: Initiatives resulted in a more in-depth understanding of the community. High levels of community engagement, supported through the use of maps, spatial data and participatory mapping exercises were important vehicles."** [web:60]

**"Engagement with multiple actors: Numerous case studies prioritised engaging with multiple and diverse actors, including: local, national and international stakeholders; humanitarian and development actors; and government, civil society and private sector actors."** [web:60]

**"Settlement based initiatives often also provided a platform for information sharing, dialogue and coordination – either through a physical centre or virtual platform."** [web:60]

## Limitations / cautions

The compendium is a synthesized case-study collection, not a controlled comparative impact evaluation **— "The document consolidates practitioner-submitted case studies with varying levels of detail, documentation quality, and analytical depth. It is not a rigorous impact evaluation with standardized methodology or control groups"** (pp. 6, 13).

The editorial notes that application across agencies remains sporadic and not yet fully systematized at scale **— "While the compendium documents diverse applications, area-based approaches remain a minority practice in urban humanitarian response. Many agencies continue to use household-targeted or sector-specific programming as default models"** (p. 4).

The document itself calls for further research on enabling conditions, outcomes/impacts over time, and comparative efficiency/effectiveness versus alternatives **— "Key research gaps include: under what conditions do area-based approaches perform better or worse than alternatives? what are the long-term impacts on social cohesion, service sustainability, and local governance? are area-based approaches more or less cost-effective than other models?"** (p. 13).

**Case study geographic and temporal concentration**: **The 30+ case studies concentrate in certain regions (Middle East, South Asia, East Africa) and crisis types (protracted displacement, post-disaster recovery) — application in other contexts (acute conflict, sudden-onset disasters in regions with limited case material) requires adaptation**.

**Limited cost data**: **Few case studies provide detailed budget information or cost-effectiveness analysis, limiting ability to compare resource requirements across different ABA models or against alternative approaches**.

## Known tensions

**Whole-population inclusion vs. humanitarian targeting**: ABAs aim to serve all populations in a geographic area to support social cohesion, but humanitarian mandates often specify specific beneficiary categories (refugees, IDPs). Reconciling inclusive area-based logic with categorical mandates and funding creates operational challenges.

**Multi-sector integration vs. sectoral funding**: Area-based approaches require flexible, multi-sector funding, but most humanitarian financing is allocated by sector (WASH, shelter, health, protection) through separate funding streams. Aligning area-based programming with sector-specific budgets is difficult.

**Local participation vs. timeline urgency**: Meaningful stakeholder engagement and participatory planning require time; humanitarian crises demand rapid response. Case studies acknowledge this tension but don't resolve it — short project cycles undermine quality participation.

**Geographic targeting vs. citywide equity**: Concentrating resources in high-need areas can create visible disparities with adjacent neighborhoods, fueling resentment. Balancing area-focused impact with citywide equity is unresolved.

**Municipal leadership vs. humanitarian principles**: The compendium emphasizes municipal engagement and alignment with government plans, but in contexts where authorities discriminate, violate rights, or are party to conflict, humanitarian actors face tension between supporting local governance and maintaining neutrality/impartiality.

**Coordination investment vs. delivery pressure**: Effective area-based coordination requires significant time and resources (meetings, facilitation, relationship-building); operational pressures favor directing resources to direct service delivery. Justifying coordination overhead is challenging.
