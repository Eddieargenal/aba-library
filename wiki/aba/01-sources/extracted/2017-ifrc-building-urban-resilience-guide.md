## Summary

This IFRC guide (2017) presents a Movement-oriented framework for engaging in urban disaster risk reduction and disaster management (urban DRR/DM) to strengthen urban resilience (pp. 1, 14-16). Full title: "Building urban resilience: A guide for Red Cross and Red Crescent engagement and contribution". It was developed as an outcome of the IFRC-Iranian Red Crescent Partnership on Urban DRR/DM (2013-2016), informed by regional consultations and five city-level pilot projects in Tehran (Iran), Nairobi (Kenya), Tegucigalpa (Honduras), Jakarta (Indonesia), and Yerevan (Armenia) (pp. 11, 15-16, 24).

The guide argues that rapid, often unplanned urbanization and growing complexity in urban settings require better risk understanding, adapted tools, and stronger cooperation with diverse stakeholders (pp. 10, 20-22). "Risks caused by rapid and often improper urbanization, compounded by natural hazards, create some of the major challenges in the 21st Century" (p. 1). It emphasizes that many existing Movement tools and capacities were historically shaped in rural contexts and require adaptation for urban conditions — "While the Red Cross and Red Crescent has long experience in disaster risk reduction and management, much of this experience has been in rural contexts or camps. Urban contexts present different challenges: density, diversity, dynamism, informal settlements, complex governance, and interdependent infrastructure systems" (pp. 14-15, 21-22).

Structurally, the guide is organized around five thematic areas (context/engagement, capacity-strengthening, awareness-raising, programme implementation, advocacy), followed by Movement "essentials" for urban resilience and key resources (pp. 4-5, 24-25, 70-80). The five thematic areas are: (1) Understanding context and engaging stakeholders; (2) Strengthening capacity of National Societies; (3) Raising awareness; (4) Implementing programmes; (5) Advocating for urban resilience (pp. 4-5, 24-25).

It is explicitly non-prescriptive: it identifies issues, tips, and considerations rather than step-by-step operational instructions, and calls for context-specific adaptation and continued tool development — "Rather than serving as a practical manual, the guide offers a strategic and analytical framework for enhancing urban resilience... It is not a 'how-to' guide with step-by-step instructions, but rather a resource for reflection, planning, and adaptation" (pp. 23, 25).

## Key concepts

**Urban resilience (UNISDR framing)**: Ability of systems/communities to resist, absorb, accommodate, and recover while preserving essential structures/functions — "The ability of a system, community or society exposed to hazards to resist, absorb, accommodate to and recover from the effects of a hazard in a timely and efficient manner, including through the preservation and restoration of its essential basic structures and functions" (UNISDR definition cited) (p. 17).

**Urban resilience (IFRC framing)**: Ability of individuals/communities/organizations/countries to anticipate, reduce impact, cope, and recover without undermining long-term prospects — "The ability of individuals, communities, organizations or countries exposed to disasters, crises and underlying vulnerabilities to anticipate, prepare for, reduce the impact of, cope with and recover from the effects of shocks and stresses without compromising their long-term prospects" (IFRC Framework for Community Resilience definition cited) (p. 17).

**Urban context characteristics**: Dynamism/mobility, density/scale, diversity, informal settlements, disrupted services, cash economy, and technology dependence (p. 18). Figure 1 visualizes these characteristics as: (1) Dynamism and mobility — rapid change, population flows, economic volatility; (2) Density and scale — concentration of people, assets, infrastructure in limited space; (3) Diversity — social, economic, cultural heterogeneity; (4) Informal settlements — unplanned development, insecure tenure, inadequate services; (5) Disrupted essential services — infrastructure failures cascade across systems; (6) Cash economy — livelihoods depend on markets and monetary exchange; (7) Technology dependence — reliance on communications, power, transport networks (p. 18).

**Multi-dimensional and multi-sector DRR/DM**: Urban resilience requires integrated approaches across hazards, sectors, and stakeholder groups — "Urban risks arise from interactions between multiple hazards (earthquakes, floods, fires, disease, violence) and multi-dimensional vulnerabilities (physical infrastructure, social systems, economic structures, governance). Effective urban DRR/DM cannot be single-sector or single-hazard; it requires integration across health, shelter, WASH, livelihoods, protection, and other domains" (pp. 16-17, 20).

**Multi-stakeholder engagement**: Active collaboration with local authorities, communities, civil society, private sector, and other actors is essential in urban DRR/DM — "In urban contexts, National Societies cannot work in isolation. Municipal governments hold regulatory authority and service delivery mandates. Civil society organizations have local networks and trust. Private sector actors provide employment, markets, and services. Affected communities hold knowledge and priorities. Effective urban resilience-building requires active partnership with this diverse stakeholder ecosystem" (pp. 31, 40-41).

**Context-specific urban adaptation**: Because urban definitions and risks vary by setting, urban approaches should be tailored per context — "There is no universal definition of 'urban'; density, administrative status, economic characteristics, and infrastructure availability vary across countries and regions. Similarly, urban risks differ dramatically — earthquake risk in Tehran differs from flood risk in Jakarta differs from conflict-related risk in Nairobi. Urban DRR/DM must be context-adapted, not template-driven" (pp. 18, 25).

**Auxiliary role in urban contexts**: "Red Cross and Red Crescent National Societies serve as auxiliaries to public authorities in the humanitarian field. In urban contexts, this auxiliary role means working closely with municipal governments, supporting rather than replacing city services, and aligning with urban development and DRR plans" (pp. 31, 40-41).

## Tools or methods extracted

**Five thematic implementation domains**: Context/engagement; capacity-strengthening; awareness-raising; programme implementation; advocacy (pp. 4-5, 24-25). Each domain includes guiding questions, considerations, and tips rather than prescriptive procedures: (1) Understanding context and engaging stakeholders — analyze urban risks, map stakeholders, build partnerships; (2) Strengthening capacity — build NS institutional capacity, train volunteers, develop urban-adapted skills; (3) Raising awareness — public education, community preparedness, risk communication; (4) Implementing programmes — multi-sector DRR/DM interventions, early warning, contingency planning; (5) Advocating for urban resilience — policy influence, global frameworks, evidence-based advocacy (pp. 24-25).

**Urban stakeholder mapping/analysis**: Identify relevant actors, mandates, capacities, access, and influence to support coordination and partnership design — "Stakeholder analysis should identify: who holds decision-making authority? who provides services? who represents community interests? what capacities and constraints do different actors face? what relationships and power dynamics exist? This analysis informs partnership strategies and coordination approaches" (pp. 31, 40-41, 77).

**Risk mapping and assessment emphasis**: Use urban-sensitive risk assessment and mapping as core planning inputs — "Risk assessments in urban contexts should go beyond household surveys to include: multi-hazard mapping; infrastructure vulnerability analysis; cascading risk dependencies (how power failure affects water, health, communications); social vulnerability mapping (informal settlements, marginalized groups); and governance capacity assessment" (pp. 71-72).

**Multi-hazard contingency planning + early warning**: Integrate monitoring, contingency planning, and warning into urban preparedness systems — "Urban contingency planning must account for: multiple concurrent hazards; infrastructure interdependencies; population density and mobility; diverse stakeholder coordination requirements; and technology-dependent communication systems. Early warning systems should be people-centered, multi-channel, and tested through regular drills" (pp. 72-73).

**Legal/policy analysis integration**: Assess frameworks (building codes, land use, DRR legislation, tenure-related constraints) to inform programming and advocacy — "Understanding the legal and policy environment is critical for urban DRR/DM: What building codes exist and are they enforced? What land use regulations govern settlement patterns? What DRR legislation assigns roles and responsibilities? What tenure policies affect informal settlement residents? This analysis shapes both programme design and advocacy strategies" (pp. 76-79).

**Movement essentials framework (Table 4)**: Nine consolidated essentials for urban DRR/DM and resilience building, linked to UNISDR Ten Essentials — (1) Organize for disaster resilience; (2) Identify, understand, and use current and future risk scenarios; (3) Strengthen financial capacity for resilience; (4) Pursue resilient urban development and design; (5) Safeguard natural buffers; (6) Strengthen institutional capacity; (7) Understand and strengthen societal capacity; (8) Increase infrastructure resilience; (9) Ensure effective preparedness, early warning, and response (pp. 72-79).

**Participatory urban risk assessment (PURA)**: Cited as an adapted tool combining participatory methods with urban technical analysis to engage communities in risk identification and planning (pp. 71-72).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Strategic scoping**: Clarify urban context, risks, and stakeholder systems before intervention design — "Before launching urban DRR/DM programmes, National Societies should invest in: understanding the specific urban context (what makes this city unique?); identifying primary hazards and vulnerabilities; mapping stakeholder landscape and governance structures; and assessing NS capacity for urban engagement. Rushing to implementation without this foundation risks ineffectiveness or harm" (pp. 26-31).

**Design and planning**: Use integrated, multi-sector DRR/DM planning informed by risk mapping, policy analysis, and stakeholder engagement — "Urban programme design should specify: which hazards/risks are prioritized; which geographic areas or population groups are targeted; which sectors are included (and why others are excluded); which stakeholders will partner; how interventions link to municipal plans; and what sustainability/handover pathways exist" (pp. 20-22, 31, 71-73).

**Capacity and implementation**: Strengthen NS institutional and operational capacities, adapting methods/tools for urban contexts — "National Societies may need to: recruit or train staff with urban expertise (urban planning, engineering, community mobilization in high-density settings); adapt assessment tools for urban complexity; develop cash/voucher programming capacity for urban cash economies; strengthen coordination skills for multi-stakeholder environments; and build flexibility for adaptive management in dynamic contexts" (pp. 21-22, 36, 74-76).

**Community engagement and awareness**: Build participatory processes, community risk communication, and preparedness awareness as continuous functions — "Urban community engagement differs from rural contexts: communities may be transient, heterogeneous, and lack traditional leadership structures. Engagement strategies should use: diverse communication channels (social media, community radio, public meetings); participatory tools adapted for density (transect walks, mapping); and sustained presence-building to establish trust in mobile, diverse populations" (pp. 42, 75-76).

**Advocacy and systems change**: Link local evidence to policy and planning influence at municipal/national levels and to global frameworks — "Urban DRR/DM advocacy should connect: local evidence from programmes and community voices; to municipal policy influence (building codes, land use planning, DRR budgets); to national frameworks (DRR legislation, urban development strategies); and to global agendas (Sendai Framework, SDGs, New Urban Agenda). This multi-level advocacy amplifies local impact and builds enabling environments" (pp. 65-69, 77-79).

## Field data collection implications

Urban data collection must include multi-hazard risk, service access, and vulnerability drivers in dense, diverse settings — "Urban assessments should capture: hazard exposure (flood zones, seismic risk, fire vulnerability, disease patterns); infrastructure functionality (water, power, transport, communications); service access and quality (health, education, social protection); livelihood patterns and economic vulnerability; social cohesion and tension dynamics; governance capacity; and tenure/HLP status" (pp. 20-22, 29-31).

Risk mapping/assessment should be systematic and context-specific, with adaptation of existing tools — "Existing Red Cross Red Crescent risk assessment tools (VCA, CBDRM) were often developed for rural contexts. Urban application requires adaptation to account for: higher density and scale; infrastructure dependencies; diverse stakeholder landscape; informal settlements; and rapid change. Where existing tools are inadequate, new urban-specific tools should be developed" (pp. 22, 71-72).

Assessments should include broad stakeholder participation (including government and community actors) to improve relevance and uptake — "Multi-stakeholder assessment processes build shared understanding, strengthen relationships, improve data quality through diverse perspectives, and increase likelihood that findings will inform decision-making by municipal authorities and other partners" (pp. 31, 77).

Monitoring and contingency planning should be integrated with assessment outputs, not treated as separate activities — "Risk assessments should feed directly into: contingency planning (what scenarios to prepare for); early warning system design (what thresholds to monitor); and monitoring frameworks (what indicators to track over time). These should not be disconnected processes" (pp. 72-73).

**Geographic information systems (GIS)**: "GIS and spatial data analysis are particularly valuable in urban contexts for: hazard mapping; infrastructure vulnerability analysis; population density visualization; service coverage gaps; and coordination (showing which organizations work where). Building NS GIS capacity is a priority for urban DRR/DM" (p. 71).

## Coordination implications

Urban DRR/DM requires coordination and collaboration across multiple sectors and stakeholders, not siloed programming — "Urban risks are interconnected and governance is multi-layered. Single-sector, single-agency programming is insufficient. Effective urban DRR/DM requires: cross-sector coordination (WASH, health, shelter, livelihoods); multi-stakeholder platforms (government, civil society, private sector); and vertical integration (community, municipal, national levels)" (pp. 20, 31).

Participation in city-level DRR/DM systems and plans is recommended to support mainstreaming and influence — "National Societies should engage with: municipal DRR/civil protection departments; city development and planning offices; urban resilience platforms (100 Resilient Cities, UNDRR Making Cities Resilient campaign); and sector coordination mechanisms (health, WASH, shelter clusters in humanitarian contexts). Active participation builds influence and ensures NS programming aligns with city priorities" (pp. 31, 41).

Local coalitions/platforms for resilience and DRR are encouraged to improve shared planning and implementation — "In contexts where formal coordination structures are weak or absent, National Societies can catalyze local resilience coalitions bringing together: community organizations; municipal departments; civil society; private sector; and other stakeholders. These platforms facilitate information sharing, joint planning, and collective action" (p. 77).

Cross-city/regional cooperation is highlighted as important for information/resource sharing and preparedness alignment — "Urban DRR challenges often span multiple cities (riverine flooding, seismic zones, disease patterns). Cross-city learning networks, regional coordination platforms, and knowledge exchange initiatives strengthen collective preparedness and enable resource-sharing during crises" (p. 77).

**Movement coordination**: "In urban contexts with multiple Red Cross Red Crescent actors (National Society, IFRC, ICRC, partner National Societies), clear coordination within the Movement is essential to avoid duplication, ensure complementarity, and present a coherent approach to external partners" (p. 41).

## DRR implications

The guide aligns Movement practice with SFDRR, SDGs, New Urban Agenda, and other global resilience commitments — "The guide explicitly links urban DRR/DM to: Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030 (particularly Priority 2 on governance and Priority 4 on preparedness); Sustainable Development Goals (especially SDG 11 on sustainable cities); New Urban Agenda (Habitat III outcome); Paris Agreement on climate change; and other global frameworks. This alignment positions National Society work within global urban resilience agendas" (pp. 19-20, 67-68).

It emphasizes that neglecting DRR in cities increases losses, while risk-informed planning and resilience investment is cost-effective — "Urban disasters generate disproportionate economic losses due to concentration of assets and infrastructure. Every dollar invested in DRR saves multiple dollars in disaster response and recovery costs. The guide makes the economic case for proactive urban resilience investment as both humanitarian imperative and cost-effective development strategy" (pp. 67-69).

It frames DRR as cross-cutting through legal frameworks, built environment standards, services, social systems, and community participation — "DRR is not a standalone sector but must be integrated across: legal and policy frameworks (building codes, land use regulations, DRR legislation); built environment (infrastructure resilience, urban planning, safe construction); service delivery (health, education, WASH, transport); social systems (community organization, social protection, inclusivity); and governance (institutional capacity, coordination, accountability)" (pp. 71-79).

**Ten Essentials for Making Cities Resilient**: The guide cross-references the nine Movement essentials with UNDRR's Ten Essentials for Making Cities Resilient, providing a bridge between Movement programming and global city-resilience frameworks (pp. 72-79).

**Pre-disaster risk reduction vs. post-disaster response**: "The guide argues that the Red Cross Red Crescent has traditionally been stronger in disaster response than in pre-disaster risk reduction, particularly in urban contexts. It calls for rebalancing toward proactive urban DRR investment, while maintaining response capacity" (pp. 14-15, 20).

## Operational implications

National Societies should shift from predominantly rural-derived DRR/DM models toward urban-adapted approaches and capacities — "Many National Societies developed their DRR/DM expertise in rural contexts or refugee camps. Urban engagement requires: different skills (urban planning, infrastructure systems, municipal engagement); different tools (adapted risk assessments, GIS, cash programming); different partnerships (municipal authorities, private sector, urban civil society); and different mindsets (accepting complexity, uncertainty, and need for multi-stakeholder coordination)" (pp. 14-15, 21-22).

Programmes should integrate multiple hazards, sectors, and actors, with explicit urban risk analysis and partnership design — "Urban programmes should avoid: single-hazard focus (cities face multiple, interacting risks); single-sector interventions (needs are interconnected); single-agency delivery (coordination is essential). Instead, design for: multi-hazard resilience; multi-sector integration; and multi-stakeholder partnerships" (pp. 20, 31, 70-71).

Urban toolsets should be upgraded/adapted, and where needed new tools should be developed for urban contexts — "Existing Red Cross Red Crescent tools (VCA, CBDRM, PASSA, etc.) provide a foundation but require urban adaptation. The guide recommends: testing and adapting existing tools for urban density, diversity, and complexity; developing new urban-specific tools where gaps exist; and documenting and sharing adapted tools across the Movement" (pp. 25, 71-72).

Approaches should remain flexible and context-specific because urban systems and risks are dynamic — "Urban contexts are volatile — populations move, economies shift, political dynamics change, risks evolve. Programming requires: adaptive management (regular monitoring, willingness to adjust); flexible funding and planning; scenario-based preparedness (planning for multiple possible futures); and continuous learning cycles" (p. 25).

**Cash and voucher programming**: "Urban populations depend on cash economies and markets. Urban DRR/resilience programming should incorporate cash and voucher assistance (CVA) where appropriate, requiring National Society capacity in market assessment, cash delivery mechanisms, and financial service provider partnerships" (pp. 18, 21).

## Limitations / cautions

The guide explicitly does not provide step-by-step instructions for each thematic area — "This is not a 'how-to' manual with detailed operational procedures. It provides strategic guidance, reflection questions, and considerations that National Societies must adapt to their specific contexts" (p. 25).

It does not provide comprehensive, ready-to-use urban-specific toolkits; it recommends adapting existing tools and developing new ones where needed — "The guide identifies the need for urban-adapted tools but does not include complete toolkits. It references existing Red Cross Red Crescent tools and recommends adaptation, while calling for development of new urban-specific tools where gaps exist" (pp. 22, 25).

It is primarily targeted at National Society DRR/DM engagement and may require adaptation for other actor types — "While the guide is primarily designed for Red Cross and Red Crescent National Societies, it also serves as a resource for other community-based organizations aiming to develop and execute resilience-building strategies in cities" (p. 25).

**2017 publication predates recent urban guidance**: This guide pre-dates IASC 2018 area-based coordination guidance, Sphere 2018 urban integration, UNHCR/IRC 2019 ABA compendium, and other contemporary urban frameworks — users should integrate with current guidance.

**Limited operational detail on specific sectors**: The guide provides high-level principles but limited technical detail on sector-specific urban programming (e.g., urban WASH, urban shelter, urban health). Sector-specific guidance from technical experts should supplement this framework.

## Known tensions

**Rural expertise vs. urban requirements**: The guide acknowledges that most National Society DRR/DM experience is rural-based, requiring significant capacity investment for urban transition — but doesn't address how to balance maintaining rural capacity while building urban expertise with limited resources.

**Multi-stakeholder coordination vs. operational efficiency**: Urban resilience requires complex multi-stakeholder engagement, but this coordination overhead can slow delivery and absorb resources — the guide emphasizes coordination importance without fully resolving efficiency trade-offs.

**Auxiliary role vs. government capacity gaps**: National Societies serve as auxiliaries supporting government DRR/DM, but in many urban contexts municipal capacity is weak or governance is fragmented — balancing auxiliary role with need to act when government is absent/unable is unresolved.

**Global frameworks vs. local realities**: The guide aligns with global agendas (Sendai, SDGs, New Urban Agenda), but implementation in contexts with weak governance, limited resources, or political instability faces practical constraints not fully addressed.

**Community participation vs. density/mobility**: Urban density and population mobility complicate community engagement — traditional participatory approaches assume stable, cohesive communities which may not exist in diverse, transient urban settings.

**Preparedness investment vs. response visibility**: The guide advocates for increased DRR investment, but disaster response generates more visibility and funding than preparedness — shifting organizational culture and donor priorities toward proactive urban resilience is challenging.
