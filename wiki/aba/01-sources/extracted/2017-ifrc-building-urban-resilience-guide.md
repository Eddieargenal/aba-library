# Summary
This IFRC guide (2017) presents a Movement-oriented framework for engaging in urban disaster risk reduction and disaster management (urban DRR/DM) to strengthen urban resilience (pp. 1, 14-16). It was developed as an outcome of the IFRC-Iranian Red Crescent Partnership on Urban DRR/DM (2013-2016), informed by regional consultations and five city-level projects (pp. 11, 15-16, 24).

The guide argues that rapid, often unplanned urbanization and growing complexity in urban settings require better risk understanding, adapted tools, and stronger cooperation with diverse stakeholders (pp. 10, 20-22). It emphasizes that many existing Movement tools and capacities were historically shaped in rural contexts and require adaptation for urban conditions (pp. 14-15, 21-22).

Structurally, the guide is organized around five thematic areas (context/engagement, capacity-strengthening, awareness-raising, programme implementation, advocacy), followed by Movement "essentials" for urban resilience and key resources (pp. 4-5, 24-25, 70-80).

It is explicitly non-prescriptive: it identifies issues, tips, and considerations rather than step-by-step operational instructions, and calls for context-specific adaptation and continued tool development (pp. 23, 25).

# Key concepts
- Urban resilience (UNISDR framing): ability of systems/communities to resist, absorb, accommodate, and recover while preserving essential structures/functions (p. 17).
- Urban resilience (IFRC framing): ability of individuals/communities/organizations/countries to anticipate, reduce impact, cope, and recover without undermining long-term prospects (p. 17).
- Urban context characteristics: dynamism/mobility, density/scale, diversity, informal settlements, disrupted services, cash economy, and technology dependence (p. 18).
- Multi-dimensional and multi-sector DRR/DM: urban resilience requires integrated approaches across hazards, sectors, and stakeholder groups (pp. 16-17, 20).
- Multi-stakeholder engagement: active collaboration with local authorities, communities, civil society, private sector, and other actors is essential in urban DRR/DM (pp. 31, 40-41).
- Context-specific urban adaptation: because urban definitions and risks vary by setting, urban approaches should be tailored per context (pp. 18, 25).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Five thematic implementation domains: context/engagement; capacity-strengthening; awareness-raising; programme implementation; advocacy (pp. 4-5, 24-25).
- Urban stakeholder mapping/analysis: identify relevant actors, mandates, capacities, access, and influence to support coordination and partnership design (pp. 31, 40-41, 77).
- Risk mapping and assessment emphasis: use urban-sensitive risk assessment and mapping as core planning inputs (pp. 71-72).
- Multi-hazard contingency planning + early warning: integrate monitoring, contingency planning, and warning into urban preparedness systems (pp. 72-73).
- Legal/policy analysis integration: assess frameworks (building codes, land use, DRR legislation, tenure-related constraints) to inform programming and advocacy (pp. 76-79).
- Movement essentials framework (Table 4): nine consolidated essentials for urban DRR/DM and resilience building, linked to UNISDR Ten Essentials (pp. 72-79).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Strategic scoping: clarify urban context, risks, and stakeholder systems before intervention design (pp. 26-31).
- Design and planning: use integrated, multi-sector DRR/DM planning informed by risk mapping, policy analysis, and stakeholder engagement (pp. 20-22, 31, 71-73).
- Capacity and implementation: strengthen NS institutional and operational capacities, adapting methods/tools for urban contexts (pp. 21-22, 36, 74-76).
- Community engagement and awareness: build participatory processes, community risk communication, and preparedness awareness as continuous functions (pp. 42, 75-76).
- Advocacy and systems change: link local evidence to policy and planning influence at municipal/national levels and to global frameworks (pp. 65-69, 77-79).

# Field data collection implications
- Urban data collection must include multi-hazard risk, service access, and vulnerability drivers in dense, diverse settings (pp. 20-22, 29-31).
- Risk mapping/assessment should be systematic and context-specific, with adaptation of existing tools (pp. 22, 71-72).
- Assessments should include broad stakeholder participation (including government and community actors) to improve relevance and uptake (pp. 31, 77).
- Monitoring and contingency planning should be integrated with assessment outputs, not treated as separate activities (pp. 72-73).

# Coordination implications
- Urban DRR/DM requires coordination and collaboration across multiple sectors and stakeholders, not siloed programming (pp. 20, 31).
- Participation in city-level DRR/DM systems and plans is recommended to support mainstreaming and influence (pp. 31, 41).
- Local coalitions/platforms for resilience and DRR are encouraged to improve shared planning and implementation (p. 77).
- Cross-city/regional cooperation is highlighted as important for information/resource sharing and preparedness alignment (p. 77).

# DRR implications
- The guide aligns Movement practice with SFDRR, SDGs, New Urban Agenda, and other global resilience commitments (pp. 19-20, 67-68).
- It emphasizes that neglecting DRR in cities increases losses, while risk-informed planning and resilience investment is cost-effective (pp. 67-69).
- It frames DRR as cross-cutting through legal frameworks, built environment standards, services, social systems, and community participation (pp. 71-79).

# Operational implications
- National Societies should shift from predominantly rural-derived DRR/DM models toward urban-adapted approaches and capacities (pp. 14-15, 21-22).
- Programmes should integrate multiple hazards, sectors, and actors, with explicit urban risk analysis and partnership design (pp. 20, 31, 70-71).
- Urban toolsets should be upgraded/adapted, and where needed new tools should be developed for urban contexts (pp. 25, 71-72).
- Approaches should remain flexible and context-specific because urban systems and risks are dynamic (p. 25).

# Claims worth citing
- Urbanization trends and informal settlement growth are increasing disaster risk complexity in cities (pp. 10, 14, 20).
- Existing Movement approaches/tools were historically more rural-oriented and need adaptation for urban contexts (pp. 14-15, 21-22).
- The guide is non-prescriptive and intended to inform context-specific National Society planning and engagement (pp. 23, 25).
- Multi-sector, multi-stakeholder approaches are necessary for effective urban resilience building (pp. 16-17, 31, 70-71).
- A structured set of Movement essentials is proposed to guide scalable, sustainable urban DRR/DM practice (pp. 70-79).

# Limitations / cautions
- The guide explicitly does not provide step-by-step instructions for each thematic area (p. 25).
- It does not provide comprehensive, ready-to-use urban-specific toolkits; it recommends adapting existing tools and developing new ones where needed (pp. 22, 25).
- It is primarily targeted at National Society DRR/DM engagement and may require adaptation for other actor types (p. 25).
