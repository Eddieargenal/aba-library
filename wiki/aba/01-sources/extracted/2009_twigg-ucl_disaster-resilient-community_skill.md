<a id="summary"></a>
## Summary

**Author and institution:** John Twigg, Senior Research Fellow at UCL / Aon Benfield UCL Hazard Research Centre. The document was commissioned by a DFID-funded interagency group comprising ActionAid, British Red Cross, Christian Aid, Plan UK, Practical Action, and Tearfund.

**Document type:** NGO guidance note, Version 2 (November 2009). Supersedes the August 2007 first edition. The revision incorporates field-testing feedback from multiple countries and agencies, adding substantial practical guidance while leaving the core framework tables unchanged.

**Scope:** Presents a comprehensive framework defining what a disaster-resilient community looks like. The framework is organized into five Thematic Areas (aligned with the Hyogo Framework for Action), 28 Components of Resilience, and 167 Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community, plus a parallel Enabling Environment column for each component. Covers the full project cycle: baseline studies, VCA, project design, indicator development, monitoring, evaluation, strategic planning, and advocacy.

**What it does NOT do:** Does not provide quantitative indicators, validated scoring systems, or prescriptive step-by-step guidance. It is explicitly a resource, not a manual or checklist.

**Production method:** Desk research, expert consultations, and extensive field testing by multiple agencies across Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

**Primary context:** Generic (written for DRR practitioners globally, with rural community settings predominating in examples). Substantial urban adaptation required.

**Urban applicability:** `requires-adaptation`. Five of seven urban applicability checklist items triggered: informal settlement dynamics absent; community consensus assumed but unlikely in fragmented urban populations; local government assumed accessible but often absent; spatial community definition insufficient for urban heterogeneity; participatory methods scaled for villages, not urban neighborhoods. Single-hazard framing is NOT an issue (document is explicitly multi-hazard). Social cohesion assumptions belong to rural contexts. Urban adaptation scope: **substantial** (5 triggers).

**Composite authority:** High. Institutional credibility is high — UCL academic authorship, DFID-funded interagency consortium, field-tested across multiple countries. Evidence quality is medium — the framework is qualitative, context-dependent, and explicitly aspirational rather than empirically validated. The divergence between these means composite is high overall but should be read alongside the medium evidence quality caveat.

<a id="key-concepts"></a>
## Key Concepts

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-CONCEPT-001: Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR)** (`conceptual`)
The broad development and application of policies, strategies and practices to minimize vulnerabilities and disaster risks throughout society (p. 8). A systematic approach to identifying, assessing and reducing risks; the collection of actions toward resilience. No single organization can address every aspect; DRR demands collective, multi-sector response. All 167 Characteristics are DRR elements.

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-CONCEPT-002: Resilience** (`conceptual`)
The capacity to: (1) anticipate, minimize and absorb potential stresses or destructive forces through adaptation or resistance; (2) manage or maintain certain basic functions and structures during disastrous events; (3) recover or 'bounce back' after an event (p. 8). Broader than 'capacity' — goes beyond specific strategies to encompass multi-faceted, layered adaptive ability. Resilience and vulnerability are opposite sides of the same coin; both are relative (resilient to what, to what extent). No community can be completely safe; the goal is the 'safest possible community that we have the knowledge to design'.

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-CONCEPT-003: Community** (`conceptual`)
Not only spatial (groups living in the same area) but also common interests, values, activities and structures. Communities are complex, often not united, dynamic, and do not exist in isolation (p. 9). Individuals can belong to multiple communities simultaneously. Community resilience is influenced by capacities outside — emergency services, administrative services, social linkages. This makes it difficult to define the 'community' one is working with. Critical for urban applicability: the spatial dimension is essential for hazard identification, but must be linked to socio-economic differentiations, linkages, and dynamics.

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-CONCEPT-004: Enabling Environment** (`conceptual`)
Wider institutional, policy and socio-economic factors that support community-level resilience (p. 12). The tables identify Enabling Environment elements in parallel with each Component of Resilience, covering local, national, and international levels. Communities are dependent on external duty bearers; many features of the ideal Enabling Environment will be missing in practice. In some situations the absence of key components creates a 'disabling' environment for local initiatives.

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-FRAMEWORK-001: Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community Framework** (`conceptual`, `diagnostic`)
A multi-level framework organized into: 5 Thematic Areas (Governance; Risk Assessment; Knowledge and Education; Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction; Disaster Preparedness and Response), 28 Components of Resilience (3–7 per Thematic Area), and 167 Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community (p. 9–14, Section 6). The framework is organized following the HFA and is intended to cover all aspects of resilience. Thematic Area 1 (Governance) is explicitly cross-cutting — it underlies all other Areas. Two-layer classification justified: serves as conceptual reference for what resilience looks like AND as a diagnostic tool for assessing community status against the ideal.

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-FRAMEWORK-002: Enabling Environment Framework** (`conceptual`)
Parallel set of characteristics for each Component of Resilience, describing external policy, institutional, and socio-economic conditions required to support community-level resilience. Located in the right-hand column of all Thematic Area tables. Not separate from community work; should be considered together with community characteristics for advocacy, partnership, and sustainability analysis.

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-TYPOLOGY-001: Five-Level Milestones Scale** (`operational`)
A five-level attainment scale for measuring progress toward resilience: Level 1 = little awareness, crisis response only; Level 2 = awareness and willingness but limited capacity; Level 3 = development and implementation of solutions; Level 4 = coherence and integration; Level 5 = culture of safety, DRR embedded in all policy and practice (p. 19–20). Level 5 approximates the ideal disaster-resilient community. Not validated across all contexts; progress may be non-linear.

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-PRINCIPLE-001: Customization Principle** (`operational`)
The Characteristics must be adapted, selected, rewritten, or augmented to match local contexts, hazard profiles, community capacities, and organizational mandates — never applied as a checklist (p. 18). Customization makes the framework locally relevant and owned. Nobody is expected to use every Characteristic; probably every user will have their own view of how well the framework reflects their work.

<a id="methods-and-tools"></a>
## Methods and Tools

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-TOOL-001: The Characteristics Tables** (`conceptual`, `diagnostic`)
A comprehensive reference tool consisting of five Thematic Area tables, each containing Components of Resilience, Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community, and Characteristics of an Enabling Environment (Section 6, pp. 28–44). Used for planning, assessment, indicator development, M&E, advocacy, strategic planning, and research. Must be adapted before use. Two-layer classification justified: serves as conceptual reference AND as diagnostic assessment lens.
*Field query trigger:* "What does a disaster-resilient community look like across all dimensions — governance, risk, knowledge, risk management, and preparedness?"

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-TOOL-002: Milestones Model for Progress Toward Resilience** (`operational`)
A multi-stakeholder exercise in which participants define intermediate steps between current state and ideal Characteristics, enabling measurement of progress over time (p. 19–20). For each selected Characteristic, stakeholders define current state, milestone levels, and indicators for each level. Requires participatory process and regular review. No predefined milestones — milestones must be constructed locally. The five-level scale (Level 1–5) provides reference points but must be operationalized per context.
*Field query trigger:* "How do we show measurable progress toward resilience when we cannot reach the ideal state within our project timeframe?"

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-METHOD-001: Characteristics-Based Baseline Study** (`diagnostic`)
Using the Characteristics tables to design baseline data collection, ensuring coverage of all relevant resilience dimensions. Organizations select relevant Components and Characteristics → design data collection instruments → match baseline findings against the framework → score using a simple scale: (1) met, (2) partly met, (3) not met → generate baseline profile → produce visual output (e.g., spider diagrams) (p. 22). Optional numerical scoring supports crude prioritization.
*Field query trigger:* "How do I set up a systematic baseline for community resilience before a project starts using a structured multi-thematic framework?"

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-METHOD-002: Characteristics-Framed VCA** (`diagnostic`)
Applying the Characteristics as an organizing and interpreting framework for vulnerability and capacity analysis, either before (to identify areas of inquiry) or after (to structure findings) a VCA (p. 22–23, Box 9). The Characteristics help field workers understand the range of vulnerabilities to look for without pre-defining indicators, which is recognized as good practice. Box 9 provides a worked example: VCA findings organized under five Thematic Areas.
*Field query trigger:* "Our VCA generates huge amounts of data in many different areas — how do we structure and interpret it without pre-determining results?"

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-METHOD-003: Scoping/Gap Mapping Exercise** (`diagnostic`, `operational`)
Using the Components of Resilience (not the full Characteristics) to identify which resilience areas an agency or consortium currently addresses, where current emphasis lies, and major gaps in DRR coverage (p. 21). Maps existing activities to Components → identifies coverage and gaps → identifies partnership needs. Can be done as a one-off exercise. Works best in multi-stakeholder settings. Two-layer classification justified: diagnostic for gap identification, operational for partnership planning and strategy.
*Field query trigger:* "How do multiple agencies quickly assess which areas of resilience they are collectively covering and where the critical gaps and partnership needs are?"

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-METHOD-004: Customization Process** (`operational`)
A participatory process for selecting, adapting, rewriting, combining, or adding Characteristics to match local context, hazards, and community capacities (p. 18, Box 5, Box 6). Requires: participatory discussion of original Characteristics, selection based on hazard relevance and achievability, rewriting in local language and context, validation with community, and option to add new Thematic Areas or Components. Examples include MACEC (Philippines) context-specific Enabling Environment, Plan's child-centred Thematic Area 0.
*Field query trigger:* "How do we adapt the global Characteristics framework to reflect our specific local hazards, community context, and organizational mandate?"

- **neighbourhood-diagnosis:** Direct, full coverage. Baseline studies, VCA, hazard/risk assessment, scoping exercise. Methods: METHOD-001, METHOD-002, TOOL-001.

- **integrated-area-strategy:** Direct coverage. Christian Aid regional strategy development (Case Study 5) demonstrates comprehensive use for multi-stakeholder strategy with diverse partner types.
- **implementation-adaptation:** Direct, substantial coverage. Customization process, capacity building, introducing the framework to users, organizational take-up. Principles: PRINCIPLE-001. Methods: METHOD-004.
- **monitoring-learning:** Direct, full coverage. Indicator development, milestones model, project reviews and evaluations (two approaches), participatory M&E. Tools: TOOL-002. Methods: METHOD-001 (repeat scoring).
- **appropriateness-decision:** Incidental — framework does not explicitly address geographic or context appropriateness decisions, but urban/conflict context caveats are relevant.
- **area-selection:** Not covered.
- **transition-handover:** Not covered.

- Local government assumed accessible and cooperative — often not the case in dense urban settings
- Spatial community definition insufficient for urban neighborhood heterogeneity
- Participatory methods designed for village scale — incompatible with urban neighborhood density and anonymity
- *Not triggered:* Single-hazard framing — the framework is explicitly multi-hazard

<a id="decision-logic"></a>
## Decision Logic

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-001: Post-disaster minimal set rule**
- **Condition:** A community has recently experienced a disaster and is in early recovery.
- **Recommendation:** Do not apply the full 167 Characteristics. Identify a small, carefully selected set of key or minimum Characteristics relevant to recovery (e.g., access to clean water, shelter, food security) as first-step milestones.
- **Exception:** Not field-tested; treat as provisional guidance only. No tested 'minimum set' has been defined.
- **Rationale:** The ideal state of resilience is far removed from post-disaster conditions; applying the full framework risks discouraging progress.
- **Field query trigger:** "A major flood just occurred in our target community — which resilience characteristics are realistic short-term targets for recovery?"

---

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-002: Conflict context warning rule**
- **Condition:** You are working in a setting of active or recent conflict.
- **Recommendation:** The Characteristics assume community consensus and may not apply in conflict settings. Expect to modify heavily or use alternative approaches. No tested guidance exists for conflict adaptation.
- **Exception:** Not applicable — there is no known exception; this is a hard stop on standard application.
- **Rationale:** Conflict destroys social cohesion, productive assets, and enabling environment; consensus-based methods are invalid.
- **Field query trigger:** "Can I apply this community resilience framework in a community with deep social divisions or recent conflict?"

---

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-003: Indicator derivation from Characteristics**
- **Condition:** You need to convert a Characteristic into a measurable project indicator.
- **Recommendation:** First identify whether the Characteristic maps to impact/outcome, output, or process level. Then specify: what is measured, target population, data source, and threshold or attainment criterion. Use participatory discussion to agree the indicator. See Box 10 (Oxfam guidance) for worked examples.
- **Exception:** Where the Characteristic is inherently qualitative (e.g., 'shared vision'), use proxy measures or participatory judgment scales rather than quantitative targets.
- **Rationale:** Characteristics are descriptors of ideal states, not ready-made indicators; derivation requires explicit decision steps.
- **Field query trigger:** "How do I convert a qualitative resilience characteristic into a measurable indicator for donor reporting?"

---

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-004: Mid-implementation indicator refinement rule**
- **Condition:** A project is mid-implementation with a donor-agreed logical framework containing fixed indicators.
- **Recommendation:** Use the Characteristics not to replace existing indicators but to refine them — by splitting, rewording, clarifying, or adding precision. Do not add new indicators or replace existing ones without donor approval.
- **Exception:** If the project is at design stage, build indicators from Characteristics from the start — no need for the refinement workaround.
- **Rationale:** Donors expect reporting against original indicators; refinement improves measurability without breaking the contractual framework.
- **Field query trigger:** "Our project has donor-agreed indicators in a logframe — how do we use the Characteristics framework without creating a conflict with our existing M&E system?"

---

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-005: Framework introduction sequence rule**
- **Condition:** You are introducing the Characteristics to users who are not yet familiar with it.
- **Recommendation:** Begin with what people already know — real situations, projects, and activities — and work backwards to see how existing actions fit the framework. This reveals gaps as useful additions, not burdens.
- **Exception:** Where users already have high DRR expertise and familiarity with resilience frameworks, direct presentation of the framework structure is acceptable.
- **Rationale:** Starting with the abstract full framework overwhelms most users; starting with the known creates psychological safety and generates motivation.
- **Field query trigger:** "How do I introduce this complex resilience framework to field staff and community partners without losing them in the first session?"

---

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-006: Characteristics selection decision rule**
- **Condition:** You need to select priority Characteristics for a project or program.
- **Recommendation:** Use a participatory process with stakeholders; ensure at least two Characteristics from each Thematic Area to maintain balance; select based on relevance to local hazard types, achievability within project scope, and measurability. Do not impose selection from the top — process must be deliberative and inclusive.
- **Exception:** For slow-onset disasters (e.g., drought, chronic food insecurity), apply different selection criteria than for rapid-onset events — Tearfund's food security selection provides a reference model (Box 7).
- **Rationale:** No single organization can address all 167 Characteristics; selection focuses effort while the balance criterion prevents systematic neglect of whole Thematic Areas.
- **Field query trigger:** "With limited budget and time, which resilience characteristics should our project focus on first?"

---

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-007: VCA organization decision rule**
- **Condition:** You have completed a VCA that generated large amounts of diverse data across many issues.
- **Recommendation:** Use the Characteristics framework retrospectively to group ('map') vulnerabilities and capacities under the five Thematic Areas, creating a structured overview. Do not pre-define indicators before the VCA — apply the framework as an organizing lens after data collection.
- **Exception:** Where the VCA was designed around a single hazard or sector, the full Thematic Area mapping may be unnecessary; apply only relevant Components.
- **Rationale:** VCA methods should not pre-define indicators (to avoid bias); the Characteristics are useful after the fact to structure findings and identify gaps.
- **Field query trigger:** "Our VCA produced enormous amounts of varied data — how do we organize and interpret it coherently without having pre-defined what we were looking for?"

---

**S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-008: Project review mapping approach selection**
- **Condition:** You are conducting a project review or evaluation using the Characteristics as a framework.
- **Recommendation:** Choose between two approaches: (1) Select relevant Characteristics as indicators against which to evaluate progress — more common, better for measuring achievement. (2) Map all project activities against the full framework to identify gaps — good for systemic overview but can demoralize staff. If using approach 2, conduct as a team exercise, not as external review.
- **Exception:** Where morale is fragile, use approach 1 only. Approach 2 is better applied to multi-agency coverage than to single-agency review.
- **Rationale:** Approach 2 will almost always reveal extensive gaps; without careful handling this can create defensive attitudes and resistance.
- **Field query trigger:** "How do I evaluate whether our DRR project actually built community resilience in a way that is honest but does not demoralize staff?"

## Findings

### Conceptual Findings: Definitions and Framework Structure

**F-001** (`concept-definition` / `conceptual` layer)
Resilience is the capacity to: anticipate and minimize potential stresses through adaptation or resistance; manage basic functions during disastrous events; and recover after an event. It is broader than 'capacity' and goes beyond specific strategies. It is complex, multi-faceted, and relative (resilient to what, to what extent). No community can be completely safe. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-CONCEPT-002
*Routing rationale:* Core definition needed to ground the wiki resilience concept page; source adds the multi-dimensional framing and the relative nature of resilience.

**F-002** (`concept-definition` / `conceptual` layer)
Community is not only a spatial concept but also encompasses common interests, values, activities and structures. Communities are complex, often not united, dynamic, and dependent on external duty bearers. This makes identification of 'the community one is working with' inherently difficult, especially in urban settings. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-CONCEPT-003
*Routing rationale:* A distinct, operationally important definition; creates concept page that downstream advisory agents can use to surface community identification challenges in fragmented settings.

**F-003** (`framework-component` / `conceptual` layer)
The Characteristics framework organizes disaster resilience into 5 Thematic Areas, 28 Components of Resilience, and 167 Characteristics, with a parallel Enabling Environment for each component. The framework is aligned with the HFA but explicitly notes artificial separations. Thematic Area 1 (Governance) is cross-cutting and underlies all others. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-FRAMEWORK-001
*Routing rationale:* The core framework object; should become the foundational framework page in the wiki for all downstream references to this source.

**F-004** (`framework-component` / `conceptual` layer)
The Enabling Environment — wider institutional, policy and socio-economic conditions — is essential for community resilience. Communities depend on external duty bearers. Many features of the ideal Enabling Environment will be missing in practice; absence of key components can create a 'disabling' environment. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-FRAMEWORK-002
*Routing rationale:* Enabling Environment is a distinct concept page target; also creates the conceptual basis for advocacy and multi-level coordination task notes.

**F-005** (`design-principle` / `operational` layer)
The Characteristics framework is an ideal and a resource — not a checklist, not a manual. No community can be completely safe; no single organization can address all aspects. Users must select, adapt, and customize. The framework will only be as useful as the customization process that precedes application. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-PRINCIPLE-001
*Routing rationale:* Critical caveat to prevent misuse; should enrich the framework page and inform any advisory response that involves the Characteristics.

### Operational Findings: Application Methods and Progress Measurement

**F-006** (`process-step` / `operational` layer)
A milestones model is needed to measure progress from current state toward ideal resilience. The five-level scale (Level 1–5) provides reference points. For each selected Characteristic, stakeholders define current state, milestone steps, and indicators for each level. Progress is non-linear; features of resilience previously achieved may be lost. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-TOOL-002
*Routing rationale:* Creates a tool page for incremental resilience progress measurement; responds directly to donor demand for demonstrable progress.

**F-007** (`decision-rule` / `operational` layer)
Using the Characteristics for baseline studies: select relevant Components and Characteristics → design data collection → score each (1=met, 2=partly met, 3=not met) → generate baseline profile → visualize (spider diagram). Scores must be debated and agreed through participatory process — not imposed. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-001 (rule for post-disaster context) and S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-METHOD-001
*Routing rationale:* Creates a decision protocol for baseline study design using this framework.

**F-008** (`decision-rule` / `operational` layer)
VCA using Characteristics: do not pre-define indicators before the VCA (to avoid bias); apply the Characteristics retrospectively as an organizing framework to group and interpret findings. Box 9 provides a worked example of vulnerabilities and capacities grouped under five Thematic Areas. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-007 and S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-METHOD-002
*Routing rationale:* Creates a protocol distinguishing pre-VCA structure from post-VCA organization — a subtle but practically important distinction.

**F-009** (`decision-rule` / `operational` layer)
When selecting priority Characteristics: use participatory process; ensure minimum two from each Thematic Area for balance; select based on local hazard relevance, achievability, and measurability; use different criteria for slow-onset disasters. Tearfund's 'top 20' (Box 7) and ADPC minimum indicators (Box 8) provide reference models. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-006
*Routing rationale:* Core prioritization decision protocol; responds to the most common field question about how to operationalize a 167-item framework.

**F-010** (`decision-rule` / `operational` layer)
When converting a Characteristic to an indicator: identify impact/outcome vs output vs process level; specify what is measured, target population, data source, and threshold; for qualitative Characteristics use proxy measures or participatory judgment scales. Box 10 provides Oxfam worked examples for Thematic Area 5. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-003
*Routing rationale:* Creates a concrete indicator derivation protocol; high demand in field advisory contexts involving donor M&E requirements.

**F-011** (`decision-rule` / `operational` layer)
When applying Characteristics to a project mid-implementation with donor-agreed indicators: use Characteristics to refine (split, reword, clarify) existing indicators — not to replace them. Do not add new indicators without donor agreement. The Christian Aid DFID programme case (Case Study 6) demonstrates this successfully. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-004
*Routing rationale:* Highly practical mid-cycle adaptation rule; creates a protocol page with direct utility for reviews and evaluations.

**F-012** (`decision-rule` / `operational` layer)
When introducing the Characteristics to users: start with what people already know — existing projects and activities — and map backwards to the framework. This creates a solution-focused, positive framing and reveals gaps as useful additions, not failures. Case Study 1 (Bangladesh) and Case Study 2 (Nepal) are documented applications. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-005
*Routing rationale:* Creates a facilitation protocol page that addresses one of the most common challenges in Characteristics adoption.

### Tension and Risk Findings

**F-013** (`tension-surface` / `diagnostic` layer)
Thematic Area 4 (Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction) is structurally overloaded: it covers 7 very different Components (environmental management, health, livelihoods, social protection, financial instruments, physical protection, planning regimes) that do not naturally fit together. Users must take particular care to investigate all Components thoroughly and involve domain specialists. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-TNS-001
*Routing rationale:* Critical alert for any advisory agent using the framework; prevents systematic under-investigation of non-structural risk factors.

**F-014** (`evidence-gap` / `conceptual` layer)
In conflict settings, the Characteristics assume community consensus and may be invalid. Conflict undermines social cohesion, destroys assets, and breaks down the enabling environment. No field experience of applying the Characteristics in conflict settings exists (as of 2009). Alterations to the framework will be needed but what those alterations are cannot be specified. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RISK-001
*Routing rationale:* Major documented limitation; requires human review before routing to any synthesis or advisory page for conflict-affected contexts.

**F-015** (`decision-rule` / `operational` layer)
In post-disaster contexts: the ideal resilience state is far removed from immediate post-disaster conditions. Select a small set of key or minimum Characteristics relevant to recovery as first-step milestones (e.g., water access, shelter, food security). This approach is not field-tested — treat as provisional. → see S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-001
*Routing rationale:* Creates a post-disaster adaptation protocol; addresses a high-priority operational context with acknowledged evidence gap.

### Psychological and Adoption Findings

**F-016** (`field-practice` / `operational` layer)
The Characteristics produces an unexpected but important psychological outcome: by being solution-focused rather than problem-driven, it creates positive attitudes and motivation among users. Practitioners shift from 'what we want to prevent' to 'what we want to achieve'. This motivational dimension was consistently reported across field testing in Bangladesh, Nepal, Malawi, and other contexts.
*Routing rationale:* Source only — explains adoption dynamics rather than providing extractable methods; useful background for any advisory note on framework introduction.

### Frameworks

**Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-FRAMEWORK-001
**Object name:** Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community Framework
**Source location:** Section 3 (pp. 9–14) and Section 6 (pp. 28–44)
**Source fidelity note:** named
**Description:** A multi-level framework comprising: 5 Thematic Areas (Governance; Risk Assessment; Knowledge and Education; Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction; Disaster Preparedness and Response), 28 Components of Resilience, and 167 Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community. Each TA is organized into Components, which are elaborated into detailed Characteristics. A parallel Enabling Environment column accompanies each Component. Aligned with the five HFA Priorities for Action.
**Purpose:** To provide a comprehensive, structured picture of what a disaster-resilient community looks like, supporting planning, assessment, M&E, advocacy, research, and strategic planning.
**Components or steps:** TA1 Governance (7 Components); TA2 Risk Assessment (3 Components); TA3 Knowledge and Education (5 Components); TA4 Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction (7 Components); TA5 Disaster Preparedness and Response (6 Components).
**Relationships:** TA1 (Governance) is cross-cutting and underlies all others. Components within each TA are not strictly independent. Many Characteristics could appear under multiple TAs but have been placed in only one for usability.
**Logic:** Based on HFA; assumes resilience is multi-faceted, multi-sectoral, and requires collective action.
**Assumptions:** Communities are spatially defined with some degree of cohesion; participatory consensus is achievable; external duty bearers exist and can be engaged.
**Limitations:** HFA Priority 4 creates structural imbalance in TA4; artificial separations (especially TA2 hazard vs vulnerability); no inherent prioritization; assumes consensus not present in conflict settings or highly fragmented communities.
**Knowledge layer:** conceptual, diagnostic
**Justification for dual layer:** Serves as conceptual reference for what resilience looks like AND as diagnostic framework for assessing community status.
**Multi-type status:** Framework (primary); also used as tool for direct assessment application.
**Comparison notes:** Aligned with HFA national-level indicators (UN ISDR, UN OCHA); more detailed than ADPC minimum indicators (Box 8); more comprehensive than Tearfund's top-20 (Box 7).

**Object name:** Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR)
**Source location:** p. 8
**Source fidelity note:** named
**Definition:** The broad development and application of policies, strategies and practices to minimize vulnerabilities and disaster risks throughout society. **Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-CONCEPT-002
**Object name:** Resilience
**Source location:** p. 8
**Source fidelity note:** named
**Definition:** The capacity to: (1) anticipate, minimize and absorb potential stresses or destructive forces through adaptation or resistance; (2) manage or maintain certain basic functions and structures during disastrous events; (3) recover or 'bounce back' after an event.
**Related concepts:** Vulnerability (opposite side of the same coin); capacity (narrower); coping capacity
**Distinctions:** Broader than 'capacity' — goes beyond specific strategies. Multi-faceted and relative: resilient to what, to what extent. Differs from engineering resilience (bounce back to same state); includes absorption and adaptation.
**Role in source:** Central organizing concept; the 167 Characteristics define what resilience looks like.
**Limitations:** Difficult to separate from 'capacity' in everyday usage; 'community resilience' is harder to operationalize than individual or system resilience.
**Knowledge layer:** conceptual
**Comparison notes:** Multi-dimensional framing; differs from engineering resilience; aligned with social-ecological resilience tradition.

**Definition:** Not only a spatial concept (groups living in the same area) but also encompasses common interests, values, activities and structures. Communities are complex, often not united, dynamic, and do not exist in isolation.

**Distinctions:** Rejects simple spatial definition of conventional emergency management. Individuals can belong to multiple communities simultaneously. Communities depend on external duty bearers.
**Role in source:** Unit of analysis for all Characteristics. The difficulties of defining 'the community' are explicitly acknowledged.
**Limitations:** Broad definition makes it difficult to identify the specific community one is working with; especially problematic in urban settings.
**Knowledge layer:** conceptual
**Comparison notes:** More nuanced than standard emergency management definition; community heterogeneity is underemphasized in practice application.

### Methods

**Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-METHOD-001
**Object name:** Characteristics-Based Baseline Study
**Source location:** p. 22
**Source fidelity note:** named
**Description:** Using the Characteristics tables to design baseline data collection ensuring coverage of all resilience dimensions, scoring against the ideal, and producing a baseline resilience profile.
**Purpose:** Systematic, comprehensive baseline development for DRR projects.
**Inputs required:** Characteristics tables, community access, stakeholder group for participatory scoring.
**Procedure:** (1) Select relevant Components and Characteristics. (2) Design data collection instruments. (3) Collect baseline data. (4) Score each Characteristic: 1=met, 2=partly met, 3=not met. (5) Debate and agree scores through participatory process. (6) Produce baseline profile; visualize as spider diagram. (7) Repeat at endline for progress assessment.
**Outputs generated:** Baseline resilience profile, crude scores for prioritization, visual comparison tool.
**Quality controls:** Participatory scoring process; scores must be debated and agreed; based on both qualitative data and value judgements.
**Limitations:** Scoring is crude and may obscure nuance; no validated weighting system; requires facilitation capacity.
**Knowledge layer:** diagnostic
**Field query trigger:** "How do I establish a comprehensive baseline for community resilience before our project starts, covering all key dimensions?"
**Multi-type status:** Method.
**Comparison notes:** Similar to VCA but structured around the resilience ideal rather than vulnerability alone.

**Case reference:** Tearfund with consortium partners in Zomba, Malawi; 20 field and management staff; single afternoon exercise focused on Thematic Area 4 (Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction).

### Typologies

**Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-TYPOLOGY-001
**Object name:** Five-Level DRR Progress Scale
**Source location:** p. 19–20
**Source fidelity note:** named
**Description:** A five-level attainment scale for measuring progress toward resilience: Level 1 = little awareness, crisis response only; Level 2 = awareness and willingness, limited capacity, piecemeal interventions; Level 3 = development and implementation of solutions, improved capacity; Level 4 = coherence and integration, extensive linked interventions; Level 5 = culture of safety, DRR embedded in all policy, practice, attitudes, and behaviour.
**Classification criteria:** Levels differentiated by: awareness, motivation, capacity, intervention scope, and coherence/integration.
**Categories:** Five levels (see description above).
**Purpose:** To provide intermediate reference points for measuring progress toward the ideal disaster-resilient community.
**Use:** Baseline and endline assessment; milestone definition; broad overview of resilience status across Thematic Areas or Components.
**Limitations:** Simple scale — broad view only. Assumes groups have already advanced beyond Level 1. Progress may be non-linear (regression is possible). Behavioural change (Level 5) is difficult to measure. Not validated across contexts.
**Knowledge layer:** operational
**Comparison notes:** Similar to UN ISDR DRR indicators attainment scale; similar to Tearfund DRR mainstreaming assessment; should be distinguished from project-level indicators.
