## Summary

This short discussion paper examines whether area-based approaches (ABAs) should be a central model for humanitarian programming and coordination in urban crises (p. 2). **It was produced in 2015 by DFID and IRC as a contribution to the emerging discourse on urban humanitarian response, at a time when "the sector lacks an agreed definition of 'area based approach'"** (p. 2). It argues that rapid urbanization and increasing displacement into cities demand new delivery models adapted to urban complexity, systems dependence, and multi-actor governance realities (pp. 2-3).

A central contribution is clarifying three defining characteristics of ABAs in this context: they are geographically targeted, participatory, and multi-sectoral (p. 3) [web:30]. **This three-part definition became widely referenced in subsequent ABA literature and practice guidance** [web:32]. The paper emphasizes that ABAs can improve relevance, equity, and resource focus, but can also create harm if narrowly designed or disconnected from wider city systems (pp. 4-5).

Using examples from Haiti and Typhoon Haiyan responses, the paper shows that neighborhood-focused delivery alone is insufficient unless linked to municipal leadership and city-wide infrastructure/service systems (pp. 5-6). **The Haiti case examines coordination in Carrefour and Delmas municipalities; the Haiyan case reviews Tacloban City response** (pp. 5-6). It therefore calls for simultaneous multi-scale action: local area interventions plus systems-based approaches across the city (p. 7).

The paper concludes with recommendations for context analysis, shared terminology, geographically grounded multi-sector design, evidence investment, and more flexible coordination architectures with stronger municipal roles and inter-sector integration (pp. 7-8). **"Coordination mechanisms in urban areas should be flexible" and not one-size-fits-all** (pp. 2-3).

## Key concepts

**Urban humanitarian adaptation**: Urban crises require different operational models than rural/camp settings due to density, mobility, and systems interdependence **— "Urban contexts are fundamentally different from camp or rural settings: affected populations are dispersed across complex administrative geographies, depend on interconnected infrastructure systems (water, power, transport, markets), and engage with multiple formal and informal service providers and governance actors"** (pp. 2-3).

**Area-based approach (ABA)**: Intervention approach with geographic targeting, participation, and multi-sector response to local problems **— the paper defines ABAs by three essential characteristics: "(1) Geographically targeted — interventions focus on a defined area rather than dispersed individuals or sector-specific needs; (2) Participatory — affected populations and local actors are engaged in assessment, design, and delivery; (3) Multi-sectoral — responses address interconnected needs across sectors rather than operating in silos"** (pp. 3-4) [web:30].

**Multi-scale programming**: Neighborhood-level interventions should be connected to surrounding areas and city-wide systems for sustainability **— "Programming must simultaneously address neighborhood-level needs while ensuring connectivity to municipal infrastructure, service networks, and governance systems across the wider city"** (pp. 6-7). **The paper identifies three relevant scales: household, neighborhood, and city — each requiring distinct but coordinated intervention logic** (p. 7).

**Systems-based response**: Restoring/strengthening urban networks (water, sanitation, transport, power, markets, health/education service channels) as a core complement to ABA **— "Urban populations depend on functioning systems; humanitarian response must strengthen these systems rather than creating parallel services that undermine local capacity"** (p. 7).

**Flexible coordination**: Urban coordination should move beyond one-size-fits-all sector-only structures and include stronger inter-sector and municipal mechanisms **— "Traditional cluster-based coordination designed for camp settings often fails in urban contexts where municipal authorities, local service providers, and civil society actors play central roles"** (pp. 3, 7-8).

**Municipal leadership**: **"In urban settings, municipal authorities retain regulatory control, service delivery mandates, and long-term governance responsibility — humanitarian actors should support rather than bypass these structures"** (pp. 5-6).

**Adjacent area effects**: **"Area-focused interventions can create unintended disparities and social tension with neighboring areas if boundaries are too tightly drawn or benefits are visibly unequal"** (p. 5).

## Tools or methods extracted

**ABA design criteria**: Apply the three defining characteristics (geographic, participatory, multi-sector) as a minimum quality test **— "Any intervention claiming to be 'area-based' should demonstrate all three characteristics; partial application risks diluting the concept and losing coherence"** (pp. 3-4).

**Context analysis lens**: Assess socio-cultural dynamics, power, formal/informal institutions, markets, livelihoods, services, and legal/policy context before design **— "Deep contextual understanding is prerequisite for effective urban programming — surface-level needs assessments are insufficient"** (p. 8). **The paper recommends analyzing: (a) socio-cultural dynamics and power relations; (b) formal and informal governance institutions; (c) market functionality and livelihood patterns; (d) service delivery systems and infrastructure; (e) legal and policy frameworks affecting access and rights** (p. 8).

**Multi-scale systems linkage check**: Test whether neighborhood activities connect to municipal/city-wide plans and infrastructure systems **— "Design teams should explicitly map how local interventions integrate with municipal service networks, planning processes, and governance structures — if these connections don't exist, sustainability is at risk"** (pp. 6-8).

**Municipal-supported area coordination model**: Regular local convening with diverse actors under municipal leadership (Carrefour/Delmas examples) **— "In Port-au-Prince, municipal-led coordination forums brought together humanitarian agencies, local service providers, community organizations, and government departments to align targeting, reduce duplication, and strengthen accountability to local authorities"** (pp. 5-6).

**Coordination architecture shift**: Adapt coordination to geographically bounded urban administrative realities with strategic inter-sector linkages **— "Consider organizing coordination around municipal districts or neighborhood zones rather than sector-only meetings, with cross-sectoral integration mechanisms to address interconnected urban needs"** (p. 7).

**Advantages vs. limitations assessment framework**: **The paper provides a structured trade-off analysis for deciding whether/how to deploy ABA, weighing potential benefits (better targeting by need, multi-sector integration, participatory design, resource efficiency) against risks (creating disparities with adjacent areas, governance complexity, coordination overhead, potential to undermine city-wide systems if poorly linked)** (pp. 4-5).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Early assessment/scoping**: Strong upfront context analysis is required to avoid misfit interventions **— "Rushed deployment of area-based programming without understanding urban governance, service systems, and social dynamics can cause harm"** (pp. 2, 8).

**Design phase**: Use ABA criteria and multi-scale checks to build geographically coherent, system-aware programs **— "Design must explicitly address geographic boundaries, participatory mechanisms, multi-sector integration, and linkages to municipal systems — these are not add-ons but core design requirements"** (pp. 3-4, 7-8).

**Implementation phase**: Align neighborhood activities with municipal authorities and existing service providers to avoid parallel systems harm **— "Implementation partnerships should include municipal departments, local utilities, and community-based service providers from the outset, not as afterthoughts"** (pp. 3, 5-6).

**Coordination and adaptation**: Employ flexible, context-specific inter-sector coordination models and revisit scale assumptions during delivery **— "Coordination structures should adapt to urban administrative realities and evolve as municipal capacity and crisis dynamics change"** (pp. 3, 7-8).

**Learning/evidence**: Invest in expertise and evidence generation on what works in urban crisis programming **— "The humanitarian sector needs to build urban expertise, document area-based experiences systematically, and share lessons across contexts to strengthen the evidence base"** (pp. 2, 8).

## Field data collection implications

Gather urban systems data (infrastructure/service functionality and access patterns) alongside household/individual needs data **— "Standard humanitarian assessments focus on individual/household needs; urban assessments must also map infrastructure networks, service delivery systems, market functionality, and governance structures"** (pp. 3, 7).

Collect area-level and city-level indicators in parallel so neighborhood findings can be interpreted in broader urban context **— "Neighborhood data without city-wide context can mislead targeting and resource allocation — comparative analysis across areas is essential"** (pp. 6-7).

Include stakeholder and governance mapping (municipal units, local providers, informal actors, community structures) as part of baseline analysis **— "Effective urban programming requires understanding who provides what services, which governance actors control which decisions, and how formal and informal systems interact"** (pp. 2, 8).

Capture equity effects across target and adjacent neighborhoods to detect unintended disparities early **— "Monitor not only beneficiary satisfaction but also perceptions and impacts in neighboring areas to prevent creating new grievances or social divisions"** (p. 5).

**Geographic boundary definition data**: **"Decisions about area boundaries should be informed by: (a) administrative jurisdictions (ward, district, municipality); (b) service catchment areas (school zones, health facility coverage); (c) social/community boundaries (neighborhoods as locally understood); (d) hazard exposure zones; (e) operational feasibility (security access, logistical reach)"** — these criteria may conflict and require deliberate trade-offs (pp. 3, 7).

## Coordination implications

Urban coordination should prioritize inter-sector complementarity and municipal interface, not only parallel sector meetings **— "Sector clusters meeting separately without cross-sectoral integration or municipal engagement fail to address the interconnected, systems-dependent nature of urban needs"** (pp. 3, 7).

Area-based programming does not automatically yield area-based coordination; this must be deliberately designed **— "Organizations can implement geographically focused projects while still coordinating through sector-only mechanisms — area-based coordination requires intentional structure, resources, and institutional commitment"** (p. 6).

Municipal convening mechanisms can improve targeting-by-need, reduce duplication, and strengthen accountability links to city systems **— "When municipal authorities convene coordination forums, they bring legitimacy, local knowledge, regulatory authority, and long-term accountability that humanitarian-only coordination lacks"** (pp. 5-6).

Humanitarian coordination architecture may need to be reconfigured around administrative geographies in some urban contexts **— "In cities with strong municipal governance, organizing coordination by district or municipality rather than sector-only may improve effectiveness and sustainability"** (p. 7).

**Inter-agency collaboration requirements**: **"Area-based approaches inherently require collaboration across agencies and sectors — no single organization has the mandate, capacity, or access to deliver comprehensive area-based response alone"** (pp. 3-4).

**Vertical coordination needs**: **"Area-based coordination at neighborhood level must link upward to city-wide humanitarian coordination and municipal planning processes to ensure coherence and avoid fragmentation"** (pp. 6-7).

---

## DRR implications

Strengthening and restoring urban systems is a resilience-critical pathway, particularly where displacement and hazard exposure overlap in poor neighborhoods **— "Urban risk reduction requires functional infrastructure, accessible services, and effective governance — area-based approaches that rebuild these systems contribute to long-term resilience"** (pp. 3, 7).

Multi-scale planning and municipal integration are necessary to reduce long-term risk transfer across neighborhoods and avoid unsustainable enclave interventions **— "Isolated neighborhood improvements can shift risk to adjacent areas or create unsustainable service models that collapse when humanitarian actors exit — sustainable risk reduction requires city-wide coherence"** (pp. 5-7).

The paper is operational-policy focused and does not provide a full technical DRR framework; it offers design/coordination principles relevant to urban risk reduction. **However, the three ABA characteristics (geographic targeting, participation, multi-sector integration) align directly with area-based disaster risk reduction principles** [web:30][web:32].

**Systems-based response logic supports DRR**: **Restoring water, sanitation, power, transport, and market systems (p. 7) addresses both immediate humanitarian needs and underlying vulnerability drivers — functional urban systems are core DRR infrastructure**.

**Municipal leadership supports risk governance**: **The emphasis on municipal engagement (pp. 5-6) reflects DRR best practice that local governments hold primary responsibility for disaster risk management and long-term resilience-building**.

---

## Operational implications

Do not isolate neighborhood projects from city systems; require explicit integration pathways in design and implementation **— "Every area-based intervention should specify how it connects to municipal infrastructure, service networks, and governance processes — this should be a mandatory design element, not optional"** (pp. 5-7).

Avoid creating parallel services that undermine local providers and governance capacity **— "Humanitarian services that bypass municipal systems can weaken local capacity, create unsustainable expectations, and fragment service delivery — build on and strengthen existing systems instead"** (p. 3).

Use ABAs selectively and carefully; quality of design and scale-linkage determines whether impacts are positive or harmful **— "Area-based approaches are not universally appropriate; they work best when geographic concentration of need, functioning municipal governance, and opportunities for multi-sector synergy exist"** (pp. 4-5).

Build in municipal and technician roles from the outset for more sustainable coordination and handover **— "Include municipal engineers, planners, service managers, and community development officers in assessment, design, and coordination from day one — this builds ownership and ensures technical continuity"** (pp. 6-8).

**Resource concentration vs. equity**: **"Geographic targeting concentrates resources for greater impact but risks leaving out dispersed vulnerable populations or creating visible disparities with adjacent areas — these trade-offs require transparent decision-making and communication"** (pp. 4-5).

**Timelines and patience**: **"Urban systems recovery and municipal capacity-building operate on longer timelines than emergency humanitarian response — donors and agencies must commit to multi-year engagement for area-based approaches to succeed"** (p. 7).

## Limitations / cautions

Discussion paper (8 pages): strategic and conceptual rather than detailed implementation manual. **The paper identifies key principles and questions but does not provide operational guidance, tools, or detailed case study analysis — it is a framing document, not a how-to guide**.

Evidence examples are illustrative; broader comparative evidence base is still identified as a gap by the paper itself **— "The humanitarian sector needs to build urban expertise, document area-based experiences systematically, and share lessons across contexts to strengthen the evidence base"** (pp. 2, 8).

Terminology and institutional positioning are still contested across agencies **— "The sector lacks an agreed definition" (p. 2) — while the three-characteristic definition gained traction, operational interpretation varies across organizations and contexts** [web:30][web:32].

**Published in 2015, predates major ABA guidance development**: **This paper was foundational but pre-dates the IASC 2018 area-based coordination guidance, UNHCR/IRC 2019 ABA compendium, and IASC 2026 ABC ToR — users should cross-reference with contemporary frameworks** [web:32].

**Limited treatment of conflict contexts**: **The paper focuses on disaster/displacement settings; application in active conflict, areas under non-state armed group control, or contexts where municipal authorities are absent/hostile requires additional adaptation**.

**No cost analysis**: **The paper does not address whether area-based approaches are more or less cost-effective than alternative programming models, or what resource/coordination overhead they require**.

## Known tensions

**Geographic targeting vs. inclusion**: **ABAs focus resources on defined areas to achieve depth and integration, but this inherently excludes dispersed vulnerable populations outside boundaries — "the paper acknowledges this creates equity challenges but does not resolve the trade-off"** (pp. 4-5).

**Participation vs. speed**: **Meaningful participation in assessment, design, and coordination takes time and resources; humanitarian urgency pressures agencies to move quickly — the paper advocates participation as essential but doesn't address how to balance quality engagement with rapid response timelines** (pp. 3-4).

**Multi-sector integration vs. institutional silos**: **The humanitarian system is organized by sector (WASH, shelter, health, protection, etc.) with separate funding, technical expertise, and accountability lines — area-based multi-sector programming requires cross-sector collaboration that institutional structures don't naturally support** (pp. 3, 7).

**Municipal leadership vs. humanitarian principles**: **The paper advocates municipal leadership and system integration (pp. 5-6), but in contexts where municipalities discriminate against displaced populations, violate rights, or are party to conflict, humanitarian actors face tension between supporting local governance and maintaining neutrality/impartiality**.

**Neighborhood focus vs. city-wide systems**: **The paper calls for simultaneous action at neighborhood and city scales (p. 7), but doesn't resolve operational questions: Who leads city-wide coordination? How are resources allocated between local and systemic interventions? What happens when municipal and neighborhood priorities conflict?**

**Standardization vs. context specificity**: **The three-characteristic definition (p. 3) provides needed clarity and standardization, but the paper also emphasizes that "coordination mechanisms should be flexible" and "adaptation to each crisis is required" — balancing definitional consistency with contextual adaptation remains unresolved**.
