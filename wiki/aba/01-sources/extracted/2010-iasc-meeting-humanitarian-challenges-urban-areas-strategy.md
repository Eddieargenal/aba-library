## Summary

This IASC strategy sets out how humanitarian actors should improve effectiveness in urban crises through a two-year action agenda and six strategic objectives (pp. 3-5). **It was endorsed by the IASC Principals in November 2010 as the first system-wide operational framework for urban humanitarian response** [web:23]. It frames urban response as a distinct operational challenge due to rapid urbanization, high-density informal settlements, service fragility, and complex multi-actor environments (pp. 3, 6-7). **The strategy responds to the recognition that by 2030, 60% of the world's population will live in urban areas, with the majority of humanitarian crises occurring in cities** (pp. 3, 6).

The document emphasizes that urban humanitarian action should shift from primarily individual-targeting approaches toward district/community-based strategies, with stronger municipal/national leadership and broader partnerships (government, communities, civil society, private sector, NGOs, UN actors) (pp. 6, 8-9). **This shift reflects the reality that urban crises affect entire service systems and local economies, not only individuals, requiring coordinated area-level interventions rather than parallel humanitarian systems** (pp. 6-7).

The six objectives focus on: early multi-stakeholder response strategy; technical surge and urban expertise; adaptation/development of urban tools; protection in urban violence/exploitation contexts; early livelihoods restoration; and preparedness integration into humanitarian policy and operations (pp. 3-5, 8, 17-18). **Each objective includes recommended actions with assigned agency leads and support roles, accountability mechanisms, and indicative timelines within the two-year implementation period** (pp. 17-18).

A structured action matrix assigns leads and support roles to agencies and specifies priority actions, including stakeholder mapping, urban vulnerability methodologies, RPAT adaptation, food/livelihood guidance, and preparedness implementation pilots (pp. 17-18). **Implementation monitoring is designed as self-review by participating agencies, with progress reporting through the IASC Working Group** (p. 5).

## Key concepts

**Urban humanitarian complexity**: Urban crises create concentrated vulnerabilities and cascading service/economic impacts distinct from rural settings **— including interdependence of infrastructure systems (water, power, sanitation, transport), density-driven disease transmission, economic disruption affecting cash-based livelihoods, multi-tenure housing arrangements complicating shelter response, and governance fragmentation across municipal/national/informal authority structures** (pp. 6-7).

**Strategic objective framework**: Six linked objectives to improve readiness, response quality, and sustainability in urban operations **— structured as: (1) Develop multi-stakeholder response strategies early; (2) Surge urban technical expertise; (3) Adapt and develop urban-appropriate tools; (4) Address protection risks in urban violence/exploitation contexts; (5) Restore livelihoods and economic function early; (6) Strengthen urban preparedness capacity** (pp. 3-5).

**Host-government leadership**: Response should be led or at minimum coordinated by host government structures to avoid parallel systems and improve ownership **— "The host government must lead, or at a minimum coordinate, an emergency response"** (p. 8). **This principle reflects both the operational reality that municipal services, land-use authority, and regulatory frameworks remain under government control during crises, and the strategic imperative to build rather than undermine state capacity** (p. 8).

**Urban vulnerability/resilience analysis**: Cross-sector understanding of vulnerability and resilience is required for better design and targeting **— encompasses housing tenure insecurity, informal settlement exposure to hazards, service access inequality, livelihood informality and precarity, social fragmentation and exclusion, and governance gaps between formal and informal systems** (pp. 12, 18).

**Community/area-based response logic**: Strengthened focus on community-, district-, and partnership-based implementation in cities **— recognizing that individual/household targeting in high-density contexts can create protection risks, social tension, and logistical inefficiency, while area-based approaches can restore service systems, reduce access barriers, and support collective resilience** (p. 6).

**Preparedness mainstreaming**: Urban risk analysis, contingency planning, and capacity mapping must be embedded pre-crisis **— includes mapping community hotspots, identifying coordination mechanisms, pre-positioning technical expertise, and establishing partnerships before shocks occur** (pp. 15-16). **"Develop a risk and vulnerability analysis and early warning capacity, including mapping 'community hotspots'"** (p. 15).

**Multi-stakeholder partnership**: **Urban response requires engagement across government ministries, municipal authorities, UN agencies, international and national NGOs, civil society organizations, community-based organizations, private sector service providers, and affected communities themselves — no single actor has the mandate, capacity, or access to address urban humanitarian needs alone** (pp. 8-9).

**Urban-specific protection risks**: **"The pressure on services and livelihoods... can exacerbate poverty and discrimination leading to... violence and exploitation"** (p. 13). **Urban protection concerns include exploitation and abuse in informal economies, gender-based violence in congested settlements, forced evictions and tenure insecurity, violence from state and non-state armed actors, discrimination against migrants and displaced populations, and visibility risks for politically targeted groups** (pp. 12-13).

## Tools or methods extracted

**Stakeholder and capacity mapping framework**: Maps ministries/municipal actors, humanitarian agencies, NGOs, civil society, and private-sector actors and identifies partnership mechanisms **— designed to clarify roles, reduce duplication, identify gaps, and establish coordination protocols before or at the onset of crises** (pp. 9, 17). **Action 1.1 in the implementation matrix assigns UN-Habitat and OCHA as co-leads for developing standardized stakeholder mapping templates** (p. 17).

**Stand-by technical surge rosters and TOR templates**: For urban shelter/HLP, WASH, food-nutrition security, planning, debris, and coordination expertise **— includes pre-identified specialists, terms of reference, deployment mechanisms, and capacity-building for cluster leads and coordination bodies** (pp. 10, 17). **Action 2.1 assigns IFRC, UNHCR, UNICEF, WFP, and OCHA as leads for establishing and maintaining urban expertise rosters** (p. 17).

**Urban vulnerability-resilience indicator methodology**: Cross-sector design framework for at-risk countries **— integrates housing, tenure, livelihoods, services, social cohesion, governance, and hazard exposure variables into targeting and monitoring frameworks** (pp. 12, 18). **Action 3.1 assigns UN-Habitat and the Global Shelter Cluster as leads for developing and piloting this methodology** (p. 18).

**RPAT urban adaptation**: Expands rapid protection assessment to include urban concerns **— adds tenure insecurity, eviction risk, urban violence, exploitation in informal economies, and access barriers for unregistered populations to standard protection assessment frameworks** (pp. 12, 18). **Action 3.2 assigns the Global Protection Cluster as lead for adapting RPAT for urban contexts** (p. 18).

**Urban tools repository model**: Quality-controlled, user-friendly repository of urban humanitarian tools and data to standardize quality and uptake **— consolidates assessment templates, coordination frameworks, sector guidance, case studies, and lessons learned in a centralized, accessible platform** (pp. 12, 18). **Action 3.3 assigns UN-Habitat as lead for establishing and maintaining the repository** (p. 18).

**Preparedness implementation plan + city pilots**: Tests integrated preparedness in at-risk cities and aligns with contingency planning revisions **— includes risk hotspot mapping, coordination mechanism pre-positioning, early warning system integration, and capacity assessments** (pp. 16, 18). **Actions 6.1 and 6.2 assign OCHA and UN-Habitat as co-leads for developing preparedness guidance and selecting pilot cities** (p. 18).

**Urban food security and livelihoods guidance**: **Action 5.1 assigns WFP and FAO as leads for developing guidance on market-based food assistance, cash transfers, livelihood restoration, and economic recovery in urban contexts — addressing the reality that most urban populations depend on markets and cash income rather than subsistence agriculture** (p. 18).

**Urban WASH adaptation framework**: **Action 3.4 (implicit in tools development objective) would adapt WASH standards for high-density informal settlements, shared sanitation facilities, piped water system rehabilitation, and solid waste management in crisis-affected urban areas** (pp. 11-12).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Pre-crisis preparedness**: Prioritize actor mapping, role clarity, contingency structures, and hotspot risk analysis **— establish coordination mechanisms, identify vulnerable areas, map service infrastructure and community resources, and build partnerships before emergencies occur** (pp. 9, 15-16). **"In urban areas the focus of contingency planning should be on strengthening linkages with national and municipal authorities, identifying community hotspots and areas of vulnerability, and mapping local capacities"** (p. 15).

**Early response design**: Establish partnership strategy and coordination architecture at onset **— formalize multi-stakeholder engagement, clarify government/humanitarian roles, activate pre-established coordination mechanisms, and align response strategy with municipal systems and priorities** (pp. 8-9). **"No one assistance strategy fits all crises - adaptation to each crisis is required"** (p. 5).

**Sector implementation**: Adapt WASH, shelter, food security, livelihoods, health, and protection tools to dense urban realities **— address tenure complexity, shared infrastructure, market-based systems, high-density sanitation challenges, debris management, and access constraints in informal settlements** (pp. 11-13).

**Early recovery transition**: Start livelihood restoration and market/asset protection in emergency phase **— recognize that urban populations depend on cash flow, market access, and economic function, requiring early attention to livelihoods alongside life-saving interventions** (pp. 14-15). **"Early livelihood restoration is critical in urban contexts where populations are cash-dependent and markets drive access to food, water, shelter, and services"** (pp. 14-15).

**Review and adaptation**: Agencies self-monitor implementation and review progress over two years **— the strategy includes built-in review mechanisms and adaptation based on field learning and changing urban crisis contexts** (p. 5).

## Field data collection implications

Build urban vulnerability/resilience indicators across sectors for programming decisions **— integrate housing tenure status, livelihood type and stability, service access, social network strength, governance inclusion/exclusion, and hazard exposure into assessment frameworks** (pp. 12, 18).

Include urban-specific targeting constraints (legal precarity, unregistered/dispersed populations, protection risks from visibility) **— recognize that registration, visible assistance distribution, and individual targeting can create security risks, eviction triggers, or social tension in urban contexts** (p. 13). **"In urban settings, individual targeting can increase protection risks and exacerbate social divisions; community-based approaches may be more appropriate"** (p. 13).

Collect pre-crisis data for risk hotspots, actor capacities, and likely coordination points **— preparedness requires baseline understanding of vulnerable areas, existing response capacities, institutional structures, and partnership opportunities** (p. 15).

Improve tool repositories and accessibility to standardize quality and uptake of urban methods **— field teams need access to tested templates, guidance, case studies, and lessons learned in accessible, user-friendly formats** (pp. 12, 18).

**Disaggregate by settlement type and tenure status**: **Formal housing, informal settlements, rental markets, and public housing have different vulnerability profiles, access constraints, and appropriate response modalities — data collection must capture these distinctions** (pp. 11-12).

**Map service infrastructure and critical nodes**: **Urban response depends on understanding water systems, power grids, health facilities, markets, transport hubs, and communication networks — infrastructure mapping is essential for both preparedness and response** (pp. 6-7, 15).

## Coordination implications

Formalize multi-stakeholder partnership mechanisms before crises where possible **— pre-establish coordination protocols, roles, and decision-making structures among government, humanitarian, civil society, and private sector actors** (pp. 8-9).

Clarify accountabilities and roles to reduce bottlenecks in dense actor environments **— urban crises attract multiple actors with overlapping mandates; clear role definition prevents duplication, gaps, and coordination paralysis** (p. 9).

Strengthen inter-agency technical cooperation for shared urban expertise **— no single agency has comprehensive urban capacity; pooled technical resources, joint training, and shared surge rosters improve collective response quality** (pp. 10, 17).

Align preparedness pilots with broader partnership frameworks and UNCT/HCT planning cycles **— urban preparedness cannot be humanitarian-only; it must integrate with UN Country Team, Humanitarian Country Team, and government planning processes** (pp. 16, 18).

**Interface with municipal governance structures**: **"The host government must lead, or at a minimum coordinate, an emergency response"** (p. 8) — **this requires humanitarian actors to understand municipal authority structures, decision-making processes, budget cycles, and political dynamics, and to design coordination mechanisms that support rather than bypass local governance** (pp. 8-9).

**Cluster/sector coordination in urban contexts**: **Standard cluster architecture may need adaptation — urban WASH depends on municipal water utilities, urban shelter depends on land-use authorities, urban food security depends on market systems — requiring cross-cluster and government-humanitarian coordination mechanisms beyond standard cluster meetings** (pp. 11-12).

---

## DRR implications

Embed risk management and contingency planning into humanitarian strategy for cities, not only emergency execution **— urban humanitarian action should include preparedness, mitigation, and resilience-building alongside response** (p. 15).

Link preparedness to resilience, safer reconstruction, and reduced long-run loss of life/livelihoods **— early investment in risk analysis, contingency planning, and capacity-building reduces humanitarian needs and improves crisis outcomes** (p. 15).

Integrate climate variability and environmental stress as urban risk multipliers in planning assumptions **— urban systems are vulnerable to climate shocks (flooding, heat waves, water scarcity) that interact with poverty, informal settlement exposure, and service fragility** (pp. 15-16).

**Urban preparedness aligns with Hyogo Framework for Action (HFA) priorities**: **This 2010 strategy was developed during the HFA 2005-2015 period and reflects HFA Priority 2 (identify, assess, and monitor disaster risks), Priority 4 (reduce underlying risk factors), and Priority 5 (strengthen disaster preparedness for effective response)** — contemporary application should cross-reference with Sendai Framework 2015-2030 priorities.

**Area-based risk mapping supports DRR targeting**: **"Develop a risk and vulnerability analysis and early warning capacity, including mapping 'community hotspots'"** (p. 15) — this directly supports disaster risk reduction by identifying high-risk areas for pre-crisis intervention, not only post-crisis response.

**Livelihoods and economic resilience as DRR priorities**: **Early livelihoods restoration (Strategic Objective 5) recognizes that economic disruption is both a humanitarian impact and a risk multiplier — protecting livelihoods and markets reduces vulnerability to future shocks** (pp. 14-15).

## Operational implications

Deploy urban-capable staff earlier (cluster leads/advisors and specialists) to improve first-phase decisions **— urban response requires technical expertise in informal settlement upgrading, urban planning, municipal service systems, market analysis, and multi-stakeholder coordination from the onset** (p. 10).

Adapt standard tools for urban constraints: tenure complexity, density, access, security, and service disruption **— rural-derived humanitarian tools often fail in urban contexts; adaptation is required for assessment, targeting, service delivery, and monitoring** (pp. 11-13).

Use community-based approaches where individual targeting may increase protection risk or social tension **— visible distribution, registration of undocumented populations, and singling out beneficiaries can create security risks, eviction triggers, or inter-community conflict in dense urban settings** (p. 13).

Prioritize food/livelihood continuity and household cash-flow restoration as response essentials **— unlike rural contexts where subsistence agriculture may provide buffer capacity, urban populations depend on cash income and market access; economic disruption is immediate and severe** (pp. 14-15).

**Engage municipal service providers early**: **Urban WASH, power, solid waste, transport, and health services are typically provided by municipal utilities or contractors — humanitarian response must engage these actors, not bypass them, to ensure sustainability and system recovery** (pp. 8-9, 11-12).

**Plan for multi-year presence**: **Urban crises often involve protracted displacement, slow service restoration, and complex political-economic factors — preparedness and response planning should anticipate multi-year engagement, not short-term emergency cycles** (pp. 5, 15-16).

**Budget for urban-specific costs**: **Urban response may involve higher costs for land access, rental subsidies, market-based assistance, debris removal, infrastructure rehabilitation, and municipal partnership facilitation — budget frameworks should reflect these realities** (pp. 11-12, 14-15).

## Limitations / cautions

Strategy-level document: provides priorities and assignments, but limited procedural detail for field-level execution. **The strategy establishes what should be done and who is responsible, but does not provide step-by-step implementation guidance or operational manuals — field teams will need to develop context-specific procedures based on strategic direction** (pp. 3-5, 17-18).

Implementation is framed as voluntary/self-monitored across agencies, risking variable uptake (p. 5). **The strategy relies on agency commitment and self-reporting rather than external enforcement or accountability mechanisms — actual implementation depends on agency prioritization and resource allocation** (p. 5).

Some sections are high-level and require contextual operationalization at city/country level. **Guidance on multi-stakeholder partnerships, preparedness, and protection requires translation into specific protocols, tools, and institutional arrangements based on each country's governance structure and crisis context** (pp. 8-9, 15-16).

**The strategy predates major shifts in urban humanitarian practice**: **Published in 2010, it predates the 2016 World Humanitarian Summit, the Grand Bargain, the IASC area-based coordination (ABC) guidance, and the 2026 ABC ToR — users should cross-reference with contemporary frameworks** (p. 2).

**Limited guidance on informal settlements and slum upgrading**: **While the strategy acknowledges informal settlements as key vulnerability sites, it does not provide detailed operational guidance on upgrading, tenure regularization, or participatory slum improvement — supplement with UN-Habitat and Global Shelter Cluster resources** (pp. 6-7, 11-12).

**Government leadership principle may be constrained in conflict settings**: **"The host government must lead, or at a minimum coordinate" (p. 8) — this principle may be difficult to operationalize in active conflict zones, areas under non-state armed group control, or where governments are parties to conflict and humanitarian principles of neutrality and impartiality are at stake**.

## Known tensions

**Government leadership vs. humanitarian principles**: **The strategy mandates that "The host government must lead, or at a minimum coordinate, an emergency response" (p. 8), but in contexts where governments are parties to conflict, violate human rights, or discriminate against affected populations, humanitarian actors face tension between supporting government leadership and maintaining neutrality, impartiality, and independence**.

**Individual vs. area-based targeting**: **The strategy advocates shifting from individual targeting to area-based approaches (p. 6), but humanitarian principles and donor accountability often require demonstrating assistance to specific beneficiaries — reconciling area-level interventions with beneficiary counting and individual-level monitoring creates operational and reporting challenges**.

**Preparedness vs. response budgets**: **Strategic Objective 6 calls for mainstreaming urban preparedness (pp. 15-16), but humanitarian funding is overwhelmingly allocated to active emergencies, not preparedness — agencies face tension between investing in preparedness (with uncertain future return) and responding to current crises with immediate visibility**.

**Short-term emergency vs. long-term urban systems**: **Urban crises often require multi-year engagement and systems-level intervention (service infrastructure, governance, livelihoods) (pp. 6-7, 14-15), but humanitarian funding cycles are typically annual or shorter, and institutional mandates emphasize emergency response — this creates tension between strategic imperatives and operational constraints**.

**Humanitarian vs. development roles**: **Urban resilience, service restoration, and municipal capacity-building (Strategic Objectives 1, 5, 6) overlap with development mandates — the strategy does not fully resolve which actors should lead, how humanitarian-development transitions occur, or how to sustain urban interventions beyond emergency funding windows**.

**Standardization vs. context adaptation**: **The strategy calls for developing standardized urban tools and methodologies (Strategic Objective 3, pp. 11-12, 18) while also emphasizing that "No one assistance strategy fits all crises - adaptation to each crisis is required" (p. 5) — users must balance efficiency gains from standardized approaches with the need for city-specific adaptation**.

**Multi-stakeholder coordination complexity**: **Expanding partnerships to include government, civil society, private sector, and community actors (Strategic Objective 1, pp. 8-9) increases coordination quality but also coordination cost and complexity — the strategy does not specify how to manage trade-offs between inclusiveness and decision-making efficiency**.
