# Summary
This Good Practice Review (GPR 12) synthesizes practical lessons for humanitarian response in urban crises. It was commissioned to address a gap between growing urban caseloads and limited practical field guidance for practitioners (pp. viii-ix).

The review frames urban response as a complexity challenge: dense and diverse populations, market dependence, fragmented governance, inequality, displacement, and rapidly changing conditions complicate assessment, targeting, coordination, and delivery (p. viii).

It is structured in four major blocks: urban context, cross-cutting themes/issues (coordination, corruption, resilience), tools/approaches across the programme cycle, and sectoral response guidance (including HLP, shelter, WASH, livelihoods, education, protection, health, food security) (pp. iii, viii-ix).

# Key concepts
- Urban complexity in humanitarian response: programming assumptions from rural contexts often require revision in cities (p. viii).
- Systems and actor ecology: humanitarian response depends on engaging municipal authorities, communities, and other local actors embedded in urban systems (pp. viii, ix).
- Programme-cycle toolchain: context analysis, assessments/profiling, targeting, response analysis, design/management, and M&E should be adapted to urban realities (pp. ix, 113, 128, 138).
- Area-based and multi-sector approaches: highlighted as practical ways to address interrelated urban needs and place-based dynamics (pp. 79, 113, 128).
- Iterative learning orientation: urban monitoring/evaluation must handle uncertainty, changing assumptions, and complex causality (pp. 138-143).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Context and assessment stage: defines urban threat/vulnerability/actor context before intervention design (pp. viii-ix, 1-55).
- Design stage: provides structured options for targeting, response analysis, and design-management adaptation (pp. 118, 123, 132).
- Implementation stage: supports modality choices (including cash/markets and ABA variants) and coordination practices (pp. 79, 88, 97).
- Monitoring and learning stage: details M&E challenges and recommended iterative/complexity-aware practices (pp. 138-143).
- Sector execution stage: links cross-cutting tools to sector-specific delivery considerations (pp. 153 onward).

# Field data collection implications
- Multi-sector assessment is recommended in urban crises because needs are interrelated and cannot be reliably addressed in isolation (p. 113).
- Profiling is especially relevant in urban displacement settings where vulnerable populations are dispersed and less visible than in camp settings (pp. ix, 113).
- Mapping and geospatial analysis can improve situational awareness and coordination in dense, rapidly changing contexts (pp. 97, 102).
- Monitoring systems should prioritize “need to know” over data-heavy collection and be designed for rapid adaptation (pp. 142-143).

# Coordination implications
- Strong engagement with city/local authorities is repeatedly emphasized for legitimacy, feasibility, and sustainability (pp. 55-58).
- The review discusses limitations of conventional coordination structures in urban settings and the need for stronger inter-sector collaboration (pp. 61-62).
- Shared/joint approaches are presented as advantageous in dense, multi-actor environments where overlap and fragmentation risks are high (p. 148).
- Coordination must include non-traditional urban actors and local governance realities, including contested authority environments (pp. viii, 48-55).

# DRR implications
- Urban resilience and risk reduction are treated as integral to humanitarian effectiveness, not only long-term development concerns (pp. 66-73).
- The review links operational practice to wider DRR/resilience frameworks and standards relevant to urban contexts (pp. 73-76).
- It highlights the need to connect immediate response to longer-term risk reduction pathways where feasible (pp. ix, 66-73).

# Operational implications
- The document is a practice-oriented reference for field managers designing, implementing, and monitoring urban programmes (p. viii).
- It explicitly notes scope limitations: each section could be a standalone publication and coverage is selective (p. x).
- It encourages adapting rather than copying tools, emphasizing contextual fit and organisational learning (pp. viii, 132-134).
- It provides a broad menu of options rather than a single prescriptive model, requiring contextual judgement by teams (pp. ix, 138).

# Claims worth citing
- Urban humanitarian crises are increasing, while many traditional humanitarian approaches require adaptation for city contexts (pp. vii-viii).
- The GPR was commissioned because practitioners still lacked practical urban guidance despite growing policy attention (p. viii).
- The review organizes response guidance across context, cross-cutting issues, tools, and sectors to support field decision-making (pp. iii, viii-ix).
- Urban monitoring and evaluation must account for complexity, uncertainty, and iterative adaptation (pp. 138-143).
- Scope limits are explicit: not exhaustive, with selective inclusion due to breadth of topic (p. x).

# Limitations / cautions
- The author explicitly notes the publication cannot be comprehensive and that substantial material is necessarily excluded (p. x).
- The review is synthesis guidance, not a single tested operational protocol for all contexts (pp. viii-x).
- Many recommended tools require adaptation to local political, governance, and market realities before use (pp. viii, 107-110).
