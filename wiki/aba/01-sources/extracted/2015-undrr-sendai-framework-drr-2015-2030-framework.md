# Summary
This document is the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030, adopted at the Third United Nations World Conference on Disaster Risk Reduction in Sendai, Japan, on 18 March 2015 (pp. 8, 4). It is positioned as the successor to the Hyogo Framework for Action 2005-2015 (pp. 4, 8).

The framework sets a 15-year expected outcome and a goal focused on preventing new risk, reducing existing risk, and strengthening resilience through integrated and inclusive measures across economic, social, environmental, legal, institutional and technical domains (p. 11).

It defines seven global targets (mortality, affected people, economic loss, critical infrastructure/basic services, national-local DRR strategies, international cooperation, and early warning/risk information access) and structures implementation around four priorities for action (pp. 11, 13).

Operationally, the framework emphasizes risk understanding, governance, risk-informed investment, preparedness/"Build Back Better," inclusive stakeholder roles, and international cooperation with enhanced support for developing countries and vulnerable country groups (pp. 13, 16-25).

# Key concepts
- Scope of DRR: applies to small/large, frequent/infrequent, sudden/slow-onset disasters from natural and human-made hazards, including environmental, technological and biological hazards/risks (p. 10).
- Expected outcome: substantial reduction of disaster risk and losses in lives, livelihoods, health, and economic/physical/social/cultural/environmental assets (p. 11).
- Goal: prevent new and reduce existing disaster risk through integrated and inclusive measures that reduce exposure/vulnerability and increase preparedness and recovery capacity (p. 11).
- Seven global targets: framework-wide progress targets through 2030, with national targets/indicators contributing to global achievement (p. 11). Specifically: (a) substantially reduce global disaster mortality; (b) substantially reduce the number of affected people; (c) reduce direct disaster economic loss relative to GDP; (d) substantially reduce damage to critical infrastructure and disruption of basic services, including health and education; (e) substantially increase the number of countries with national and local DRR strategies by 2020; (f) substantially enhance international cooperation to developing countries; (g) substantially increase the availability of and access to multi-hazard early warning systems and disaster risk information (p. 11).
- Four priorities for action: understanding risk; governance; investment for resilience; preparedness and Build Back Better (p. 13).
- Guiding governance principle: States hold primary responsibility, with shared responsibilities across sectors/stakeholders and all-of-society engagement (p. 12).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Global target framework (7 targets): standardized outcome-tracking structure for DRR progress and accountability (p. 11).
- Risk information system approach: promote collection, analysis, management, dissemination, and open access to non-sensitive risk/loss data (pp. 13-15).
- Baseline and periodic assessment method: establish baselines and repeatedly assess vulnerability, capacity, exposure, hazard characteristics, and sequential effects (p. 13).
- Location-based risk mapping: develop/update/disseminate risk maps and location-specific risk information for decision-makers and at-risk communities (p. 14).
- Governance implementation architecture: national/local DRR strategies with targets, indicators, time frames, plus coordination platforms/focal points (pp. 16-17).
- Build Back Better recovery method: integrate DRR into recovery, rehabilitation and reconstruction planning before disasters and throughout post-disaster action (pp. 20-22).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Context and diagnosis: requires multi-dimensional risk understanding (vulnerability, capacity, exposure, hazards, environment) before intervention design (p. 13).
- Strategy and planning: requires national/local DRR plans across timescales with targets, indicators and timelines (p. 16).
- Investment/design: prioritizes structural and non-structural investments in resilience, especially critical facilities/infrastructure (pp. 17-19).
- Preparedness and response: requires contingency plans, early warning systems, exercises, evacuation readiness, and continuity planning (pp. 20-21).
- Recovery and reconstruction: requires DRR-integrated recovery and Build Back Better measures linked to long-term development (pp. 20-22).

# Field data collection implications
- Collect disaggregated risk/loss data (including sex, age, disability dimensions where relevant) and ensure accessibility of non-sensitive information (pp. 12, 14).
- Maintain periodic baseline-driven assessments of risk, exposure, vulnerability, capacity, and hazard interactions at relevant social/spatial scales (p. 13).
- Record and publicly account for disaster losses and multi-domain impacts (economic, social, health, education, environmental, cultural heritage) (p. 14).
- Use geospatial information and location-based risk mapping as standard inputs to planning and communication (pp. 14-15).
- Strengthen real-time data access, measurement tools, and ICT-enabled dissemination mechanisms (p. 14).

# Coordination implications
- DRR governance requires coordination within and across sectors and full engagement of executive/legislative institutions plus public/private stakeholders (p. 12).
- National and local coordination forums/platforms with clear roles/authority are explicitly recommended (pp. 16-17).
- Regional and transboundary cooperation mechanisms are emphasized for shared and cross-border risks (pp. 17, 24).
- Global and regional platforms are framed as mechanisms for partnership, implementation review, and policy/investment learning (p. 17).

# DRR implications
- The document is a core global DRR normative framework for 2015-2030, centered on reducing risk creation and existing risk while strengthening resilience (pp. 11, 13).
- It shifts emphasis toward disaster risk management and risk governance, not only post-disaster management (pp. 4, 16).
- It broadens DRR to include natural and human-made hazards and links DRR coherence with sustainable development, climate, health, environment, and poverty agendas (pp. 10, 12).
- It embeds preparedness, response, and recovery as DRR functions and formalizes Build Back Better as a critical post-disaster opportunity (pp. 20-22).

# Operational implications
- Agencies and governments should align programmes to the four priority areas and relevant target metrics (pp. 11, 13).
- DRR implementation should include legal/policy integration, budgeted institutional capacity, and compliance mechanisms (pp. 16-18).
- Investment decisions should prioritize resilience of critical infrastructure, health systems, and livelihoods, with risk transfer/financial protection where appropriate (pp. 18-20).
- Preparedness operations should include people-centered multi-hazard warning systems, regular drills, and area-based support systems for response/displacement (pp. 20-21).
- Recovery planning should be pre-arranged and integrated with land-use, standards improvement, and long-term development of affected areas (pp. 21-22).

# Claims worth citing
- The framework was adopted at the Third UN World Conference on Disaster Risk Reduction (Sendai, March 2015) (p. 8).
- Expected outcome: substantial reduction in disaster risk and losses across life, livelihood, health and asset dimensions (p. 11).
- Goal: prevent new and reduce existing disaster risk through integrated/inclusive measures that reduce exposure and vulnerability and strengthen preparedness/recovery (p. 11).
- Seven global targets include mortality, affected people, economic loss, infrastructure/services disruption, strategy coverage, cooperation, and early warning/risk information access (p. 11).
- Four priorities for action structure implementation: understanding risk, governance, investment, and preparedness/Build Back Better (p. 13).

# Limitations / cautions
- The framework is a high-level normative policy instrument and does not provide detailed sector-by-sector implementation manuals (inferred from structure and action-list format, pp. 13-26).
- Global targets were to be complemented by later indicator development work, so measurement detail is not fully defined in this text (pp. 11, 26).
- Implementation is explicitly conditioned by national circumstances, legal systems, capacities and available international support (pp. 12-13, 23-25).
- The PDF references www.unisdr.org (the former UNDRR web identity, p. 37); UNISDR was renamed UNDRR in 2019. The authoritative download URL is now resolved in frontmatter via undrr.org.
