## Summary

This document is the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030, adopted at the Third United Nations World Conference on Disaster Risk Reduction in Sendai, Japan, on 18 March 2015 (pp. 8, 4). **It is the successor to the Hyogo Framework for Action (HFA) 2005-2015 and represents the global blueprint for disaster risk reduction through 2030, endorsed by 187 UN Member States** (pp. 4, 8). **The framework was negotiated over 18 months through regional consultations and three preparatory committee meetings before adoption at the Sendai Conference attended by 6,500 participants including government delegations, civil society, private sector, and scientific communities** (p. 4).

The framework sets a 15-year expected outcome and a goal focused on preventing new risk, reducing existing risk, and strengthening resilience through integrated and inclusive measures across economic, social, environmental, legal, institutional and technical domains (p. 11). **"The expected outcome is: The substantial reduction of disaster risk and losses in lives, livelihoods and health and in the economic, physical, social, cultural and environmental assets of persons, businesses, communities and countries"** (p. 11).

It defines seven global targets (mortality, affected people, economic loss, critical infrastructure/basic services, national-local DRR strategies, international cooperation, and early warning/risk information access) and structures implementation around four priorities for action (pp. 11, 13). **The seven targets are accompanied by the commitment to develop agreed indicators by 2016 through an open-ended intergovernmental expert working group** (p. 11).

Operationally, the framework emphasizes risk understanding, governance, risk-informed investment, preparedness/"Build Back Better," inclusive stakeholder roles, and international cooperation with enhanced support for developing countries and vulnerable country groups (pp. 13, 16-25). **A key shift from HFA is the explicit emphasis on managing disaster risk rather than managing disasters, strengthening disaster risk governance, investing in resilience, and integrating Build Back Better into recovery** (pp. 4, 8, 16, 20-22).

## Key concepts

**Scope of DRR**: Applies to small/large, frequent/infrequent, sudden/slow-onset disasters from natural and human-made hazards, including environmental, technological and biological hazards/risks **— "The present Framework will apply to the risk of small-scale and large-scale, frequent and infrequent, sudden and slow-onset disasters, caused by natural or manmade hazards as well as related environmental, technological and biological hazards and risks"** (p. 10).

**Expected outcome**: Substantial reduction of disaster risk and losses in lives, livelihoods, health, and economic/physical/social/cultural/environmental assets **— measured across persons, businesses, communities and countries through 2030** (p. 11).

**Goal**: Prevent new and reduce existing disaster risk through integrated and inclusive measures that reduce exposure/vulnerability and increase preparedness and recovery capacity **— "Prevent new and reduce existing disaster risk through the implementation of integrated and inclusive economic, structural, legal, social, health, cultural, educational, environmental, technological, political and institutional measures that prevent and reduce hazard exposure and vulnerability to disaster, increase preparedness for response and recovery, and thus strengthen resilience"** (p. 11).

**Seven global targets**: Framework-wide progress targets through 2030, with national targets/indicators contributing to global achievement (p. 11). Specifically: **(a) Substantially reduce global disaster mortality by 2030, aiming to lower average per 100,000 global mortality rate in the decade 2020-2030 compared to the period 2005-2015; (b) Substantially reduce the number of affected people globally by 2030, aiming to lower the average global figure per 100,000 in the decade 2020-2030 compared to the period 2005-2015; (c) Reduce direct disaster economic loss in relation to global gross domestic product (GDP) by 2030; (d) Substantially reduce disaster damage to critical infrastructure and disruption of basic services, among them health and educational facilities, including through developing their resilience by 2030; (e) Substantially increase the number of countries with national and local disaster risk reduction strategies by 2020; (f) Substantially enhance international cooperation to developing countries through adequate and sustainable support to complement their national actions for implementation of this Framework by 2030; (g) Substantially increase the availability of and access to multi-hazard early warning systems and disaster risk information and assessments to people by 2030** (p. 11).

**Four priorities for action**: **(1) Understanding disaster risk; (2) Strengthening disaster risk governance to manage disaster risk; (3) Investing in disaster risk reduction for resilience; (4) Enhancing disaster preparedness for effective response and to "Build Back Better" in recovery, rehabilitation and reconstruction** (p. 13).

**Guiding governance principle**: States hold primary responsibility, with shared responsibilities across sectors/stakeholders and all-of-society engagement **— "States have the primary responsibility to prevent and reduce disaster risk, including through cooperation. Disaster risk reduction requires an all-of-society engagement and partnership. It also requires empowerment and inclusive, accessible and non-discriminatory participation, paying special attention to people disproportionately affected by disasters, especially the poorest"** (p. 12).

**Build Back Better**: **"The recovery, rehabilitation and reconstruction phase is a critical opportunity to build back better, including through integrating disaster risk reduction into development measures, making nations and communities resilient to disasters"** (p. 21). **This concept operationalizes the window of opportunity post-disaster to reduce future risk rather than recreating pre-disaster vulnerabilities** (pp. 20-22).

**All-of-society engagement**: **"Disaster risk reduction practices need to be multi-hazard and multisectoral, inclusive and accessible in order to be efficient and effective. While recognizing their leading, regulatory and coordination role, Governments should engage with relevant stakeholders, including women, children and youth, persons with disabilities, poor people, migrants, indigenous peoples, volunteers, the community of practitioners and older persons in the design and implementation of policies, plans and standards"** (p. 12).

**Resilience**: **Defined as "The ability of a system, community or society exposed to hazards to resist, absorb, accommodate, adapt to, transform and recover from the effects of a hazard in a timely and efficient manner, including through the preservation and restoration of its essential basic structures and functions through risk management"** (p. 10).

## Tools or methods extracted

**Global target framework (7 targets)**: Standardized outcome-tracking structure for DRR progress and accountability **— each target was to be measured through indicators developed by an open-ended intergovernmental expert working group by 2016** (p. 11).

**Risk information system approach**: Promote collection, analysis, management, dissemination, and open access to non-sensitive risk/loss data **— "Promote and improve dialogue and cooperation among scientific and technological communities, other relevant stakeholders and policymakers in order to facilitate a science-policy interface for effective decision-making in disaster risk management"** (pp. 13-15).

**Baseline and periodic assessment method**: Establish baselines and repeatedly assess vulnerability, capacity, exposure, hazard characteristics, and sequential effects **— "To systematically evaluate, record, share and publicly account for disaster losses and understand the economic, social, health, education, environmental and cultural heritage impacts, as appropriate, in the context of event-specific hazard-exposure and vulnerability information"** (p. 13).

**Location-based risk mapping**: Develop/update/disseminate risk maps and location-specific risk information for decision-makers and at-risk communities **— "Develop, periodically update and disseminate, as appropriate, location-based disaster risk information, including risk maps, to decision makers, the general public and communities at risk of exposure to disaster in an appropriate format by using, as applicable, geospatial information technology"** (p. 14).

**Governance implementation architecture**: National/local DRR strategies with targets, indicators, time frames, plus coordination platforms/focal points **— "Adopt and implement national and local disaster risk reduction strategies and plans, across different timescales with targets, indicators and time frames, aimed at preventing the creation of risk, the reduction of existing risk and the strengthening of economic, social, health and environmental resilience"** (pp. 16-17).

**Build Back Better recovery method**: Integrate DRR into recovery, rehabilitation and reconstruction planning before disasters and throughout post-disaster action **— "Prepare or review and periodically update disaster preparedness and contingency policies, plans and programmes with the involvement of the relevant institutions, considering climate change scenarios and their impact on disaster risk, and facilitating, as appropriate, the participation of all sectors and relevant stakeholders"** (pp. 20-22).

**Multi-hazard early warning systems**: **"Promote the development of people-centred multi-hazard, multisectoral forecasting and early warning systems, disaster risk and emergency communications mechanisms, social technologies and hazard-monitoring telecommunications systems"** (p. 21).

**Participatory risk assessment**: **"Empower local authorities and local communities to reduce disaster risk, including through resources, incentives and decision-making responsibilities, as appropriate"** (p. 17).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Context and diagnosis**: Requires multi-dimensional risk understanding (vulnerability, capacity, exposure, hazards, environment) before intervention design **— Priority 1 emphasizes that "Policies and practices for disaster risk management should be based on an understanding of disaster risk in all its dimensions of vulnerability, capacity, exposure of persons and assets, hazard characteristics and the environment"** (p. 13).

**Strategy and planning**: Requires national/local DRR plans across timescales with targets, indicators and timelines **— "Adopt and implement national and local disaster risk reduction strategies and plans... across different timescales with targets, indicators and time frames"** (p. 16).

**Investment/design**: Prioritizes structural and non-structural investments in resilience, especially critical facilities/infrastructure **— Priority 3 calls for "Public and private investment in disaster risk prevention and reduction through structural and non-structural measures... essential to enhance the economic, social, health and cultural resilience of persons, communities, countries and their assets, as well as the environment"** (pp. 17-19).

**Preparedness and response**: Requires contingency plans, early warning systems, exercises, evacuation readiness, and continuity planning **— Priority 4 emphasizes that "The steady growth of disaster risk, including the increase of people and assets exposure, combined with the lessons learned from past disasters, indicates the need to further strengthen disaster preparedness for response, take action in anticipation of events, and ensure capacities are in place for effective response and recovery at all levels"** (pp. 20-21).

**Recovery and reconstruction**: Requires DRR-integrated recovery and Build Back Better measures linked to long-term development **— "Integrate disaster risk reduction measures into post-disaster recovery and rehabilitation processes, facilitate the link between relief, rehabilitation and development, use opportunities during the recovery phase to develop capacities that reduce disaster risk in the short, medium and long term, including through the development of measures such as land-use planning, structural standards improvement and the sharing of expertise, knowledge, post-disaster reviews and lessons learned"** (pp. 20-22).

## Field data collection implications

Collect disaggregated risk/loss data (including sex, age, disability dimensions where relevant) and ensure accessibility of non-sensitive information **— "Enhance the development and dissemination of science-based methodologies and tools to record and share disaster losses and relevant disaggregated data and statistics, as well as to strengthen disaster risk modelling, assessment, mapping, monitoring and multi-hazard early warning systems"** (pp. 12, 14).

Maintain periodic baseline-driven assessments of risk, exposure, vulnerability, capacity, and hazard interactions at relevant social/spatial scales **— "Assess the technical, financial and administrative disaster risk management capacity to deal with the identified risks at the local and national levels"** (p. 13).

Record and publicly account for disaster losses and multi-domain impacts (economic, social, health, education, environmental, cultural heritage) **— "To systematically evaluate, record, share and publicly account for disaster losses and understand the economic, social, health, education, environmental and cultural heritage impacts"** (p. 14).

Use geospatial information and location-based risk mapping as standard inputs to planning and communication **— "Develop, periodically update and disseminate, as appropriate, location-based disaster risk information, including risk maps, to decision makers, the general public and communities at risk of exposure to disaster in an appropriate format by using, as applicable, geospatial information technology"** (pp. 14-15).

Strengthen real-time data access, measurement tools, and ICT-enabled dissemination mechanisms **— "Promote real time access to reliable data, make use of space and in situ information, including geographic information systems (GIS), and use information and communications technology innovations to enhance measurement tools and the collection, analysis and dissemination of data"** (p. 14).

## Coordination implications

DRR governance requires coordination within and across sectors and full engagement of executive/legislative institutions plus public/private stakeholders **— "Promote coherence across systems, sectors and organizations related to sustainable development and to disaster risk reduction in their policies, plans, programmes and processes"** (p. 12).

National and local coordination forums/platforms with clear roles/authority are explicitly recommended **— "Enhance collaboration among people at the local level to disseminate disaster risk information through the involvement of community-based organizations and non-governmental organizations"** (pp. 16-17).

Regional and transboundary cooperation mechanisms are emphasized for shared and cross-border risks **— "Promote transboundary cooperation to enable policy and planning for the implementation of ecosystem-based approaches with regard to shared resources, such as within river basins and along coastlines, to build resilience and reduce disaster risk, including epidemic and displacement risk"** (pp. 17, 24).

Global and regional platforms are framed as mechanisms for partnership, implementation review, and policy/investment learning **— "The Global Platform for Disaster Risk Reduction and the regional platforms for disaster risk reduction are mechanisms for coherence, the review of progress on implementation, and the sharing of practice and knowledge among stakeholders"** (p. 17).

**Multi-stakeholder partnerships**: **"Encourage the establishment of necessary mechanisms and incentives to ensure high levels of compliance with the existing safety-enhancing provisions of sectoral laws and regulations, including those addressing land use and urban planning, building codes, environmental and resource management and health and safety standards, and update them, where needed, to ensure an adequate focus on disaster risk management"** (p. 16).

## DRR implications

The document is a core global DRR normative framework for 2015-2030, centered on reducing risk creation and existing risk while strengthening resilience (pp. 11, 13). **It represents the international community's commitment to disaster risk reduction as a development and climate adaptation priority, replacing the HFA 2005-2015**.

It shifts emphasis toward disaster risk management and risk governance, not only post-disaster management **— "There has to be a broader and a more people-centred preventive approach to disaster risk. Disaster risk reduction practices need to be multi-hazard and multisectoral, inclusive and accessible in order to be efficient and effective"** (pp. 4, 16).

It broadens DRR to include natural and human-made hazards and links DRR coherence with sustainable development, climate, health, environment, and poverty agendas **— "Enhanced work to reduce exposure and vulnerability, thus preventing the creation of new disaster risks, and accountability for disaster risk creation are needed at all levels. More dedicated action needs to be focused on tackling underlying disaster risk drivers, such as the consequences of poverty and inequality, climate change and variability, unplanned and rapid urbanization, poor land management and compounding factors such as demographic change, weak institutional arrangements, non-risk-informed policies, lack of regulation and incentives for private disaster risk reduction investment, complex supply chains, limited availability of technology, unsustainable uses of natural resources, declining ecosystems, pandemics and epidemics"** (pp. 10, 12).

It embeds preparedness, response, and recovery as DRR functions and formalizes Build Back Better as a critical post-disaster opportunity **— Priority 4 integrates the full disaster cycle from preparedness through Build Back Better recovery** (pp. 20-22).

**Urban DRR emphasis**: **While not urban-specific, the framework explicitly addresses "unplanned and rapid urbanization" as an underlying risk driver and calls for risk-informed land use and urban planning throughout** (pp. 10, 14, 16, 18-19).

## Operational implications

Agencies and governments should align programmes to the four priority areas and relevant target metrics **— implementation should demonstrate contribution to one or more of the seven global targets through national/local targets and indicators** (pp. 11, 13).

DRR implementation should include legal/policy integration, budgeted institutional capacity, and compliance mechanisms **— "Mainstream and integrate disaster risk reduction within and across all sectors and review and promote the coherence and further development, as appropriate, of national and local frameworks of laws, regulations and public policies, which, by defining roles and responsibilities, guide, encourage and incentivize the public and private sectors to take action and address disaster risk"** (pp. 16-18).

Investment decisions should prioritize resilience of critical infrastructure, health systems, and livelihoods, with risk transfer/financial protection where appropriate **— "Promote the resilience of new and existing critical infrastructure, including water, transportation and telecommunications infrastructure, educational facilities, hospitals and other health facilities, to ensure that they remain safe, effective and operational during and after disasters in order to provide live-saving and essential services"** (pp. 18-20).

Preparedness operations should include people-centered multi-hazard warning systems, regular drills, and area-based support systems for response/displacement **— "Promote the development of people-centred multi-hazard, multisectoral forecasting and early warning systems, disaster risk and emergency communications mechanisms, social technologies and hazard-monitoring telecommunications systems"** (pp. 20-21).

Recovery planning should be pre-arranged and integrated with land-use, standards improvement, and long-term development of affected areas **— "Prepare or review and periodically update disaster preparedness and contingency policies, plans and programmes... Promote regular disaster preparedness, response and recovery exercises, including evacuation drills, training and the establishment of area-based support systems"** (pp. 21-22).

## Limitations / cautions

The framework is a high-level normative policy instrument and does not provide detailed sector-by-sector implementation manuals (inferred from structure and action-list format, pp. 13-26).

Global targets were to be complemented by later indicator development work, so measurement detail is not fully defined in this text **— "Targets (a) to (g) will be pursued with the target of reducing disaster risk and losses and will contribute to international development goals and the post-2015 development agenda. Their measurement will be guided by the Technical Guidance on Comprehensive Risk Assessment and Planning in the Context of Climate Change, which will be regularly updated, and their review will be supported by the open-ended intergovernmental expert working group on indicators and terminology relating to disaster risk reduction established by the General Assembly"** (pp. 11, 26).

Implementation is explicitly conditioned by national circumstances, legal systems, capacities and available international support **— "The implementation of this Framework, at all levels, is important. In this regard, each State has the primary responsibility to prevent and reduce disaster risk, including through international, regional, subregional, transboundary and bilateral cooperation. The extent to which developing countries are able effectively to implement this Framework will depend on the support provided"** (pp. 12-13, 23-25).

The PDF references www.unisdr.org (the former UNDRR web identity, p. 37); **UNISDR was renamed UNDRR (United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction) in 2019**. The authoritative download URL is now resolved in frontmatter via undrr.org.

**Implementation progress varies**: As of 2026 (11 years into the 15-year framework), users should cross-reference with Sendai Framework Monitor data, midterm review findings, and updated UNDRR guidance on indicator measurement.

## Known tensions

**State responsibility vs. all-of-society engagement**: The framework assigns primary responsibility to States while also calling for all-of-society engagement and multi-stakeholder partnerships — balancing government leadership with inclusive participation creates operational tensions in practice.

**Global targets vs. national circumstances**: The seven global targets are universal, but the framework acknowledges that implementation depends on national circumstances, capacities, and support — reconciling standardized measurement with context-specific realities is challenging.

**Prevention vs. response**: The framework emphasizes preventing new risk and reducing existing risk (proactive DRR) but also includes preparedness and response (reactive disaster management) — resource allocation between prevention and response remains contested.

**Development integration vs. humanitarian urgency**: Integrating DRR into development planning requires long-term perspectives, but disasters create urgent humanitarian needs — balancing Build Back Better aspirations with immediate recovery pressures is difficult.

**Data requirements vs. capacity constraints**: The framework calls for comprehensive risk data, disaggregated loss recording, and geospatial information systems, but many at-risk countries lack technical and financial capacity for these systems.
