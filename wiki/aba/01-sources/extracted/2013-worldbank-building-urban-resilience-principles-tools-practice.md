# Summary
This handbook provides a practical framework for integrating disaster and climate resilience into urban governance and infrastructure investment decisions (pp. 6, 9). It is structured into three parts: principles of urban resilience, tools for building resilience, and sectoral practice guidance (water/sanitation, energy/communications, and transport) (p. 6).

The core argument is that rapid urbanization, concentration of people/assets in hazard-prone areas, ecosystem degradation, and uncertainty from climate/development dynamics are increasing urban risk and losses (pp. 9-10). The handbook promotes a risk-based, evidence-driven approach that combines near-term no-regret actions with longer-term planning under uncertainty (pp. 10, 13).

It emphasizes that resilience goes beyond narrow mitigation: cities should plan for residual risk, maintain critical functions during shocks, and recover quickly through robust system design, redundancy, preparedness, and institutional coordination (pp. 10-11). It also argues that social resilience and inclusive governance are as important as physical infrastructure resilience (pp. 11, 24).

Operationally, the handbook highlights decision support through risk information systems, GIS/open data, risk assessment and CBA integration, land use planning, ecosystem management, emergency planning, and risk financing/transfer options (pp. 11-13, 29-30).

# Key concepts
- Urban resilience: Capacity of systems/communities to resist, absorb, adapt, and recover from hazards efficiently (p. 9).
- Risk under uncertainty: Cities must assume unknown/uncertain futures and avoid over-reliance on single “optimal” engineering designs (p. 10).
- Residual risk management: Even with mitigation, risks remain and require preparedness, warning, evacuation, response, and recovery systems (pp. 10-11).
- Redundancy and graceful failure: Critical systems should be robust enough to continue core functions and fail safely during extreme shocks (p. 11).
- Risk calculation framework: Risk derives from hazard, exposure, and vulnerability interactions (p. 30).
- Multi-pillar DRM: Identification/assessment, prevention/mitigation, financing/insurance, preparedness, and resilient recovery/reconstruction (p. 27).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Risk information systems and open-data cycle: build and share credible hazard/risk data across institutions and stakeholders (p. 29).
- Risk assessment + cost-benefit integration: embed risk analysis into public investment decision-making and planning cycles (p. 13).
- Risk-based land use planning: steer development and infrastructure location toward safer zones and reduce episodic/everyday risk (pp. 11, 30).
- Ecosystem management for resilience: integrate ecosystem services and green/blue approaches into urban risk reduction strategies (pp. 10-11).
- Risk reduction strategy mix: combine locational, structural, operational, and fiscal measures based on context (p. 30).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Strategic planning: supports long-horizon planning (20-30+ years) while accounting for uncertain hazard trajectories (p. 10).
- Project appraisal/design: promotes quantitative risk-informed appraisal and robust option selection for infrastructure investments (pp. 10, 13).
- Implementation: stresses cross-sectoral coordination and institutional arrangements for risk mainstreaming (pp. 11, 13).
- Preparedness/response: embeds warning, contingency, evacuation, and continuity planning as core components (pp. 10-11).
- Recovery/reconstruction: advocates rebuilding approaches that reduce future risk rather than restoring pre-disaster vulnerability (p. 27).

# Field data collection implications
- Invest in sustainable risk data systems with interoperable formats and institutional sharing protocols (pp. 13, 29).
- Use indicators/visualization tools to compare risk-reduction options and support prioritization (pp. 12-13).
- Combine geospatial layers (hazards, assets, vulnerabilities) to identify exposure hotspots and critical-system dependencies (pp. 11-12, 29-30).
- Ensure risk information is accessible to public and non-government stakeholders to strengthen collective resilience decision-making (p. 29).

# Coordination implications
- Resilience requires coordination across sectors, jurisdictions, and levels of government plus community/private actors (pp. 7, 11).
- Public authorities hold core accountability for risk reduction but need enabling participation and social accountability mechanisms (pp. 11, 26).
- Institutional frameworks should clearly define roles/responsibilities for DRM and climate risk management (pp. 26-27).
- Cross-sector communication and shared information platforms are critical to avoid fragmented planning and cascading failures (pp. 25, 29).

# DRR implications
- The handbook strongly aligns with DRM principles and Hyogo-based multi-pillar risk management (p. 27).
- It shifts DRR from event-response toward continuous risk-informed urban development and investment governance (pp. 6, 13).
- It explicitly links DRR with climate adaptation, infrastructure planning, and social resilience as integrated rather than separate agendas (pp. 9-11).

# Operational implications
- Prioritize no-regret and flexible measures where uncertainty is high and lock-in risk is significant (pp. 10, 13).
- Build critical infrastructure for continuity and rapid recovery, not only for average-case efficiency (p. 11).
- Use mixed intervention portfolios instead of single-measure solutions to reduce vulnerability across domains (pp. 11, 30).
- Strengthen local capacity to interpret and use risk data in day-to-day planning and budget decisions (p. 13).
