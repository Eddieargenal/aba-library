## Summary

This guidance note introduces a practical urban context analysis toolkit for humanitarian practitioners working in urban crises, especially displacement settings (pp. 1, 3). Developed by IIED under the 'Urban Crises Learning Fund' managed by IIED and funded by the UK Department for International Development (DFID), the toolkit was tested in multiple urban crisis contexts (pp. 1, 3). It is positioned as a user-friendly, relatively quick, and adaptable mechanism to improve understanding of complex urban dynamics before programme design and implementation (pp. 3, 8-9).

The toolkit is designed to help actors analyze political/governance, social/cultural, economic, service/infrastructure, and spatial/settlement factors that influence crisis response effectiveness (pp. 8, 17-19). "The toolkit will enable users to identify relevant stakeholders, existing power relations, resource distribution, governance and legal frameworks, sources of livelihoods, social networks, and access to services that will help responders to determine suitable entry points and improve the effectiveness and responsiveness of their programmes" (p. 3). It emphasizes that context analysis is distinct from a needs assessment and should complement, not replace, sector or vulnerability assessments — "Needs assessment tools tell us what the current situation is, i.e. the visible effects of the problem... Context analysis seeks to understand why the situation is the way it is, focusing on structural and systemic issues" (pp. 8-9).

Methodologically, the guide provides a 10-step process across three phases: preparation, data collection, and analysis/documentation, supported by ten practical tools (workplan, desk review summary, stakeholder analysis, data collection plan, KII/FGD guides, debrief, key findings, programme implications, validation workshop, report template) (pp. 10-11, 24, 34, 38-39). The ten tools are: Tool 1 - Workplan; Tool 2 - Desk review summary; Tool 3 - Stakeholder analysis; Tool 4 - Data collection plan; Tools 5A-5G - Interview and focus group guides; Tool 6 - Debrief template; Tool 7 - Key findings; Tool 8 - Programme implications; Tool 9 - Validation workshop; Tool 10 - Final report template (pp. 10-11, 34).

The note is explicit about scope: it is not a prescriptive programme design manual and does not provide complete urban-specific tools/training packages; rather, it offers an approach to structure analysis and identify entry points, risks, and potential partnerships — "This is not a step-by-step programme design manual. It provides a structured approach to understanding urban context, but programme design requires additional sector-specific assessment and technical planning" (pp. 9, 23-25).

## Key concepts

**Urban context analysis**: A way to unpack political, economic, social, service-delivery and spatial dynamics that can enable or hinder response effectiveness — "Urban context analysis examines the systems, structures, actors, and relationships that shape how crises unfold and how responses are received in urban settings. It focuses on 'why' questions — why are certain groups more vulnerable? why do services function or fail? why do some actors have more influence than others?" (p. 8).

**Complementary role to needs assessment**: Context analysis focuses on "why" and systems dynamics, while needs assessment focuses on "what" needs and gaps — "Context analysis is not a substitute for needs or vulnerability assessments. It complements them by explaining the underlying systems, power dynamics, and structural factors that produce the needs and vulnerabilities identified in assessments" (pp. 8-9).

**System-thinking thematic model**: Analysis organized around politics/governance, social/cultural, economic, service/infrastructure, and space/settlements, with gender and Do No Harm as cross-cutting themes (pp. 17-19). Figure 3 presents a typology of urban systems adapted from Campbell (2016 ALNAP) comprising: (1) Politics and governance — formal/informal institutions, power relations, decision-making, accountability; (2) Social and cultural — identity, networks, cohesion/tension, norms, displacement dynamics; (3) Economic — livelihoods, markets, inequality, financial systems; (4) Infrastructure and services — WASH, energy, transport, health, education, communications; (5) Space and settlements — land use, housing, public space, hazards, planning. Gender and Do No Harm considerations cut across all five (pp. 17-19).

**Stakeholder-centered approach**: Explicit focus on actor interests, influence, incentives, and relationships for realistic engagement and coordination — "Stakeholder analysis goes beyond listing who is present to analyzing: what interests and incentives drive each actor? what power and influence do they hold? what relationships exist between stakeholders — cooperation, competition, conflict? This depth of analysis enables realistic partnership strategies" (pp. 8, 29, 31).

**Iterative analysis process**: Steps are interlinked and should be revisited as findings evolve — "The 10-step process is presented sequentially for clarity, but in practice analysis is iterative. Desk review findings shape stakeholder mapping; stakeholder interviews reveal new desk review gaps; early data collection findings require adjusting interview guides and sampling. Teams should expect to loop back and refine earlier steps" (pp. 10-11).

**Entry point identification**: "Context analysis aims to identify 'entry points' — opportunities where external actors can engage productively with existing systems, actors, and processes to support crisis response. Entry points consider what's possible given power dynamics, institutional capacity, and stakeholder interests, not just what's technically ideal" (pp. 29-31).

## Tools or methods extracted

**10-step process across 3 phases**: Structured workflow from launch/scoping to communication of findings (pp. 10-11, 34). Phase 1 - Preparation (Steps 1-4): (1) Develop workplan and timeline; (2) Conduct desk review; (3) Conduct stakeholder analysis; (4) Develop data collection plan. Phase 2 - Data Collection (Steps 5-6): (5) Conduct key informant interviews and focus group discussions; (6) Daily debriefs and mid-collection synthesis. Phase 3 - Analysis and Documentation (Steps 7-10): (7) Identify key findings; (8) Analyze programme implications; (9) Validation workshop; (10) Finalize and communicate report (pp. 10-11, 34).

**Tool 3 stakeholder analysis**: Map actors, interests, incentives, and influence to inform partnerships and coordination strategy — "Stakeholder analysis matrix should capture: Who are the key actors (government, civil society, private sector, community, donors, international)? What are their mandates, interests, and incentives? What power/influence do they hold? What capacity/constraints do they face? What relationships exist between them? How accessible are they to external engagement?" (pp. 20, 29, 31).

**Tool 4 data collection plan + Tools 5A-5G**: Practical planning and interview/focus-group instruments for city-level and sub-area data collection — Tool 4 provides a matrix for planning: who to interview, what topics to cover, sampling approach, logistics. Tools 5A-5G provide interview and focus group guides for: 5A - City-level governance/coordination actors; 5B - City-level service providers; 5C - Sub-area governance actors; 5D - Sub-area service providers; 5E - Community leaders/organizations; 5F - Affected populations (mixed groups); 5G - Women-specific focus groups (pp. 24-27).

**Tool 6 debrief template**: Daily/mid-collection synthesis mechanism to adjust sampling and probe emerging hypotheses — "Daily debriefs (15-30 minutes at end of each data collection day) allow teams to: compare findings across interviewers; identify emerging patterns or contradictions; flag data gaps requiring additional interviews; adjust sampling or interview guides; and maintain data quality and consistency" (pp. 27, 29).

**Tool 7 key findings + Tool 8 programme implications**: Convert qualitative findings into programmatic entry points, risks, and partnership options — "Tool 7 structures synthesis of findings across the five thematic areas plus gender and Do No Harm. Tool 8 translates findings into: What are the entry points for effective programming? What risks should be mitigated? What partnerships should be pursued or avoided? What coordination opportunities exist? What advocacy priorities emerge?" (pp. 29-31).

**Tool 9 validation workshop + Tool 10 final report outline**: Challenge/refine findings and document internal analysis outputs — "Validation workshop (half-day to full-day) invites key stakeholders to review preliminary findings, challenge interpretations, add nuance, and build shared understanding. Tool 10 provides report outline covering: executive summary, methodology, context overview, findings by theme, programme implications, annexes (stakeholder list, interview guides, bibliography)" (pp. 31-33).

**Desk review guidance (Tool 2)**: "Desk review should synthesize: crisis background and timeline; demographic data; hazard/risk profiles; governance structures and key actors; economic/livelihood patterns; service delivery systems; relevant policies/legislation; previous assessments and programme documentation. Tool 2 provides template for capturing key information and identifying gaps for primary data collection" (pp. 20-21).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Pre-design stage**: Intended for use at the start of programme cycles, before or alongside needs/vulnerability assessments — "The toolkit is most effective when applied at the beginning of programme planning, before major design decisions are made. It can inform: which geographic areas to prioritize; which sectors to focus on; which partnerships to pursue; what risks to mitigate; and what approaches are contextually appropriate" (p. 9).

**Assessment design stage**: Helps frame key questions, prioritize sub-areas, and plan mixed KII/FGD data collection — "Context analysis findings guide subsequent detailed assessments by identifying: which sub-areas warrant deeper assessment; which population groups require specific attention; which sectors are most critical; which governance/service actors should participate in assessments; and what local sensitivities must be navigated" (pp. 15-17, 24-27).

**Analysis and strategy stage**: Supports synthesis into key findings, stakeholder implications, and risk mitigation options for programming — "The toolkit's final outputs (key findings, programme implications, validation workshop) support strategic decision-making: what approaches are feasible given power dynamics? what partnerships will strengthen effectiveness? what risks require mitigation? how should coordination be structured?" (pp. 29-32).

**Communication/adaptation stage**: Includes internal/external sharing guidance and recommends periodic updates in dynamic crisis contexts — "Context analysis findings should inform: internal programme design and proposal development; coordination with other actors (where sharing is appropriate and safe); donor communication and reporting; and ongoing programme adaptation. In rapidly changing urban crises, context analysis may need updating every 6-9 months" (pp. 33-34).

## Field data collection implications

Primary data should combine KIIs and FGDs with targeted city-level and sub-area sampling, informed by desk review gaps — "Data collection typically combines: 20-40 key informant interviews with governance, service, civil society, and coordination actors at city and sub-area levels; 6-12 focus group discussions with affected populations (including women-specific groups); and observation through site visits. Sampling should fill gaps identified in desk review and stakeholder analysis" (pp. 20-21, 24-27).

Sampling should deliberately include diversity and representation (including women and marginalized groups) and be adjusted iteratively — "Sampling must account for urban diversity: different geographic areas (central vs. peripheral, formal vs. informal settlements); different population groups (host community, displaced, returnees, different ethnic/religious groups); different socio-economic strata; and deliberate inclusion of women and marginalized groups. Initial sampling plan should be adjusted based on emerging findings" (pp. 25-27).

Data collection should apply protection-sensitive, informed-consent, and secure data handling practices — "Humanitarian data collection standards apply: informed consent; confidentiality and anonymity; do no harm (assess risks before interviewing vulnerable groups); secure data storage; and awareness that context analysis can touch sensitive political and governance issues requiring discretion" (pp. 27-28).

Mid-data-collection reflection is recommended to refine methods and fill gaps before final analysis — "Daily debriefs and a mid-collection team synthesis session (after ~50% of interviews completed) allow teams to: assess whether saturation is occurring or new information is still emerging; identify unexpected patterns requiring deeper probing; flag data gaps requiring additional sampling; and adjust interview guides to follow emerging themes" (p. 27).

**Time investment**: "The toolkit assumes roughly 2 weeks of in-country data collection (Phase 2) preceded by 1-2 weeks of desk review and preparation (Phase 1) and followed by 1-2 weeks of analysis and documentation (Phase 3). Total elapsed time typically 4-6 weeks, though this can be compressed or extended based on context complexity and resource availability" (pp. 9, 34).

## Coordination implications

Stakeholder mapping and actor relationship analysis are central for identifying realistic coordination and partnership opportunities — "Understanding who does what, who has influence, what incentives drive actors, and what relationships exist between them is foundational for effective coordination. Stakeholder analysis reveals: potential partners (aligned interests, complementary capacities); coordination entry points (existing platforms, influential convenors); risks to avoid (actors working at cross-purposes, competitive dynamics); and advocacy targets" (pp. 8, 29, 31).

The process encourages early engagement of local partners/government/peer actors to improve ownership and response complementarity — "Validation workshop (Tool 9) deliberately engages stakeholders in reviewing preliminary findings. This: builds relationships with key actors; improves analysis quality through local knowledge; strengthens local ownership of findings; and creates foundation for future coordination and partnership" (pp. 23-25, 33).

Sharing findings externally (where feasible and safe) is recommended to support wider adaptation among urban actors — "Where politically and operationally safe, sharing context analysis findings with coordination platforms, peer agencies, and municipal authorities can: improve collective understanding; reduce duplication of analysis efforts; strengthen coordination coherence; and build evidence base for advocacy. Sensitivity assessment should precede external sharing" (p. 33).

**Multi-agency application**: "Context analysis can be conducted by a single agency for internal use, or jointly by multiple agencies to build shared understanding and strengthen coordination. Joint analysis requires more time for coordination but produces collective buy-in and shared strategic vision" (pp. 9, 31).

## DRR implications

The toolkit is designed for urban crises and displacement contexts where systemic risk drivers and weak service systems can worsen humanitarian outcomes — "Urban crises are shaped by underlying risk drivers: rapid urbanization, informal settlement growth, inadequate infrastructure, weak governance, social inequality, environmental hazards. Context analysis surfaces these systemic factors, enabling programmes to address root causes not just symptoms" (pp. 7-8).

It supports risk-informed programming by identifying structural/contextual factors and entry points, including governance/service dynamics — "Understanding governance capacity, service delivery systems, land/tenure dynamics, and hazard exposure patterns enables risk-informed programming: where to locate interventions to reduce hazard exposure; how to strengthen local governance for long-term resilience; which service systems to support; what land/HLP constraints to navigate" (pp. 8-9, 29-31).

Cross-cutting Do No Harm and gender considerations aim to reduce risk of exacerbating tensions or exclusions — "Do No Harm lens prompts analysis of: What tensions exist between population groups? How might interventions exacerbate or reduce these tensions? What power imbalances could programmes reinforce or challenge? Gender lens examines: How do men and women experience crisis differently? Who has voice in decision-making? How do gender norms shape vulnerability and resilience?" (pp. 17-19).

**Urban-specific risk factors**: The toolkit's thematic framework surfaces urban-specific DRR concerns: infrastructure interdependencies (cascading failures); informal settlements (concentrated hazard exposure and vulnerability); weak governance (limited enforcement of building codes, land use regulations); and economic volatility (livelihood shocks in cash-dependent economies) (pp. 17-19, 29-31).

## Operational implications

Organizations should allocate skilled staff (programme/assessment experience) and resources to complete context analysis effectively — "Context analysis requires: at least one team member with urban programme experience and qualitative research skills; budget for desk review, travel, data collection, and analysis time; and organizational commitment to use findings in programme design. Under-resourced or rushed context analysis produces limited value" (p. 9).

The process is designed for adaptability; teams should tailor scope, questions, and sampling to context and available time/resources — "The toolkit provides a full 10-step process, but teams should adapt: geographic scope (citywide vs. specific districts/neighborhoods); thematic focus (all five systems vs. priority themes); data collection intensity (20 vs. 40 interviews); and timeline (compressed rapid assessment vs. in-depth analysis). Adaptation should be deliberate and documented" (pp. 9, 15-17, 24-25).

Findings have a limited shelf life in crisis settings; reassessment is recommended if major shifts occur or if analysis is older than ~6-9 months — "Urban crises are dynamic — populations move, political situations shift, services collapse or recover, new actors arrive. Context analysis findings degrade over time. Major shocks (conflict escalation, natural disasters, policy changes) or passage of 6-9 months typically warrant updating the analysis" (p. 33).

**Internal capacity building**: "Applying the toolkit builds organizational capacity for urban programming: staff develop urban systems thinking; organizations build institutional knowledge of specific urban contexts; and agencies strengthen qualitative analysis skills. Repeated application across contexts builds transferable urban expertise" (pp. 8-9).

**Resource realism**: "The toolkit is designed to be 'relatively quick' compared to full political economy analysis or ethnographic research, but still requires significant investment — typically 4-6 weeks elapsed time and dedicated skilled staff. Organizations should assess whether they have resources to complete the process meaningfully before starting" (pp. 9, 34).

## Limitations / cautions

The toolkit does not provide comprehensive step-by-step programme design guidance — "Context analysis identifies entry points, risks, and partnership opportunities, but translating these insights into detailed programme design requires additional sector-specific technical planning, detailed needs assessment, and operational planning" (p. 25).

It does not itself provide full urban-specific training/tool packages; adaptation and complementary tools are expected — "The toolkit provides guidance on process and framing, plus generic interview guides, but users must adapt these to specific contexts and may need to supplement with urban-specific technical tools (urban WASH assessment, informal settlement mapping, HLP analysis, etc.)" (pp. 23, 25).

It is a qualitative process by default; quantitative components can be added only if time/resources permit — "The toolkit emphasizes qualitative key informant interviews and focus groups. Quantitative data (household surveys, spatial data analysis) can complement context analysis but are not core to the method. Organizations with capacity can integrate quantitative components, but this extends timeline and resource requirements" (p. 9).

The guide's own timing assumptions (eg roughly two weeks in-country collection baseline) may not fit all contexts — "The 2-week in-country data collection estimate assumes: reasonable security access; availability of key informants; experienced data collection team; and moderate urban complexity. High-insecurity contexts, very large cities, or highly fragmented governance may require more time" (p. 9).

**Relationship to other IIED urban tools**: This toolkit was developed alongside the Urban Response Analysis Framework (URAF) — context analysis toolkit focuses on 'why' and systems understanding; URAF focuses on 'what' response options to pursue. The two are complementary (pp. 3, 7).

**2017 publication**: The toolkit pre-dates more recent urban humanitarian guidance (IASC 2018 ABC, UNHCR/IRC 2019 ABA compendium, Sphere 2018 urban integration) — users should integrate with current frameworks.

## Known tensions

**Analysis depth vs. operational speed**: The toolkit aims to be "relatively quick" but still requires 4-6 weeks total elapsed time — this may be too slow for rapid-onset emergencies or too quick for deep understanding in complex contexts.

**Qualitative rigor vs. quantitative demand**: The toolkit is primarily qualitative (KIIs, FGDs), but donors and coordination platforms often demand quantitative data for decision-making — balancing qualitative depth with quantitative evidence expectations is challenging.

**Internal learning vs. external sharing**: Context analysis often surfaces sensitive political dynamics, governance failures, and power imbalances — sharing findings externally can strengthen coordination but risks political backlash or access restrictions.

**Comprehensive coverage vs. resource constraints**: The toolkit recommends analyzing five urban system domains with diverse stakeholder sampling across city and sub-area levels — comprehensiveness competes with limited staff time and budget.

**Stakeholder validation vs. critical analysis**: Validation workshops (Tool 9) engage stakeholders in reviewing findings, building ownership but potentially diluting critical findings that challenge powerful actors' interests or narratives.

**Urban systems complexity vs. programmatic focus**: Context analysis reveals interconnected urban systems and structural issues, but humanitarian programmes typically have narrow sectoral mandates and short timelines — analysis may surface problems the organization cannot address.
