<a id="summary"></a>
## Summary

This guidance note introduces a practical urban context analysis toolkit for humanitarian practitioners working in urban crises, especially displacement settings. Developed by the International Rescue Committee (IRC) Stronger Cities Initiative and published by IIED under the Urban Crises Learning Fund (funded by DFID), the toolkit was piloted in five urban crisis contexts: Maiduguri (Nigeria), Juba (South Sudan), Amman (Jordan), Dar es Salaam (Tanzania), and Bangkok (Thailand).

The toolkit provides a user-friendly, relatively quick, and adaptable mechanism for understanding complex urban dynamics before programme design and implementation. It is structured as a 3-phase, 10-step process supported by 10 practical instruments (workplan, desk review summary, stakeholder analysis matrix, data collection plan, 7 KII/FGD question guides, debrief template, key findings analysis, programme implications template, validation workshop tool, and final report outline). The toolkit enables users to identify relevant stakeholders, existing power relations, resource distribution, governance and legal frameworks, sources of livelihoods, social networks, and access to services — information that helps determine suitable entry points and improve the effectiveness and responsiveness of humanitarian programmes.

A critical scope boundary is explicitly stated: context analysis is not a substitute for needs or vulnerability assessments. Context analysis answers "why" questions (structural and systemic factors shaping crisis dynamics), while needs assessment answers "what" questions (visible effects and service gaps). Both are required. The document also notes the toolkit is primarily designed for organisations already established in-country; teams needing rapid response context analysis are directed to alternative tools (GECARR, IRC conflict sensitivity toolkit).

The analysis is organized around five interconnected thematic areas — politics and governance, social and cultural, economic, service delivery and infrastructure, and space and settlements — with Do No Harm and gender equality as mandatory cross-cutting lenses. A systems-thinking approach examines interconnections between thematic areas rather than analysing each in isolation. The toolkit has been tested and refined through field implementation, though the evidence base remains practitioner-generated rather than peer-reviewed, giving it mixed composite authority.

<a id="key-concepts"></a>
## Key Concepts

### Urban Context Analysis Thematic Framework
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-FRAMEWORK-001`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A systems-thinking framework organising urban context analysis around five interconnected thematic areas: (1) politics and governance (stakeholder power, governance structure, policy/legal frameworks); (2) social and cultural (social cohesion, identities, protection risks); (3) economic (labour market, livelihoods, constraints); (4) service delivery and infrastructure (access, supply, coordination); and (5) space and settlements (urban plans, environmental risks, housing/land, public space). Do No Harm and gender equality serve as cross-cutting themes integrated throughout all five areas. The framework is applied across all phases: framing questions (Step 3), desk review (Tool 2), question guides (Tools 5A–5G), debrief synthesis (Tool 6), and final findings analysis (Tool 7). Based on Campbell (2016) ALNAP typology of urban systems; explicitly systems-thinking in design — aims to understand both individual thematic areas and their inter-relations.

*Limitations:* Qualitative only; designed primarily for displacement crises; assumes sufficient time and access to explore all five themes; may require selective focus under resource constraints.

---

### Urban Displacement Crisis Typology
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TYPOLOGY-001`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A four-category classification used to frame the scope and focus of the context analysis, differentiating both the origin and location of crisis:

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| I | Conflict that causes internal displacement TO a city (IDPs) |
| II | Conflict that causes refugee displacement TO a city |
| III | Violent conflict WITHIN a city (in-situ urban population affected) |
| IV | Naturally triggered hazards WITHIN a city or displacement TO a city |

Applied in Step 2 to determine which populations of interest to prioritise, which sub-areas to select, and which thematic questions are most salient. Types are not mutually exclusive; multiple types may co-occur in the same city.

---

### Urban Context Analysis
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-CONCEPT-001`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A structured inquiry into the political, economic, social, service delivery, and spatial factors that enable or hinder effective crisis responses in urban settings. Answers "why" questions — why things are the way they are, how they are connected, what influences decision-makers — rather than "what" needs and gaps exist. Enables identification of entry points, stakeholder interests, power dynamics, governance structures, and systemic drivers of vulnerability. Distinct from needs assessment (which identifies gaps), vulnerability assessment (household-level), and macro-level political economy analysis (which is narrower in scope).

*Limitations:* Primarily designed for urban displacement crises; qualitative in nature; requires existing country presence; findings become outdated in dynamic contexts (see RULE-002 on 6–9 month reassessment).

---

### Sub-Area as Unit of Analysis
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-CONCEPT-002`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A geographically-bounded community within a city used as the primary spatial unit for primary data collection. May be defined by formally designated administrative boundaries (municipality or ward) or by physical landscape features (roads, rivers) combined with social analysis considerations, citing Parker and Maynard (2015). Approximately one to two days of data collection per sub-area is typical. The toolkit operates at two scales: city-wide level (governance, systems, stakeholders) and sub-area level (community dynamics, local service access).

*Limitations:* Sub-area boundaries are socially constructed and may not match administrative boundaries; urban heterogeneity may mean even a single sub-area contains multiple distinct communities.

---

### Context Analysis vs Needs Assessment Distinction
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-CONCEPT-003`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A fundamental distinction between two complementary but non-substitutable analytical approaches. Context analysis focuses on "why" questions — systemic and structural factors, power dynamics, institutional capacities, and actor incentives. Needs assessment focuses on "what" questions — visible effects, current service gaps, and quantifiable needs. The source states explicitly: "A context analysis is not focused on needs and, therefore, must be understood as quite distinct from a needs assessment." Both are required; context analysis must complement, not replace, needs or vulnerability assessments.

---

### Entry Point Identification
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-CONCEPT-004`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

The identification of practical and realistic opportunities where external humanitarian actors can engage productively with existing urban systems, actors, and processes to support crisis response. Entry points are grounded in analysis of power relations, stakeholder interests, institutional capacities, and governance structures — they represent what is contextually feasible, not only technically ideal. Positioned as the terminal output purpose of the entire context analysis process. Documented in Tool 8 (Programme implications). Urban entry points are more constrained than rural: more actors, more institutional layers, limited humanitarian leverage.

---

### Do No Harm
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-PRINCIPLE-001`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual | cross-cutting principle

Programmes must not increase tensions or undermine existing local systems — including existing service providers and local government support structures. Integrated as a cross-cutting analytical theme throughout all 10 steps and all 5 thematic areas. Operationalised through: Do No Harm guiding questions in Table 3; protection-sensitive data collection protocols (RULE-005, IMPL-004); programme implications analysis (Tool 8); and partner selection risk assessment (RULE-003). See also decision rule RULE-004 for operational application.

---

### Gender Equality Analytical Lens
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-PRINCIPLE-002`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual | cross-cutting principle

Every step of the context analysis must systematically examine gender disparities — differences between women and men in assigned responsibilities, activities, resource access, service access, and decision-making opportunities. Implemented through: gender-specific guiding questions (Table 3); gender-separated FGDs with same-sex facilitators (RULE-001); gender comparison column in Tool 7 findings analysis; and women-only working groups in validation workshops when contextually needed (RULE-011). Note: if gender equality is the primary focus, a dedicated in-depth gender analysis tool is recommended instead (RULE-009).

## Methods and Tools

### Methods

#### Urban Context Analysis Process (3-Phase, 10-Step Method)
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-001`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

A 3-phase, 10-step iterative workflow for conducting urban humanitarian context analysis, designed to be adaptable to varied urban crisis settings. Total elapsed time: typically 4–6 weeks (1–2 weeks preparation, 2 weeks data collection, 1–2 weeks analysis/documentation).

| Phase | Steps |
|---|---|
| Phase 1 — Preparation | Step 1: Launch (workplan, budget, team); Step 2: Frame analysis (scope, sub-areas, crisis type); Step 3: Select initial questions (adapt thematic framework) |
| Phase 2 — Data Collection | Step 4: Collate secondary data (desk review, stakeholder mapping); Step 5: Prepare primary data collection (plan, adapt guides, train team); Step 6: Carry out primary data collection (KIIs, FGDs, daily debriefs, mid-collection review) |

The process is iterative — desk review shapes stakeholder mapping; early data collection findings refine question guides and sampling; mid-collection review adjusts focus; validation workshop challenges preliminary findings before final documentation.

#### Desk Review Method
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-002`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Secondary data synthesis conducted 2–3 weeks before primary data collection to map existing knowledge, identify gaps, and conceptualise efficient fieldwork. Synthesizes crisis background, demographic data, governance structures, service delivery systems, policies, and prior assessments. Duration: 2 days to 1 week. Led by team lead; in-country staff involvement required. Outputs: completed desk review (Tool 2), initial stakeholder analysis (partial Tool 3), revised thematic guiding questions, input to data collection plan (Tool 4).

*field_query_trigger: How do I conduct a desk review of secondary data to map existing knowledge and identify gaps before primary data collection in an urban humanitarian context analysis?*

#### Key Informant Interview (KII) Method
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-003`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Semi-structured interviews with influential stakeholders at city-wide and sub-area levels, informed by prior stakeholder analysis. Sampling uses purposive selection from Tool 3 expanded via snowball/referral (METHOD-008). Uses adapted Tools 5C–5G. Requires: team lead oversight of notetaking quality and probing; daily debriefs and mid-collection review; deliberate inclusion of "difficult stakeholders." KII notes must exclude identifiable information.

*field_query_trigger: How do I plan and conduct key informant interviews with government, civil society, and community actors to map power dynamics and service delivery in an urban displacement context?*

#### Focus Group Discussion (FGD) Method
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-004`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Structured group discussions with displaced and host community populations in selected urban sub-areas, separated by gender and age with same-sex facilitators (RULE-001). Uses adapted Tools 5A–5B. Requires: informed consent; protection risk briefing for all team members including translators; deliberate inclusion of marginalized groups; daily debrief and mid-collection gap review. FGD notes must exclude identifiable information.

*field_query_trigger: How do I facilitate focus group discussions with displaced and host community populations in urban sub-areas while managing protection risks and ensuring gender-disaggregated data?*

---

#### Stakeholder Analysis / Actor Mapping
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-005`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Iterative identification and analysis of influential stakeholders — type, level, role, interests, incentives, influence, and relationships. Updated progressively from desk review (Step 4, initial mapping) through data collection to final analysis (Step 7, full update). Explicit requirement to include "difficult stakeholders" — armed groups, gatekeepers, corrupt officials. Outputs: stakeholder analysis matrix (Tool 3), KII candidate list, coordination/partnership strategy inputs.

*field_query_trigger: How do I map and analyze key stakeholders to understand actor interests, influence, and relationships for programming and coordination in an urban humanitarian response?*

#### Participatory Validation Workshop
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-006`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

One- to two-facilitator workshop with 15–20 diverse participants (analysis team, consulted stakeholders, internal staff, external local partners) to challenge and refine preliminary findings before final documentation. Inputs: preliminary drafts of Tools 7, 8, and 3. Step is optional under time or resource constraints. Requires: gender diversity of participants; women-only working groups if contextually needed (RULE-011); structured facilitation (Tool 9); confidentiality of data sources maintained.

*field_query_trigger: How do I run a validation workshop to challenge and refine urban context analysis findings with diverse stakeholders before finalizing the report?*

#### Systems-Thinking Analytical Approach
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-007`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Analytical approach that examines interconnections and relations between urban thematic areas rather than analysing each theme in isolation, based on Campbell (2016) ALNAP typology of urban systems. Requires covering all 5 themes; Do No Harm and gender equality applied cross-cuttingly, not separately; team debriefs specifically structured to surface cross-thematic patterns. Outputs: cross-thematic analytical findings, system-level entry points, structural risk drivers.

*field_query_trigger: How do I apply systems-thinking analysis to understand how political, economic, social, and spatial factors interact to shape urban crisis response effectiveness?*

#### Snowball / Referral Sampling
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-008`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

During KIIs and FGDs, continuously solicit referrals from participants for additional informants, particularly to reach marginalized, displaced, or inaccessible groups. Key mechanism for reaching actors invisible in formal stakeholder analyses. Monitor for over-representation of particular networks; monitor daily for representation gaps in vulnerable groups.

<a id="known-tensions"></a>
## Known Tensions

### TNS-001: Assumption of representative sampling vs. structural bias risk

**Tension type:** Methodological

**First position (ASM-002):** The toolkit assumes that adequate diversity of participants — including women, marginalized groups, ethnic minorities, and displaced populations — can be achieved through deliberate sampling design and careful team composition. Guidance across Steps 5–6 and the validation workshop treats representation as an achievable standard if proper steps are followed.

**Conflicting position (RISK-001):** The toolkit simultaneously acknowledges that relying on key powerholders to organize participation is a structural bias risk that deliberate design may not fully overcome. Teams operating under time pressure default to accessible participant channels (community leaders, gatekeepers), which systematically exclude marginalized voices.

**Why it matters:** If the assumption holds, the toolkit's guidance on deliberate sampling constitutes sufficient mitigation and practitioners can follow it with confidence. If RISK-001 dominates, the toolkit overstates its ability to produce representative data in access-constrained urban settings, and practitioners may need to supplement with additional bias-mitigation methods not described in this document. The source does not resolve this tension — it is an honest design limitation.

**Related objects:** ASM-002, RISK-001, METHOD-004, SAFE-002

---

### Internal Contradiction: Primary use case vs. rapid-response adaptability (low confidence)

The document states the toolkit "is generally designed with the assumption of approximately two weeks in country of data collection" and assumes prior country presence (p. 9), while simultaneously noting in footnote 3 that it can be adapted for rapid-response by referencing alternative tools (GECARR, IRC conflict sensitivity toolkit). This creates mild tension between the explicit primary use case (established in-country organisations conducting structured analysis) and the implied adaptability of the approach. RULE-009 partially addresses this by pointing practitioners to alternative tools when the toolkit's full structure is not appropriate.

<a id="decision-logic"></a>
## Decision Logic

All decision rules extracted from this source. Rules are grouped thematically.

---

### Partner and Team Composition

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-003`** — Restrict or exclude new local partners when significant conflict or protection dynamics are present

- **Condition:** When considering including new local or international partners in context analysis execution, and a significant conflict or protection dynamic is active.
- **Action:** Assess three factors before inclusion: (1) protection concerns for partner staff in data collection; (2) risks of bias from partner that cannot be mitigated through team composition; (3) risks to participants from public sharing of findings. Exercise caution — do not include by default.
- **Exception:** Partners with established conflict-sensitivity protocols, strong data protection practices, and pre-existing trusted community relationships may be included with documented safeguards.
- **Cross-references:** IMPL-003 (joint analysis design), RISK-003 (partner bias risk), TOOL-003
- **⚠ human_review_required:** The source does not define criteria for what constitutes a "significant" conflict or protection dynamic — requires contextual judgment.

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-013`** — Recruit facilitators and interviewers from the same social groups as target participants

- **Condition:** When composing the data collection team for any analysis involving specific displaced or minority population groups.
- **Action:** Recruit interviewers and facilitators from the same country, city, or social group as participants (same refugee community, same ethnic minority, same gender). Use counterparts trained in question nuances rather than generic translators. Where same-group recruitment is not possible, document the gap and take mitigating steps.
- **Rationale:** Source states this as "essential" — strongest normative language used for any team composition guidance. Ties to LESSON-002 (Dar es Salaam identity masking case).
- **Cross-references:** IMPL-001 (team composition), METHOD-003 (KII), METHOD-004 (FGD), RULE-001, SAFE-002

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-010`** — Conduct participatory planning workshop when in partnership or when staff have limited local knowledge

- **Condition:** When analysis is conducted in formal partnership with another organisation, OR when team staff have limited knowledge of local communities in target areas.
- **Action:** Conduct an optional participatory data collection planning workshop (half-day to one-day) with locally-knowledgeable stakeholders before fieldwork begins. Use Optional Tool: City overview workshop.
- **Exception:** Not required when the team has strong existing local knowledge and is not in formal partnership.
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-004, METHOD-006, RULE-003

---

### Data Collection Design

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-001`** — Separate FGDs by gender and age with same-sex facilitators

- **Condition:** When planning FGDs in urban humanitarian context analysis, particularly when adolescents are included or gender/protection sensitivities are present.
- **Action:** Plan separate FGDs for men and women / adolescent boys and girls; assign same-sex facilitators to each group.
- **Rationale:** "Adolescents in particular may be less likely to speak in a mixed-gender group" (p. 25); essential for capturing women's and girls' experience of barriers to service access.
- **Cross-references:** METHOD-004, TOOL-005, PRINCIPLE-002, RULE-011 (extends to validation workshop)

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-007`** — Complete desk review 2–3 weeks before primary data collection

- **Condition:** When planning the timeline for urban context analysis, prior to initiating KIIs and FGDs.
- **Action:** Complete desk review 2–3 weeks before primary data collection begins; use the window to revise the data collection plan (Step 5) based on gaps identified.
- **Exception:** In rapid response contexts, timeline may be compressed, but desk review must still precede fieldwork.
- **Cross-references:** METHOD-002, TOOL-002, TOOL-004

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-008`** — Adapt question guides to each context — never use default templates without adaptation

- **Condition:** When preparing KII and FGD question guides for any new urban context analysis.
- **Action:** (1) Eliminate irrelevant questions or those already answered by desk review; (2) add context-specific questions; (3) validate with country staff; (4) brief the team on protection risks around sensitive questions (GBV, child exploitation). Note: questions on legal protection are less relevant for IDP contexts than refugee contexts.
- **Rationale:** "Just as no context is the same, no context analysis will be the same" (p. 25) — un-adapted guides risk collecting irrelevant data and missing critical local dynamics.
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-005, FRAMEWORK-001, IMPL-007

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-009`** — Use a dedicated gender analysis tool when gender equality is the sole analytical interest

- **Condition:** When the organisation's analytical interest is limited to gender equality as the primary or sole focus.
- **Action:** Conduct an in-depth gender analysis (e.g. IRC Gender Analysis and Program Design Toolkit) instead of this toolkit. The toolkit's gender lens is cross-cutting, not a substitute for dedicated gender analysis.
- **Exception:** When gender is one of multiple analytical interests — in that case, the toolkit's cross-cutting gender theme is appropriate.
- **Cross-references:** PRINCIPLE-002, FRAMEWORK-001

---

### Protection and Data Security

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-004`** — Apply Do No Harm throughout all data collection and analysis activities

- **Condition:** Applies at all stages — questionnaire design, sampling, data collection, analysis, and findings communication.
- **Action:** Apply Do No Harm lens at each stage: tailor questionnaires to avoid exacerbating tensions; apply geographic and social sensitivity in sampling; ensure findings communication does not expose participants to harm; assess how programmes might inadvertently increase tensions or undermine local systems.
- **Cross-references:** PRINCIPLE-001, FRAMEWORK-001, TOOL-005, RULE-005, RULE-012

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-005`** — Protect participant data — anonymize, limit access, destroy raw notes after analysis

- **Condition:** After primary data collection is complete and debrief notes have been consolidated.
- **Action:** Store interview notes securely with access limited to few persons; notes must not include identifiable information (names, specific locations); destroy raw notes after analysis completes unless retention is specifically planned and resourced (with full transcription and anonymization).
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-006, TOOL-005, SAFE-001, RISK-002

---

### Analysis and Validation

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-006`** — Triangulate findings from multiple sources before concluding analysis

- **Condition:** During Step 7 data analysis and interpretation.
- **Action:** Triangulate information across multiple sources, methods (desk review + KII + FGD), and team perspectives; ensure team members and key stakeholders have opportunity to challenge findings before conclusions are drawn.
- **Cross-references:** METHOD-006, TOOL-007, IMPL-005

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-011`** — Designate women-only working groups in validation workshop when women face obstacles to mixed participation

- **Condition:** When women face obstacles to participating in mixed group discussions during the validation workshop.
- **Action:** Designate women-only working groups with documented products; ensure facilitators include these contributions in synthesis; use targeted invitations rather than blanket organisational invitations to ensure gender diversity.
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-009, METHOD-006, RULE-001, PRINCIPLE-002

---

### Communication and Reporting

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-012`** — Produce internal-only report when findings contain sensitive or confidential information

- **Condition:** When context analysis findings contain sensitive contextual, political, or protection information, or commercially confidential content; assessed before beginning the write-up process.
- **Action:** Produce internal-only report (Tool 10 assumes this by default); clarify before writing whether an external summary is also needed; when sharing externally, anonymize all sources and respect informed consent commitments.
- **Exception:** If analysis did not involve sensitive dynamics, a non-sensitive external brief may be produced alongside the internal report (Maiduguri case lesson, LESSON-003).
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-010, IMPL-006, RULE-005, LESSON-003

---

### Context Analysis Currency

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-002`** — Reassess context analysis after 6–9 months or significant context change

- **Condition:** When any of these triggers apply: (a) findings are more than 6–9 months old before use for programming; (b) significant crisis change occurred (policy change, major incident, resolution); (c) new data collection is planned; (d) new proposal requires deeper context information.
- **Action:** Revise or redo the context analysis; partial update is acceptable when changes are limited to specific themes or sub-themes.
- **Limitations:** The 6–9 month threshold is a practitioner heuristic, not an evidence-based figure; highly volatile contexts may require more frequent updates.
- **Cross-references:** METHOD-001, TOOL-010, RISK-004
