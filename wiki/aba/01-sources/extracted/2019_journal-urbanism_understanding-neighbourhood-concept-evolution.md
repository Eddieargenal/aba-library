# Summary
This article reviews the evolution of the neighbourhood concept and argues that it remains highly relevant in both policy and research, while also being fluid and contested with no single agreed definition (p. 1).

Using a literature review approach, the author synthesizes neighbourhood scholarship across planning, sociology/anthropology and urban policy, and frames neighbourhood as variably construed as place, community, and policy unit (pp. 1-2, 4-5).

The review traces conceptual roots to planning (for example, Howard/Perry lineages) and Chicago School scholarship, then discusses why neighbourhood remains analytically useful for understanding social processes, policy experimentation and local-level planning interventions (pp. 2-3, 5-7).

# Key concepts
- Conceptual fluidity: neighbourhood is context-dependent, contested, and not reducible to a single universal definition (pp. 1-2).
- Neighbourhood as place: emphasizes locale, lived experience, social interaction and identity formation in everyday urban life (pp. 4-5).
- Neighbourhood as community: more formalized or bounded social unit, often used for governance/administrative purposes (pp. 4-5).
- Neighbourhood as policy unit: operational scale for decentralization, participation, resource targeting, and local interventions (pp. 5-6).
- Context-driven definition: the article argues definitions should reflect research/policy purpose and local realities (pp. 2, 7).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Narrative literature review method based on books, articles, and chapters to synthesize conceptual evolution and application (pp. 1-2).
- Typological framing of neighbourhood boundaries/constructs (place, community, policy unit) for analytical clarity (pp. 4-5).
- Boundary logic options cited in prior literature: physical landmarks/resources, social-interaction extents, and cognitive/perceptual mapping (p. 3).
- Use of neighbourhood as spatial sampling unit to reduce spatial autocorrelation issues in empirical analysis (p. 6).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Scoping and framing: helps define what “neighbourhood” means before designing area-based assessment or intervention logic (pp. 2, 7).
- Assessment design: supports selecting boundary logic and unit-of-analysis strategies according to the research/policy question (pp. 2-3, 6).
- Policy design: useful for structuring decentralised, participatory and localised interventions at sub-city scale (p. 5).
- Learning and adaptation: highlights context differences (especially global south) that require case-specific empirical designs rather than rigid transfer (p. 7).

# Field data collection implications
- The article highlights that neighbourhood boundaries are often subjective and contextually negotiated, affecting sampling and inference (pp. 3-5).
- For research validity, neighbourhood definition should be aligned to the specific issue under investigation (p. 2).
- In data-scarce contexts, case-study approaches are commonly used, but boundary specification often remains under-articulated (p. 7).
- Using well-defined neighbourhood units can improve independence in spatial sampling by reducing autocorrelation bias (p. 6).

# Coordination implications
- Neighbourhood-as-policy-unit framing supports decentralised governance and local participation in decision-making (p. 5).
- Policy-driven neighbourhood creation can reflect administrative priorities (or political agendas), which may conflict with local perceptions (p. 5).
- The review suggests negotiated and consented definitions among residents/planners/practitioners to improve social legitimacy and objectivity (p. 7).

# DRR implications
- The source is not a DRR manual; it provides conceptual guidance rather than hazard-specific methods.
- Indirectly, it supports DRR/urban risk work by improving sub-city unit definition for vulnerability, behaviour and service analysis (pp. 6-7).
- The conceptual triad (place/community/policy unit) maps to three core DRR functions: place-based framing supports hazard/exposure mapping; community-based framing supports social vulnerability and collective resilience analysis; policy-unit framing supports resource targeting and accountability in DRR plans.
- The review's emphasis on negotiated, socially validated boundary definitions reduces the risk of DRR resource misallocation from administrative and felt community boundary mismatches — a documented problem in urban risk targeting where formal zones diverge from local perceptions of neighborhood.
- Insights on neighbourhood social processes (social capital, informal networks, trust, collective efficacy) from Chicago School scholarship are directly applicable to community preparedness and disaster recovery capacity assessments in urban settings.

# Operational implications
- Neighbourhood remains a useful practical scale for studying and intervening on immigration, crime, unemployment, housing and social cohesion dynamics (pp. 6-7).
- Policies and programmes are more likely to be effective when neighbourhood framing is context-sensitive and co-owned by relevant actors (pp. 2, 7).
- In global south contexts, definitional fluidity, data limitations and weak research infrastructure can constrain neighbourhood-based operationalization (p. 7).

# Claims worth citing
- The neighbourhood concept is relevant but contested, with no universally agreed definition (p. 1).
- Neighbourhood has evolved as both an academic concept and a practical policy/research unit (pp. 1-2).
- It can be meaningfully viewed as place, community, and policy unit, each with distinct analytical implications (pp. 4-5).
- Neighbourhoods are useful for understanding social processes and behaviour at micro-urban scales (pp. 6-7).
- Context-specific and negotiated definitions are recommended to improve objectivity and social acceptability (p. 7).

# Limitations / cautions
- The article is a conceptual review and does not provide an empirical protocol for delimiting neighbourhood boundaries in all contexts (pp. 1-2).
- It acknowledges major constraints in global south neighbourhood research, including data/method/infrastructure limitations (p. 7).
- Much seminal theorization is sourced from global north scholarship, which may limit direct transfer without contextual adaptation (p. 7).
