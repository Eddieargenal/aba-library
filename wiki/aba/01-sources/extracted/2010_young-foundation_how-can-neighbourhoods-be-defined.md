## Summary

The Young Foundation (2010) produced this policy and practice synthesis on neighbourhood definition and governance as part of the Transforming Neighbourhoods programme — a multi-stakeholder research and innovation consortium (2006–2008) that included UK government departments, local authorities, and civil society organisations. The document was first published in 2006 and revised in 2010. It is a review paper, not a field guide or evaluation study; many proposals are framed explicitly as "hypotheses" requiring testing.

The source's primary contribution is a three-lens framework for understanding neighbourhoods: administrative geography (wards, statistical zones), personal identifications (lived experience, mental maps), and the local public realm (shared spaces, services, civic nodes). It argues that no single definition is adequate and that neighbourhood units must be defined through a combination of lenses. It also introduces a dual-scale typology — proximity neighbourhoods (100–1,500 people, social coordination scale) and strategic neighbourhoods (5,000–10,000 people, service infrastructure scale) — and proposes that neighbourhood empowerment should be understood ecologically, not as single-sector interventions.

Primary context is urban UK, with explicit focus on fragmented, diverse urban settings. Urban applicability requires moderate adaptation: informal settlement dynamics, weak local government, and non-Western civic infrastructure are not addressed. Composite authority is mixed — the Young Foundation has medium institutional credibility, but the evidence quality is low (synthesis without primary empirical research; proposals untested).

## Findings

**Group 1: Neighbourhood definition and conceptual foundations**

**F-001** — There is no consensus definition of "neighbourhood"; it can range from a street of 100 people to a ward of 10,000, making neighbourhood the central design problem for any area-based approach. → see S-youngfoundation2010-CONCEPT-001
*Routing rationale:* Core definitional problem for ABA; routes to neighbourhood-concept page as an enrichment.

**F-002** — A working definition of neighbourhood for governance purposes should combine three lenses: administrative geography, personal identifications, and the local public realm — treating these as complementary rather than competing. → see S-youngfoundation2010-FRAMEWORK-001
*Routing rationale:* This is the source's primary analytical contribution; routes to a new framework page. Human review required: this is a foundational framework that could conflict with or supersede related wiki concept pages — agent-09 should verify before creating.

**F-003** — The local public realm (public spaces + public services/civic centres + third spaces + local public sphere) constitutes the frame for lived neighbourhood experience and can serve as both a diagnostic and a prescriptive tool for neighbourhood action. → see S-youngfoundation2010-CONCEPT-002
*Routing rationale:* High-value concept with no existing wiki page; routes to create-concept on local-public-realm.

**F-004** — Neighbourhoods operate at two complementary scales with different functions: proximity scale (100–1,500 people, social coordination, mutual aid) and strategic scale (5,000–10,000 people, service infrastructure). Both scales should be used in parallel. → see S-youngfoundation2010-TYPOLOGY-002
*Routing rationale:* Directly relevant to ABA geographic design; routes to create neighbourhood-scales concept page.

**F-005** — Administrative geography and personal identifications represent two dominant but divergent approaches to neighbourhood definition, each with structural strengths and weaknesses; neither alone is sufficient. → see S-youngfoundation2010-TYPOLOGY-001
*Routing rationale:* Enriches existing neighbourhood-boundaries concept page.

**F-006** — The neighbourhood attributes spectrum (Galster 2001, Power et al 2004) provides a multi-dimensional diagnostic framework covering environmental, proximity, buildings, infrastructure, demography, services, social-interactive, sentiment, and political dimensions. → see S-youngfoundation2010-FRAMEWORK-002
*Routing rationale:* Useful diagnostic framework for neighbourhood assessment; routes to create-framework.

**Group 2: Methods and tools**

**F-007** — Combining top-down administrative boundary data with bottom-up resident mental maps and service-anchor data produces a more complete picture of the lived neighbourhood and helps identify "neighbourhood gaps." → see S-youngfoundation2010-METHOD-001
*Routing rationale:* Enriches existing neighbourhood boundary definition tool page.

**F-008** — Mapping the four components of the local public realm (public spaces, service/civic centres, third spaces, civic media) provides a structured diagnostic for neighbourhood anchors and service gaps, grounding neighbourhood definition in observable infrastructure. → see S-youngfoundation2010-TOOL-001
*Routing rationale:* New tool page warranted; strong operational relevance for neighbourhood diagnosis.

**Group 3: Governance and operational concepts**

**F-009** — Neighbourhood outcomes emerge from interacting factors (leadership, engagement, services, investment, safety, public realm), not single interventions — requiring integrated rather than siloed programme design. → see S-youngfoundation2010-CONCEPT-006
*Routing rationale:* Core ecological empowerment concept; consistent with ABA logic; routes to create new concept page.

**F-010** — Neighbourliness (friendliness, helpfulness, respect for privacy; deprivation does not create a "bad" neighbourhood where social cohesion prevails) is a valid proxy indicator for social cohesion and collective action capacity in neighbourhood interventions. → see S-youngfoundation2010-MEASURE-001
*Routing rationale:* Creates a social-cohesion indicator concept page; also routes to field instruments section.

**Group 4: Decision rules**

**F-011** — Decision rule: choose proximity scale (100–1,500 people) for coordination and mutual aid functions; choose strategic scale (5,000–10,000) for service infrastructure functions; both scales can operate in parallel. → see S-youngfoundation2010-RULE-001
*Routing rationale:* New decision-protocol page warranted for neighbourhood scale selection.
