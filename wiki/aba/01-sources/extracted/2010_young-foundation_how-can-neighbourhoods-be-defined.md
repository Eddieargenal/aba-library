## Summary

This paper is a policy and practice review on neighbourhoods, how they are defined, and how neighbourhood-level governance and empowerment can be designed more effectively (p. 2). It was originally published in 2006 and updated in 2010 under the Young Foundation's Transforming Neighbourhoods programme **— a research and innovation consortium on neighbourhood governance and empowerment that ran from 2006 to 2008, bringing together government departments, community and research organisations including the Department for Communities and Local Government, the Home Office, the Joseph Rowntree Foundation, CABE, The Community Alliance and 15 local authorities** (p. 2).

A central argument is that there is no single, settled definition of neighbourhood. The document contrasts administrative-geographic definitions (e.g., wards, statistical zones) with socially experienced neighbourhoods (mental maps, social ties, lived interaction), and suggests that each captures different aspects of local reality (pp. 9-12). **The paper explicitly states: "There is no consensus answer to the question, 'What is a neighbourhood?'"** (p. 9).

The paper proposes the "local public realm" (public spaces, public services, and civic interaction points) as a bridging lens between top-down boundaries and bottom-up lived experience (pp. 13-14, 17). **The local public realm encompasses four elements: local public spaces (streets, parks, squares), local public services and civic centres (schools, health centres, libraries), third spaces (cafés, pubs, shops), and the local public sphere (civic media, newsletters, noticeboards, virtual spaces)** (pp. 17-21). It also emphasizes that neighbourhood scales are layered and overlapping rather than fixed and mutually exclusive (pp. 15-17). **The document proposes a dual-scale framework: "proximity neighbourhoods" (100-1500 people, where personal acquaintance is possible) and "strategic neighbourhoods" (5000-10,000 people, large enough to support primary schools, GP surgeries, parks, and other service infrastructure)** (pp. 15-16).

Operationally, it reviews neighbourhood empowerment and governance approaches (e.g., neighbourhood management, participatory budgeting, devolved services, local forums/boards), drawing lessons on what improves engagement and public value and what risks require mitigation (pp. 33-41). **The document emphasizes that neighbourhood outcomes emerge from interacting factors (leadership, engagement, services, investment, safety, public realm), not from single interventions — termed "ecological empowerment"** (p. 35).

## Key concepts

**Neighbourhood as ultra-local community of place**: A practical but contested object for policy, with no consensus single definition **— "most people feel they intuitively understand what they mean, in the shape of neighbourly interactions, mutual support, gathering places... But rich descriptions of what neighbourhood can mean... is not a definition of what it is"** (p. 9).

**Administrative geography vs personal identifications**: Two dominant models for defining neighbourhoods, each with trade-offs for legitimacy, inclusiveness, and manageability (pp. 10-11). **Administrative geography (wards, Super Output Areas) offers simplicity, clear patches for ward councillors, and ensures no one is left out — but is less rooted in lived experience and varies wildly in size (300 to 300,000 people). Personal identifications (self-defined communities) grow from popular demand and local challenges and are likelier to be cohesive — but create complexity, competing claims, and risk leaving marginal citizens out** (p. 10).

**Social neighbourhood**: Neighbourhood produced through social proximity, networks, routines, and patterned local interaction **— "some analysts employ qualitative social models that use the level of interactions between residents to distinguish 'neighbourhoods', good or bad, from generic 'residential areas' where there are few or no patterned relations"** (p. 12). **Social homogeneity is used as an administrative proxy for these diverse and overlapping local networks, but is "clearly inadequate to the task"** (p. 12).

**Local public realm**: Ensemble of local spaces, services, and civic meeting points that anchor neighbourhood experience and action **— comprises local public spaces, local public services and civic centres, third spaces (cafés, pubs), and the local public sphere (civic media, newsletters)** (pp. 13-14, 17). **"Understanding the contribution the local public realm makes to neighbourhood may help us build a bridge between the lenses of administrative geography and personal identifications... mental maps of neighbourhood are often based around these common landmarks: public buildings, public spaces, public services and community dynamics"** (p. 14).

**Layered/overlapping neighbourhoods**: Residents participate in multiple neighbourhood scales and structures simultaneously **— "residents of a community may find themselves simultaneously located within a network of neighbourhoods of different scales and types... a housing estate, a policing beat, an electoral ward, a school catchment area, a local faith community... each have its own boundaries and dynamics"** (pp. 16-17). **The document asks: "Is there any reason why [these] should not each have its own boundaries and dynamics? Should they not be shaped by residents' organisations, beat meetings, school governors, ward councillors, religious and community leaders case by case?"** (p. 17).

**Ecological empowerment**: Neighbourhood outcomes emerge from interacting factors (leadership, engagement, services, investment, safety, public realm), not from single interventions **— this concept positions neighbourhood transformation as a systems problem requiring integrated action across multiple domains rather than single-sector solutions** (p. 35).

**Neighbourliness**: **Defined in the British context as "friendliness, helpfulness, and respect for privacy", "maintaining the tension between cooperation and privacy, helpfulness and non-interference, between friendliness and distance" — distinct from the American emphasis on visiting neighbours' homes** (p. 15). **"What gives an area its sense of community is the willingness of its members to engage in reciprocity or collective action... Neighbourliness is therefore a valuable indicator for measuring the success, failure or progress of neighbourhood initiatives"** (p. 15).

**Mental maps of neighbourhood**: **Personalised spatial understandings shaped by individual life paths and experiences — "we may not realise that a local early years centre exists until we become parents. We may not think about a neighbourhood watch area or police beat until we are mugged or burgled"** (p. 13). **A Future Foundation study found that "facilities and buildings (doctor's surgery, post office, primary school) are key features of people's mental model of neighbourhood"** (p. 14).

## Tools or methods extracted

**Dual-lens neighbourhood diagnosis**: Compare administrative boundaries with lived social maps to identify mismatches and actionable local units (pp. 10-11, 14). **This approach uses both "top-down (geodemographic or ONS) and bottom-up definitions [to] produce a more complete picture of lived neighbourhood... [and] help local authorities and other actors to map and address 'neighbourhood gaps'"** (p. 14).

**Local public realm mapping**: Identify neighborhood anchors (spaces, facilities, services, civic nodes) as a basis for design and prioritization (pp. 14, 17). **The ensemble of the local public realm includes: local public spaces (streets, parks, squares), local public services/civic centres (schools, health centres, libraries, community centres, leisure centres, faith organisations, transport stops), third spaces (cafés, pubs, shopping centres), and the local public sphere (community radio, civic websites, email lists, newsletters, noticeboards)** (pp. 17-21).

**Layered-scale analysis**: Use proximity and strategic scales together (small-scale social coordination + larger-scale service/governance planning) (p. 16). **"Proximity neighbourhoods" (streets or estate areas where neighbourliness is personal, people know one another as individuals — 500-1500 people based on anthropological evidence about coordination limits) and "strategic neighbourhoods" (5000-10,000 inhabitants, large enough to include a primary school, park, GP surgery, library or leisure centre, and shops/offices)** (pp. 15-16).

**Participatory budgeting model**: Neighbourhood assemblies set priorities and elect delegates into district/city decision structures **— "the participative budget of Porto Alegre in Brazil, rooted in neighbourhood, has been adopted in many countries"** (p. 33).

**Neighbourhood governance prototyping**: Test combinations of forums, panels, boards, elected/selected representatives, and participatory practices based on local context (pp. 37, 40). **"The diagram below [p. 40] illustrates how different circumstances and challenges may lead to different responses: one size will never fit all"** (p. 40).

**Super Output Areas (SOAs) recombination method**: **UK's 2004 Neighbourhood Statistics framework established "Super Output Areas" — "Lower layer" SOAs average 1500 population, "middle layer" SOAs average 7200 — built from census data and designed to be stable over time. "The relationship of SOAs to the lived experience of neighbourhoods is not direct. But the existence of this 'lower layer' establishes the possibility for recombination, so that, if it makes more sense locally, data can be collected for neighbourhoods on non-ward boundaries"** (p. 11).

**Neighbourliness indicators**: **Track "friendliness, helpfulness, and respect for privacy" as proxy for social cohesion and collective action capacity — "studies show that deprivation and poverty do not necessarily make a neighbourhood into a 'bad' one so long as social cohesion and feelings of 'neighbourliness' prevail"** (p. 15).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Scoping/problem framing**: Clarifies what "neighbourhood" means in a given context before governance or tool design (pp. 9-11). **"Understanding of the scales, characteristics and dynamics of neighbourhoods is necessary if we are to set a framework for targeted and effective action. Otherwise we run the risk of public disappointment and policy failure"** (p. 9).

**Baseline/context analysis**: Maps social dynamics, service anchors, and local public realm assets/gaps (pp. 12-14). **Requires collecting "both boundary data and lived-experience data (mental maps, service-use patterns, interaction networks)" to define practical neighbourhood units** (pp. 10-12, 14).

**Design**: Supports selection of fit-for-context governance models and engagement mechanisms rather than one-size-fits-all structures (pp. 36-40). **"Composition choices (elected reps, selected citizens, co-opted public figures, open forums) should be explicit and locally negotiated"** (pp. 37-38).

**Implementation**: Offers practical approaches for leadership development, street-level engagement, service devolution, and local investment mechanisms (pp. 24-33, 36). **Emphasizes that "neighbourhood empowerment [should be treated] as a portfolio of practices (engagement, investment, leadership, service redesign), not only institutional structure"** (pp. 35-37).

**Adaptation/review**: Highlights governance lifecycles, participation peaks/troughs, and need to revise structures over time (pp. 40-41). **"Plan for lifecycle management: governance structures may need to scale up, merge, pause, or sunset as issues evolve"** (pp. 40-41).

## Field data collection implications

Collect both boundary data and lived-experience data (mental maps, service-use patterns, interaction networks) to define practical neighbourhood units (pp. 10-12, 14). **A Future Foundation study "asked residents to describe their neighbourhood, circle it on a map of their area and then list the characteristics that made it their neighbourhood. The results showed that facilities and buildings (doctor's surgery, post office, primary school) are key features of people's mental model of neighbourhood"** (p. 14).

Include indicators of neighbourliness, cohesion, and local interaction quality as part of neighbourhood performance assessment **— "neighbourliness is therefore a valuable indicator for measuring the success, failure or progress of neighbourhood initiatives"** (p. 15).

Track neighbourhood-realm infrastructure and service nodes (schools, surgeries, public spaces, transport points, civic media) as key contextual variables (pp. 14, 17). **The document provides a "Counting neighbourhood structures" table showing infrastructure density: UK has 10,661 electoral wards (1 per 5,590 people), 10,683 GP practices (1 per 5,580), 18,069 primary schools (1 per 3,300), 44,648 churches/mosques/synagogues (1 per 1,310), ~60,000 public houses (1 per 990), ~4,500 libraries (1 per 13,240)** (p. 16).

Use locally accessible data products (e.g., neighbourhood-scale statistics) to support collective judgement and participatory planning **— "It is worth asking whether the neighbourhood statistics process could be accelerated, compiled together with other neighbourhood data and made available to the general public in accessible form to inform collective judgement"** (p. 11).

**Collect social network data to understand tenure and ethnicity divides**: **A recent UK estates study found that "64% knew more than five people of the same housing tenure, but only 17% knew more than five people of different tenure. This pattern is also frequently seen for ethnicity and other characteristics"** (p. 12).

## Coordination implications

Neighbourhood governance should connect service silos and institutions around local priorities, not just create new standalone committees **— "The mission of 'community leadership'... means focusing more on cross-cutting vision, joining up services and democratic accountability"** (pp. 35-37).

Effective models require constructive interface between neighbourhood bodies and higher-tier authorities (pp. 38-40). **"No general answer yet leaps out as to where governance should be service-specific and where a more holistic approach reaps dividends"** (p. 35).

Cross-neighbourhood coordination is needed for shared spaces/services and to avoid conflictual fragmentation **— "A key centre for a neighbourhood such as a local park or school may fall outside its nominal boundaries. In such cases, recourse to control by a higher body is not the only option. There are a number of other possibilities — basing neighbourhoods around centres rather than on borders, neighbourhood decisions to influence or support services outside their nominal boundaries, co-operation between several neighbourhoods, enabling non-residents to be involved in neighbourhood governance"** (p. 40).

Composition choices (elected reps, selected citizens, co-opted public figures, open forums) should be explicit and locally negotiated (pp. 37-38). **Different governance structures reviewed include: neighbourhood forums (Birmingham), urban parishes (Milton Keynes), participatory budgeting (Porto Alegre model), neighbourhood management initiatives (189 in UK), and tenant management organisations (250 in UK)** (pp. 33-41).

## DRR implications

The document is not DRR-specific; it provides governance and place-definition foundations that can support risk-informed local planning.

**The distinction between administrative and socially experienced neighbourhoods is directly applicable to ABA geographic targeting in DRR**: Hazard exposure and coping mechanisms do not align with administrative boundaries, and community-felt neighbourhood units may better reflect shared risk and collective response capacity. **The document notes: "In an inner city area where an Asian-majority council estate adjoins a white-majority estate and a row of derelict warehouses turned into luxury flats, should existing communal divides be reinforced or bridged?"** (p. 9) — a question equally relevant for DRR area selection where risk exposure may differ across adjacent communities.

**The "local public realm" concept** (shared spaces, services, civic nodes) **maps directly onto critical infrastructure and service continuity logic in urban DRR** — identifying these nodes helps prioritise resilience investment for maximum community reach and recovery speed within a neighbourhood. **The document states: "mental maps of neighbourhood are often based around these common landmarks: public buildings, public spaces, public services and community dynamics"** (p. 14) — suggesting that critical infrastructure mapping should align with socially recognized neighbourhood anchors.

**The ecological empowerment model** (p. 35) — that neighbourhood outcomes emerge from interacting factors (leadership, engagement, services, investment, safety, public realm), not from single interventions — **is consistent with multi-hazard, multi-dimensional DRR thinking and validates integrated rather than siloed programme design**. This directly supports the area-based approach logic that risk reduction requires simultaneous action across governance, physical infrastructure, social cohesion, and service delivery.

**Neighbourhood governance models reviewed** (participatory budgeting, devolved services, local forums) **can serve as organisational templates for community preparedness committees, early warning dissemination systems, and local DRR steering bodies**. **The Porto Alegre participatory budgeting model — "neighbourhood assemblies set priorities and elect delegates into district/city decision structures" — provides a tested mechanism for community-level DRR priority setting and resource allocation** (p. 33).

**The layered-scale analysis** (proximity neighbourhoods 100-1500 people + strategic neighbourhoods 5000-10,000 people) **aligns with DRR coordination needs**: early warning and mutual aid work at proximity scale where personal acquaintance exists, while risk assessments, evacuation planning, and service continuity require strategic-scale coordination with infrastructure and service providers.

**The social neighbourhood concept** — neighbourhood as interaction networks rather than administrative zones — **is critical for understanding how early warning information spreads, how mutual aid mobilizes during crises, and where social cohesion may enable or hinder collective preparedness**. **The document notes: "in some areas, individuals or communities living alongside one another lead practically separate existences. Their social networks are superimposed but seldom-connected, meeting largely through friction and conflict"** (p. 12) — a condition that undermines collective DRR capacity.

Direct hazard/DRR technical methodology is not specified in this source; supplement with DRR-specific frameworks (e.g., Twigg 2009 characteristics framework, Sendai Framework four priorities) for a complete operational approach.

## Operational implications

Avoid imposing one universal neighbourhood unit; select and validate units against local social and service realities (pp. 9-11). **"It would be inappropriate to impose a single meaning of neighbourhood, but our working definition for neighbourhood governance structures could combine geographic and social characteristics, be drawn together by the local public realm, and permit layering and overlapping where appropriate"** (p. 17).

Treat neighbourhood empowerment as a portfolio of practices (engagement, investment, leadership, service redesign), not only institutional structure (pp. 35-37). **The ecological empowerment model emphasizes that outcomes emerge from interacting factors, not single interventions** (p. 35).

Plan for lifecycle management: governance structures may need to scale up, merge, pause, or sunset as issues evolve (pp. 40-41). **The document notes that "participation peaks/troughs [require] need to revise structures over time"** (pp. 40-41).

Mitigate known risks early: capture by narrow interests, exclusion, weak accountability, service fragmentation, and double-taxation concerns **— these are identified as recurring problems in neighbourhood governance experiments** (p. 41).

**Start with orientation and facilitation**: **"A great deal more work now needs to be done to clarify what works, to design workable new models and to put them into practice. A series of experiments in community participation, often dictated centrally, have had mixed results"** (p. 5).

**Recognize UK context specificity**: **The UK "has become one of the most centralised of the OECD countries. Our lowest tier of executive government is at an average population of 118,500, when for international comparators the figures are below 10,000. We have far fewer elected representatives per head of population, and our citizens have less experience of participatory governance"** (p. 5) — this contextual note is important when adapting UK-derived models to other settings.

## Limitations / cautions

The paper is a synthesis/review and conceptual framework, not a full evaluation study with standardized comparative metrics. **It explicitly frames key proposals as "hypotheses" requiring testing rather than proven solutions** (pp. 8, 17).

Strong UK policy and institutional framing may require adaptation before direct use in other contexts. **The document notes the UK's exceptional centralization: "Our lowest tier of executive government is at an average population of 118,500, when for international comparators the figures are below 10,000"** (p. 5) — this means UK-derived governance models may not translate directly to more devolved systems.

Several proposals are framed as hypotheses/questions and require testing rather than immediate universal adoption (pp. 8, 35, 41). **The Issues Map (p. 8) lists 10 unanswered decision questions, including "What models... would work best, maximizing benefits and minimizing costs?" and "What is the best methodology for introducing and testing the new options?"**

**The document is focused on stable urban/suburban contexts** — transience and diversity are noted as challenges, but highly mobile populations or informal settlements may require different framing.

**Limited treatment of digital/virtual neighbourhoods**: The local public sphere section mentions "civic websites and email lists" (p. 20) but this is a 2010 update; social media and digital community platforms post-date the analysis.

**No cost-benefit analysis or fiscal sustainability discussion** — the paper addresses governance structures and empowerment practices but does not model the fiscal implications of devolved budgets or service provision at neighbourhood scale.

## Known tensions

**Administrative boundaries vs. lived experience**: **"Ward boundaries do not always reflect the dynamics of lived experience and common problems... [yet] pure self-identification of neighbourhoods could be complex, risky, overlapping, full of holes"** (pp. 9-10). The document proposes the local public realm as a bridging concept but does not resolve the fundamental tension — operational systems require boundaries, while social experience is fluid.

**Bonding vs. bridging capital**: **Self-defined neighbourhoods risk "more bonding than bridging capital, reinforcing social divides"** (p. 10). **The document asks: "In an inner city area where an Asian-majority council estate adjoins a white-majority estate... should existing communal divides be reinforced or bridged?"** (p. 9) — presenting the dilemma without prescribing a universal answer.

**Comprehensiveness vs. fragmentation**: **"Different mental models of neighbourhood are rooted in a diversity of lived experience. Is there any reason why a housing estate, a policing beat, an electoral ward, a school catchment area, a local faith community, should not each have its own boundaries and dynamics?"** (p. 17) — yet the document also notes that "there is value that can be unlocked through a holistic view of a neighbourhood" (p. 17). The tension between recognizing multiplicity and achieving coordination is acknowledged but not resolved.

**Empowerment vs. service quality**: **"Done well, the devolution of powers to the very local level can improve the quality of decision-making and levels of citizen engagement... Done badly, it can lead to conflict, growing inequalities and failing public services"** (p. 5). The document does not specify the conditions that determine which outcome occurs.

**Inclusion vs. efficacy**: **Open forums maximize participation but may lack decision-making power; elected representatives provide accountability but may not reflect diversity; selected citizens can bring expertise but lack democratic mandate** — composition trade-offs are mapped (p. 37) but the document states "no general answer yet leaps out" (p. 35).

**Scale trade-offs**: **Proximity neighbourhoods (100-1500 people) enable personal acquaintance and direct coordination, but are too small to support service infrastructure. Strategic neighbourhoods (5000-10,000 people) can sustain schools, health centres, and parks, but exceed the scale at which everyone knows each other** (pp. 15-16). The layered approach is proposed as a solution but requires dual governance structures, adding complexity.
