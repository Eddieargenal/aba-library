## Summary
This guidance note presents a structured framework for defining and assessing what a disaster-resilient community could look like. It is designed for government and civil society actors working on local DRR and CCA, and is explicitly positioned as a practical resource rather than a rigid manual (pp. 6-7). The second edition (Version 2, November 2009) builds on desk research, expert input, and field-testing feedback from multiple agencies **including Tearfund, Practical Action, Christian Aid, and ActionAid** (p. 7).

The core of the document is a comprehensive table-based framework organized around five Thematic Areas aligned to the Hyogo Framework for Action: Governance; Risk Assessment; Knowledge and Education; Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction; and Disaster Preparedness and Response (pp. 6, 9, 28, 31, 33, 36, 41). Within these areas, the note lays out 28 Components of Resilience and 167 Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community, plus corresponding Enabling Environment factors **at national and sub-national levels** (p. 11).

The note gives extensive operational guidance for adapting and applying the framework across project-cycle stages, including baseline studies, strategic planning, indicator design, review/evaluation, research, and advocacy (pp. 14, 22-26). It repeatedly emphasizes contextual adaptation: users should customize, select, and modify characteristics to fit local hazards, capacities, and institutional realities rather than attempting to apply all characteristics uniformly (pp. 7, 16, 18). **The document explicitly warns against treating the framework as a compliance checklist or attempting to address all 167 characteristics in a single intervention** (p. 7).

The document also highlights methodological cautions: resilience is an ideal rather than a fully attainable state; framework structures can over-separate interconnected issues; and indicator conversion requires deliberative, participatory judgment (pp. 7, 13, 57). A five-level milestone scale is proposed for tracking progress over time without forcing false precision (p. 20). **The framework acknowledges that "no community can be free of risk" and positions resilience as directional progress toward safer conditions, not absolute safety** (p. 7).

## Key concepts

**Disaster risk reduction (DRR)**: A systematic approach to identifying, assessing, and reducing disaster risk across development and relief work; should be integrated, not treated as an add-on **or separate project stream** (p. 8). **Defined as "the concept and practice of reducing disaster risks through systematic efforts to analyse and manage the causal factors of disasters"** (p. 8).

**Resilience**: Community/system capacity to anticipate and absorb shocks, maintain core functions during events, and recover after them **"in a timely and efficient manner, including through the preservation and restoration of its essential basic structures and functions"** (p. 8). **The document notes academic debate about whether resilience and vulnerability are opposite ends of a single spectrum or distinct concepts** (p. 8).

**Disaster-resilient community**: An idealized target state ("the safest possible community"), used as a directional benchmark rather than a literal endpoint (pp. 7-8). **Explicitly described as "a process not an end-state"** (p. 8).

**Community**: Not only a geographic unit; includes social differentiation, multiple identities, internal divisions, and external dependencies that affect resilience **— incorporating "people of different classes, castes, ethnicities, ages, abilities, genders"** (p. 9). **The document emphasizes that assuming homogeneity within communities can obscure differential vulnerabilities** (p. 9).

**Enabling Environment**: Wider institutional, policy, and socio-economic conditions that shape community-level resilience outcomes **— including "national disaster legislation, land tenure systems, decentralization policies, building codes, and education curricula"** (pp. 12-13). **Explicitly distinguished from community-level characteristics because communities often cannot directly change these factors** (p. 12).

**Characteristics framework**: A structured schema (Thematic Areas → Components → Characteristics) designed for usability but requiring users to preserve linkages across categories **— "interconnections exist and should not be lost"** (pp. 13-14). **The three-tier hierarchy enables both comprehensive assessment and selective focusing** (p. 11).

**Milestones**: A five-level progression model (from **Level 1: "little or no knowledge or motivation regarding DRR"** to **Level 5: "culture of safety fully embedded"**) for assessing DRR maturity and distance traveled (p. 20). **Progress is non-linear and can regress under political or environmental shocks** (p. 20).

## Tools or methods extracted

**Thematic-Area resilience tables**: Use the five-area table architecture to map current conditions and desired resilience outcomes (pp. 28-44); used in scoping, design, and reviews. **Each table specifies Component-level outcomes, detailed Characteristics, and corresponding Enabling Environment factors** (pp. 28-44).

**Participatory hazard/risk assessment + VCA mapping**: Combine hazard/risk and vulnerability/capacity assessment findings and map them against the framework for planning (pp. 31-32, 55-56). **Box 9 provides step-by-step guidance for organizing VCA outputs by Thematic Area** (pp. 55-56).

**Indicator translation method**: Convert selected Characteristics into measurable qualitative/quantitative indicators for logframes and M&E (pp. 24, 56-57). **Box 10 demonstrates conversion examples: "Community members take steps to make their homes and places of work safer" becomes "% of households that have taken at least one specified hazard-reduction measure"** (pp. 56-57).

**Milestone scaling method**: Apply the 5-level attainment scale to track progress across Thematic Areas or selected Components over time **— can be applied at community, Component, or Thematic Area level** (p. 20). **Designed for participatory self-assessment, not external rating** (p. 20).

**Gap/coverage lens**: Map existing project activities against Characteristics to identify strengths, omissions, and partnership needs **— "may highlight weaknesses in ways that can feel threatening to agencies"** (pp. 25, 55-56). **Requires skilled facilitation to remain constructive** (p. 25).

**Customization/prioritization method**: **Tearfund's "Top 20" approach demonstrates selecting high-priority characteristics based on organizational mandate, local hazard profile, and community capacity** (pp. 51-54). **The document provides explicit guidance on three customization approaches: by hazard type, by Thematic Area focus, or by organizational expertise** (p. 18).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Problem framing / scoping**: Builds shared risk and resilience understanding across stakeholders before intervention selection (pp. 7-9, 14). **Helps identify whether DRR integration is feasible within existing project scope** (p. 22).

**Baseline and diagnosis**: Supports baseline studies through structured hazard/risk and VCA-aligned characteristics (pp. 22-23, 31-32). **Can reveal differential vulnerabilities across social groups that aggregate community assessments miss** (p. 9).

**Design and indicator selection**: Provides "signposts" for indicator development and target setting in project plans (pp. 24, 56-57). **Explicitly cautions against replacing donor-agreed indicators mid-cycle; instead recommends using Characteristics to refine or clarify existing indicators** (p. 25).

**Implementation management**: Helps sequence and integrate actions across governance, risk analysis, livelihoods, and preparedness domains (pp. 10-11, 36-44). **The 28 Components provide natural clustering for multi-year programming** (p. 11).

**Monitoring, review, and evaluation**: Can refine existing indicators and provide a systematic review "lens" for progress and gaps (pp. 24-25, 77). **Supports mid-term reviews by identifying which Components have advanced and which remain weak** (p. 25).

**Learning and adaptation**: Encourages periodic milestone reassessment and customization as context changes (pp. 18-20). **The framework is designed to be revisited, not applied once** (p. 20).

**Advocacy and mainstreaming**: Enabling Environment characteristics can structure policy asks and government engagement **— "making it clear which factors fall outside community control and require institutional change"** (p. 26). **Particularly useful for presenting evidence-based policy recommendations to local and national government** (p. 26).

## Field data collection implications

Use participatory hazard/risk and VCA processes that include representatives of all vulnerable groups **— "women, children, elderly, disabled, marginalized castes/ethnicities, and economically poorest households"** (pp. 31-32).

Collect both quantitative and qualitative evidence; some characteristics require participatory judgment rather than fixed numeric thresholds **— "qualitative changes in attitudes, relationships, and governance are as important as measurable outputs"** (p. 57).

Include indigenous/local knowledge alongside scientific data and technical assessments **— "local people's understanding of their own risk environment is central to effective DRR"** (p. 32).

Build repeat data cycles (monitoring and periodic reassessment) instead of one-off studies **— resilience changes over time and single snapshots miss trajectory** (pp. 31-32).

Organize collected evidence under the five Thematic Areas to make analysis actionable for planning (pp. 55-56). **This prevents analytical paralysis when large volumes of qualitative data are collected** (p. 55).

**Disaggregate data by social group wherever feasible — aggregate "community" data can obscure inequality and differential risk exposure** (p. 9).

---

## Coordination implications

Treat DRR as inherently multi-actor; no single organization can address all dimensions, so partnership design is central **— "the breadth of the Characteristics framework makes this explicit"** (p. 8).

Use governance components (partnerships, accountability, institutional roles) as cross-cutting references regardless of sector focus **— Thematic Area 1 provides the coordination architecture for all other areas** (p. 11).

Connect community structures with local government, NGOs, businesses, and emergency actors through defined roles and mechanisms (pp. 29-30, 41-43). **Characteristics 1.1.3 through 1.1.7 specify the types of partnerships required** (pp. 28-29).

Use Enabling Environment elements to frame coordination with higher-level policy and institutional systems **— "these create the conditions within which community-level action succeeds or fails"** (pp. 12, 26).

**The framework can support cluster/sector coordination by mapping which actors address which Components, revealing duplication and gaps** (pp. 25, 55-56).

---

## DRR implications

Align local resilience work with Hyogo priorities while adapting to context-specific risk realities **— the five Thematic Areas directly map to HFA Priorities for Action 2-5** (pp. 6-7, 13).

Integrate preparedness, risk reduction, and recovery linkages instead of treating them as separate tracks **— "structural risk reduction (safer buildings) and preparedness (early warning systems) reinforce each other"** (pp. 29, 41-44).

Combine structural measures (e.g., safer construction, protective infrastructure) with social, governance, and livelihood measures **— "technical solutions fail without governance, knowledge, and sustained community engagement"** (pp. 36-40).

Maintain emphasis on long-term resilience culture, not only crisis-response capacity **— Milestone Level 5 describes resilience as "embedded in everyday decision-making, not activated only during crises"** (p. 20).

**Address underlying risk drivers (poverty, environmental degradation, weak governance) not just proximate hazards — these appear throughout Thematic Areas 4 and 5** (pp. 36-44).

---

## Operational implications

Start with orientation and facilitation: the framework is detailed and users need guided onboarding to apply it well **— "the breadth and complexity can overwhelm first-time users without structured introduction"** (pp. 16-17).

Customize before deployment: select and adapt relevant characteristics to local hazards, capacities, and organizational mandates **— "attempting to address all 167 Characteristics is neither feasible nor desirable"** (p. 18).

Avoid replacing donor-agreed indicators midstream; refine and clarify them using selected characteristics where needed **— "use the framework to sharpen existing M&E, not renegotiate project contracts"** (p. 25).

Plan for potential resistance/demoralization when gap-mapping reveals weak coverage; facilitate discussion constructively **— "gaps should prompt partnership planning, not defensive responses"** (p. 25).

Reassess regularly because resilience gains may regress under fast-changing socio-political or environmental conditions **— "a community at Milestone Level 3 can drop to Level 2 after governance collapse or conflict"** (p. 20).

**Budget time for participatory adaptation workshops — translating 167 generic Characteristics into locally meaningful language requires community engagement, not desk work** (pp. 16-18).

**The framework works best when integrated into existing project cycles, not imposed as parallel DRR assessment exercise** (p. 22).

## Limitations / cautions

Framework structures can create artificial separation between linked resilience elements; users must actively preserve interconnections **— "governance affects every other Thematic Area; treating it in isolation misses the point"** (p. 13).

The Hyogo-aligned structure is useful but not always a perfect operational fit, especially for broad underlying-risk issues **like poverty, land tenure, and macroeconomic policy that cut across all five areas** (p. 13).

The resource describes an ideal state and should not be treated as a direct checklist of project indicators **— "it describes what we're aiming toward, not what any single project can deliver"** (p. 7).

The note does not provide detailed CCA implementation guidance; users must integrate CCA intentionally during application **— climate change is referenced but not systematically integrated into the 2009 version** (p. 15).

Mapping projects against all characteristics can expose gaps in ways that feel threatening or demoralizing if facilitation is weak **— "requires skilled facilitation to remain constructive rather than defensive"** (p. 25).

**The framework is based on 2009 evidence and Hyogo Framework (2005-2015) — users should cross-reference with Sendai Framework (2015-2030) priorities** (p. 6).

**Indicator translation requires contextual judgment — mechanical conversion ("% of households that...") can miss qualitative dimensions of resilience** (p. 57).

**The Enabling Environment section describes factors communities cannot directly change — this creates potential for frustration or learned helplessness if not framed as advocacy targets** (p. 12).

---

## Known tensions

**Resilience vs. vulnerability framing**: The document presents both as "opposite sides of the same coin" but notes ongoing academic debate about whether they're inverse measures or distinct concepts requiring separate assessment (p. 8).

**Community boundaries**: Geographic vs. interest-based definitions of community create operational ambiguity — "a neighborhood" vs. "women's self-help groups" are both valid communities but require different engagement approaches (p. 9).

**Comprehensiveness vs. usability**: The 167 Characteristics provide comprehensive coverage but risk overwhelming users; tension between completeness and practical application drives the customization emphasis (pp. 7, 18).

**External vs. community control**: Enabling Environment factors are critical to resilience but outside community agency — framework must balance what communities can do with what requires institutional change (p. 12).
