## Summary

This guidance note, authored by John Twigg at UCL and commissioned by an interagency group (ActionAid, British Red Cross, Christian Aid, Plan UK, Practical Action, Tearfund), presents a comprehensive framework defining what a disaster-resilient community looks like. Version 2 (2009) incorporates field-testing feedback from multiple countries. The framework comprises five Thematic Areas aligned with the Hyogo Framework for Action: Governance, Risk Assessment, Knowledge and Education, Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction, and Disaster Preparedness and Response. Each area is subdivided into Components of Resilience and detailed Characteristics (167 total) of a disaster-resilient community, plus an Enabling Environment section. The document is explicitly a resource, not a manual or checklist, intended to be adapted and customized. It covers project cycle applications (baselines, VCA, design, M&E), strategic planning, research, and advocacy. The composite authority is high due to institutional backing and field testing, though evidence quality is medium (qualitative, context-dependent). Urban adaptation is substantial – the source is written for generic/rural communities and requires major modification for urban informal settlements, fragmented governance, and multi-hazard environments.

**Urban applicability gaps (checked items: 6 → substantial adaptation required):**
- ✅ Informal settlement dynamics absent (land tenure, density, service gaps)
- ✅ Community consensus assumed – unlikely in fragmented urban populations
- ✅ Local government assumed accessible and cooperative – often false in cities
- ❌ Single-hazard framing – source addresses multi-hazard (Thematic Area 2 includes "all major hazards")
- ✅ Spatial community definition insufficient for urban heterogeneity
- ✅ Social cohesion assumptions built for rural settings
- ✅ Scale assumptions incompatible with urban neighborhoods (participatory methods designed for villages)

### Frameworks

**Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-FRAMEWORK-001  
**Object name:** Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community framework  
**Source location:** Section 6 (tables), pp. 28-44  
**Source fidelity note:** named  
**Description:** A multi-level framework organized into 5 Thematic Areas (based on HFA priorities), subdivided into 28 Components of Resilience, further detailed as 167 Characteristics of a disaster-resilient community, plus parallel Characteristics of an Enabling Environment for each component.  
**Purpose:** To provide a comprehensive, structured picture of what a disaster-resilient community looks like, to support planning, assessment, M&E, research, and advocacy.  
**Components or steps:** 5 Thematic Areas – (1) Governance, (2) Risk Assessment, (3) Knowledge and Education, (4) Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction, (5) Disaster Preparedness and Response. Each with 3-7 Components.  
**Relationships:** Thematic Area 1 (Governance) is cross-cutting; Enabling Environment links community to external systems; components are not strictly independent.  
**Logic:** Based on Hyogo Framework for Action; assumes resilience is multi-faceted and requires action across all areas.  
**Assumptions:** Communities are spatially defined and have some consensus; external duty bearers exist and can be engaged.  
**Limitations:** HFA Priority 4 creates imbalance; artificial separations (e.g., hazard vs vulnerability assessment); no inherent prioritization.  
**Knowledge layer:** conceptual, diagnostic  
**Multi-type status:** Framework (primary) – also used as tool for assessment. **Justification for dual layer:** Serves conceptual structuring of resilience domains and diagnostic assessment of community status against those domains.  
**Comparison notes:** Aligned with HFA indicators at national level; complements ADPC's key indicators (Box 8) and Tearfund's 'top 20' (Box 7).

**Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-FRAMEWORK-002  
**Object name:** Enabling Environment framework  
**Source location:** Each Thematic Area table, right-hand column  
**Source fidelity note:** named  
**Description:** Parallel set of characteristics describing external (policy, institutional, socio-economic) conditions required to support community-level resilience. Covers local, national, and occasionally international levels.  
**Purpose:** To acknowledge that community resilience depends on external duty bearers and systems, and to provide advocacy targets.  
**Components or steps:** Organized under same Components of Resilience as community characteristics.  
**Relationships:** Linked to each Component of Resilience; not separate.  
**Logic:** Communities cannot achieve full resilience without supportive enabling environment.  
**Assumptions:** External actors have responsibility and capacity.  
**Limitations:** General/abstract; needs sharpening for specific advocacy targets.  
**Knowledge layer:** conceptual  
**Multi-type status:** Framework only.  
**Comparison notes:** Complements UN ISDR national-level indicators.

**Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-CONCEPT-002  
**Object name:** Resilience  
**Source location:** p. 8  
**Source fidelity note:** named  
**Definition:** The capacity to: anticipate, minimize and absorb potential stresses or destructive forces through adaptation or resistance; manage or maintain certain basic functions and structures during disastrous events; recover or 'bounce back' after an event.  
**Related concepts:** Vulnerability (opposite side of same coin), capacity, coping capacity  
**Distinctions:** Broader than 'capacity' because it goes beyond specific behaviours and measures.  
**Role in source:** Central organizing concept; Characteristics define what resilience looks like.  
**Limitations:** Difficult to separate from 'capacity' in everyday usage; relative (resilient to what, to what extent).  
**Knowledge layer:** conceptual  
**Comparison notes:** Multi-dimensional; differs from engineering resilience (bounce back to same state).

### Decision Rules

**Object name:** Indicator refinement decision rule  
**Source location:** p. 24, 77, Case Study 6  
**Source fidelity note:** named  
**Condition:** A DRR project has an existing logical framework with donor-agreed indicators (mid-implementation).  
**Recommendation:** Use Characteristics to refine existing indicators by splitting, rewording, clarifying, or adding precision – do not replace or add new indicators without donor approval.

**Multi-type status:** Decision rule.  
**Comparison notes:** Distinguishes retrospective refinement from prospective design.

### Risks

**Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RISK-001  
**Object name:** Checklist misuse of Characteristics  
**Source location:** p. 7, 18, 23  
**Source fidelity note:** explicit  
**Failure mode:** Users treat Characteristics as a checklist to be ticked off mechanically.

### Case Lessons

**Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-LESSON-001  
**Object name:** Reverse mapping from known projects to framework  
**Source location:** Case Study 1 (Bangladesh), p. 60-61  
**Source fidelity note:** explicit  
**Case reference:** Tearfund workshop with HEED Bangladesh, using floor map of coastal village and cards for vulnerabilities/activities.  
**Situation:** Practitioners previously focused on what to prevent (negative).  
**Lesson:** Working backwards from existing community activities and mapping them to Characteristics shifts focus to positive achievements, reveals gaps, and increases motivation.  
**Evidence:** Participants saw positive value, discovered new aspects, recognized gaps as useful not burdensome.  
**Transferability:** High – can be replicated in any community workshop using participatory mapping.  
**Related objects:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RULE-002, F-008, F-010  
**Knowledge layer:** operational  
**Comparison notes:** Contrasts with top-down presentation that overwhelms users.

**Object ID:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-LESSON-002  
**Object name:** Use of Characteristics for capacity gap analysis  
**Source location:** Case Study 3 (Malawi), p. 68-69  
**Source fidelity note:** explicit  
**Case reference:** Tearfund consortium in Zomba, Malawi, using Thematic Area 4 to assess knowledge, skills, attitudes of staff against Characteristics.  
**Situation:** Organizations had limited capacity across the full range of resilience components.  
**Lesson:** A rope exercise (placing cards from 'Fully equipped' to 'Limited Scope') visually revealed strengths and weaknesses, highlighted need for partnerships, and showed that in-depth technical expertise (e.g., environmental knowledge) is required.  
**Evidence:** Clustered cards mostly between 'Fully equipped' and 'Medium Capacity', with flow toward 'Limited Scope'. Partners recognized need to share skills and work with government.  
**Transferability:** High – can be adapted for any consortium or multi-organization capacity assessment.  
**Related objects:** S-2009-twigg-ucl-disaster-resilient-community-RISK-002 (demoralization) – but here done collectively, safety in numbers.  
**Knowledge layer:** diagnostic  
**Comparison notes:** Differs from individual agency assessment; safety in consortium reduces exposure.

**Source location:** Case Study 5 (Central America), p. 74-76
