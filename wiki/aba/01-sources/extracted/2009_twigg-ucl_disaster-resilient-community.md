# Summary
This guidance note presents a structured framework for defining and assessing what a disaster-resilient community could look like. It is designed for government and civil society actors working on local DRR and CCA, and is explicitly positioned as a practical resource rather than a rigid manual (pp. 6-7). The second edition (Version 2, November 2009) builds on desk research, expert input, and field-testing feedback from multiple agencies (p. 7).

The core of the document is a comprehensive table-based framework organized around five Thematic Areas aligned to the Hyogo Framework for Action: Governance; Risk Assessment; Knowledge and Education; Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction; and Disaster Preparedness and Response (pp. 6, 9, 28, 31, 33, 36, 41). Within these areas, the note lays out 28 Components of Resilience and 167 Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community, plus corresponding Enabling Environment factors (p. 11).

The note gives extensive operational guidance for adapting and applying the framework across project-cycle stages, including baseline studies, strategic planning, indicator design, review/evaluation, research, and advocacy (pp. 14, 22-26). It repeatedly emphasizes contextual adaptation: users should customize, select, and modify characteristics to fit local hazards, capacities, and institutional realities rather than attempting to apply all characteristics uniformly (pp. 7, 16, 18).

The document also highlights methodological cautions: resilience is an ideal rather than a fully attainable state; framework structures can over-separate interconnected issues; and indicator conversion requires deliberative, participatory judgment (pp. 7, 13, 57). A five-level milestone scale is proposed for tracking progress over time without forcing false precision (p. 20).

# Key concepts
- Disaster risk reduction (DRR): A systematic approach to identifying, assessing, and reducing disaster risk across development and relief work; should be integrated, not treated as an add-on (p. 8).
- Resilience: Community/system capacity to anticipate and absorb shocks, maintain core functions during events, and recover after them (p. 8).
- Disaster-resilient community: An idealized target state ("the safest possible community"), used as a directional benchmark rather than a literal endpoint (pp. 7-8).
- Community: Not only a geographic unit; includes social differentiation, multiple identities, internal divisions, and external dependencies that affect resilience (p. 9).
- Enabling Environment: Wider institutional, policy, and socio-economic conditions that shape community-level resilience outcomes (pp. 12-13).
- Characteristics framework: A structured schema (Thematic Areas -> Components -> Characteristics) designed for usability but requiring users to preserve linkages across categories (pp. 13-14).
- Milestones: A five-level progression model (from low awareness to a "culture of safety") for assessing DRR maturity and distance traveled (p. 20).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Thematic-Area resilience tables: Use the five-area table architecture to map current conditions and desired resilience outcomes (pp. 28-44); used in scoping, design, and reviews.
- Participatory hazard/risk assessment + VCA mapping: Combine hazard/risk and vulnerability/capacity assessment findings and map them against the framework for planning (pp. 31-32, 55-56).
- Indicator translation method: Convert selected Characteristics into measurable qualitative/quantitative indicators for logframes and M&E (pp. 24, 56-57).
- Milestone scaling method: Apply the 5-level attainment scale to track progress across Thematic Areas or selected Components over time (p. 20).
- Gap/coverage lens: Map existing project activities against Characteristics to identify strengths, omissions, and partnership needs (pp. 25, 55-56).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Problem framing / scoping: Builds shared risk and resilience understanding across stakeholders before intervention selection (pp. 7-9, 14).
- Baseline and diagnosis: Supports baseline studies through structured hazard/risk and VCA-aligned characteristics (pp. 22-23, 31-32).
- Design and indicator selection: Provides "signposts" for indicator development and target setting in project plans (pp. 24, 56-57).
- Implementation management: Helps sequence and integrate actions across governance, risk analysis, livelihoods, and preparedness domains (pp. 10-11, 36-44).
- Monitoring, review, and evaluation: Can refine existing indicators and provide a systematic review "lens" for progress and gaps (pp. 24-25, 77).
- Learning and adaptation: Encourages periodic milestone reassessment and customization as context changes (pp. 18-20).
- Advocacy and mainstreaming: Enabling Environment characteristics can structure policy asks and government engagement (p. 26).

# Field data collection implications
- Use participatory hazard/risk and VCA processes that include representatives of all vulnerable groups (pp. 31-32).
- Collect both quantitative and qualitative evidence; some characteristics require participatory judgment rather than fixed numeric thresholds (p. 57).
- Include indigenous/local knowledge alongside scientific data and technical assessments (p. 32).
- Build repeat data cycles (monitoring and periodic reassessment) instead of one-off studies (pp. 31-32).
- Organize collected evidence under the five Thematic Areas to make analysis actionable for planning (pp. 55-56).

# Coordination implications
- Treat DRR as inherently multi-actor; no single organization can address all dimensions, so partnership design is central (p. 8).
- Use governance components (partnerships, accountability, institutional roles) as cross-cutting references regardless of sector focus (p. 11).
- Connect community structures with local government, NGOs, businesses, and emergency actors through defined roles and mechanisms (pp. 29-30, 41-43).
- Use Enabling Environment elements to frame coordination with higher-level policy and institutional systems (pp. 12, 26).

# DRR implications
- Align local resilience work with Hyogo priorities while adapting to context-specific risk realities (pp. 6-7, 13).
- Integrate preparedness, risk reduction, and recovery linkages instead of treating them as separate tracks (pp. 29, 41-44).
- Combine structural measures (e.g., safer construction, protective infrastructure) with social, governance, and livelihood measures (pp. 36-40).
- Maintain emphasis on long-term resilience culture, not only crisis-response capacity (p. 20).

# Operational implications
- Start with orientation and facilitation: the framework is detailed and users need guided onboarding to apply it well (pp. 16-17).
- Customize before deployment: select and adapt relevant characteristics to local hazards, capacities, and organizational mandates (p. 18).
- Avoid replacing donor-agreed indicators midstream; refine and clarify them using selected characteristics where needed (p. 25).
- Plan for potential resistance/demoralization when gap-mapping reveals weak coverage; facilitate discussion constructively (p. 25).
- Reassess regularly because resilience gains may regress under fast-changing socio-political or environmental conditions (p. 20).

# Limitations / cautions
- Framework structures can create artificial separation between linked resilience elements; users must actively preserve interconnections (p. 13).
- The Hyogo-aligned structure is useful but not always a perfect operational fit, especially for broad underlying-risk issues (p. 13).
- The resource describes an ideal state and should not be treated as a direct checklist of project indicators (p. 7).
- The note does not provide detailed CCA implementation guidance; users must integrate CCA intentionally during application (p. 15).
- Mapping projects against all characteristics can expose gaps in ways that feel threatening or demoralizing if facilitation is weak (p. 25).
