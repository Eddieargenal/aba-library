## Summary

The Urban Response Analysis Framework (URAF) is a practical decision-support framework designed to help humanitarian teams identify appropriate multi-sector responses in urban crises, with a focus on displaced and host populations (pp. 3, 7, 16). Developed by IIED in 2017 as part of the 'Stronger Cities' initiative (previously 'Urban Crises Learning Fund'), in partnership with IRC and other organizations, and funded by DFID (pp. 3, 7).

URAF promotes a systems approach and encourages use of multipurpose cash grants (MPGs) where appropriate, alongside complementary sector-specific responses, advocacy, and technical support. "The URAF endorses, where appropriate, the use of multipurpose cash grants alongside complementary sector-specific responses, including advocacy and technical support. Therefore, the URAF recommends assistance that meets the basic needs of the displaced and host populations whilst addressing sector specific needs" (p. 3). The framework explicitly links response choices to needs, context, feasibility, and risks rather than modality preferences alone (pp. 3, 8-10, 12-13).

The core of the note is a six-step process from multi-sector needs/vulnerability assessment through response recommendation and monitoring design. The six steps are: (1) Multi-sector needs and vulnerability assessment; (2) Prioritise needs and identify people in need (PiN); (3) Operational context analysis and SWOT; (4) Define objectives and develop response long list; (5) Develop response recommendation shortlist; (6) Define monitoring indicators (pp. 15, 21). It is meant to be used in interactive programme decision discussions and to produce transparent, documented outputs on priorities, target groups, response options, assumptions, and monitoring indicators (pp. 15, 18, 21, 46).

URAF is part of a suite of three complementary urban tools: (1) Urban Multi-Sector Vulnerability Assessment Tool (UMVAT) for data collection; (2) Urban Response Analysis Framework (URAF) for response option identification; (3) Targeting in Urban Displacement Contexts guidance for beneficiary selection (pp. 3, 7, 16).

## Key concepts

**Multi-sector response analysis**: The link between situation analysis and programme planning/implementation, used to select response options, modalities, and target groups based on appropriateness and feasibility while minimizing harm — "Response analysis is the analytical process that links needs and vulnerability assessment to programme planning and implementation. It involves identifying appropriate response options (what to do), modalities (how to deliver), and target groups (who to assist), based on assessment of appropriateness, feasibility, and potential for harm" (p. 8).

**Systems approach in urban response**: Analysis of interlinked urban systems (economy/livelihoods, politics/governance, social/cultural, infrastructure/services, space/settlements) to improve response relevance and resilience outcomes — "Urban contexts are characterized by interconnected systems. Understanding these linkages — between livelihoods and markets, between housing and infrastructure, between governance and service delivery — enables more effective and resilient responses. URAF applies the five-system typology from Campbell (2016) to structure contextual analysis" (pp. 13, 17).

**Multipurpose cash grants (MPGs)**: Unrestricted cash support for basic needs prioritization by affected households; may be conditional or unconditional and often needs complementary interventions — "Multipurpose cash grants are unrestricted cash transfers designed to cover multiple basic needs as prioritized by recipients. The transfer value is typically calculated based on a Minimum Expenditure Basket (MEB) or other monetized needs calculation. MPGs offer choice and dignity but are not always appropriate or sufficient — complementary sector-specific assistance, advocacy, and technical support are often necessary" (pp. 9, 13).

**Needs severity score**: A structured scoring approach derived from selected UMVAT questions to support prioritization discussions; intended as indicative input, not a stand-alone decision rule — "The needs severity scoring method provides a semi-quantitative way to compare severity across sectors and population groups, using a 0-4 scale applied to key UMVAT indicators. Scores are indicative tools for discussion, not definitive measures — they must be triangulated with qualitative assessment, community validation, and contextual judgment" (p. 29; Annex 2A p. 49).

**Response layering**: Combining basic-needs responses (often cash) with sector-specific and governance/coordination measures to match heterogeneous needs — "Urban populations are diverse with heterogeneous needs. Response layering involves combining: (1) Basic needs support (often multipurpose cash) for households with general vulnerability; (2) Sector-specific assistance (WASH, shelter, health) for acute needs or when cash alone is insufficient; (3) Governance/coordination/advocacy to address systemic issues. Different population sub-groups may receive different response layers" (pp. 14, 43, 45).

**Appropriateness and feasibility**: "Response options must be assessed for appropriateness (do they match needs and preferences? will they cause harm?) and feasibility (are markets functional? is security adequate? do we have capacity?). Neither appropriateness nor feasibility alone is sufficient — both are necessary" (pp. 8-10, 36, 42).

## Tools or methods extracted

**URAF six-step flow**: Step 1 assessment; Step 2 prioritize needs/identify PiN; Step 3 operational context/SWOT; Step 4 objectives and response long list; Step 5 recommendation shortlist; Step 6 monitoring indicators — Each step includes: key questions to answer; expected outputs; and guidance on what to consider and incorporate into response analysis discussions (pp. 15, 21, 22, 26, 33, 36, 46).

**Needs severity scoring method (UMVAT-linked)**: Sectoral scoring with separate treatment of education/ICLA/protection relative to immediate basic-needs sectors — "Scoring uses 0-4 scale: 0 = no/negligible severity; 1 = low severity; 2 = medium severity; 3 = high severity; 4 = very high severity. Education, ICLA (Information, Counselling, Legal Assistance), and protection/governance are scored separately from immediate basic needs (food, WASH, shelter, health, NFI) because they operate on different timescales and require different response modalities" (p. 29; pp. 49, 52-58).

**Response option framework matrix**: Long-listing options by advantages, disadvantages, feasibility, and timing before narrowing for deeper analysis — "Step 4 long-listing process documents: response option (what); advantages (why consider); disadvantages (concerns); feasibility (preconditions); timing (immediate/medium/longer-term). This structured matrix enables systematic comparison of diverse options before short-listing" (p. 43).

**Response recommendation matrix**: Short-listing options by factors such as cost efficiency/effectiveness, risk/mitigation, coherence, preference, and acceptance — "Step 5 short-listing applies decision criteria: cost efficiency/effectiveness; risk and mitigation measures; coherence with other actors; beneficiary preferences; community/authority acceptance; organizational capacity. Each criterion is scored or discussed, producing documented rationale for recommendations" (p. 45).

**Cash feasibility and delivery decision trees**: Urban precondition checks for cash and CDM assessment support (Figure 9 and Annex 3) — "Cash feasibility decision tree assesses: Are markets functioning and accessible? Are prices stable? Is security adequate for delivery and use? Do households have decision-making capacity? Are cash delivery mechanisms available? Cash Delivery Mechanism (CDM) decision tree compares: mobile money, bank transfers, hawala/remittance agents, cash-in-hand, vouchers — assessing each for access, cost, security, and acceptability" (pp. 42, 59).

**SWOT analysis (Step 3)**: "Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats analysis applied to operational context including: organizational capacity; coordination environment; government relationships; security situation; access constraints; funding availability. SWOT informs realistic response option identification" (p. 33).

**Multi-sector vulnerability assessment integration**: "URAF assumes completion of multi-sector needs and vulnerability assessment (ideally using UMVAT) before response analysis. Assessment should cover: demographics; displacement status; needs across sectors; vulnerabilities (gender, age, disability, protection); coping strategies; preferences; access to services and markets" (pp. 22-24).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Assessment phase**: Requires multi-sector needs/vulnerability data and triangulation before response analysis (Step 1) — "URAF Step 1 does not provide assessment methodology (that's UMVAT's role) but specifies data requirements: quantitative household data; qualitative community/stakeholder data; secondary data; triangulation across sources. Assessment should cover both displaced and host populations to enable whole-population inclusion and tension mitigation" (p. 22).

**Analysis and design phase**: Structures prioritization of unmet needs and target populations using needs data, severity scoring, and gap analysis (Step 2) — "Step 2 answers: Which sectors show highest unmet needs? Which population sub-groups are most vulnerable? What geographic areas should be prioritized? What is the People in Need (PiN) calculation? Severity scoring, gap analysis (comparing needs to existing assistance), and sub-group comparison inform prioritization" (pp. 26, 29).

**Strategic optioning phase**: Integrates context constraints/opportunities and agency/policy factors before setting objectives and response options (Steps 3-4) — "Steps 3-4 shift from needs analysis to strategic planning. Step 3 SWOT analysis surfaces operational constraints and opportunities. Step 4 defines objectives (what change do we want to achieve?) and generates response option long list (MPG, sector-specific in-kind, cash-for-work, service provision, advocacy, capacity building, etc.)" (pp. 33, 36).

**Decision and setup phase**: Supports response recommendation and mitigation planning with explicit trade-off criteria (Step 5) — "Step 5 applies decision criteria to short-list options, documents trade-offs (e.g., cash offers choice but may not address protection; in-kind ensures specific needs met but reduces flexibility), identifies risk mitigation measures, and produces recommendation with explicit rationale for transparency and accountability" (p. 45).

**Adaptive implementation phase**: Defines context and programme indicators to maintain ongoing appropriateness (Step 6) — "Step 6 monitoring framework tracks: needs evolution (are needs changing?); preference shifts (do recipients still prefer this response?); risk factors (are tensions emerging? market disruptions?); delivery quality (access, timeliness, adequacy); outcomes (needs met? resilience strengthened?). Monitoring enables adaptive management" (p. 46).

## Field data collection implications

Collect both qualitative and quantitative multi-sector data at household and community levels before response-option selection — "Assessment data should combine: household surveys (quantitative needs, demographics, vulnerabilities); focus group discussions (qualitative needs, coping strategies, preferences, social dynamics); key informant interviews (service providers, authorities, market actors); and secondary data (service coverage, market prices, governance structures)" (p. 22).

Triangulate assessment findings using community consultations, FGDs, KIIs, and secondary data validation — "Single-source data is insufficient for urban response analysis. Triangulation across multiple sources (household survey + FGDs + KIIs + secondary data + observation) improves validity, surfaces divergent perspectives, and builds confidence in findings. Contradictions between sources require investigation and explanation" (pp. 22, 24).

Ensure demographic disaggregation (eg age, sex, dependency characteristics) to enable subgroup targeting logic — "Data should be disaggregated by: displacement status (refugee, IDP, returnee, host community); demographics (sex, age groups, household composition); vulnerabilities (disability, chronic illness, female-headed households, unaccompanied minors); and geography (neighborhood, settlement type). Disaggregation enables equity analysis and targeted response design" (p. 23).

Include host populations and comparative needs analysis to reduce tensions and improve social cohesion outcomes — "Assessing only displaced populations risks: creating visible inequities that fuel resentment; missing opportunities for whole-population interventions; and undermining social cohesion. Comparative needs analysis (displaced vs. host community severity) informs inclusive targeting and reduces tension risks" (p. 23).

Use pre-selected UMVAT essential questions for severity scoring, but contextualize and verify scores through discussion and triangulation — "Annex 2A links severity scores to specific UMVAT indicators (e.g., water: liters per person per day; shelter: persons per room; food: consumption score). These provide starting points but must be adapted to context and validated through qualitative assessment and community feedback. Scores are discussion tools, not mechanistic decision rules" (pp. 29, 49).

**Market assessment integration**: "Cash feasibility requires market assessment data: what goods/services are available? are markets accessible to all groups? are prices stable and affordable? is there risk of inflation from cash injection? who controls markets? Market assessment should be ongoing, not one-time, especially in volatile urban contexts" (pp. 36, 42).

## Coordination implications

Response analysis should include active engagement of municipalities, public institutions, service providers, and other key stakeholders in urban settings — "Urban responses depend on municipal services, private providers, and local governance. Response analysis should involve: municipal authorities (to understand service delivery plans and constraints); service providers (water utilities, health facilities, schools); private sector (market actors, employers); and civil society (local NGOs, community organizations). Engagement builds buy-in and improves feasibility" (p. 10).

Vertical and horizontal coordination is presented as necessary for coherent response selection and stronger impact — "Vertical coordination links community-level interventions to municipal/national systems and policies. Horizontal coordination aligns interventions across agencies, sectors, and geographic areas. URAF Step 3 SWOT explicitly examines coordination environment; Step 5 recommendation criteria include coherence with other actors' programmes" (p. 16).

Government/UN/donor policy alignment and coordination platform strategies are explicit Step 3 inputs — "Operational context analysis (Step 3) should assess: What are government policies on cash assistance? displacement? informal settlements? What coordination platforms exist (clusters, cash working groups, area-based coordination)? What are donor priorities and funding mechanisms? Response options must be feasible within this policy/coordination landscape" (p. 33).

Cash working groups and sector coordination resources are recommended as practical decision inputs — "URAF recommends consulting: Cash Working Group for market assessments, transfer value guidance, delivery mechanism options; Sector clusters/coordinators for gap analysis and complementarity; Area-based coordination platforms for geographic coherence; and Protection/GBV specialists for risk analysis and mitigation" (p. 16).

**Inter-agency response analysis**: "While URAF can be used by a single agency, multi-agency application (bringing together organizations working in the same urban area) strengthens coordination, reduces duplication, improves complementarity, and builds collective capacity. Joint response analysis requires more facilitation but produces shared strategic vision" (pp. 15, 18).

## DRR implications

URAF promotes resilience-oriented response design by combining immediate needs support with complementary measures that address systemic risk factors — "By recommending a systems approach to understanding the urban context and the wider response analysis process, the URAF also aims to encourage the identification of resilience-building responses" (p. 3). "Resilience responses go beyond immediate needs relief to address underlying vulnerabilities: strengthening livelihoods, improving access to services, supporting tenure security, building community organization, and advocating for policy change" (pp. 3, 13).

The framework references Hyogo priorities as relevant to urban resilience and risk reduction integration — "The systems approach draws on urban resilience frameworks including the Hyogo Framework for Action (HFA) priorities. URAF encourages responses that: reduce exposure (e.g., supporting relocation from high-hazard areas); reduce vulnerability (improving housing quality, diversifying livelihoods); and strengthen capacity (community organization, early warning, contingency planning)" (p. 13).

Monitoring design includes attention to conflict-sensitive indicators and urban emergency-trigger indicators to detect evolving risk conditions — "Step 6 monitoring framework should include context indicators tracking: social tension signs (incidents, community perception); service disruption (infrastructure failures); market volatility (price spikes, shortages); security deterioration; and hazard exposure (seasonal flooding, disease outbreaks). These enable early detection and adaptive response" (p. 46).

**Urban-specific vulnerabilities**: "URAF's urban systems approach surfaces DRR-relevant vulnerabilities: informal settlements in hazard zones; infrastructure interdependencies (cascading failures); economic precarity in cash-dependent livelihoods; weak governance and limited enforcement; and environmental degradation. Response analysis should consider how interventions affect these underlying risk drivers" (pp. 13, 17, 33).

## Operational implications

URAF is intended for use before targeting, after needs/vulnerability assessment, during programme identification/preparedness phases — "URAF sits between needs assessment and targeting/implementation. It should be applied: after multi-sector assessment data is available; before detailed targeting criteria and beneficiary lists are developed; during programme design/proposal development phase. Using URAF before assessment or after targeting reduces effectiveness" (p. 18).

Target users are programme managers and technical specialists using a workshop format; estimated completion time is 4-6 hours (ideally over two days) when data are available — "URAF is designed for facilitated workshop application with: programme managers who will design/oversee implementation; sector technical specialists (WASH, shelter, health, livelihoods, protection); M&E staff; and ideally representatives from coordination/partnerships. Workshop format enables discussion and consensus-building. Timing assumes assessment data is already available — if not, preparation extends timeline" (p. 18).

Cash should not be assumed feasible by default; feasibility analysis and delivery mechanism assessment are required — "URAF challenges the assumption that cash is always appropriate in urban contexts. Cash feasibility depends on: functional markets; price stability; security for delivery and use; accessibility (do all groups have market access? can all use delivery mechanisms?); and absence of protection concerns. Decision trees (Figure 9, Annex 3) structure feasibility assessment" (pp. 10, 36, 42).

Outputs should be documented to strengthen accountability, evaluation, and learning — "Each URAF step produces documented outputs: prioritized needs list; target group rationale; response options considered and rationale for short-listing/rejection; monitoring framework. Documentation supports: internal accountability (transparent decision-making); proposal development (donor communication); evaluation (comparing outcomes to intended objectives); and learning (capturing assumptions and testing them)" (p. 18).

**Resource requirements**: "Effective URAF application requires: availability of multi-sector assessment data; staff time for workshop participation (multiple technical specialists for 1-2 days); facilitation capacity; and willingness to engage in structured decision-making. Organizations without these preconditions should build capacity before expecting URAF to add value" (pp. 18, 22).

**Adaptive management**: "URAF is not a one-time exercise. In dynamic urban crises, response appropriateness can shift as needs evolve, markets change, security deteriorates, or new actors arrive. Step 6 monitoring enables periodic review and response adjustment. Major context changes may require repeating response analysis" (p. 46).

## Limitations / cautions

The document notes that evidence on multi-sector approaches and vulnerability assessments is still evolving and guidance should be iteratively updated — "Multi-sectoral approaches and vulnerability assessments are evolving areas of practice and evidence on definitive 'best practices' is still emerging. This guidance should be taken as a starting point and be built upon and/or revised as further evidence emerges" (p. 7).

URAF is an analytical support framework; outcomes depend on quality and sufficiency of assessment/context data — "URAF cannot compensate for poor-quality or incomplete needs assessment. If assessment data is weak (small samples, single-sector, no host community comparison, no market data), response analysis will be weak. URAF adds value when assessment foundation is solid" (pp. 22, 33).

Needs severity scores are indicative and should not be treated as definitive without triangulation and community validation — "Severity scores provide semi-quantitative prioritization inputs but are not mechanistic decision rules. Scores should be triangulated with qualitative assessment, validated with communities, and contextualized with local knowledge. Over-reliance on scores without critical interpretation risks inappropriate prioritization" (p. 29).

Some sector scores (education, ICLA, protection/governance) should be interpreted separately from immediate basic-needs sectors — "Education, ICLA, and protection/governance needs operate on different timescales and require different response modalities than immediate basic needs (food, water, shelter, health). High severity in these sectors doesn't necessarily mean they should be prioritized over acute basic needs, but they shouldn't be ignored. Interpretation requires contextual judgment" (pp. 29, 49).

**URAF assumes certain preconditions**: "Effective URAF application assumes: organizational commitment to multi-sector response (not just single-sector programming); access to assessment data; staff capacity for facilitated workshop process; and willingness to document decision-making. Organizations without these preconditions should address them before expecting URAF to improve programming" (pp. 18, 22).

**2017 publication date**: URAF pre-dates more recent cash guidance (2018 CaLP updates, 2019 Grand Bargain cash commitments evolution, 2020s anticipatory action frameworks) and urban guidance (IASC 2018 ABC, Sphere 2018 urban integration). Users should integrate with contemporary frameworks.

## Known tensions

**Multi-sector integration vs. sectoral expertise**: URAF promotes multi-sector response analysis, but organizations are typically structured by sector with specialized technical staff and funding streams — integrating across sectors requires institutional commitment and coordination overhead.

**Cash preference vs. context appropriateness**: URAF endorses MPG where appropriate, but faces tension between: growing global policy push for cash-first approaches; and context-specific realities where cash may be inappropriate (non-functional markets, protection concerns, specific technical needs cash cannot address).

**Comprehensive analysis vs. operational speed**: URAF's six-step process with workshop facilitation, severity scoring, long-listing, and short-listing requires time and resources — this may conflict with rapid-onset emergency timelines or organizational pressure for quick programme launch.

**Needs prioritization vs. organizational mandate**: Severity scoring and prioritization may identify highest needs in sectors outside an organization's mandate or expertise — should agencies respond to priority needs or stay within their capacity? URAF surfaces but doesn't resolve this tension.

**Host population inclusion vs. mandate restrictions**: URAF recommends whole-population inclusion to reduce tension, but some agencies have mandates or funding restricted to specific populations (refugees only, IDPs only) — legal/policy constraints may prevent implementing URAF recommendations.

**Quantitative scores vs. qualitative judgment**: Severity scoring provides semi-quantitative prioritization tool, but over-reliance on numbers can obscure qualitative nuance, community voice, and contextual factors that scores don't capture.

**Documentation vs. decision efficiency**: URAF emphasizes documenting rationale, assumptions, and criteria (for accountability and learning), but documentation requirements can slow decision-making and feel bureaucratic to operational staff facing urgent needs.
