# Summary
The Urban Response Analysis Framework (URAF) is a practical decision-support framework designed to help humanitarian teams identify appropriate multi-sector responses in urban crises, with a focus on displaced and host populations (pp. 3, 7, 16).

URAF promotes a systems approach and encourages use of multipurpose cash grants (MPGs) where appropriate, alongside complementary sector-specific responses, advocacy, and technical support. The framework explicitly links response choices to needs, context, feasibility, and risks rather than modality preferences alone (pp. 3, 8-10, 12-13).

The core of the note is a six-step process from multi-sector needs/vulnerability assessment through response recommendation and monitoring design. It is meant to be used in interactive programme decision discussions and to produce transparent, documented outputs on priorities, target groups, response options, assumptions, and monitoring indicators (pp. 15, 18, 21, 46).

# Key concepts
- Multi-sector response analysis: the link between situation analysis and programme planning/implementation, used to select response options, modalities, and target groups based on appropriateness and feasibility while minimizing harm (p. 8).
- Systems approach in urban response: analysis of interlinked urban systems (economy/livelihoods, politics/governance, social/cultural, infrastructure/services, space/settlements) to improve response relevance and resilience outcomes (pp. 13, 17).
- Multipurpose cash grants (MPGs): unrestricted cash support for basic needs prioritization by affected households; may be conditional or unconditional and often needs complementary interventions (pp. 9, 13).
- Needs severity score: a structured scoring approach derived from selected UMVAT questions to support prioritization discussions; intended as indicative input, not a stand-alone decision rule (p. 29; Annex 2A p. 49).
- Response layering: combining basic-needs responses (often cash) with sector-specific and governance/coordination measures to match heterogeneous needs (pp. 14, 43, 45).

# Tools or methods extracted
- URAF six-step flow: Step 1 assessment; Step 2 prioritize needs/identify PiN; Step 3 operational context/SWOT; Step 4 objectives and response long list; Step 5 recommendation shortlist; Step 6 monitoring indicators (pp. 15, 21, 22, 26, 33, 36, 46).
- Needs severity scoring method (UMVAT-linked): sectoral scoring with separate treatment of education/ICLA/protection relative to immediate basic-needs sectors (p. 29; pp. 49, 52-58).
- Response option framework matrix: long-listing options by advantages, disadvantages, feasibility, and timing before narrowing for deeper analysis (p. 43).
- Response recommendation matrix: short-listing options by factors such as cost efficiency/effectiveness, risk/mitigation, coherence, preference, and acceptance (p. 45).
- Cash feasibility and delivery decision trees: urban precondition checks for cash and CDM assessment support (Figure 9 and Annex 3) (pp. 42, 59).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Assessment phase: requires multi-sector needs/vulnerability data and triangulation before response analysis (Step 1) (p. 22).
- Analysis and design phase: structures prioritization of unmet needs and target populations using needs data, severity scoring, and gap analysis (Step 2) (pp. 26, 29).
- Strategic optioning phase: integrates context constraints/opportunities and agency/policy factors before setting objectives and response options (Steps 3-4) (pp. 33, 36).
- Decision and setup phase: supports response recommendation and mitigation planning with explicit trade-off criteria (Step 5) (p. 45).
- Adaptive implementation phase: defines context and programme indicators to maintain ongoing appropriateness (Step 6) (p. 46).

# Field data collection implications
- Collect both qualitative and quantitative multi-sector data at household and community levels before response-option selection (p. 22).
- Triangulate assessment findings using community consultations, FGDs, KIIs, and secondary data validation (pp. 22, 24).
- Ensure demographic disaggregation (eg age, sex, dependency characteristics) to enable subgroup targeting logic (p. 23).
- Include host populations and comparative needs analysis to reduce tensions and improve social cohesion outcomes (p. 23).
- Use pre-selected UMVAT essential questions for severity scoring, but contextualize and verify scores through discussion and triangulation (pp. 29, 49).

# Coordination implications
- Response analysis should include active engagement of municipalities, public institutions, service providers, and other key stakeholders in urban settings (p. 10).
- Vertical and horizontal coordination is presented as necessary for coherent response selection and stronger impact (p. 16).
- Government/UN/donor policy alignment and coordination platform strategies are explicit Step 3 inputs (p. 33).
- Cash working groups and sector coordination resources are recommended as practical decision inputs (p. 16).

# DRR implications
- URAF promotes resilience-oriented response design by combining immediate needs support with complementary measures that address systemic risk factors (pp. 3, 13).
- The framework references Hyogo priorities as relevant to urban resilience and risk reduction integration (p. 13).
- Monitoring design includes attention to conflict-sensitive indicators and urban emergency-trigger indicators to detect evolving risk conditions (p. 46).

# Operational implications
- URAF is intended for use before targeting, after needs/vulnerability assessment, during programme identification/preparedness phases (p. 18).
- Target users are programme managers and technical specialists using a workshop format; estimated completion time is 4-6 hours (ideally over two days) when data are available (p. 18).
- Cash should not be assumed feasible by default; feasibility analysis and delivery mechanism assessment are required (pp. 10, 36, 42).
- Outputs should be documented to strengthen accountability, evaluation, and learning (p. 18).

# Claims worth citing
- "The URAF aims to support the identification of appropriate multi-sector responses for urban programmes" and endorses MPGs alongside complementary sector-specific responses (p. 3).
- Response analysis should be informed by appropriateness and feasibility while minimizing harmful side effects (p. 8).
- Cash responses may not be appropriate in all contexts or for all assisted populations (p. 10).
- URAF should be applied before targeting and after needs/vulnerability assessment in PCM programming phases (p. 18).
- Step 6 requires monitoring of needs, preferences, risks, market/pricing, acceptance, and capacity to maintain response appropriateness (p. 46).

# Limitations / cautions
- The document notes that evidence on multi-sector approaches and vulnerability assessments is still evolving and guidance should be iteratively updated (p. 7).
- URAF is an analytical support framework; outcomes depend on quality and sufficiency of assessment/context data (pp. 22, 33).
- Needs severity scores are indicative and should not be treated as definitive without triangulation and community validation (p. 29).
- Some sector scores (education, ICLA, protection/governance) should be interpreted separately from immediate basic-needs sectors (pp. 29, 49).
