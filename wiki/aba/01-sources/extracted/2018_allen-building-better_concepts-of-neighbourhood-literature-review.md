# Summary
This working paper is a structured literature review on neighbourhood concepts, developed to support the “Shaping Places: Future Neighbourhoods” research programme. It aims to provide a reference frame for subsequent research and explain why neighbourhood concepts matter for urban outcomes (pp. 3, 25).

The review synthesizes how neighbourhood has been defined across urban studies and policy literatures, finding persistent conceptual plurality. It identifies a broad pattern: neighbourhoods are treated as spatial units, social constructs, or socio-spatial combinations of both (pp. 5, 59).

Methodologically, the review groups prior studies into three major streams: observational/theoretical approaches, perception/satisfaction surveys and interviews, and mapping/GIS-based approaches. It also examines New Zealand policy/strategy usage and highlights definitional gaps in local policy language (pp. 4, 25, 37, 53).

# Key concepts
- Neighbourhood conceptual plurality: no single, stable definition dominates; usage is context-dependent and often contested (pp. 5, 59).
- Spatial vs social vs socio-spatial neighbourhood: the review’s core typology for organizing findings (p. 59).
- Resident perception and neighbourhood satisfaction: subjective assessments are central in many empirical studies and often complement objective measures (pp. 27, 59).
- Boundary ambiguity: resident-defined neighbourhood boundaries frequently diverge from administrative boundaries (pp. 37, 53).
- Neighbourhood as policy object: New Zealand planning documents often reference neighbourhood in operational terms without robust conceptual definitions (p. 53).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Structured literature review methodology with narrative synthesis and embedded abstract/reference text boxes for usability (pp. 3, 25).
- Method grouping framework: theoretical/observational, survey/interview (revealed preferences/satisfaction), and mapping/GIS studies (pp. 4, 25, 27, 37).
- Heuristic neighbourhood-definition approaches (for action where definitions are unclear) referenced from prior planning literature (p. 25).
- Mixed-method socio-spatial analysis approaches combining subjective and objective indicators (pp. 37, 59).
- NZ policy scan approach linking conceptual literature to applied planning language and strategy references (pp. 53, 60).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Scoping/diagnosis stage: helps frame what “neighbourhood” means before design or targeting decisions (pp. 25, 59).
- Assessment design stage: provides methodological options for defining and studying neighbourhood boundaries and functions (pp. 25, 37).
- Evidence synthesis stage: supports comparative interpretation of objective indicators and resident perceptions (pp. 27, 59).
- Policy alignment stage: useful for checking how local plans operationalize neighbourhood concepts and where conceptual gaps exist (pp. 53, 60).

# Field data collection implications
- Neighbourhood research should match method choice to conceptual intent (spatial, social, or socio-spatial) (pp. 25, 59).
- Resident-defined boundary mapping can surface divergence from census/administrative units and reduce proxy bias (p. 37).
- Survey/interview approaches on neighbourhood perception/satisfaction capture subjective dimensions often missed by administrative data (p. 27).
- Mixed methods (subjective + objective + GIS) are highlighted as increasingly useful for socio-spatial analysis and policy comparison (p. 59).

# Coordination implications
- The review implies cross-disciplinary coordination is needed between planning, sociology, and policy analysis to avoid narrow definitions (pp. 5, 59).
- It highlights practical tension between top-down administrative neighbourhood definitions and citizen understandings (p. 53).
- For policy use, neighbourhood analysis should coordinate conceptual framing with local governance/planning scales (pp. 53, 60).

# DRR implications
- Direct DRR guidance is limited in this source; contribution is conceptual and methodological rather than hazard-specific.
- Indirectly, the socio-spatial framing and boundary analysis can strengthen area definition in risk/vulnerability assessments where neighbourhood scale is used (pp. 37, 59).
- Resident-defined boundary mapping methods that surface divergence from administrative units reduce the risk of systematically excluding high-vulnerability populations from area-based risk assessments that rely on administrative proxies for neighbourhood boundaries.
- The three-stream method typology (observational, survey/interview, mapping/GIS) maps onto DRR assessment design: observational approaches suit hazard/exposure mapping; survey/interview methods capture social vulnerability and perceived risk; GIS enables integration of physical and social risk layers.
- Mixed socio-spatial methods (combining subjective and objective indicators) align with established DRR practice, where household-level survey data alone may miss informal settlement dynamics and perception-only data lacks spatial precision for targeting.

# Operational implications
- Attempting a universal neighbourhood definition may be less productive than context-specific empirical investigation (p. 59).
- Policy and programme design should account for both resident perceptions and physical/planning structures (pp. 53, 59).
- Practitioners should treat “neighbourhood” as an analytical construct that may vary by purpose, population, and scale (pp. 25, 59).
- The review is intended as a reusable reference tool for future research outputs, not a prescriptive implementation manual (p. 3).

# Claims worth citing
- The review is designed as a reference frame for subsequent neighbourhood research and policy-relevant analysis (p. 3).
- Research on neighbourhood concepts generally falls into spatial, social, and socio-spatial categories (p. 59).
- Attempts to produce one stable neighbourhood definition have repeatedly faced difficulty due to conceptual ambiguity and context variation (p. 59).
- Mixed methods combining subjective and objective measures are increasingly used to understand neighbourhood as socio-spatial (p. 59).
- In New Zealand policy and strategy, explicit conceptual definitions of neighbourhood are often absent (p. 53).

# Limitations / cautions
- The review itself notes conceptual ambiguity and that neighbourhood boundaries/meanings vary by context and users (pp. 53, 59).
- It is a literature synthesis, not a primary empirical dataset or intervention evaluation (pp. 3-4).
- The NZ-focused policy scan highlights definitional inconsistency, limiting direct transferability of any single conceptual model (p. 53).
