## Summary

This document is a practical toolbox for housing associations to design integrated neighbourhood development strategies that combine physical and socio-economic interventions (pp. 1, 5, 7). It was developed through EFL collaboration among Dutch and German housing actors **— the European Federation for Living (EFL) working group on Social Integration, chaired by Chrétien Mommers (director of Eigen Haard) and including members from VIVAWEST, Havensteder, GEWOBAG, and De Woonplaats** — based on field experience in several cities **including Amsterdam, Berlin, and Gelsenkirchen** (p. 5).

The toolbox proposes a four-step framework: align with corporate goals, analyze and select neighbourhoods, design neighbourhood strategy, and implement coordinated interventions (p. 9). A core principle is "Basics first": prioritize the most urgent, foundational neighbourhood issues first, while working with other stakeholders **— "Essential to our approach is the 'Basics first'-concept which may help housing companies to distinguish problems according to need and responsibility felt by housing companies to fulfil a significant role"** (pp. 10-11).

Methodologically, it provides a two-stage diagnosis process: (1) quick scan across all neighbourhoods using three performance fields (attractiveness of property, liveability/safety, social-economic situation), then (2) in-depth analysis of selected areas using five domains linked to the Basics First Triangle **— the five domains are: Living and Renting; Safety and Care; Employment, Economics, and Education; Participation in Society and Integration to Enhance Social Cohesion; and Sports, Culture, Art** (pp. 12-13).

The toolbox also includes cases, practical do's/don'ts, and an intervention catalogue, plus appendices with example indicator sets and scoring sheets for both quick scan and in-depth analysis **— featuring six detailed case studies from Rotterdam, Berlin, Enschede, and Amsterdam demonstrating practical application of the framework** (pp. 41-51).

**The framework is explicitly presented as a "do it yourself" resource requiring contextual adaptation**: **"The tools are not prêt-a-porter. It always takes effort and willingness from your organisation to adjust them to your own situation"** (p. 5).

## Key concepts

**Integrated neighbourhood development**: Coordinated physical + social-economic strategy across multiple actors **— recognizing that "well maintained houses are not the only requirement to guarantee the quality of neighbourhoods" and that European neighbourhoods face "different kinds of social-economic problems" requiring multi-party collaboration** (pp. 5, 7).

**Corporate-goal alignment**: Neighbourhood interventions should add value to long-term strategic objectives and financial sustainability **— "When you start working on a neighbourhood strategy, it's important to relate your measures and the expected output to the strategic effects. Do they match with your corporate goals? Do they add value?"** (p. 10). **This principle ensures that social-economic investments are linked to core business benefits: tenant satisfaction, reduced vacancy, improved property values, and long-term neighbourhood stability** (pp. 8, 10).

**Basics First concept**: Rank problem domains by urgency and by role/responsibility, addressing basic needs first (pp. 10-11). **The framework explicitly states that the five problem areas are ranked in two ways: (1) The extent the housing company can influence the problems (their role and responsibility, large or small); and (2) How urgent the distinguished problems are for inhabitants** (p. 10). **"We cannot solve all problems in our worst neighbourhoods. We need to create focus at a strategic level"** (p. 8).

**Two-stage diagnosis model**: Quick scan for prioritization, then deep analysis for agenda-setting and strategy design **— "To deal efficiently and effectively with integrated information on numerous neighbourhoods within our property we designed two research steps"** (pp. 12-13). **The quick scan ranks all neighbourhoods with traffic-light labels (red/orange/green), while in-depth analysis identifies specific problems and sets the neighbourhood "agenda" for strategy design** (pp. 12-13).

**Coordinated intervention logic**: Use standardized analysis plus customized neighbourhood action packages **— "This method and analysis is a well thought way to have a standardised way of working. Because the characteristics of each neighbourhood differ, an intervention is always different as well"** (p. 9). **Coordination at company level is critical because interventions span multiple departments and external partners** (p. 9).

**Multi-actor delivery**: Interventions are implemented with municipalities, welfare/social organizations, police, schools, and community actors **— "A lot of European neighbourhoods deal with different kinds of social-economic problems. In order to solve these problems many parties are involved, such as: local government, health care and housing companies"** (pp. 5, 9, 41-47).

**Well-performing neighbourhoods as strategic goal**: **"Housing companies benefit themselves from well performing neighbourhoods. These neighbourhoods are in general popular among people and attract business, which attracts more people making the neighbourhood even more popular. This has also a positive effect on dwellings. They are easy to rent out and in case of a sale strategy: your housing stock is increasing in value"** (p. 10).

**Civil society responsibility**: **"The concept of 'Civil society' is getting more important. It means that people, who live and work in a neighbourhood, have a (huge) responsibility for their community as well. That implicates that everyone has to take his or her role and responsibility to make their neighbourhood liveable"** (p. 8).

## Tools or methods extracted

**4-step neighbourhood approach framework**: Goal → Analysis → Strategy → Implementation **— provides end-to-end planning model structured as: (1) Corporate strategy and strategic goals; (2) Quantitative and qualitative research and analysis for neighbourhood selection and problem diagnosis; (3) Strategy design using Basics First concept; (4) Programming instruments for effective intervention** (p. 9).

**Quick-scan ranking method**: Score all neighbourhoods on 3 performance fields and label with traffic-light categories (red/orange/green) **— Red = Need for intensive, integrated neighbourhood approach; Orange = Need to solve specific problems (projects); Green = Continue regular business processes** (p. 13). **"The idea is to rank the neighbourhoods according to their condition... In theory the red neighbourhoods are so-called multi-problem areas"** (p. 13).

**In-depth diagnostic template**: Assess selected neighbourhoods with mixed quantitative + qualitative indicators across 5 domains **— uses the five Basics First Triangle domains (Living and Renting, Safety and Care, Employment/Economics/Education, Participation/Integration, Sports/Culture/Art) as analytical framework** (pp. 13, 49-51). **"We include both quantitative and qualitative information. The aim is to further identify the problems from an integral perspective"** (p. 13).

**Basics First Triangle**: Prioritization framework that links thematic urgency with actor role/responsibility **— visually represented as a triangle with five levels, where lower levels indicate less housing company responsibility and greater responsibility for other parties** (pp. 11-12). **"The first and most basic level is living and renting. And so on. Each lower level means a less important role and responsibility of the housing company in solving problems of that kind. In other words: in these domains the responsibility of other parties increases"** (p. 11).

**Intervention/instrument library**: Practical examples (e.g., child-friendly neighbourhood, district facilities, screening tenants, youth/sports programs, neighbourhood economy tools) **— documented through six detailed case studies: Project Plan De Hoeken (Rotterdam), Programme Creating a Child Friendly Neighbourhood (Rotterdam), We Are Active Boxing and More (Berlin), Charrette Heart of the Neighbourhood (Enschede), Go Ahead! Alive and Kicking Neighbourhood Projects (Amsterdam), and Schillerpark Radical Change (Oer-Erkenschwick)** (pp. 41-47).

**SWOT analysis for strategy validation**: **"Make a SWOT (Strength, Weakness, Opportunities and Threat) analysis. The exercise of making a SWOT is necessary and the basis to determine the efficiency of the various instruments and which problems the housing company wants to solve and – most important – can solve"** (p. 16). **The framework structures SWOT as: internal (strengths/weaknesses) vs. external (opportunities/threats), and useful for goals vs. harmful** (p. 16).

**Strategy field inquiry checklist**: **Seven domains for gathering strategy development information: Target groups, Image of the neighbourhood, Social activities, Structural measures (physical interventions), Neighbourhood/surroundings, Cooperation/integration (with tenants, institutions, entrepreneurs), Relation between local and bigger issues** (p. 16).

**Feasibility and desirability check**: **Four-criteria filter for longlist ideas: Added value (business), Costs, Negative impact (ecological or other), SMART criteria (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, Time-bound)** (p. 16).

**Performance field indicators**: **Three fields with specific indicators — Attractiveness of property (waiting time for rental, number of reactions per advertised dwelling, satisfaction with house, appreciation of technical condition/maintenance); Liveability and safety (satisfaction with neighbourhood liveability, expected development perception, safety index objective, safety index subjective); Social-economic situation (household income, unemployment benefits as % of population 15-64 years)** (p. 13).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Portfolio prioritization**: Identify which neighbourhoods need integrated intervention most urgently **— "The result of this research and analysis phase will be that we have selected the neighbourhoods that need an integrated approach most"** (pp. 12-13).

**Baseline diagnosis**: Establish neighbourhood agenda and problem profile before strategy design **— "We have also identified the major problems that have to be addressed. As a matter of fact: the neighbourhood 'agenda' is set as important input to the next phase, the design of a neighbourhood strategy"** (p. 13).

**Strategy formulation**: Translate diagnosis into coordinated neighbourhood strategy using Basics First priorities **— "The in-depth analysis of the five Basics First Triangle domains helps us to focus on the most problematic areas and role and responsibilities of the housing company and that of other parties"** (pp. 9, 13). **Strategy development requires "an interactive and creative process to look for effective interventions" involving local experts** (p. 14).

**Implementation planning**: Assign intervention instruments and cooperating parties by domain/problem **— case studies demonstrate practical implementation across domains: Communities That Care (De Hoeken), child-friendly neighbourhood programme (Oude Noorden), boxing club (Mehringplatz Berlin), charrette process (Velve-Lindenhof Enschede), student volunteer programme (SUHA Amsterdam)** (pp. 41-47).

**Monitoring/learning**: Use indicator sheets and repeated scoring as a base for measuring effects over time **— the framework provides standardized scorecards for both quick scan and in-depth analysis enabling longitudinal tracking** (pp. 12, 48-51).

## Field data collection implications

Collect standardized indicators for three quick-scan fields: property attractiveness, liveability/safety, and social-economic status **— "Look for available indicators that provide information on the 3 key performance fields"** (p. 13).

Combine organization data with municipal/police/tax and other external sources **— "Check which information is already available from the City Council, the Tax department, the Police, or own research"** (p. 14). **The example scoring card shows data sources including: own business information, own research, joined research, and statistics municipality** (p. 13).

Use both quantitative and qualitative information in in-depth analysis to avoid narrow metric bias **— "We include both quantitative and qualitative information. The aim is to further identify the problems from an integral perspective"** (p. 12). **"It is necessary, if not very much needed, to involve local experts for they know the specific situation, the neighbourhood and the people in the neighbourhood"** (p. 14).

Maintain domain-specific indicator sets to support comparability across neighbourhoods **— appendices provide template indicator sheets for both quick scan (3 performance fields) and in-depth analysis (5 Basics First domains)** (pp. 49-51).

**Scoring methodology**: **Three-point scale for quick scan indicators: +1 (above average), 0 (average score of municipality), -1 (below average) — aggregated across all indicators to produce total neighbourhood score enabling ranking** (p. 13).

**Indicator availability constraint**: **"The availability of information in your own organisation is important. If there is a lack of availability or no capacity within the organisation to develop them, it is an impediment to get the instrument(s) implemented"** (p. 14).

## Coordination implications

Integrated neighbourhood work requires joint action among housing companies, local government, care/welfare actors, and community stakeholders **— "Together with the local community they work on safety, poverty and better circumstances to get a more liveable neighbourhood"** (p. 5).

The model explicitly supports role clarification and shared responsibility discussions with partners **— the Basics First Triangle "may help housing companies to distinguish problems according to need and responsibility felt by housing companies to fulfil a significant role" and facilitates discussion about "which problem to solve first? Who is going to be in charge of solving that problem and why?"** (pp. 10-11).

Coordinated management at company level is presented as critical because interventions are distributed across departments and external actors **— "For a good result and the success of the invention of the housing company it is desirable to keep the coordination on company level. The different instruments, that are part of the intervention, are being performed by different colleagues at various departments but also by other parties"** (p. 9).

Several instrument cases show shared or rotating leadership rather than single-actor control **— the De Hoeken case demonstrates "more than ten organisations work intensively together"; the child-friendly neighbourhood involves "parties in the area of welfare and health, sport and recreation, childcare and community work... The involved parties see each other as partners"; the charrette process involved "local authority, three housing companies, local residents and a spectrum of partners in the field of education, care and welfare, culture and sport"** (pp. 41-47).

**Decision-maker alignment**: **"Step 4 and 5 are steps where the organisation is needed. Despite the good intentions a plan is not a good plan when the ideas are not shared and incorporated. Colleagues who support the plan are necessary and more: decision makers have to face the same direction"** (p. 17).

## DRR implications

The toolbox is not a DRR manual, but its integrated diagnosis-prioritization-coordination model is transferable to urban risk-reduction programming.

**The "Basics First" concept maps directly onto DRR triage logic**: Address foundational life-safety and exposure-reduction measures first, before more complex resilience investments, using the same urgency-and-role-scoping criteria the toolbox describes (pp. 10-11). **The dual prioritization logic (urgency for affected populations + actor role/responsibility) provides a framework for distinguishing life-saving DRR interventions (housing company/humanitarian core mandate) from longer-term resilience measures (shared responsibility with government/development actors)**.

**The two-stage diagnosis model** (quick scan → in-depth analysis) **provides a valid and replicable structure for ABA neighbourhood prioritisation in DRR contexts**, particularly where resources are limited and multiple areas compete for attention simultaneously (pp. 12-13). **The traffic-light scoring approach (red/orange/green across performance fields) is directly adaptable for combined multi-hazard/vulnerability scoring in DRR assessments**, with hazard exposure, social vulnerability, and service fragility substituting or augmenting the EFL's original three fields.

**The multi-actor coordination model** — housing companies, municipality, care/welfare, police, community — **mirrors the multi-stakeholder design requirements of ABA in DRR** (NGOs, local government, technical agencies, communities), validating joint delivery over single-actor models (pp. 5, 9, 41-47). **The Basics First Triangle's explicit role/responsibility mapping addresses the same coordination challenge DRR programmes face: clarifying who leads which intervention type based on mandate, capacity, and proximity to affected populations**.

**Corporate-goal alignment principle applies to humanitarian DRR programming**: **The framework's insistence that neighbourhood interventions "add value to long-term strategic objectives and financial sustainability" (p. 10) translates to DRR as: interventions should align with organizational mandate, donor priorities, and sustainable exit strategies — not mission creep or indefinite presence**.

**The in-depth diagnostic template's five-domain structure** (Living and Renting, Safety and Care, Employment/Economics/Education, Participation/Integration, Sports/Culture/Art) **can be adapted for urban DRR vulnerability assessment** by mapping to: Shelter and HLP, Protection and WASH, Livelihoods and Markets, Social Cohesion and Governance, Community Infrastructure and Services.

**The SWOT analysis step** (p. 16) **provides a structured decision framework for DRR programme design** — assessing internal strengths/weaknesses (organizational capacity, sectoral expertise, local access) and external opportunities/threats (government partnerships, funding windows, political constraints, security risks) before committing to area-based intervention.

Direct hazard/disaster technical protocols are not specified in this source; supplement with DRR-specific risk assessment frameworks (e.g., HEVC risk mapping, Sendai-aligned indicators) for a complete operational approach.

## Operational implications

Start with clear strategic intent and boundaries of organizational role before neighborhood-level action **— "Before starting to think about the neighbourhoods and problems to solve, it's wise to give some thought about the corporate strategy of your company. Which strategic goals are formulated to realize"** (p. 10).

Use a repeatable analytic pipeline to avoid ad hoc intervention selection **— the four-step framework (Goal → Analysis → Strategy → Implementation) provides standardization while "an intervention is always different" based on neighbourhood characteristics** (pp. 9, 12-13).

Adapt indicator sets to local context and data availability, but keep core field coverage intact **— "Every organisation should be able to choose specific indicators, as long as they do indicate the condition of the performance field. The availability of information in your own organisation is important"** (p. 14).

Expect interventions to be custom-made by neighbourhood while still using standard analytical scaffolding **— "Every neighbourhood is different and has it own characteristics. So, when an intervention is necessary, a standard approach is not the solution. It has to be a customised approach"** (p. 9).

**Invest time in organizational buy-in**: **"Especially because it takes a couple of years to develop and fine-tune the framework and method"** — the Eigen Haard case notes the framework was developed from 2007 and finalized in 2010, then "fully incorporated in their organisation" (p. 17).

**Engage local experts early**: **"It is necessary, if not very much needed, to involve local experts for they know the specific situation, the neighbourhood and the people in the neighbourhood. Question these experts"** — they provide historical context, actor dynamics, and intervention feasibility insights (p. 14).

**Three process tips for strategy development**: **(1) Involve people who can think out of the box, with positivity; (2) Ask open questions — not "how to..." but "what if...", what, where, when; (3) Don't jump into solutions** (p. 15).

**Test ideas against SMART criteria**: **Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, Time-bound** — before moving from longlist to shortlist (p. 16).

## Limitations / cautions

Practice-oriented guide based on partner experience; not a controlled comparative evaluation study. **The toolbox is "created for housing associations" and reflects "knowledge and experiences about the way they improve and change the hoods" — it documents practice, not experimental research** (p. 5).

Designed for housing-association context; adaptation may be required for other institutional mandates. **The framework assumes housing company core business (property management, rental, renovation) as foundation — humanitarian/development organizations would need to translate the corporate-goal alignment and role/responsibility logic to their mandates** (pp. 8-10).

Indicator choice is context-dependent and data availability can constrain implementation quality **— "The availability of information in your own organisation is important. If there is a lack of availability or no capacity within the organisation to develop them, it is an impediment to get the instrument(s) implemented"** (p. 14).

**Strong European/Western European context**: **The framework reflects Dutch and German housing association models, national welfare systems, and regulatory environments — direct application in Global South contexts, informal settlements, or post-conflict settings would require significant adaptation**.

**Limited treatment of conflict, displacement, or rapid-onset crises**: **Case studies address chronic urban decline, not sudden shocks — DRR/humanitarian users should supplement with crisis-specific assessment and response frameworks**.

**The Basics First Triangle ranking is debatable**: **"The working group had in particular discussion about level 3 and 4" (Employment/Economics/Education vs. Participation/Integration) — organizations may sequence priorities differently based on mandate and context** (p. 11).

**Time investment requirement**: **Eigen Haard took 3 years (2007-2010) to develop, fine-tune, and fully incorporate the framework — organizations should plan for multi-year adoption timeline** (p. 4, 17).

## Known tensions

**Standardization vs. customization**: **The framework advocates "a standardised way of working" while insisting "an intervention is always different" and warning against "standard approaches"** (pp. 9, 14) — users must balance analytical consistency with neighbourhood-specific adaptation.

**Housing company responsibility vs. multi-actor dependency**: **The Basics First Triangle ranks domains by housing company role/responsibility (pp. 10-11), but "we cannot solve all problems" and "other parties take theirs [responsibilities] as well" (p. 8) — the framework does not resolve how to proceed when other actors fail to deliver their share**.

**Business logic vs. social mission**: **Neighbourhood interventions must "add value to long-term strategic objectives and financial sustainability" (p. 10), yet address "poverty and better circumstances" in "the poorest areas" (pp. 5, 19) — the framework does not specify how to handle cases where social need and financial return diverge**.

**Top-down analysis vs. bottom-up participation**: **The quick scan and in-depth diagnostic are centralized analytical processes (pp. 12-13), but strategy development "must involve local experts" and "local community involvement is crucial" (pp. 14, 24-25) — the framework does not prescribe when and how to transition from expert-led diagnosis to participatory strategy design**.

**Efficiency vs. inclusiveness**: **The traffic-light triage (red/orange/green) enables "focus and set priorities" (p. 8), but risks leaving "orange" and "green" neighbourhoods without intervention — the framework does not address equity concerns or political backlash from de-prioritized areas**.

**Indicator availability vs. analytical rigor**: **"Every organisation should be able to choose specific indicators" based on data availability (p. 14), but comparability and validity require consistent measurement — the framework allows flexibility that may undermine cross-neighbourhood or cross-organization benchmarking**.

**Short-term projects vs. long-term neighbourhood development**: **Case studies show multi-year engagement (Communities That Care started 2007, framework finalized 2010, p. 19), but funding cycles, political timelines, and organizational priorities often favor short-term interventions — the framework does not address sustainability or exit strategy planning**.
