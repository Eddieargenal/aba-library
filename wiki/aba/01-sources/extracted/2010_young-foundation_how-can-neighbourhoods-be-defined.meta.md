## Summary

This paper, produced by the Young Foundation as part of the _Transforming Neighbourhoods_ research and innovation consortium (2006–2008), is a systematic review of what is and is not known about neighbourhoods and communities. It analyses the characteristics of neighbourhoods, including how they are defined, the role of the local public realm in structuring neighbourhood life, and the approaches and lessons for community empowerment and governance. The consortium brought together UK government departments, the Home Office, the Joseph Rowntree Foundation, and 15 local authorities. Although originating in a UK policy context, the document's analytical framework — distinguishing geographic, social, and public realm dimensions of neighbourhood — is directly applicable to ABA design, area selection, community engagement methodology, and governance integration in humanitarian and development programming. It is particularly valuable for grounding the conceptual foundations of what a "neighbourhood" actually is before designing a place-based intervention.[](https://youngfoundation.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/How-can-neighbourhoods-be-understood-and-defined-August-2010.pdf)

## Key Concepts

**Neighbourhood (Three-Lens Definition)**  
The document proposes that no single definition of neighbourhood is adequate and that a working definition for governance and programming should combine three lenses:

- _Geographic neighbourhood_ — administratively bounded units (wards, data zones, catchment areas) providing spatial consistency and data comparability but often diverging from lived experience
    
- _Social neighbourhood_ — self-defined units based on residents' mental maps, interaction patterns, and shared identities; rooted in lived experience but complex, overlapping, and potentially exclusionary
    
- _Local public realm_ — the ensemble of shared physical and civic spaces (parks, schools, health centres, third spaces, civic media) that provides a practical bridge between administrative geography and personal identification
    

The three lenses are explicitly framed as complementary, not competing. A working definition "could combine geographic and social characteristics, be drawn together by the local public realm, and permit layering and overlapping where appropriate."

**Administrative Geography vs. Personal Identification**  
A core analytical distinction: two dominant but divergent approaches to neighbourhood definition with fundamentally different policy implications. Administrative geography (wards, Super Output Areas, data zones) offers simplicity, data stability, and universal coverage — but may not correspond to lived realities and can range from 100 to 300,000 people. Personal identification (self-defined communities) grows from popular demand and local challenges, is more likely to be cohesive, but risks complexity, competing claims, social exclusion of marginal groups, and reinforcing social divides rather than bridging them. Neither is sufficient alone.

**Local Public Realm**  
A named structural concept and the document's most distinctive analytical contribution. The local public realm is defined as the ensemble of neighbourhood spaces open to people, comprising four elements:

1. _Local public spaces_ — streets, parks, squares, estate areas
    
2. _Local public services and civic centres_ — primary schools, community centres, health surgeries, places of worship, transport facilities, leisure centres
    
3. _Third spaces_ — cafes, pubs, shops as informal social infrastructure
    
4. _Civic media and the local public sphere_ — local newsletters, noticeboards, community radio, civic websites, neighbourhood maps
    

The local public realm is positioned as both a _diagnostic framework_ (where it is derelict or absent, neighbourhood life is weak) and a _prescriptive framework_ (investing in it is the primary mechanism for neighbourhood transformation). It bridges the gap between administrative geography and personal identification by grounding both in shared physical and civic anchor points.

**Neighbourhood Scale Layers**  
Neighbourhood is not a single scale — it is a nested, overlapping set of layers. Two functional scales are identified:

- _Proximity neighbourhood (100–1,500 people)_ — street, block, or estate level; personal neighbourliness, face-to-face interaction, mutual recognition; management functions work well at this scale; aligned with anthropological evidence that human coordination capacity is limited above 500–1,500 people
    
- _Strategic neighbourhood (5,000–10,000 people)_ — sufficient scale to contain a primary school, park, health surgery, shops, library; common issues requiring a more strategic response; aligned with walking-distance service access logic
    

The two scales are explicitly described as complementary. Programming that conflates them — using strategic-scale boundaries for proximity-level engagement, or proximity-scale boundaries for service-level planning — will produce misfit interventions.

**Overlapping Neighbourhood**  
A resident simultaneously belongs to multiple neighbourhoods defined by different functions and scales: housing tenure committee, neighbourhood watch, school catchment, leisure centre catchment, faith community. These overlapping realities challenge precisely bounded, mutually exclusive administrative geography as a governance basis. The document argues that neighbourhood governance should be organised around _centres and dynamics_ rather than fixed borders — and that co-operation between overlapping neighbourhoods, or enabling non-residents to participate in governance, are legitimate responses to boundary complexity.

**Neighbourliness as Indicator**  
Neighbourliness — defined as "friendliness, helpfulness, and respect for privacy" — is positioned as a measurable proxy for neighbourhood health and a stepping-stone to local collective action. It is the social disposition that enables reciprocity and collective action. Critically: "deprivation and poverty do not necessarily make a neighbourhood into a 'bad' one so long as social cohesion and feelings of neighbourliness prevail." This dissociates neighbourhood quality from physical conditions alone — directly relevant to ABA programming in informal settlements.

**Neighbourhood Attributes Framework (Galster 2001 / Power et al. 2004)**  
A named multi-dimensional taxonomy of neighbourhood attributes cited from the literature, spanning from physical to social:

- Environmental — topography, pollution
    
- Proximity — location, transport infrastructure
    
- Buildings — type, design, material, density, repair
    
- Infrastructure — roads, streetscape
    
- Demography — age profile, class, ethnic diversity, population mobility
    
- Services — existence and quality of local services
    
- Social-interactive — friend/family networks, local associations, informal interactions, social control mechanisms
    
- Sentiment — place identification, historical significance, local stories
    
- Political — local parties, political networks, resident involvement
    

This taxonomy is directly applicable as a diagnosis framework for ABA area profiling — mapping a neighbourhood across all nine attribute dimensions before designing interventions.

**Community Empowerment and Neighbourhood Governance**  
The document's policy application layer: "Done well, the devolution of powers to the very local level can improve the quality of decision-making and levels of citizen engagement, helping to rebuild legitimacy, make bureaucracies more responsive and develop new sources of social value. Done badly, it can lead to conflict, growing inequalities and failing public services." This risk framing directly parallels ABA's Do No Harm logic — neighbourhood empowerment is not inherently positive; it requires deliberate design, equity screening, and governance architecture.

**Neighbourhood Leadership**  
A named functional requirement for effective neighbourhood governance: neighbourhood leadership is distinct from formal political representation and from community consultation. It requires people who can bridge between residents, local institutions, and external actors — and who can sustain collective action beyond single-issue campaigns. The absence of neighbourhood leadership is identified as a structural gap in many empowerment initiatives.

**Governance Models for Neighbourhood (Typology)**  
Five governance model types are identified in the literature:

1. _Neighbourhood forums_ — open participatory assemblies for neighbourhood issues
    
2. _Neighbourhood panels_ — representative consultative bodies
    
3. _Neighbourhood boards_ — decision-making governance structures with delegated authority
    
4. _Parish/town councils_ — statutory local governance units with legal powers and sometimes tax-raising authority
    
5. _Tenant management organisations_ — sector-specific (housing) governance models
    

Each has different implications for accountability, authority, and sustainability — directly relevant to ABA governance integration and handover architecture design.

## Tools or Methods Extracted

**Three-Lens Neighbourhood Definition Framework** _(Stage 1 — Program strategy / Stage 3 — Community engagement)_  
The geographic/social/public realm typology provides a structured analytical framework for area selection and boundary justification. Applied at Stage 1 to determine which lens should dominate area definition for a given program context; at Stage 3 to design engagement methodology that reflects the actual neighbourhood experience of residents rather than administrative boundaries.

**Neighbourhood Attributes Taxonomy (Galster/Power)** _(Stage 3 — Area profile and diagnosis)_  
The nine-dimension attribute taxonomy (environmental through political) provides a structured checklist for ABA area profiling. Each dimension corresponds to a data collection domain — enabling systematic multi-source diagnosis rather than ad hoc assessment. Directly applicable as a structuring framework for the Area Profile and Diagnosis stage.

**Neighbourhood Scale Decision Tool** _(Stage 1 — Area selection)_  
The proximity (100–1,500) / strategic (5,000–10,000) scale typology provides a practical decision framework for selecting the appropriate scale of ABA intervention. Proximity-scale interventions are suited to face-to-face community engagement and neighbourhood management. Strategic-scale interventions are suited to service-level planning, infrastructure programming, and governance integration.

**Local Public Realm Diagnostic Framework** _(Stage 3 — Diagnosis / Stage 7 — Design)_  
The four-element public realm typology (public spaces, public services/civic centres, third spaces, civic media) provides a diagnostic checklist for mapping neighbourhood assets and gaps. Where public realm elements are derelict or absent, this signals both a vulnerability and a programming opportunity. Directly applicable to infrastructure prioritization, community gathering point design, and service integration strategy.

**Neighbourhood Governance Architecture Reference** _(Stage 5 — Area strategy / Stage 8 — Implementation)_  
The five governance model typology (forums, panels, boards, parish councils, tenant organizations) provides a reference framework for designing neighbourhood-level governance and handover structures. Different models are appropriate for different contexts, scales, and authority levels — selection should be explicit and justified, not defaulted.

## Project Lifecycle Relevance

**Stage 1 — Program strategy and area selection:**  
The three-lens definition framework and scale typology provide the analytical basis for justifying area boundary selection. The distinction between proximity and strategic scale is essential for matching intervention type to area size — a common source of ABA misfit. The document's emphasis that "it would be inappropriate to impose a single meaning of neighbourhood" grounds context-specific boundary justification as a methodological requirement.

**Stage 3 — Community and stakeholder engagement:**  
The social neighbourhood concept, mental map analysis, and neighbourliness indicator provide the theoretical grounding for participatory engagement design. The document's finding that two-thirds of people express strong neighbourhood identity and belonging — but one-third (disproportionately younger and higher-status) identify more with communities of interest than place — has direct implications for who is engaged, how, and through which channels.

**Stage 5 — Integrated area strategy:**  
The overlapping neighbourhood concept and governance model typology provide the analytical basis for designing area-level coordination and governance architecture. The document's insight that neighbourhood governance should be organized around centres and dynamics, not fixed borders, grounds a flexible approach to area-level coordination design.

**Stage 7 — Detailed technical design:**  
The local public realm framework provides a design vocabulary for physical and service interventions — prioritizing public spaces, civic centres, and third spaces as the infrastructure of neighbourhood life. The Galster/Power attribute taxonomy structures multi-sector intervention design across all relevant neighbourhood dimensions.

**Stage 9 — Monitoring, learning, and adaptation:**  
Neighbourliness as an indicator provides a qualitative proxy for neighbourhood health that complements quantitative output metrics. The document's finding that deprivation does not determine neighbourhood quality so long as social cohesion prevails grounds a social cohesion monitoring dimension in ABA MEL frameworks.

## Field Data Collection Implications

- **Mental map methodology:** The document references the Future Foundation's method of asking residents to describe their neighbourhood, circle it on a map, and list defining characteristics — a directly applicable field data collection technique for participatory boundary justification and area profiling at Stage 3
    
- **Administrative data gap:** Super Output Areas, ward data, and census data provide a geographic baseline but do not capture lived neighbourhood experience — field teams should not rely solely on administrative data for area profiling; community-based mapping must complement it
    
- **Multi-scale data collection:** The proximity/strategic scale distinction requires data collection at two levels — individual/household interaction networks (proximity scale) and service/infrastructure use patterns (strategic scale) — which may require different methods and different informant types
    
- **Public realm asset mapping:** The four-element public realm typology translates directly into a field checklist: mapping existing public spaces, civic centres, third spaces, and local communication channels as part of the area profile
    
- **Social cohesion indicators:** Neighbourliness (reciprocal interactions, collective action willingness, mutual support) can be assessed through key informant interviews, observation, and participatory ranking — providing a social dimension to complement physical and service assessments

## Coordination Implications

**Neighbourhood governance architecture must be designed, not assumed:**  
The document's five governance model typology (forums, panels, boards, parish councils, tenant management organisations) establishes that neighbourhood-level governance takes multiple forms with different authority levels and accountability structures. ABA programs must design the governance architecture explicitly — selecting the appropriate model for the context, scale, and program objectives — rather than defaulting to informal community consultation as the only participation mechanism.

**Overlapping boundaries require co-operative governance design:**  
Since residents simultaneously belong to multiple overlapping neighbourhoods defined by different functions, ABA coordination mechanisms must be designed to operate across overlapping jurisdictions, not only within fixed geographic boundaries. Co-operation between adjacent neighbourhood governance structures, or enabling non-residents to participate in specific governance decisions, are named as legitimate coordination mechanisms.

**Local public realm as coordination anchor:**  
Public buildings, parks, schools, and civic centres are the natural coordination points where neighbourhood governance is grounded and where cross-sector service integration is most practically achieved. ABA coordination architecture should use these existing civic anchor points as meeting and accountability venues rather than creating new parallel structures.

## DRR Implications

- **Neighbourhood quality is not determined by deprivation:** Social cohesion and neighbourliness can maintain neighbourhood resilience even under severe resource constraints — this directly supports ABA's asset-based approach to DRR programming in informal settlements and crisis contexts
    
- **Local public realm as DRR infrastructure:** The public realm elements — parks, schools, community centres, civic communication channels — are simultaneously the social infrastructure of daily neighbourhood life and the functional infrastructure of disaster preparedness, early warning communication, and emergency response
    
- **Neighbourhood scale and DRR function:** Proximity-scale (100–1,500) neighbourhoods are the appropriate unit for community-level DRR — early warning dissemination, evacuation coordination, mutual aid — while strategic-scale (5,000–10,000) neighbourhoods are more appropriate for service-level DRR planning, infrastructure investment, and governance integration
    
- **Community empowerment risk framing:** The document's explicit warning that neighbourhood empowerment "done badly can lead to conflict, growing inequalities and failing public services" directly parallels ABA's Do No Harm logic — poorly designed neighbourhood governance can increase rather than reduce vulnerability

## Claims Worth Citing

> _"There is no consensus answer to the question, 'What is a neighbourhood?'"_ (p.9) — Grounds the methodological necessity of explicit, justified boundary definition in ABA design.

> _"It would be inappropriate to impose a single meaning of neighbourhood, but our working definition for neighbourhood governance structures could combine geographic and social characteristics, be drawn together by the local public realm, and permit layering and overlapping where appropriate."_ (p.17) — The document's core definitional conclusion; grounds context-specific boundary justification.

> _"Understanding of the scales, characteristics and dynamics of neighbourhoods is necessary if we are to set a framework for targeted and effective action. Otherwise we run the risk of public disappointment and policy failure."_ (p.9) — Grounds the investment in neighbourhood analysis before programming.

> _"Studies show that deprivation and poverty do not necessarily make a neighbourhood into a 'bad' one so long as social cohesion and feelings of 'neighbourliness' prevail."_ (p.15) — Grounds asset-based, social cohesion–first approach to neighbourhood programming; challenges deficit framing.

> _"Done well, the devolution of powers to the very local level can improve the quality of decision-making and levels of citizen engagement... Done badly, it can lead to conflict, growing inequalities and failing public services."_ (p.4) — Grounds the risk logic for neighbourhood empowerment; parallels ABA Do No Harm requirement.

> _"Neighbourhood is a compelling human metaphor, often used to domesticate the unfamiliar. Neighbourhoods can and do vary in size."_ (p.15) — Grounds scale relativity; warns against fixed-size assumptions in ABA boundary setting.

> _"Residents of a community may find themselves simultaneously located within a network of neighbourhoods of different scales and types."_ (p.16) — Grounds the overlapping neighbourhood concept; supports flexible boundary design.

> _"It is worth asking whether precisely bounded and mutually exclusive zones of administrative geography provide the most constructive basis for governing overlapping realities."_ (p.17) — Directly challenges ward/administrative boundary use as default ABA area unit.

## Limitations / Cautions

- **UK policy context:** The document was produced for a UK government policy programme. Governance models (ward councils, parish councils, Super Output Areas, tenancy management organisations) are UK-specific. Application to humanitarian or Global South contexts requires translation — the analytical frameworks are transferable but the institutional examples are not
    
- **Urban/suburban bias:** The document draws primarily on UK urban and suburban experience. Application to humanitarian informal settlement contexts — high transience, fluid social networks, absent formal governance, multi-hazard environments — requires explicit adaptation, particularly for the governance models typology and the local public realm diagnostic
    
- **Social cohesion focus may underweight power dynamics:** The document's emphasis on neighbourliness and cohesion as indicators of neighbourhood health risks underweighting internal power asymmetries, elite capture, and exclusion dynamics — which are typically more pronounced in crisis and displacement contexts than in stable UK neighbourhoods
    
- **2006/2010 vintage:** Predates the digital transformation of local public spheres (social media as primary local communication channel), the urbanization acceleration of the 2010s, and the post-2015 humanitarian urban response evidence base. The civic media section requires significant updating for current ABA application
    
- **No measurement instruments:** The document identifies neighbourliness and public realm quality as indicators but does not provide standardized measurement tools or scales — field teams must develop context-specific instruments
