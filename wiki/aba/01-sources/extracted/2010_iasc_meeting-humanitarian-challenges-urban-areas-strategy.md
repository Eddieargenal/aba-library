# Summary

This strategy document was approved by the IASC Task Force on Meeting Humanitarian Challenges in Urban Areas in Rome, November 2010, following a set of case studies from Nairobi, Eldoret, Manila, and Port-au-Prince. Its principal aim is "to recommend actions which humanitarian actors can take to make their responses to humanitarian crises in urban areas more effective and thereby save more lives and accelerate early recovery". The strategy establishes four foundational elements for urban humanitarian response: (1) strengthened multi-stakeholder partnerships; (2) host country national and local leadership; (3) preparedness planning and vulnerability analysis; (4) community resilience and beneficiary targeting. It addresses the unique coordination, programming, and protection challenges of cities — including population heterogeneity, density, complex governance, multi-actor stakeholder environments, and the central role of community networks in delivering protection, shelter, services, and livelihoods in crisis. The strategy is a foundational inter-agency reference for area-based coordination and cluster-municipal alignment in urban response design.

# Key concepts
**Urban Humanitarian Response (Definitional Frame)**  
Urban crises are structurally distinct from rural and camp-based emergencies. Key differentiating characteristics include: population heterogeneity and cultural/linguistic diversity; density and dynamism; physical and geographic scale; range of stakeholders and political interests; and multi-hazard, multi-actor governance environments. Standard humanitarian tools and approaches developed for rural or camp settings require explicit adaptation — the strategy was motivated precisely by the recognition that the humanitarian system was systematically underperforming in urban settings.

**Four Strategic Strengthening Elements**  
The framework's primary analytical structure for improving urban humanitarian response:

- _Partnerships:_ Multi-stakeholder operational strategies for enhanced coordination and effectiveness
    
- _Host country national and local leadership:_ Primary responsibility of national and local government; humanitarian actors as complement, not substitute
    
- _Preparedness and vulnerability understanding/analysis:_ Pre-crisis investment in urban assessment, mapping, and analysis capacity
    
- _Community resilience and beneficiary targeting:_ Recognition that urban dwellers rely heavily on community networks for protection, housing, services, and livelihoods
    

- **Community as Primary Protection Architecture**  
	In all four case study cities (Nairobi, Eldoret, Manila, Port-au-Prince), "urban dwellers relied heavily on the community setting for their protection, housing, access to basic services and support for their livelihoods". The challenge was to develop knowledge of these communities and tap their strengths — not to bypass them with external parallel delivery. This positions the community network as the primary protective and service architecture, not a beneficiary population.

- **Multi-Stakeholder Partnership Architecture**  
	Urban response requires "operational strategies that ensure multi-stakeholder partnerships for enhanced coordination, impact and effectiveness" from the earliest phase. This goes beyond inter-agency coordination — it includes national government, local authorities, civil society, local NGOs, community-based organizations, private sector actors, and urban planning institutions. No single agency mandate covers the full scope of urban humanitarian need.

- **Municipal and Local Government Primacy**  
	The strategy explicitly positions national and local government leadership as foundational — not optional or aspirational. Humanitarian actors are framed as partners supporting and complementing government systems, not substituting for them. This directly grounds the Neighbourhood Approach principle that formal systems are the destination, not a parallel track.

**Protection in Urban Crisis**  
"The security and protection needs of affected urban populations constitute one of the most significant urban challenges." Post-crisis environments are characterized by increased urban violence as populations compete for scarce resources — food, water, shelter. At the same time, national and local government capacity tends to become overstretched by IDP arrivals. Protection is not a sector add-on — it is a structural feature of the urban crisis environment.
**Livelihoods as Early Recovery Priority**  
Restoration of livelihoods and economic opportunities is explicitly named as a priority "starting in the emergency phase for expedited early recovery". Urban livelihoods are embedded in market systems, employment networks, and economic corridors that are disrupted by crisis — and that cannot wait for a "recovery phase" to be addressed.
**Preparedness as Structural Investment**  
"Build preparedness into humanitarian assistance policies for more effective emergency responses that save more lives and livelihoods". The strategy frames preparedness not as a contingency plan but as a structural investment in understanding vulnerability and building response capacity before crises occur — directly linking to DRR logic.
**Urban Assessment Gap**  
A named systemic failure: the humanitarian sector lacks "impartial, regular and timely needs assessments and collective coherent analysis to enable effective prioritisation of appropriate responses" in urban settings. Urban assessment is more complex than rural or camp assessment — beneficiary identification, heterogeneous needs, multi-actor stakeholder environments, and the absence of clear geographic boundaries all present methodological challenges. The strategy calls for development, testing, and dissemination of urban-specific assessment tools.[](https://www.rescue.org/sites/default/files/document/1214/10796iied.pdf)

**Peri-Urban Camp Critique**  
"The establishment of peri-urban camps without a clear strategy to develop permanent settlements is contributing to chaotic urban sprawl". Camps in urban peripheries are explicitly critiqued as a failure mode — they produce urban sprawl, informal settlement proliferation, and long-term governance problems without a resolution pathway. Relocation may conform to planning regulations but requires urgent strategy for permanent settlement development.[](https://www.fmreview.org/zetter-deikun/)

# Tools or methods extracted

**Multi-Stakeholder Partnership Framework** _(Stage 5 — Integrated area strategy / Stage 8 — Implementation)_  
Operational guidance for designing multi-actor partnerships in urban settings, covering national government, local authorities, civil society, local NGOs, and the private sector. Directly applicable to ABA stakeholder mapping and coordination design at Stages 5 and 8.[](https://www.citiesalliance.org/newsroom/news/urban-news/interagency-task-force-proposes-strategy-humanitarian-action-urban-areas)

**Urban Vulnerability and Preparedness Analysis Framework** _(Stage 3 — Community engagement / Stage 5 — Area strategy)_  
The strategy frames preparedness as requiring systematic pre-crisis investment in urban vulnerability analysis and mapping. Provides the inter-agency rationale for building urban assessment capacity and collecting baseline data on hazard, vulnerability, and community resilience before crises occur.[](https://www.fmreview.org/zetter-deikun/)

**Cluster System Adaptation Reference** _(Stage 5 — Coordination design)_  
The strategy provides the foundational inter-agency justification for adapting the cluster system to urban contexts — moving from sector-siloed coordination to multi-stakeholder, area-based coordination architectures. Cross-reference with the IASC Reference Module for Cluster Coordination (2015) for updated operational guidance.[](https://reliefweb.int/report/world/reference-module-cluster-coordination-country-level-revised-july-2015)

**Early Recovery Livelihoods Integration Model** _(Stage 7 — Technical design / Stage 8 — Implementation)_  
Positions livelihoods restoration as an emergency-phase activity, not a post-emergency one. Provides the inter-agency policy basis for integrating livelihoods programming into emergency response design in urban settings from day one.[](https://www.citiesalliance.org/newsroom/news/urban-news/interagency-task-force-proposes-strategy-humanitarian-action-urban-areas)
# Project lifecycle relevance

- **Stage 1 — Program strategy:**  
	The four strategic strengthening elements (partnerships, local leadership, preparedness, community resilience) provide the inter-agency policy framework for scoping urban response strategy. The strategy's framing of community networks as the primary protection architecture grounds an asset-based area selection logic.

- **Stage 3 — Community and stakeholder engagement:**  
	The community resilience and beneficiary targeting element directly grounds participatory engagement design. The emphasis on understanding community networks and tapping their strengths (not bypassing them) aligns with ABA diagnosis methodology.

- **Stage 5 — Integrated area strategy:**  
	Multi-stakeholder partnership architecture and the cluster adaptation guidance provide the inter-agency coordination framework for area-level strategy design. Municipal leadership principle grounds formal system integration at strategy stage, not only at handover.

- **Stage 7 — Detailed technical design:**  
	Livelihoods-as-emergency-priority grounds early recovery integration in technical design. Protection as structural urban feature grounds Do No Harm screening requirements across all technical interventions.

- **Stage 8 — Area-based implementation:**  
	Multi-stakeholder coordination mechanisms, technical surge capacity guidance, and municipal engagement principles are directly applicable to implementation architecture. The peri-urban camp critique informs settlement and displacement response design.

# Field data collection implications
- **Urban assessment gap is a named systemic problem:** the strategy calls for development, testing, and dissemination of urban-specific assessment tools — field teams should not assume rural or camp assessment methodologies transfer directly[](https://www.rescue.org/sites/default/files/document/1214/10796iied.pdf)
    
- **Beneficiary identification complexity:** urban heterogeneity, mobility, and the absence of clear geographic boundaries make conventional beneficiary enumeration unreliable — multi-method approaches combining secondary data, community mapping, and key informant interviews are implied
    
- **Community network mapping:** the strategy's emphasis on understanding community networks as protective architecture implies a specific data collection requirement — mapping existing community-based organizations, mutual aid networks, and informal leadership structures before designing response
    
- **Multi-stakeholder actor mapping:** urban environments involve a range of stakeholders and political interests not present in rural or camp settings — actor mapping at assessment stage is essential, not optional[](https://www.rescue.org/sites/default/files/document/1214/10796iied.pdf)
    
- **Food security and livelihoods ahead of other sectors:** the strategy notes that food security and livelihoods tools are more developed for urban contexts than other sectors — field teams should draw on urban food security assessment tools as the most mature urban-specific methodology available
# Coordination implications
- **Municipal primacy as coordination architecture:**  
	National and local government hold primary responsibility for urban humanitarian response. The humanitarian coordination architecture — clusters, inter-agency working groups — must be designed to support and complement municipal systems, not substitute for them or create competing governance structures.

- **Cluster system requires urban adaptation:**  
	The cluster approach as designed for rural and camp settings does not map cleanly onto urban coordination complexity. Urban coordination requires: multi-stakeholder inclusion beyond UN/INGO, active engagement of municipal actors, area-based sub-national coordination where geographic concentration warrants it, and cross-cluster coordination mechanisms reflecting the interconnected nature of urban needs.

- **Generic Terms of Reference for urban coordination functions:**  
	The IASC reference group on humanitarian challenges in urban areas created generic terms of reference for a number of coordination functions specifically for urban contexts. These ToRs provide the inter-agency basis for designing area-based coordination mechanisms, and should be cross-referenced when designing Stage 5 coordination architecture.

- **Local NGO and civil society as primary operational partners:**  
	Urban humanitarian response depends on local civil society and community-based organizations in ways that field-to-headquarters humanitarian pipelines do not capture. The strategy positions local actors as structural partners — not implementing subcontractors — in the urban response architecture.
# DRR implications
- **Preparedness as structural investment:** The strategy explicitly builds preparedness into humanitarian assistance policy — pre-crisis vulnerability analysis, community resilience investment, and preparedness planning are named strategic priorities, not optional add-ons
    
- **Community resilience as primary DRR vehicle:** The community resilience and beneficiary targeting element positions community networks as the foundational layer of urban DRR — consistent with Twigg's framework and the Neighbourhood Approach's capacity-first logic
    
- **Prevention dimension acknowledged but underdeveloped:** The strategy addresses preparedness and early recovery but provides limited operational guidance on prevention and risk reduction as distinct from emergency response — a known gap for urban DRR programming
    
- **Peri-urban camp critique as DRR logic:** The explicit critique of peri-urban camps as producing chaotic urban sprawl and long-term governance problems applies DRR thinking to displacement response — avoiding solutions that create new risk concentrations

# Limitations / cautions
- **Age (2010):** Coordination architecture references reflect pre-Transformative Agenda (2011) and pre-Sendai (2015) governance structures. The IASC Transformative Agenda (2011) and Reference Module for Cluster Coordination (2015) substantially updated the cluster system logic — cross-reference required for current application[](https://reliefweb.int/report/world/reference-module-cluster-coordination-country-level-revised-july-2015)
    
- **Pre-Syria, pre-Ukraine:** The case studies (Nairobi, Manila, Port-au-Prince) are drawn from a narrow set of crisis types. The strategy predates the large-scale urban displacement crises in Syria (2011–), Ukraine (2022–), and Gaza (2023–) that have significantly advanced the evidence base for urban humanitarian response
    
- **Operational depth limited:** The strategy is a high-level inter-agency policy document, not an operational manual. It establishes priorities and principles but provides limited operational guidance for field-level implementation — cross-reference with ALNAP GPR 12 (2019), IRC Urban Context Analysis Toolkit (2017), and the Global Shelter Cluster ABA Compendium (2019) for operational depth
    
- **Cluster system urban adaptation underdeveloped:** The strategy acknowledges that the cluster system requires urban adaptation but does not provide a detailed alternative architecture. The 2026 Standard ToR for Area-Based Coordination should be cross-referenced for current guidance
    
- **Livelihoods assessment tools gap:** The strategy notes that livelihoods tools are ahead of other sectors but still underdeveloped for urban post-conflict contexts specifically — a gap that remains relevant in current programming[](https://www.rescue.org/sites/default/files/document/1214/10796iied.pdf)
    
- **Limited guidance on informal settlements:** The strategy addresses urbanization and displacement but provides limited specific guidance on informal settlement contexts — where the majority of urban poor and displaced populations live and where ABA programming is most needed
