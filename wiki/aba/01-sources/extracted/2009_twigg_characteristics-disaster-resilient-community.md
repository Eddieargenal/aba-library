# Summary

**Twigg, J. (2007/2009). _Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community: A Guidance Note_ (Version 1 / Version 2). DFID DRR Interagency Coordination Group / Benfield UCL Hazard Research Centre.**  

This guidance note by John Twigg, developed for the DFID DRR Interagency Coordination Group, presents a detailed framework of the characteristics of a disaster-resilient community organized across five thematic components. It is widely used in humanitarian and DRR programming to design community engagement activities, technical interventions, and monitoring indicators. The framework connects community-level resilience to broader DRR systems including governance, knowledge, risk assessment, and preparedness, and was originally developed to operationalize the Hyogo Framework for Action (HFA) at community level — filling a recognized gap in community-scale measurement that national-level ISDR/OCHA indicators did not address.
# Key concepts
**Resilience (Three-Capacity Definition)**  
Defined operationally across three dimensions: (1) capacity to absorb stress or destructive forces through resistance or adaptation; (2) capacity to manage or maintain basic functions and structures during disastrous events; (3) capacity to recover or "bounce back" after an event. Resilience is explicitly framed as the inverse of vulnerability — two sides of the same coin — and as the _outcome_ that DRR processes are working toward.

**Five Thematic Components of Resilience**  
The framework's primary organizing structure, derived from the HFA:

- _Thematic Area 1 — Governance:_ Policy, planning, political commitment, legal and regulatory systems, integration with development, institutional mechanisms, partnerships, accountability and community participation
    
- _Thematic Area 2 — Risk Assessment:_ Hazard/risk data and assessment, vulnerability and impact data, scientific and technical capacities
    
- _Thematic Area 3 — Knowledge and Education:_ Public awareness, information management and sharing, education and training, cultures/attitudes/motivation, learning and research
    
- _Thematic Area 4 — Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction:_ Environmental/natural resource management, health and well-being, sustainable livelihoods, social protection, financial instruments, physical protection, planning regimes
    
- _Thematic Area 5 — Disaster Preparedness and Response:_ Organizational capacities and coordination, early warning systems, preparedness and contingency planning, emergency resources and infrastructure, emergency response and recovery, participation and voluntarism
    

**Enabling Environment**  
A named structural concept: the wider institutional, policy, and socio-economic factors _outside_ the community that enable or disable local resilience — national DRR policy, local government commitment, legal frameworks, external service providers. Distinguished analytically from community characteristics to avoid confusing community-level action with system-level preconditions. A "disabling environment" is explicitly acknowledged when key enabling conditions are absent.

**Characteristics vs. Indicators**  
A precise conceptual distinction: characteristics describe an _ideal end state_ of resilience in qualitative terms; they are not project-level indicators. Some characteristics correspond to outcome indicators (end states), others to output indicators (activities that must be completed). Project teams must translate selected characteristics into context-specific measurable indicators for MEL use.

**Community (Complexity Definition)**  
Community is defined beyond spatial grouping — it encompasses common interests, values, activities, and structures. Communities are explicitly non-homogeneous: differences in wealth, gender, status, and social networks must be mapped. A community is not isolated — its resilience is co-determined by external services, infrastructure, and governance linkages.

**Invulnerable Development**  
A referenced concept (McEntire 2000): development intentionally designed to reduce vulnerability and raise resilience to disaster — not standard development with DRR added on. Offered as a framing device for selecting which characteristics are most relevant to a given program.

# Tools or methods extracted

- **Community Resilience Characteristics Tables** _(Stage 3 — Community engagement)_  
	Five thematic tables, each structured with three columns: (1) Components of resilience; (2) Characteristics of a resilient community; (3) Characteristics of an enabling environment. Used for baseline studies, vulnerability and capacity analysis, and participatory assessment design. Selection of relevant characteristics requires community validation — not top-down imposition.

- Components of Resilience Scoping Matrix** _(Stage 3 — Community engagement)_  
	A one-page matrix listing all five thematic areas and their sub-components. Used as a rapid mapping or scoping tool to identify which resilience dimensions are currently addressed by which agencies, and to surface gaps and potential collaboration areas in multi-stakeholder settings.

- Community-Based DRR Design Tables** _(Stage 7 — Detailed technical design)_  
	The full characteristics tables function as a design reference: practitioners select characteristics relevant to their context and translate them into project-specific technical interventions. Governance (Thematic Area 1) is explicitly flagged as a cross-cutting theme that applies regardless of which other thematic areas a program focuses on.

-Community Resilience Indicator Set** _(Stage 9 — MEL)_  
	Characteristics are convertible to indicators via a two-step process: (1) select characteristics appropriate to context and project type; (2) translate qualitative characteristics into measurable project-specific indicators, which may be quantitative or qualitative. The framework provides no fixed quantitative thresholds — these are set contextually. Milestones (intermediate steps toward full characteristics) are introduced as a practical MEL device.

# Project lifecycle relevance

- Stage 3 — Community and stakeholder engagement:**  
	The characteristics framework and scoping matrix support participatory assessment design, helping teams identify resilience assets and gaps across all five thematic areas. The framework's explicit instruction to select characteristics _with_ communities — not for them — anchors it to participatory methodology.

- Stage 7 — Detailed technical design:**  
	The DRR design tables guide technical intervention design aligned with community resilience goals. Governance (Thematic Area 1) must be cross-referenced regardless of sector focus. Physical protection and planning regimes (Thematic Area 4) are directly relevant to shelter and settlement design.

- Stage 9 — Monitoring, learning, and adaptation:**  
	The indicator set provides a structured path from qualitative characteristics to measurable project indicators. The characteristic/indicator distinction guards against conflating ideal-state descriptions with project targets. Milestones provide intermediate reference points for adaptive MEL.

# Field data collection implications
The framework does not prescribe a fixed data collection method — it is explicitly a resource, not a manual. Implied methods include:

- **Participatory vulnerability and capacity assessments (VCA):** The framework cross-references VCA as a primary field tool; hazard/risk assessment characteristics explicitly require participatory process including all community sections
    
- **Community scoring and validation:** Qualitative judgments about whether characteristics have been achieved must be reached through transparent, participatory, collective processes — not externally imposed
    
- **Multi-stakeholder scoping workshops:** The Components of Resilience matrix is suited to facilitated multi-agency mapping sessions
    
- **Quantitative sub-indicators:** Permitted at project level (e.g., % households with insurance, number of volunteers trained) as inputs to broader qualitative judgments — not as universal benchmarks
# Coordination implications

**Governance as cross-cutting theme:** Thematic Area 1 is explicitly positioned as foundational — policy, planning, institutional mechanisms, partnerships, and accountability apply to every other thematic area and to every DRR initiative regardless of sector. Municipal alignment, decentralization of DRR responsibilities, and local government policy and strategy are all named components.

**Multi-agency gap analysis:** The scoping matrix is specifically designed for multi-stakeholder settings to surface which organizations are covering which resilience dimensions and where collective gaps exist — directly supporting inter-agency coordination and division of labor.

**Enabling environment assessment:** Larger organizations operating across multiple communities are advised to conduct enabling environment assessments to inform advocacy — particularly where disabling conditions (absent policy, hostile governance, weak legal frameworks) constrain local-level resilience work.
# DRR implications

Strong HEVC (Hazard-Exposure-Vulnerability-Capacity) logic throughout:

- **Hazard awareness:** Thematic Area 2 — comprehensive community hazard/risk assessments, participatory, shared with all stakeholders, feeding into disaster planning, with ongoing monitoring
    
- **Vulnerability reduction:** Thematic Area 4 — livelihoods, health, social protection, financial instruments, physical protection, environmental management, land-use planning regimes
    
- **Capacity strengthening:** Thematic Area 3 — public awareness, education, training, information sharing; Thematic Area 1 — institutional capacity, partnerships
    
- **Preparedness:** Thematic Area 5 — early warning systems, contingency planning, organizational coordination, emergency resources, voluntarism
    
- **HFA alignment:** All five thematic areas map directly to HFA Priority Areas 1–5, making the framework compatible with Sendai Framework successor logic

# Claims worth citing
"The 'disaster-resilient community' is an ideal. No community can ever be completely free of natural and man-made hazards." (p. 7) — Grounds realistic goal-setting; counters perfectionist or output-only program logic.

"A focus on resilience means putting greater emphasis on what communities can do for themselves and how to strengthen their capacities, rather than concentrating on their vulnerability to disaster or their needs in an emergency." (p. 6) — Core asset-based framing; directly supports Neighbourhood Approach capacity-first logic.

"DRR is the responsibility of development and relief agencies alike and it should be an integral part of the way such organisations do their work, not an add-on or one-off action." (p. 6) — Counters bolt-on DRR design; supports integration requirement.

"The characteristics will be most useful (and most used) when they are selected by, or at least with, those who need to use them." (p. 10) — Participatory selection requirement; cross-links to Communities as Architects concept.

"Communities do not exist in isolation. The level of a community's resilience is also influenced by capacities outside the community." (p. 7) — Grounds the Enabling Environment concept; counters community-only framing.
# Limitations / cautions
- **Rural/semi-urban origin:** The framework was developed primarily through desk study and expert consultation oriented toward rural and semi-urban community contexts. Application in dense urban settings — informal settlements, multi-tenure environments, polyglot communities — requires explicit adaptation, particularly for Thematic Areas 1 (governance complexity), 2 (multi-hazard urban risk), and 4 (land and housing)
    
- **Version 1 (2007) age:** Coordination architecture references (HFA, ISDR, OCHA indicator systems) reflect pre-Sendai governance structures. The 2009 Version 2 (UCL) partially updates this; the 2017 Sendai Framework successor logic is not reflected
    
- **Non-prescriptive MEL:** The absence of fixed quantitative thresholds is intentional but creates challenges for donors requiring standardized indicators across programs — project teams must invest in contextualized indicator development
    
- **Enabling environment treated lightly:** The tables acknowledge the enabling environment but provide less analytical depth than community-level characteristics — a known gap for programs operating in disabling governance contexts
    
- **Community homogeneity risk:** Despite explicitly defining communities as non-homogeneous, the tables' formatting can inadvertently suggest a single "community" voice; facilitators must actively disaggregate by gender, tenure, disability, and socioeconomic status
