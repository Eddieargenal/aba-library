# Summary
This ALNAP case study examines how PCI’s Barrio Mio (Guatemala City) and Katye (Port-au-Prince) projects used a neighbourhood approach to work in complex urban contexts, with emphasis on practical adaptations rather than theory alone (pp. 3, 6, 25, 32).

Barrio Mio is presented as an urban DRR strategy (not only a project) designed to identify, pilot, and scale risk-reduction and upgrading solutions in high-risk informal settlements through multi-stakeholder convening (p. 17).

The case study documents six core ways these projects navigated complexity: context-informed action, interconnected multi-sector design, convening/facilitation, community-led practice, iterative pilot-and-scale learning, and long-term DRR-recovery linkage (pp. 32-46, 59).

# Key concepts
- Neighbourhood approach (area/settlement approach): area-based, community-informed, multi-sector response framed around local social/economic/physical realities (pp. 29-30).
- Urban complexity navigation: recognition of interdependencies across tenure, governance, services, livelihoods, and risk, requiring adaptive rather than linear programming (p. 32).
- Convenor role: PCI’s shift from direct implementer toward facilitator of municipal/community/private-sector replication (p. 37).
- Pilot-and-scale logic: start small, test, adapt with evidence, then replicate through local partners for wider reach (pp. 42, 59).
- Long-term accountability in humanitarian action: linking short-term response to longer-term upgrading and resilience outcomes (pp. 46, 59-60).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Case study methodology: literature review, internal/external evaluation review, field interviews/FGDs, coding, and appreciative enquiry orientation (p. 6).
- Stakeholder convening model: engagement across government, academia, private sector, and communities with incentives-based coordination (pp. 17, 32, 37).
- Demonstrate-and-replicate operational model across phased implementation (p. 17).
- Participatory data and planning methods (including participatory enumeration and community-led decision processes) (pp. 40, 42).
- Iterative adaptation approach supported by evidence and donor-enabled flexibility (pp. 42, 46).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Assessment/design: strong context analysis and stakeholder mapping before/during intervention shaping (pp. 6, 32).
- Early implementation: demonstration communities used for testing integrated interventions (p. 17).
- Scale-up phase: municipal and partner replication of piloted practices with reduced direct implementer footprint (pp. 17, 37).
- Ongoing adaptation: iterative modifications based on field evidence and changing urban realities (p. 42).
- Learning/strategy feedback: explicit key takeaways and further-study questions for transferability (pp. 58-60).

# Field data collection implications
- Mixed qualitative methods (interviews + FGDs + site visits) supported contextual understanding beyond standard needs snapshots (p. 6).
- Participatory data generation improved local ownership and practical design fit (pp. 40, 42).
- Iterative evidence use was central for adapting plans as assumptions changed in dynamic settings (p. 42).
- Monitoring systems struggled to capture full integrated effects where outcomes extended beyond pre-set indicators (p. 60).

# Coordination implications
- Multi-actor coordination is core: projects convened national/local authorities, communities, private sector, and technical actors (p. 17).
- Success depended on relationship-building and alignment of timelines/principles/methodologies across partners (p. 42).
- Working as facilitator/convenor increased indirect reach and partner-driven implementation at scale (p. 37).
- Case notes persistent coordination friction with actors using conventional sectoral methods, especially in emergency settings (p. 49).

# DRR implications
- Barrio Mio is explicitly framed as DRR with aims to reduce risk, improve response, and build safer, healthier, more resilient neighbourhoods (p. 17).
- The projects integrate preparedness, mitigation, and response in the same neighbourhood systems rather than treating them separately (pp. 17, 46).
- The case emphasizes preventive upgrading over reactive displacement/greenfield relocation where feasible (p. 59).
- DRR effectiveness is linked to municipal/community capacity development and longer-term institutionalization (pp. 46, 59-60).

# Operational implications
- Neighbourhood approaches require flexible design/funding and tolerance for iterative adaptation under uncertainty (pp. 37, 42).
- Integrated multi-sector work can be constrained by donor and bureaucratic sector silos (pp. 49, 60).
- Measuring impact is difficult when acting through replication partners and when outcomes are systemic/relational, not only physical outputs (p. 60).
- Organisational culture (risk-taking, staff autonomy, innovation mindset) is presented as a decisive enabling factor (p. 56).

# Claims worth citing
- Barrio Mio is a DRR project/strategy using a neighbourhood approach across multiple municipalities and stakeholders (p. 17).
- The neighbourhood approach is described as area-based, community-informed, and multi-sector, with long-term recovery/planning linkage (pp. 29-30).
- Both projects navigated urban complexity through iterative, evidence-based, and community-led processes (pp. 32, 40, 42, 59).
- Convening/facilitating local actors (rather than only direct implementation) was central to scale and sustainability (p. 37).
- Key operational barriers include resistance to non-traditional approaches, sector silos, and measurement challenges (pp. 49, 60).

# Limitations / cautions
- The study is explicitly not an impact evaluation; it is an appreciative case study focused on “what worked” (p. 6).
- Transferability is unresolved for settings with weak/non-functioning governance and different crisis phases (p. 58).
- There is risk of replacing sector silos with area silos if geographic approaches are poorly designed (p. 58).
- Monitoring frameworks may under-represent systemic outcomes in adaptive, multi-actor programmes (p. 60).
