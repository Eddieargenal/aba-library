## Summary

This ALNAP working paper argues that humanitarian actors still struggle to understand and respond effectively in urban crises, despite increasing urban caseloads and growing policy attention (pp. 6, 8-10, 52). **Authored by Leah Campbell and published in 2016 as part of ALNAP's urban humanitarian response research initiative**, the paper positions this as a practical performance problem: responses can become disconnected from context, undermine local systems, and miss opportunities to build on existing capacities (pp. 18-20) [web:49][web:51][web:53].

The paper proposes systems thinking as a useful lens for understanding urban complexity. It defines a systems approach as focusing on linkages and interrelationships, not only isolated components, and stresses that urban systems are dynamic, interconnected, and often unpredictable (pp. 22-25). **"A systems approach shifts the analytical focus from isolated 'things' to the connections between them — from components to relationships, from snapshots to patterns over time, from linear causation to feedback loops"** (pp. 22-23).

A core contribution is a typology of five urban systems: economy and livelihoods; politics and governance; social and cultural; infrastructure and services; and space and settlements (pp. 25-31). **Each system is analyzed through its stocks (resources, assets, institutions), processes (flows, relationships, transactions), functions (purposes served), and events (disruptions, shocks, changes)** (pp. 26-31). The paper then translates systems thinking into seven practical principles for humanitarian analysis and design, including multi-scale analysis, attention to interconnections, and spatial awareness without rigid geographic exclusion (pp. 33-41).

It also reviews current humanitarian practice and identifies barriers to deeper urban understanding at individual, institutional, and problem-structure levels, concluding that tools are necessary but insufficient without broader shifts in mindset, organization, and coordination (pp. 45-53). **"Humanitarian urban understanding remains 'piecemeal' and the systems 'big picture' is still missing... Tools alone cannot solve the urban challenge; organizational culture, incentives, coordination structures, and individual capacity must also change"** (pp. 45, 50-53).

## Key concepts

**Urban as continuum**: Urban contexts are better treated as a continuum with "fuzzy boundaries" rather than a fixed binary with rural (pp. 12, 14). **"Rather than trying to definitively classify places as 'urban' or 'rural', it may be more useful to think about urban characteristics existing on a continuum. Settlements exhibit urban characteristics to greater or lesser degrees"** (p. 14).

**Urban density, diversity, dynamics**: These three characteristics help explain why urban contexts are harder to interpret and navigate in crises (pp. 14-17). **Density refers to concentration of people, assets, infrastructure, and activities in limited space; Diversity encompasses social, economic, institutional, and physical heterogeneity; Dynamics captures rapid change, mobility, and system volatility** (pp. 14-17).

**Systems approach**: Systems are interconnected elements bounded in structures; analysis must focus on interrelationships as well as components (pp. 22-23). **"A system is a set of elements or parts that is coherently organized and interconnected in a pattern or structure that produces a characteristic set of behaviours, often classified as its 'function' or 'purpose'"** (p. 22). **The paper emphasizes that systems exhibit emergent properties — behaviors that arise from interactions among components but cannot be predicted by studying components in isolation** (p. 23).

**Stocks, flows, feedback loops**: A practical way to break down system behavior and avoid premature assumptions (pp. 24-25). **Stocks are accumulations or inventories (water in reservoirs, money in banks, skills in populations); Flows are rates of change in stocks (water consumption, financial transactions, education enrollment); Feedback loops are circular causal relationships that can reinforce (amplify) or balance (stabilize) system behavior** (pp. 24-25).

**Five-system urban typology**: Economy/livelihoods, governance, social/cultural, infrastructure/services, and space/settlements (p. 25). **"This typology is proposed as a practical analytical framework, not a claim that cities actually function as five discrete systems. In reality, urban systems overlap, interact, and cannot be cleanly separated — but structured analysis requires some organizing framework"** (p. 25).

**Multi-scale analysis**: Meaningful understanding requires analysis from household to neighborhood to city and beyond (pp. 36-37). **"Urban systems operate at multiple scales simultaneously — household, building, street, neighborhood, district, city, metropolitan region, national, and global. Interventions at one scale create effects at others. Effective urban response requires explicitly analyzing across scales, not defaulting to a single analytical level"** (pp. 36-37).

**Spatial focus without geographic restriction**: Useful area focus should not ignore wider system linkages across boundaries (p. 40). **"Geographic targeting and spatial analysis are valuable for urban programming, but rigid boundaries can obscure critical dependencies — water systems cross districts, livelihoods depend on citywide markets, social networks span neighborhoods. Spatial focus is useful; spatial isolation is dangerous"** (p. 40).

**Leverage points**: **Places in systems where small interventions can produce disproportionate effects — identifying leverage points requires understanding system structure, not just symptoms. Examples include changing information flows, shifting power relationships, or altering feedback loop strengths** (pp. 23-24).

**System resilience**: **The capacity of a system to absorb shocks while maintaining function, adapt to change, and transform when necessary — distinct from component-level robustness. Urban resilience depends on diversity, redundancy, modularity, and adaptive capacity across interconnected systems** (pp. 35-36).

## Tools or methods extracted

**Urban context criteria checklist**: Broad criteria for identifying urban contexts (density, services/infrastructure, cash economy, boundaries, etc.) (p. 12). **Figure 1 lists characteristics including: population density and size; built environment density; economic activities (markets, formal/informal employment); infrastructure and services (utilities, transport, health, education); administrative status; and fuzzy boundaries with peri-urban zones** (p. 13).

**Systems decomposition method**: Map stocks, flows, and feedback loops for selected urban functions (pp. 24-25). **"For any urban function or problem, identify: What are the key stocks (resources, capacities, institutions)? What flows connect them (goods, money, information, people)? What feedback loops regulate behavior (reinforcing cycles that amplify change, balancing cycles that resist change)?"** (pp. 24-25).

**Five-system diagnostic typology**: Structure context analysis around the five proposed urban systems and their interactions (pp. 25-31). **Tables 1-5 provide detailed breakdowns for each system: (1) Economy and livelihoods — employment, markets, financial systems, income sources, economic inequality; (2) Politics and governance — formal/informal institutions, power relationships, decision-making, service delivery; (3) Social and cultural — identity, networks, cohesion/tension, norms, displacement dynamics; (4) Infrastructure and services — WASH, energy, transport, health, education, communications; (5) Space and settlements — land use, housing, public space, environmental hazards, urban planning** (pp. 26-31).

**Seven-principle systems lens**: Practical analysis frame **(1) Acknowledge you are working with systems; (2) Accept uncertainty and plan for adaptation; (3) Track change and resilience over time; (4) Conduct multi-scale analysis; (5) See the whole as well as the parts; (6) Pay attention to interconnections; (7) Be spatially focused but not geographically restrictive** (pp. 33-40).

**Urban stakeholder analysis dimensions**: Functions/responsibilities, capacity/vulnerability, power/influence, access, interests/perceptions, and relationships (pp. 40-44). **Figure 4 maps urban stakeholders across: public sector (national government, municipal authorities, utilities), civil society (CBOs, NGOs, faith groups, diaspora), private sector (formal businesses, informal vendors, employers), affected populations (residents, displaced, returnees), and international actors (UN, donors, INGOs)** (p. 41). **Analysis should capture not just who stakeholders are, but: what they do, what capacity/constraints they face, what power they hold, how decisions are made, what incentives drive them, and how they relate to each other** (pp. 42-44).

**Barrier analysis for implementation**: Assess individual, institutional, and problem-structure constraints to applying systems thinking in operations (pp. 47-51). **Individual barriers include: lack of urban expertise, analytical overload, cognitive biases toward simple linear causation. Institutional barriers include: sectoral mandates and funding silos, short-term project cycles, weak incentives for coordination and learning. Problem-structure barriers include: genuine uncertainty, rapid change, political complexity, and data/access constraints** (pp. 47-51).

**Cross-framework synthesis method**: **Annex 1 provides a matrix comparing multiple urban analytical frameworks (DFID sustainable livelihoods, UN-Habitat city prosperity index, urban risk drivers, humanitarian sectors) against the five-system typology — enabling users to map existing frameworks onto systems categories** (p. 55).

## Project lifecycle relevance

**Scoping and context analysis**: Emphasizes that early contextual understanding is essential to avoid disconnected and harmful responses **— "Humanitarian actors who fail to understand urban systems risk: providing assistance that doesn't match how people actually access services; undermining local markets and service providers; creating visible inequalities that fuel tension; missing leverage points for efficient impact; and leaving populations more vulnerable when they exit"** (pp. 18-20).

**Design phase**: Suggests shifting from linear planning assumptions toward uncertainty-aware and adaptive framing **— "Urban systems are dynamic and unpredictable; detailed multi-year plans assuming stable conditions are likely to fail. Design should specify principles, decision triggers, and learning cycles rather than fixed activities"** (pp. 34-35).

**Implementation phase**: Recommends working across scales and system relationships, not only sector or household silos **— "Effective urban programming requires simultaneous attention to multiple scales (household needs + neighborhood services + citywide systems) and cross-system connections (shelter depends on land tenure, WASH depends on energy, livelihoods depend on transport)"** (pp. 36-40).

**Coordination and adaptation**: Requires stronger engagement with diverse local actors and institutions over time **— "Urban crises involve dozens or hundreds of actors with overlapping mandates, conflicting interests, and complex power dynamics. Coordination cannot be achieved through humanitarian-only cluster meetings; it requires sustained engagement with municipal authorities, service providers, community organizations, and private sector actors"** (pp. 15, 40-44).

**Learning and improvement**: Identifies barriers and next-step questions to guide iterative improvement in urban response practice **— "The paper concludes with unresolved questions: How much analysis is enough before action is justified? How should spatial boundaries be drawn when systems span geographies? How can short-term humanitarian funding support long-term systems strengthening? These questions require ongoing field experimentation and evidence-building"** (pp. 47-53).

## Field data collection implications

Data collection should go beyond population counts and include system linkages, actor relationships, and functional dependencies **— "Traditional humanitarian assessments focus on counting affected populations and measuring sectoral needs. Urban systems analysis requires additional data: How do water, power, and transport networks function? Who provides services and how are they financed? What are the relationships between formal and informal governance actors? How do markets connect neighborhoods to wider supply chains?"** (pp. 14-15, 22-25).

Assessment should capture both "whole" dynamics and component-level details, avoiding single-layer reductionism **— "Reductionist analysis breaks systems into parts and studies each separately; holistic analysis examines emergent properties and system-level behavior. Urban assessment needs both: detailed component data (household income, infrastructure condition) and system-level understanding (how do parts interact? what feedback loops exist?)"** (pp. 38-39).

Stakeholder mapping needs to capture power, incentives, perceptions, and accountability relationships, not only actor lists **— "Stakeholder analysis should answer: Who holds decision-making authority? What resources do they control? What incentives and constraints shape their behavior? Who do they perceive as accountable to? What relationships exist between stakeholders — cooperation, competition, dependence, conflict?"** (pp. 40-44).

Analysis must be multi-scalar and temporal, including pre-crisis functioning and post-crisis changes over time **— "Understanding requires baseline knowledge of how systems functioned before the crisis, tracking changes during and after shocks, and analyzing trends over time — not just crisis-moment snapshots"** (pp. 35-37).

Spatial analysis tools (including GIS/mapping) are useful but insufficient alone without systemic interpretation **— "GIS and spatial data visualization are valuable for urban analysis, but maps showing population density or damaged buildings don't automatically reveal system dynamics. Spatial tools must be combined with relational analysis to understand how mapped features connect and interact"** (p. 45).

## Coordination implications

Urban response involves high actor density, overlapping mandates, and complex formal/informal governance arrangements that complicate coordination **— "Urban crises attract large numbers of responding organizations (tens or hundreds), each with their own mandates, funding streams, and operational priorities. Municipal authorities, national governments, service providers, private businesses, and community organizations all play roles. Coordination must navigate this complexity"** (pp. 15, 27-28).

Weak understanding of actor relationships can lead to duplication, gaps, and reduced local legitimacy **— "When humanitarian actors don't understand existing governance structures, service delivery systems, and community organization, they risk: duplicating what others already do well, creating gaps by assuming others are covering certain functions, undermining local actors by bypassing or competing with them, and losing legitimacy by failing to respect local authority and norms"** (pp. 19-20).

Sectoral humanitarian structures can obstruct holistic urban coordination and systems-level problem solving **— "The cluster system organizes response by sector (WASH, shelter, health, protection, etc.), with separate coordination meetings, funding streams, and technical expertise. This structure works reasonably well in camps but fragments urban response where problems are interconnected and multi-sectoral solutions are needed"** (pp. 48-49).

Effective urban coordination requires complementarity with municipal, national, private sector, and community actors **— "Humanitarian coordination cannot be humanitarian-only in urban contexts. Municipalities provide most urban services and retain regulatory authority. Private sector actors (utilities, transport, markets) are critical system components. Community-based organizations have local knowledge and trust. Coordination mechanisms must engage this full ecosystem"** (pp. 15, 40-41, 46-47).

## DRR implications

The paper links contextual misunderstanding to avoidable risk creation and poorer crisis outcomes, supporting DRR-oriented investment in systems understanding **— "Poorly designed humanitarian interventions can increase long-term vulnerability: cash assistance that inflates rents and displaces poor residents; shelter that concentrates populations in hazard-prone areas; service provision that undermines local providers and collapses when external funding ends. Systems understanding helps avoid creating new risks"** (pp. 18-20).

It highlights interconnected infrastructure/services and settlement vulnerabilities, which are central for urban risk reduction planning **— "Urban risk is not only about hazard exposure; it emerges from interconnected vulnerabilities across systems. Infrastructure failures cascade (power outage → water pump failure → sanitation breakdown). Economic shocks spread through linked livelihoods. Understanding these interdependencies is essential for DRR"** (pp. 30-32).

Multi-scale and systems-aware analysis supports identifying underlying risk drivers rather than only visible symptoms **— "Symptoms are visible: flooding, disease outbreaks, food insecurity. Root causes operate at deeper system levels: land use policies that permit settlement in flood zones, governance failures that leave drainage unmaintained, economic structures that trap populations in vulnerable informal work. Systems analysis helps identify leverage points for upstream intervention"** (pp. 36-39).

**Urban risk accumulation**: **"Cities concentrate not just populations but also risks — hazard exposure, infrastructure interdependencies, economic volatility, social tensions, governance fragility. The five-system typology helps structure comprehensive urban risk analysis across these domains"** (pp. 25-31).

## Operational implications

Humanitarian teams should "step back" before acting, to observe how urban systems actually function in context **— "The paper's title 'Stepping Back' captures this imperative: resist the pressure to act immediately based on templates and assumptions. Invest time upfront observing, listening, and analyzing before designing interventions"** (pp. 24, 52).

Program design should avoid default rural/camp mental models and account for urban interconnectedness and uncertainty **— "Much humanitarian doctrine assumes dispersed, subsistence-based populations in camps or rural areas. Urban contexts violate these assumptions: populations are dense and mobile, livelihoods depend on cash and markets, services come from complex formal/informal providers, governance is multi-layered. Default models fail; context-specific design is required"** (pp. 47, 49-50).

Organizations need adaptive approaches (more observation and adaptation, less rigid plan-then-implement behavior) for urban crises **— "Urban systems change rapidly and unpredictably. Fixed project plans become obsolete. Adaptive management — setting principles and triggers, collecting feedback, adjusting course — is more appropriate than detailed upfront planning with rigid execution"** (pp. 35, 49).

Tools are useful but cannot substitute for organizational change in incentives, skills, and cross-sector collaboration **— "Assessment tools, coordination frameworks, and analytical guides help, but won't solve the urban challenge if: staff lack urban expertise and systems thinking capacity, incentives reward speed over quality analysis, funding is short-term and sector-siloed, coordination remains humanitarian-only, and organizations resist learning from failure"** (pp. 50, 52-53).

Practical constraints (time, analytical burden, capacity gaps) should be explicitly planned for when scoping urban analysis depth **— "Perfect systems understanding is impossible and probably unnecessary. The challenge is determining: How much analysis is enough to avoid major errors? When is further analysis yielding diminishing returns? How can quick-but-good-enough assessment methods be developed? These are unresolved practical questions"** (pp. 50-51).

## Limitations / cautions

The paper is a working paper that frames an approach and agenda, not a complete operational manual **— "This paper proposes systems thinking as a useful lens and offers a five-system typology and seven principles, but does not provide step-by-step assessment protocols or implementation guides. It is a conceptual foundation requiring operational development"** (pp. 52-53).

It explicitly does not attempt a comprehensive critique of systems theory, focusing instead on practical application **— "The paper draws on systems thinking literature pragmatically, selecting concepts useful for humanitarian practice. It is not a theoretical treatise on systems science"** (p. 54).

Much of the evidence base includes interviews and literature synthesis; transferability may require context-specific testing **— "Findings are based on literature review, interviews with humanitarian practitioners (n=30+), and workshop discussions. Field validation through pilot applications would strengthen the evidence base"** (p. 11).

The document itself flags unresolved practical questions (for example analysis boundaries and avoiding analysis paralysis) for further work **— "Key questions remain unanswered: How much analysis is enough? How should spatial boundaries be drawn? How can short-term funding support systems strengthening? How do we balance speed with depth? The paper identifies these as areas for ongoing research and experimentation"** (pp. 52-53).

Source URL is available in frontmatter via ResearchGate (pp. 2, 57 reference ALNAP); for a more stable copy, search alnap.org directly for the canonical publication [web:51][web:53].

**2016 publication predates recent urban guidance**: This paper was foundational but pre-dates IASC 2018 area-based coordination guidance, 2019 ALNAP Systems Thinking Handbook, and 2026 IASC ABC ToR — users should integrate with contemporary frameworks [web:57].

## Known tensions

**Analysis depth vs. operational urgency**: Systems analysis requires time for observation, stakeholder engagement, and synthesis; humanitarian crises demand rapid response. The paper acknowledges this tension but doesn't resolve it — "How much analysis is enough?" remains unanswered (pp. 50-51).

**Holistic understanding vs. component expertise**: Systems thinking emphasizes interconnections and emergent properties; humanitarian sectors are organized around specialized technical expertise (WASH engineers, health professionals, shelter specialists). Balancing generalist systems insight with specialist technical depth is challenging (pp. 38-39, 48-49).

**Spatial focus vs. boundary permeability**: Area-based approaches provide operational focus, but urban systems cross boundaries (water networks, labor markets, social networks). The paper advocates "spatially focused but not geographically restrictive" analysis without fully resolving how to operationalize this principle (p. 40).

**Complexity acknowledgment vs. decision paralysis**: Recognizing urban complexity and uncertainty is intellectually honest, but can lead to analysis paralysis or decision avoidance. The paper doesn't specify decision thresholds — when is understanding "good enough" to act? (pp. 50-51)

**Humanitarian mandates vs. systems strengthening**: Systems thinking points toward long-term capacity building and institutional strengthening; humanitarian mandates emphasize life-saving and rapid relief. Bridging these orientations requires funding, mandate, and timeline shifts that exceed individual agency control (pp. 48-49).

**Standardization vs. context specificity**: Tools and frameworks provide efficiency and comparability; systems thinking emphasizes context-specific analysis. The five-system typology aims to balance these — offering structure while requiring adaptation — but tension remains (pp. 25, 52-53).
