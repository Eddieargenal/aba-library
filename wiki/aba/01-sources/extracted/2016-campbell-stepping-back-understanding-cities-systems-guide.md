# Summary
This ALNAP working paper argues that humanitarian actors still struggle to understand and respond effectively in urban crises, despite increasing urban caseloads and growing policy attention (pp. 6, 8-10, 52). It positions this as a practical performance problem: responses can become disconnected from context, undermine local systems, and miss opportunities to build on existing capacities (pp. 18-20).

The paper proposes systems thinking as a useful lens for understanding urban complexity. It defines a systems approach as focusing on linkages and interrelationships, not only isolated components, and stresses that urban systems are dynamic, interconnected, and often unpredictable (pp. 22-25).

A core contribution is a typology of five urban systems: economy and livelihoods; politics and governance; social and cultural; infrastructure and services; and space and settlements (pp. 25-31). The paper then translates systems thinking into seven practical principles for humanitarian analysis and design, including multi-scale analysis, attention to interconnections, and spatial awareness without rigid geographic exclusion (pp. 33-41).

It also reviews current humanitarian practice and identifies barriers to deeper urban understanding at individual, institutional, and problem-structure levels, concluding that tools are necessary but insufficient without broader shifts in mindset, organization, and coordination (pp. 45-53).

# Key concepts
- Urban as continuum: urban contexts are better treated as a continuum with "fuzzy boundaries" rather than a fixed binary with rural (pp. 12, 14).
- Urban density, diversity, dynamics: these three characteristics help explain why urban contexts are harder to interpret and navigate in crises (pp. 14-17).
- Systems approach: systems are interconnected elements bounded in structures; analysis must focus on interrelationships as well as components (pp. 22-23).
- Stocks, flows, feedback loops: a practical way to break down system behavior and avoid premature assumptions (pp. 24-25).
- Five-system urban typology: economy/livelihoods, governance, social/cultural, infrastructure/services, and space/settlements (p. 25).
- Multi-scale analysis: meaningful understanding requires analysis from household to neighborhood to city and beyond (pp. 36-37).
- Spatial focus without geographic restriction: useful area focus should not ignore wider system linkages across boundaries (p. 40).

# Tools or methods extracted
- Urban context criteria checklist: broad criteria for identifying urban contexts (density, services/infrastructure, cash economy, boundaries, etc.) (p. 12).
- Systems decomposition method: map stocks, flows, and feedback loops for selected urban functions (pp. 24-25).
- Five-system diagnostic typology: structure context analysis around the five proposed urban systems and their interactions (pp. 25-31).
- Seven-principle systems lens: practical analysis frame (acknowledge systems, accept uncertainty, track change/resilience, multi-scale analysis, whole-plus-parts view, interconnections, spatially focused but non-exclusionary) (pp. 33-40).
- Urban stakeholder analysis dimensions: functions/responsibilities, capacity/vulnerability, power/influence, access, interests/perceptions, and relationships (pp. 40-44).
- Barrier analysis for implementation: assess individual, institutional, and problem-structure constraints to applying systems thinking in operations (pp. 47-51).

# Project lifecycle relevance
- Scoping and context analysis: emphasizes that early contextual understanding is essential to avoid disconnected and harmful responses (pp. 18-20).
- Design phase: suggests shifting from linear planning assumptions toward uncertainty-aware and adaptive framing (pp. 34-35).
- Implementation phase: recommends working across scales and system relationships, not only sector or household silos (pp. 36-40).
- Coordination and adaptation: requires stronger engagement with diverse local actors and institutions over time (pp. 15, 40-44).
- Learning and improvement: identifies barriers and next-step questions to guide iterative improvement in urban response practice (pp. 47-53).

# Field data collection implications
- Data collection should go beyond population counts and include system linkages, actor relationships, and functional dependencies (pp. 14-15, 22-25).
- Assessment should capture both "whole" dynamics and component-level details, avoiding single-layer reductionism (pp. 38-39).
- Stakeholder mapping needs to capture power, incentives, perceptions, and accountability relationships, not only actor lists (pp. 40-44).
- Analysis must be multi-scalar and temporal, including pre-crisis functioning and post-crisis changes over time (pp. 35-37).
- Spatial analysis tools (including GIS/mapping) are useful but insufficient alone without systemic interpretation (p. 45).

# Coordination implications
- Urban response involves high actor density, overlapping mandates, and complex formal/informal governance arrangements that complicate coordination (pp. 15, 27-28).
- Weak understanding of actor relationships can lead to duplication, gaps, and reduced local legitimacy (pp. 19-20).
- Sectoral humanitarian structures can obstruct holistic urban coordination and systems-level problem solving (pp. 48-49).
- Effective urban coordination requires complementarity with municipal, national, private sector, and community actors (pp. 15, 40-41, 46-47).

# DRR implications
- The paper links contextual misunderstanding to avoidable risk creation and poorer crisis outcomes, supporting DRR-oriented investment in systems understanding (pp. 18-20).
- It highlights interconnected infrastructure/services and settlement vulnerabilities, which are central for urban risk reduction planning (pp. 30-32).
- Multi-scale and systems-aware analysis supports identifying underlying risk drivers rather than only visible symptoms (pp. 36-39).

# Operational implications
- Humanitarian teams should "step back" before acting, to observe how urban systems actually function in context (pp. 24, 52).
- Program design should avoid default rural/camp mental models and account for urban interconnectedness and uncertainty (pp. 47, 49-50).
- Organizations need adaptive approaches (more observation and adaptation, less rigid plan-then-implement behavior) for urban crises (pp. 35, 49).
- Tools are useful but cannot substitute for organizational change in incentives, skills, and cross-sector collaboration (pp. 50, 52-53).
- Practical constraints (time, analytical burden, capacity gaps) should be explicitly planned for when scoping urban analysis depth (pp. 50-51).

# Claims worth citing
- 54% of the world population lives in urban environments (as cited in the paper) (p. 6).
- There is "no clear, common understanding" of what urban systems are for humanitarian practice (p. 6).
- Not understanding urban contexts can produce disconnected responses and exacerbate tensions (pp. 18-19).
- A systems approach focuses on linkages/interconnections and can deepen understanding in complex urban crises (pp. 23-24).
- Humanitarian urban understanding remains "piecemeal" and the systems "big picture" is still missing (p. 45).

# Limitations / cautions
- The paper is a working paper that frames an approach and agenda, not a complete operational manual (pp. 52-53).
- It explicitly does not attempt a comprehensive critique of systems theory, focusing instead on practical application (p. 54).
- Much of the evidence base includes interviews and literature synthesis; transferability may require context-specific testing (p. 11).
- The document itself flags unresolved practical questions (for example analysis boundaries and avoiding analysis paralysis) for further work (pp. 52-53).
- Source URL is available in frontmatter via ResearchGate (pp. 2, 57 reference ALNAP); for a more stable copy, search alnap.org directly for the canonical publication.
