Standard Terms of Reference for Area-Based Coordination (endorsed by the IASC Humanitarian Working Group, March 2026)

Background Wherever possible, coordination of responses to humanitarian needs should be led by national and local actors, including, but not limited to, States, which have the responsibility, ﬁrst and foremost, to take care of the victims of natural disasters and other emergencies occurring on their
territory.1 However, in contexts where national response or coordination capacity is unable to meet needs in a manner that respects the humanitarian principles,2 or where response and coordination gaps exist due to a sharp deterioration or signiﬁcant change in the humanitarian situation, there
may be a need for coordination that is supported by the Inter-Agency Standing Committee.3

For the past twenty years, this coordination has primarily taken the form of sectoral “clusters” at both national and subnational levels, supported by inter-cluster coordination mechanisms to ensure overall coordination in an integrated and multisectoral way. Over time, more diversiﬁed
models of coordination have evolved and, following the 2025 funding crisis and ongoing systemic changes and reforms, including the Humanitarian Reset, there is an increasing shift towards more ﬂexible, contextualised, and multi-sector coordination models, including area-based coordination
(ABC) and hybrid models.

The following Terms of Reference (TOR) are designed to support operations that are adopting ABC that falls under the remit of the IASC – namely, where national response or coordination capacity is unable to meet needs in a manner that respects the humanitarian principles, or where response
and coordination gaps exist due to a sharp deterioration or signiﬁcant change in the humanitarian situation.4 The TOR offers a working draft that can be adapted to speciﬁc humanitarian operations as needed. They outline the purpose, roles, responsibilities, and functions of ABC structures and
were developed in consultation with key humanitarian stakeholders to capture operational perspectives and good practices across different humanitarian operations. Operations that have already adopted ABC approaches are not required to realign their TORs to this document.

In general, ABC and clusters are not mutually exclusive. ABC models should not replace the cluster coordination system but co-exist with it, so that different coordination tools can be leveraged to respond to operational needs in each context.

These TORs do not yet fully tackle a number of important questions on which inter-agency
discussions are ongoing at the time of dissemination, including:
• Understanding the interlinkages, and respective value-add, of ABC and clusters/sectors
(noting also that ABC and clusters/sectors can be complementary, including at sub-national level).
• Clarifying the links between ABC and other humanitarian structures (e.g. the HCT), functions
(e.g. Provider of Last Resort), and processes (e.g. the Humanitarian Programme Cycle).
• Identifying how best to ensure coherence in service delivery sectors where services are
managed as a system across different localities.

1. Deﬁnition
For the purposes of humanitarian coordination under the IASC, area-based coordination (ABC) is deﬁned as a light, operational coordination and response structure or mechanism focused on a distinct subnational geographic area. 5 It is chaired by an organisation which is present in the
geographic area and has the capacity to coordinate other actors to deliver a humanitarian response that is principled6, accountable, well-coordinated, and context-speciﬁc. At its core, ABC is multi-sectoral and participatory, ensuring the subnational response is tailored to local needs.
An ABC group is linked to relevant IASC coordination structures in the country7, ensuring alignment with, and support from, the wider in-country coordination system. It is primarily focused on coordinating the identiﬁcation of needs, protection risks, and the delivery of goods and services in
response to priorities and preferences of affected people. It also leads operational humanitarian planning relevant to that geographic area and contributes to national strategic planning (i.e. HNRP).

2. Deﬁning features
ABC can take different forms8 based on the context, but it has some deﬁning features, namely it:
• Promotes the leadership and engagement of local actors in the coordination process,
capitalizing and augmenting their capacity, while ensuring this is approached in a conﬂictsensitive manner.
• Is aimed at ensuring the delivery of humanitarian assistance and protection in a manner that
adheres to humanitarian principles, international law (IHL and IHRL), and based on Accountability to the Affected Populations.
• Coordinates humanitarian response geographically by bringing together frontline responders
across sectors within a deﬁned subnational area to deliver a multi-sectoral response during the period for which the area is prioritised for humanitarian response.
• Supports partners to rapidly scale and adapting response to shifting needs, risks, dynamics,
and access.
• Reports service delivery disruptions in non-commoditized sectors such as health.
• Ensures local needs are met holistically through multisectoral, integrated and community-
centred assessments and interventions that reﬂect the priorities and feedback of affected populations.
• Ensures inclusive, multisectoral, and community-driven coordination.

3. Establishment
The HC and HCT determine the coordination structure needed for a particular context and for the requirements of the crisis, building on existing structures wherever feasible and appropriate. Key considerations include: the scale and size of the response; the complexity of the context; conﬂict
or disaster escalations, existing protection risks, large-scale and / or protracted displacement, or other response requirements that require a conﬂict-sensitive, localised, and devolved operational approach to effectively address needs. If a humanitarian ABC structure already exists outside of
the IASC humanitarian architecture, the HC and ABC lead/co-leads should discuss connections with and expected accountability and support to/from IASC structures in the country. Where clusters are activated, linkages and roles/ responsibilities should be clearly mapped out.

3.1. Establishment criteria The following minimum conditions should be considered for the establishment of an ABC group:
• Signiﬁcant multi-sectoral humanitarian needs and protection risks are concentrated within a
clearly deﬁned geographic area.
• There is a need for coordination of multiple actors involved in the humanitarian response to
ensure a coherent and efficient response.
• Coordination needs are not covered by other mechanisms.
• At least one organisation has the capacity and willingness to chair or co-chair the group.
• The area is prioritised for humanitarian response.
• Where existing multi-sector coordination mechanisms already exist and adhere to
humanitarian principles, these should be strengthened and adapted rather than establishing new ABC structures. 3.2.  Selection of (Co-)Chair(s)
The Chair (or Co-Chairs) should represent an organisation with sustained physical presence and
capacity to coordinate in the deﬁned area. Selection is based on:
• Ability to convene partners, facilitate inclusive coordination, ensure coordination is carried
out in a neutral manner (i.e. on behalf of all partners and not in the interests of any single organisation), and liaise effectively with other humanitarian coordination structures.
• Ability and willingness to coordinate a response, conﬂict sensitivity, and ability to operate
neutrally across actors in a manner that adheres to humanitarian principles.
• Acceptance and trust from local actors and partners, and ability to engage with local
authorities.
• Physical presence or sustained operational presence in a given geographic location.

Leadership and co-leadership should be by national and local NGOs by default, where feasible and based on operational presence and capacity. Strong representation of NGOs in leadership/coleadership roles is critical. The respective roles of chairs and co-chairs should be clearly deﬁned
in-country to ensure equity in the sharing of administrative and secretarial responsibilities. Where an international organisation or actor serves as a Co-Chair, they will be expected to provide

• Outline the core functions and expected deliverables in a short concept note or ToR.
• Agree on the establishment of the ABC structure.
• Conduct outreach to inform, engage, and invite relevant local actors to participate, and where
feasible based on operational presence and capacity, lead/co-lead ABC.

4. Functions
The ABC structure/mechanism is responsible for the following core functions within its designated
area:
• Operational coordination of multi-sectoral, efficient, humanitarian action in the
speciﬁed geographic area: Coordinates the response to humanitarian needs and protection risks in the geographic area. The ABC provides a platform for partners to collectively address operational challenges, collaboratively solving problems at the local level when possible and
escalating to higher coordination levels only when necessary. It encourages the sharing of best operational practices for aid distribution across sectors. In coordination with clusters/sectors, supports service mapping to facilitate cross-sectoral referral pathways and
ensure that sectoral standards deﬁned by clusters are applied (this will ensure quality response).
• Needs assessment, planning, and prioritisation: Facilitates the execution of context-
sensitive joint needs assessments to build a common, real-time intersectoral understanding of needs and protection risks, followed by collective integrated analysis to inform response,

• Identiﬁcation of service delivery disruptions: Contributes to identifying and responding to
the absence of, or severe constraints to public service delivery (e.g. health or water systems).
This will be done in coordination with relevant and present sectoral actors including, where feasible, national, and local governments. Service gaps and priorities identiﬁed through ABC mechanisms should be shared with relevant clusters/sectors, particularly where these inform
resource allocation, response scale-up, or escalation decisions.
• Centrality of protection: Supports the continuous area-speciﬁc situational understanding of
the protection risk environment and implements action and/or advocacy to address protection risks and support protection outcomes at area-level. Aligns with national efforts on the centrality of protection (CoP), including the CoP strategy where one has been
elaborated (whether separately or within the HNRP).
• Access: Ensures humanitarian response in the relevant geographic area reaches those with
the most severe needs, and develops and implements localised access approaches and negotiations to facilitate response in geographic areas with high access constraints, together with Access Working Groups or other similar structures where appropriate.
• Advocacy: Provides a common platform for partners to raise key humanitarian concerns (e.g.
access constraints, protection risks) at local level and to agree on how best to channel these to relevant authorities and national humanitarian structures, including clusters/sectors/systems as appropriate. Supports in analysing local risks of different
advocacy approaches or initiatives.
• Linkages and complementarity: Wherever possible, ABCs should establish operational
linkages with development, durable solutions, and peacebuilding actors to ensure complementarity between humanitarian response and other processes, including to identify opportunities for transition.
• Engagement with authorities: The ABC Chair or Co-Chair should engage with relevant local
authorities and technical departments, in line with humanitarian principles and taking into account access and protection considerations, including to identify opportunities for transition, wherever feasible and appropriate. Engagement with local authorities by ABCs
should be guided by HCT-endorsed parameters (such as Joint Operating Procedures, where they exist), informed by context analysis and risk assessments, and aligned with nationallevel coordination positions.
• Accountability to affected people: ABCs should leverage their proximity to affected
communities to enhance community participation in humanitarian action. ABCs should ensure two-way communication with affected people and should systematically channel community preferences, priorities, and concerns into response planning at area-level, as well

• Protection against Sexual Exploitation and Abuse: ABCs should undertake all possible

If these functions are delivered effectively, the ABC should facilitate efficient, multi-sectoral humanitarian responses; enhanced community participation and accountability to affected people; localised ownership and leadership; improved service delivery.

5. Roles and Responsibilities of key structures/actors vis-à-vis ABC
Effective ABCs rely on clear roles among key actors:
• ABC Chair (and Co-Chair): Convene partners to solve problems, manage operational
challenges, and coordinate the local humanitarian response. Ensure the work of the ABC feeds into national level planning and decision-making. Where the chair and/or co-chair are not national/local actors, they should proactively support capacity building of national/local
actors involved in coordination to enable eventual hand over to them, and ensure succession planning is prioritised by ABC co-chairs.
• HC and HCT: Agree on the convening of ABC based on operational requirements, ensure
alignment with national and subnational coordination including cluster/sectors, and provide strategic oversight and resource mobilisation for partners leading ABC mechanisms.
• OCHA: Supports the ABC, provides facilitation and information management whenever
possible and subject to existing resources, and promotes coherence across ABCs, clusters/sectors, and other coordination mechanisms, including the HC/HCT and ICCG.
Where designated, OCHA (co-)chairs ABC groups, ensuring consistent facilitation, information ﬂow and connection with HCT/ICCG and subnational coordination mechanisms.
• ICCG: Ensures alignment with national and subnational cluster coordination, feeds ABC
operational priorities into planning and funding processes, and maintains two-way information ﬂow between subnational and national levels.
• Cluster/Sector: Provides sectoral technical standards and technical guidance on delivery
and integrates area-level priorities into cluster strategies and coordination of actors engaged in the clusters/sectors. ABC mechanisms and clusters should agree on modalities to exchange information and align understanding of needs, risks, response, and planning.

engaged in the response13 in order to preserve space for local and national NGOs.

7. Dissolution or transition
An ABC structure is not intended to exist indeﬁnitely. Dissolution or transition of the ABC should
be considered when one or more of the following conditions are met:
• Humanitarian needs in the area have signiﬁcantly decreased or response has changed (e.g.
from emergency to recovery).
• Existing coordination structures (such as government-led mechanisms) can absorb the
functions of the ABC, without compromising humanitarian principles.
• The number of actors involved in humanitarian response in the area has declined to the point
where a dedicated ABC group is no longer needed.
• ABC participants collectively decide that the ABC is not serving its intended purpose or
another coordination model would be more effective (e.g. clusters at sub-national level). This

8. Relationship between ABC and cluster coordination
ABCs work in complementarity with established clusters/sectors. They do not replace sectoral coordination but facilitate integrated operational delivery at area level, drawing on technical guidance and other functions from relevant clusters/sectors. ABCs facilitate the operational
coordination of multi-sector activities within a deﬁned area, whereas sectoral standards, analysis, and strategy development remain the responsibility of the relevant clusters.
