<a id="trigger"></a>
## Trigger Conditions

Use this protocol whenever the *Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community*
are put in front of users for the first time — government or civil-society staff,
local partners, or vulnerable communities — and the question is *how* to bring them
in. *"How do I train field staff on a complex resilience framework without losing
them?"*

Introduction is appropriate when there is a concrete reason to use the
Characteristics — a baseline, a VCA, priority-setting, or evaluation — and **time
and facilitation** to do it properly (Twigg 2009, p. 16). It is rarely a single
formal training event; in practice it is "more usual to run planning workshops and
group activities" using participatory methods, best seen as "part of a longer-term
process" (p. 16). Activate the protocol when deciding the *entry route*. Field
testing found a precondition: the document is "quite long and complex, the language
can appear abstract and conceptual, and the tables are detailed," so brief,
unfacilitated exposures fail unless participants are "already very knowledgeable
about DRR" (p. 16). If that precondition is absent, the protocol applies.

<a id="decision-logic"></a>
## Decision Logic

There is **no standard approach for all situations**; the way the Characteristics
are introduced "must be matched to the potential users' existing knowledge and
capacities" (Twigg 2009, p. 17). Decide as follows.

- **Pick the entry route by user expertise.** The source offers three (p. 17):
  (1) start from the **overall framework** — Thematic Areas, Components, Enabling
  Environment — to stimulate a "semi-conceptual discussion" about DRR; (2) run
  **group exercises on specific Components** against real-life tasks (baselines,
  priorities, evaluation); or (3) **begin with what people already know** — real
  situations and projects — and work *backwards* to see how existing actions fit
  the framework, surfacing strengths, weaknesses, and gaps (p. 17). The backwards
  route is the default for lower-expertise audiences, documented in Case Studies 1
  (rural Bangladesh, Tearfund) and 2 (Nepal, Practical Action). Reserve route (1)
  for users who already have high DRR knowledge (RULE-002; p. 17).
- **Make it participatory, not top-down.** Vulnerable people "must" be given a
  chance to explore and validate the Characteristics; the resource should be
  adopted "from the grassroots upwards," not only imposed from above, because this
  creates the **ownership** that makes successful application more likely — and it
  requires facilitators experienced in community-based approaches (p. 16).
- **Treat introduction as flexible adaptation.** The Characteristics are "a
  resource, not a checklist to be ticked off"; they "should stimulate and
  facilitate discussion" and "must be adapted to the context" and to users' needs
  and capacities (p. 16). Before deep use, investigate the framework's "usefulness
  and relevance" to the organisation, its partners, and the communities, including
  fit with the organisation's own systems and structures (p. 16).
- **Sequence over time.** Prefer a longer process with refresher or more advanced
  sessions as users gain experience; brief one-off exercises are "of limited value"
  for non-experts (p. 16).

<a id="escalation"></a>
## Escalation

Escalate to the **capacity-building lead** before proceeding when:

- **Baseline DRR understanding is missing.** The source records cases where
  organisations "did not have a sufficient level of understanding of DRR issues and
  terminology to use the Characteristics easily," or where participants and
  facilitators held different understandings that "had to be resolved first"
  (Twigg 2009, p. 17). Resolve shared understanding before introducing the tables.
- **Language or translation barriers block shared meaning.** Translating key terms
  (e.g. "resilience" into Nepali) can be difficult, and "there is no simple
  solution to this problem"; what matters is to "get the process right" by
  discussing terms and agreeing their meaning — work needing senior judgement and
  good facilitation (p. 17).
- **Facilitation capacity is unavailable.** Field testing reached "general
  agreement about the need for facilitation"; without an experienced facilitator,
  introduction is likely to fail and the decision should be reviewed (p. 16).
- **Context mismatch.** Where the framework's relevance to the organisation,
  partners, or communities is in doubt, investigate it first (p. 16) rather than
  pushing the framework in regardless.
