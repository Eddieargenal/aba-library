<a id="trigger"></a>
## Trigger Conditions

This protocol activates when an organisation must choose **where and how to
intervene** but cannot pursue everything at once. *"We have limited budget — which
resilience gaps should we tackle first?"*

Twigg treats this as the normal condition, not a failure: "operational agencies
have to choose priorities because they cannot address all aspects of resilience at
once" (Twigg 2009, p. 19). The 167 Characteristics describe an **ideal** — "no
single group or organization can address every aspect of DRR" (p. 7) and "nobody
is expected to use every single one" (p. 18). The trigger is met whenever gaps
surfaced by the resilience assessment (T-assess-community-resilience-level) exceed
the organisation's capacity, budget, or mandate (p. 19).

<a id="decision-logic"></a>
## Decision Logic

The source does **not** rank Characteristics — it "does not differentiate between
different types of DRR in terms of their significance." The framework "help[s] users
visualize the widest possible range of options"; priority-setting belongs to "the
group or organization itself" and depends "on a number of factors including needs,
contexts and operational capacities" (p. 19).

1. **Do not attempt the whole framework.** Working with all 167 Characteristics is
   "unrealistic for field projects" (p. 20); select a smaller set rather than spread
   effort thinly.
2. **Decide whether a "key" set is even needed.** "If selection of 'key'
   Characteristics is necessary (and it should not be assumed that it is), it
   should be participatory and deliberative, not imposed from the top" (p. 18).
3. **Select by achievability, measurability, relevance, and balance.** Choose
   Characteristics realistic for the organisation's mandate and capacity, keeping at
   least two per Thematic Area for breadth (Tearfund "top 20", Box 7), and anchor
   choices in the community's own assessed needs through a participatory process
   (pp. 18–19).
4. **Sequence by connections, not in isolation.** "Look for connections between the
   different Thematic Areas," since an activity "may have more impact if it is
   reinforced by addressing Components of Resilience from another" — e.g.
   preparedness (Area 5) built on risk assessment (Area 2) and participation
   (Area 1). Risk/vulnerability assessment is "often seen as a priority at the
   start," but wider "gaps must also be identified before operational choices can be
   made" (p. 19).
5. **Bound ambition by the enabling environment.** Plans must rest on "realistic
   assessments of the type and level of external support" expected (p. 12); a
   "disabling" environment caps what is feasible.
6. **Treat the list as provisional.** "The exercise must not be a one-off; there
   should be regular reviews," since a fixed list lets managers "forget about other
   aspects of resilience" (p. 18); many gains "come about in the long term" under
   limited capacity (p. 20).

<a id="escalation"></a>
## Escalation

Escalate to the **programme lead** where the participatory process cannot resolve
the choice or where capacity is the binding constraint.

- **No consensus on priorities.** Selection "might be difficult to reach consensus
  about" (p. 18); unresolved disagreement needs senior judgement, not the top-down
  imposition the source warns against.
- **The highest-need gap is infeasible** within budget, mandate, or a "disabling"
  enabling environment (p. 12) — partnership or advocacy may be required rather than
  direct delivery (no single organisation can cover everything, p. 7).
- **Minimum-standard framing.** If pressure to pick a "minimum" set risks
  complacency, recall that "nobody should be satisfied with minimum standards"
  (p. 18).
