<a id="trigger"></a>
## Trigger Conditions

This decision arises whenever a programme must fix the **geographic scale** of a
neighbourhood unit before targeting, coordinating, or delivering anything — the
question the source frames as "What are we dealing with — a street of 100 people
or a ward of 10,000? What might the units be, and where does each begin or end?"
(Young Foundation 2010, p. 9). The source treats this as the central design
problem: "Understanding of the scales, characteristics and dynamics of
neighbourhoods is necessary if we are to set a framework for targeted and
effective action. Otherwise we run the risk of public disappointment and policy
failure" (p. 9).

Trigger this protocol when you are about to:

- define a unit for area-based targeting or for a governance structure;
- decide whether to work at block, estate, or wider district level;
- assign a specific **function** (mutual aid, early warning, service delivery) to
  a geographic area and need to know how large that area should be.

The decision is forced by the fact that neighbourhoods "can and do vary in size,"
and that "large and small conceptions of neighbourhoods can be considered as
complementary" (p. 15) — so the scale is a choice to be made deliberately against
the function being served, not a given.

<a id="decision-logic"></a>
## Decision Logic

Field query this should answer: *"At what geographic scale should we define our
area of operation — block level, estate level, or wider district?"*

The source offers a **dual-scale typology** and routes the choice by the
**function** being served (Young Foundation 2010, pp. 15–16):

- **Choose the proximity scale (100–1,500 people)** for social-coordination
  functions — mutual aid, neighbourliness, decision-making, and neighbourhood
  management. These are "streets or estate areas where neighbourliness is
  personal, people know one another as individuals" (p. 16). The threshold is
  grounded in "anthropological evidence that a human group finds it hard to
  coordinate to reach decisions if its population is above a range from 500 to
  1500," and the source notes that "neighbourhood management is a function that
  seems to work well when grounded in smaller-scale neighbourhoods" (p. 16).
- **Choose the strategic scale (5,000–10,000 people)** for **service-infrastructure**
  functions. A neighbourhood of this size "is large enough to include a primary
  school, park and playground space, a doctor's surgery, library or leisure
  centre, and a number of shops and offices," providing "a range of services and
  activities within walking distance of home" and presenting "common issues that
  require a more strategic response" (p. 16).
- **Use both scales in parallel** when functions of both kinds are in play. The
  scales are explicitly "complementary," following "a layered understanding of
  neighbourhood, under which neighbourhoods of several thousand people bring
  together several smaller areas" (p. 15). Residents may "find themselves
  simultaneously located within a network of neighbourhoods of different scales
  and types" (p. 16), so layering the two scales for different purposes is the
  source's recommended default rather than an exception.

The rule of thumb: **map the function to the scale.** If the function depends on
people coordinating and knowing one another, go small (proximity). If it depends
on shared physical services within walking distance, go larger (strategic).

<a id="escalation"></a>
## Escalation

Seek senior or human judgement where the source's typology runs into its own
unresolved tensions:

- **Mismatched scales for a single body.** Where one governance structure is being
  asked to carry both coordination functions (proximity) and service functions
  (strategic), the layered model adds complexity and implies dual structures —
  a trade-off the source raises but does not resolve. Escalate rather than force
  a single boundary.
- **Centres that fall outside boundaries.** The source warns that "a key centre
  for a neighbourhood such as a local park or school may fall outside its nominal
  boundaries," and that imposing "control by a higher body is not the only
  option" — alternatives include "basing neighbourhoods around centres rather than
  on borders," cross-neighbourhood co-operation, and "enabling non-residents to be
  involved in neighbourhood governance" (p. 17). Choosing among these is a
  judgement call for stakeholders.
- **Contested or overlapping boundaries.** Because the source declines to impose a
  single definition — "It would be inappropriate to impose a single meaning of
  neighbourhood" (p. 17) — and because operational systems still require fixed,
  non-overlapping units, any case where lived overlap collides with the need for a
  clean administrative unit should be flagged for stakeholder decision, not
  resolved automatically.
- **Untested thresholds.** The population bands carry question marks in the source
  ("100-1500?", "5000-10,000?", p. 15) and the proposals are framed as
  "Hypotheses" (p. 17). Treat the bands as starting points, not settled values,
  and escalate where local evidence pulls against them.
