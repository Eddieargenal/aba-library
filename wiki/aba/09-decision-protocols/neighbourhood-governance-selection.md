<a id="trigger"></a>
## Trigger Conditions

Apply this protocol when a neighbourhood-level **governance-arrangement choice
arises** — when you must decide what structure (if any) should hold local voice,
decision-making, or leadership for an area. The Young Foundation identifies the
moments that surface this choice: an area **faces a particular challenge** (e.g.
community safety); an area faces a **complex set of challenges** with **low
community capacity**; an area **feels underserved and wants empowerment**; or an
estate already has **strong existing bodies** that now seek **legitimacy or
further powers** (Young Foundation 2010, p. 40, governance paths diagram).

Two preconditions should hold before selecting a structure. First, the source
warns that "governance models and lifecycles will vary case by case" and "one
size will never fit all," so the trigger is a *design* decision, not the
application of a template (p. 40). Second, structures should be chosen *with*
local people and "guided by the outcomes sought" — empowerment "may be extended
to every neighbourhood" but "not everyone will want to take it up in the same way
– or even at all" (pp. 37–38). The choice also recurs over time: structures may
need to be re-selected, merged, made dormant or closed as challenges and
participation rise and fall (p. 41).

<a id="decision-logic"></a>
## Decision Logic

Field query this should answer: *"What governance structure should we establish
for a neighbourhood that faces a specific challenge but has low community
capacity?"*

The source offers a **repertoire of structures**, not a fixed selection formula,
and is explicit that frameworks "should grow out of local specificities: history,
capacity, and the nature of the challenges faced" (p. 36). It lists the available
structures along a spectrum "from occasional open public meetings to permanent
elected neighbourhood boards" (pp. 37–38):

- **Neighbourhood forums** — open assemblies, on-demand or regular; raise issues,
  hold public servants to account, "even make decisions" (p. 37).
- **Neighbourhood panels** — for citizen voice and deliberation; can be elected
  or chosen by lot, meeting regularly or on demand (p. 37).
- **Neighbourhood boards / parish or town councils** — for decision-making and
  leadership, largely elected, with a wider range of powers, fund-raising and
  devolved responsibilities (pp. 37, 39).
- **Neighbourhood managers** — employed or elected, providing joined-up
  leadership and channelling spending (p. 37).

The source maps these options to conditions through its **governance-paths
diagram**, which reads off two axes — *lead actor* (public authorities take the
lead ↔ neighbourhood takes the lead) and *initial community capacity* (lower ↔
higher) — against the triggering situation (p. 40):

- **Particular challenge (e.g. community safety):** neighbourhood policing, a
  youth panel, or a residents' association / neighbourhood watch (p. 40).
- **Complex challenges + low community capacity:** neighbourhood management, a
  neighbourhood assembly, or community visioning — i.e. **management-led**,
  authority-supported structures where capacity is low (p. 40). This is the
  holistic, bottom-up approach the source favours for connecting up issues that
  "often connect (e.g. around public space and community safety)" (p. 36).
- **Underserved area wanting empowerment:** a campaign, a neighbourhood board, or
  a development trust (p. 40).
- **Estate with strong bodies seeking legitimacy / further powers:** a
  neighbourhood assembly/panel or neighbourhood board — i.e. **community-led**
  structures where capacity is high (p. 40).

Two cross-cutting rules constrain the mapping. **Grow from what exists:** new
bodies should not replace "the ecology of local association … from tenant
management organisations and social enterprises to community policing," but
gather these together and fill gaps (p. 37). **Scale powers to capacity:** a
wider range of powers and stronger legitimacy (e.g. a new parish or town council,
established by petition of 10% of residents or 250 people) is appropriate only
"in cases (perhaps relatively few)" where that is desired and supportable
(p. 39).

<a id="escalation"></a>
## Escalation

Escalate to senior or human judgement — beyond the read-off above — where the
source flags structural risk or unresolved questions:

- **Capture and unrepresentative voice.** The source lists "capture by interests
  and failure to represent all parts of the community" as a recognised problem,
  and notes existing parish councils skew unrepresentative (1% of councillors
  from ethnic minorities, over half aged over 60, many candidates unopposed)
  (pp. 39, 41). Where a structure may entrench narrow control, "particular
  attention" must go to building consensus and "bridging capital" and managing
  conflicts "based in social, ethnic, interest-based or identity struggles"
  (p. 41).
- **Fragmentation and loss of strategic context.** Service-specific or
  issue-specific structures risk "fragmentation of services, lack of strategic
  context, lack of data," plus loss of economies of scale and "conflict with
  neighbouring areas and other public authorities" (p. 41). The source leaves
  open the question of where governance should be service-specific versus
  holistic ("no general answer yet leaps out," p. 36).
- **Devolved budgets without capacity.** Putting large budgets in the hands of a
  community "without sufficient capacity can be a recipe for mismanagement,
  schism or professional capture"; senior judgement is needed before devolving,
  with smaller budgets first except where "substantial capacity has developed
  over time" (p. 40, New Deal for Communities lesson).
- **Lifecycle decisions.** Deciding how structures are "merged, made dormant or
  closed down," and "under what circumstances … powers [should] be taken back by
  a higher level," is named as an open question requiring deliberate judgement
  rather than a rule (p. 41).
- **Interface with higher authorities.** Where service delivery "goes wrong" or
  more financial powers are in play, the relationship between neighbourhood
  bodies and larger public authorities must be reviewed as a higher-tier decision
  (p. 40).

> **Evidence note.** Single-source page (Young Foundation 2010, S-RULE-002), so
> this sits at `limited` trust. The source supplies a governance *repertoire* and
> a case-by-case paths diagram rather than a prescriptive selection algorithm,
> and frames its proposals as "hypotheses" requiring testing (p. 41); the mapping
> above is grounded in the paths diagram (p. 40) and structures spectrum
> (pp. 37–39), not invented criteria. Claims assume a UK context with accessible
> local government and formal tenure; corroboration from a second source would be
> needed to promote to `usable`.
