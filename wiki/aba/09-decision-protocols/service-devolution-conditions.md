<a id="trigger"></a>
## Trigger Conditions

Use this protocol when a programme is **considering moving a service, or part of a
service, below local-authority level** to a neighbourhood body — whether on a
delegated or a fully devolved basis (Young Foundation 2010, p. 23). The decision
typically arises in `coordination-design`, when an issue is "strongly rooted in
area," "affects the community as a whole," and has "local specificity" — the three
conditions the source names as giving the **strongest public-value rationale** for
neighbourhood empowerment (p. 23). Community safety, the quality of public spaces,
and regeneration and planning are the source's worked examples of issues that meet
these criteria (p. 23).

The trigger is sharpened by what is already on the table: the source observes a
"rich emerging variety of services being devolved below authority level" —
tenant management organisations, leisure and youth services, even street cleaning
(p. 32). When such a candidate service appears, run the decision logic below
before committing. Note that the source assumes a legitimate neighbourhood
governance body already exists to receive the service; where one does not, that
question precedes this protocol rather than being answered by it.

<a id="decision-logic"></a>
## Decision Logic

Field query this should answer: *"When is it appropriate to devolve service
delivery to neighbourhood bodies rather than keeping it with local authorities?"*

The source's clearest statement (p. 32) gives three positive conditions under which
devolution **"is desirable"**, all of which should hold:

1. **Local knowledge matters.** Devolution is desirable "where local knowledge and
   particularities are important" (p. 32) — i.e. where service quality depends on
   detail that only residents reliably hold.
2. **Co-production opportunities exist.** It is desirable "where there are
   opportunities for volunteering and co-production" (p. 32); the source's
   community service agreements illustrate the reciprocal community-provider
   commitments this implies (p. 31).
3. **Effectiveness can be improved.** It is desirable "where effectiveness can be
   improved" (p. 32) — devolution is justified by better outcomes, not by
   principle alone, consistent with the source's view that empowerment "is not an
   end in itself" (p. 23).

Two further conditions are **implied by the source's framing** rather than stated
as a checklist (flagged as inference):

- **Matched scale and scope.** Where "a service has a long value chain, just the
  frontline elements can sometimes be devolved to great benefit" (p. 32) — so the
  unit devolved should be the part where local advantage is real, not the whole
  chain by default.
- **Demonstrated capacity.** The source's New Deal for Communities lesson is that
  responsibility should be scaled to capacity (p. 33; see escalation). Inferring
  forward, a body should hold capacity proportionate to what it is being given
  before the service is handed over.

If the positive conditions are absent — the issue is not area-rooted, there is no
co-production gain, and effectiveness would not improve — the source's logic points
to **retaining the service with the authority, or pursuing reshaping/co-production
short of devolution** (service level agreements, community service agreements,
pp. 31–32).

<a id="escalation"></a>
## Escalation

Halt and seek senior judgement where devolution risks **fragmentation or capture** —
the two failure modes the source repeatedly flags.

- **Capacity-mismatched budgets (capture / mismanagement).** The New Deal for
  Communities lesson is explicit: "putting millions in the hands of a community
  without sufficient capacity can be a recipe for mismanagement, schism or
  professional capture" (p. 33). Where the proposed transfer is large relative to
  the body's demonstrated capacity, **do not proceed** — "direct responsibility for
  much smaller budgets is more appropriate at the neighbourhood level, except where
  substantial capacity has developed over time" (p. 33). Start small and scale as
  capacity develops (e.g. strong development trusts, Best Value/Quality Parishes).
- **Loss of capacity or value on transfer.** The source records that devolving "too
  many budgets" has gone wrong — in the Tower Hamlets devolution "social care in
  particular lost capacity and value" (p. 33). If a transfer threatens the
  service's capacity or value, escalate rather than devolve.
- **Fragmented responsibility.** "When devolution fragments responsibility, it can
  make engagement harder rather than easier" (p. 5). Issue- or service-specific
  devolution that splinters accountability should be escalated; the source's remedy
  is to gather processes together and provide "broader local vision, leadership and
  accountability" (p. 37) and to act on a "coordinated rather than piecemeal basis"
  (p. 6).

The underlying caution is the source's own: done well, devolution "can improve the
quality of decision-making"; done badly, "it can lead to conflict, growing
inequalities and failing public services" (p. 5). When the conditions in the
decision logic are not clearly met, treat that as a signal to pause, not proceed.

> **Evidence note.** Single-source page (Young Foundation 2010), so this sits at
> `limited` trust. The positive conditions and the capacity lesson are stated
> directly (pp. 23, 32–33); the matched-scale and demonstrated-capacity conditions
> are inferred from the source's framing rather than given as a checklist, and are
> flagged as such. Corroboration from a second source — especially for non-UK or
> humanitarian contexts the source does not address — would be needed to promote to
> `usable`.
