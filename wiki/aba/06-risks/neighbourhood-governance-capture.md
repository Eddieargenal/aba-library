<a id="risk-description"></a>
## Risk Description

**Capture** is the risk that a neighbourhood governance structure comes to serve
a narrow set of interests rather than the community as a whole — so devolution
empowers those already best placed to participate, while citizens at the margins
are left out. The Young Foundation lists this explicitly among the problems
"sometimes associated with neighbourhood empowerment": **"Capture by interests
and failure to represent all parts of the community"** (Young Foundation 2010,
p. 41). It is the concrete form of the source's broader warning that empowerment
"done badly" can lead to "conflict, growing inequalities and failing public
services" (p. 5).

The source locates the root of the risk in how neighbourhood units get defined
and who turns out to participate. Self-defined and demand-driven neighbourhoods
are more cohesive, but the same logic that makes them cohesive also makes them
exclusionary: their listed weaknesses are **"competing claims," "citizens on the
margins may be left out,"** and **"more bonding than bridging capital,
reinforcing social divides"** (p. 10). Where participation is voluntary,
governance tends to answer to a community's "**activist members**" rather than
the whole (p. 26). A distinct, sharper form is **professional capture**: the New
Deal for Communities lesson is that "putting millions in the hands of a community
without sufficient capacity can be a recipe for mismanagement, schism or
professional capture" (p. 33). The source frames these as live concerns rather
than proven failure modes — its governance proposals are presented as
hypotheses for testing — and it does not resolve the question of which
conditions tip empowerment toward capture (pp. 5, 41).

<a id="indicators"></a>
## Indicators

Signs the source associates with capture or its preconditions:

- **A definition built only on self-identification.** Where the neighbourhood is
  drawn purely from popular demand, the source flags "competing claims" and the
  prospect that "citizens on the margins may be left out" (p. 10).
- **More bonding than bridging.** Governance that strengthens ties within an
  already-organised group while reinforcing "social divides" across the wider
  area (p. 10).
- **Participation narrowing to activists.** Leadership and engagement that
  effectively answer to "activist members" rather than the community "at large"
  (p. 26), particularly where attendance at meetings is hard to sustain across
  the wider population.
- **Budgets outrunning capacity.** Large funds devolved to a body without the
  capacity to manage them — the precondition the source ties to "mismanagement,
  schism or professional capture" (p. 33).
- **Exclusive claims on shared assets.** "Exclusive ownership claims laid to
  public spaces by private interest groups," which the source calls "undesirable"
  and contrasts with public ownership based on "welcoming and guardianship rather
  than exclusion" (p. 34).
- **Conflict along social or identity lines.** Governance that "exacerbate[s]
  conflicts based in social, ethnic, interest-based or identity struggles"
  rather than bridging them (p. 41).

<a id="mitigation"></a>
## Mitigation

The source offers direction rather than a worked-out protocol — it flags capture
as a risk to "assess... with a view to minimising" during its programme, without
yet specifying a tested method (p. 41). What it does suggest:

- **Match financial responsibility to capacity, and start small.** Against
  professional capture, the New Deal lesson is to give newly established bodies
  "direct responsibility for much smaller budgets," scaling up only "where
  substantial capacity has developed over time" (p. 33).
- **Design deliberately for bridging capital and consensus.** The source calls
  for "particular attention" to "how governance structures minimise or
  exacerbate conflicts based in social, ethnic, interest-based or identity
  struggles, and how to build consensus and 'bridging capital'" (p. 41) —
  treating representativeness as a design parameter, not an assumed outcome.
- **Do not rely on a single self-identified unit.** Because purely self-defined
  neighbourhoods risk leaving the margins out, the source's wider argument is to
  combine lenses — administrative geography (where "no-one is left out") with
  lived identifications — rather than letting one organised group define the
  unit (p. 10).
- **Connect neighbourhood priorities upward.** The Porto Alegre participatory
  budgeting model — assemblies setting priorities and electing delegates into
  district and city-wide planning — is cited as improving participation
  "in particular for the poor" (p. 33/Porto Alegre discussion), a structural
  counterweight to local capture.

The source does **not** offer thresholds, audit tools, or representativeness
tests; mitigation here is principle-level and, by its own framing, provisional.
