<a id="risk-description"></a>
## Risk Description

**Neighbourhood governance fragments rather than integrates.** Reviewing
neighbourhood-empowerment processes in the UK, the Young Foundation found them
"broad but fragmentary": many are **service-specific or issue-specific**, set up
to address one challenge — community safety, anti-social behaviour, a particular
estate — without joining up across the others (Young Foundation 2010, p. 36).
Because bottom-up empowerment reflects the patchwork of individualistic and
associational civil society, the source notes it "may be complex, overlapping and
fragmented" (p. 17). The risk arises from two structural mismatches the source
identifies. First, **boundaries do not line up**: residents sit simultaneously
within "a network of neighbourhoods of different scales and types" — a block, a
street, a neighbourhood-watch area, a school catchment, a parish — so single-issue
bodies drawn on different lines accumulate into overlapping, "artificial"
divisions (pp. 16–17, 36). Second, **structures multiply without strategic
context**: when every challenge spawns its own body, the result is a register of
problems the source names directly — "Fragmentation of services, lack of strategic
context, lack of data," "Reinventing the wheel," "Lack of economies of scale," and
"Lack of scrutiny or accountability" (p. 41). The consequence is that coordination
and accountability suffer: there is no body holding the "broader local vision,
leadership and accountability" that decision-making, service devolution and
community assets increasingly require (p. 37).

<a id="indicators"></a>
## Indicators

Signs that neighbourhood governance is fragmenting, drawn from the source's
problem register (p. 41) and its review of current practice (pp. 16–17, 36–37):

- **Proliferation of single-purpose bodies.** Governance is "service-specific or
  issue-specific," with separate structures for safety, environment, youth and so
  on, rather than one body bringing "the range of ultra-local issues together"
  (pp. 36, 17).
- **Overlapping, mismatched boundaries.** Initiatives are drawn on different,
  "precisely bounded and mutually exclusive" zones that do not match how residents
  actually move between block, street, ward and catchment (pp. 16–17, 22).
- **Loss of strategic context.** Activity continues but no one holds the
  cross-cutting vision; the source flags "lack of strategic context, lack of data"
  as a recurring problem (p. 41).
- **Duplication and waste.** "Reinventing the wheel" and "lack of economies of
  scale" appear as separate bodies repeat work and lose the benefits of joining up
  (p. 41).
- **Accountability gaps.** "Lack of scrutiny or accountability" — no single
  structure can be held to account for outcomes across the fragmented map (p. 41).

<a id="mitigation"></a>
## Mitigation

The source's response is to **join up rather than impose**. It argues that the
best practice involves "looking beyond artificial boundaries and connecting up
agendas and resources on the ground," taking a "holistic, bottom-up approach" to
the full range of a neighbourhood's challenges (p. 36).

- **Gather existing processes together; fill gaps.** Rather than replacing the
  "ecology of local association" already present, a neighbourhood body should help
  "in gathering these processes together, filling gaps and providing broader local
  vision, leadership and accountability" (p. 37). The aim is integration, not a new
  silo.
- **Govern overlapping realities instead of forcing exclusive zones.** The source
  questions whether "precisely bounded and mutually exclusive zones" are the best
  basis for governing overlapping neighbourhoods, and proposes alternatives:
  "basing neighbourhoods around centres rather than on borders," neighbourhood
  decisions that influence services outside their nominal boundaries, and
  "co-operation between several neighbourhoods" (pp. 16–17).
- **Permit layering, but anchor it.** A working definition for governance can
  "permit layering and overlapping where appropriate," provided it is drawn
  together by the local public realm so that the layers connect rather than
  fragment (p. 17).
- **Match scale to function.** Single-issue coordination can sit at the proximity
  scale, but issues needing "a more strategic response" belong at the strategic
  scale (5,000–10,000), which keeps strategic context and economies of scale in
  view (pp. 15–16). See [Neighbourhood scale selection](../09-decision-protocols/neighbourhood-scale-selection.md).
