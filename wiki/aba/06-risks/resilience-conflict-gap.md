<a id="risk-description"></a>
## Risk Description

The Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community "are written with so-called 'natural' disasters in mind… **assuming a degree of consensus within communities**" (Twigg 2009, p. 15). The framework treats consensus not as one variable to assess but as a built-in precondition: shared vision, a "consensus view of risks faced," negotiation, collective action and trust appear as Characteristics under Thematic Area 1 (Governance) and run implicitly through the whole structure (p. 14). The ideal resilient community it describes is, in effect, one that already agrees.

In conflict and post-conflict settings that precondition can fail in two directions at once, which makes this a genuine tension and not a simple data shortfall. First, "in situations of underlying instability or conflict, [consensus] may be difficult to achieve" — the organising assumption is absent exactly where it is most needed. Second, "conflict often **undermines** community resilience… by breaking down social cohesion, destroying productive assets and local infrastructure, denying access to natural resources… and forcing families to flee their homes" (p. 15). The very thing the framework measures is being dismantled by the context in which it is applied. Twigg already cautions that "communities are complex and often not united," with "more serious divisions within the community" (p. 9); conflict is the extreme case.

Why it matters: applied as a neutral consensus checklist, the framework can mask the conflict instead of surfacing it, producing a tidy resilience profile that papers over contested authority and unsafe disagreement. Twigg is explicit that "alterations… will surely be needed — exactly what is difficult to say, since to date there is **no field experience** of applying the Characteristics in such contexts" (p. 15). So this is both a conceptual tension and a hard evidence gap.

<a id="indicators"></a>
## Indicators

Signs the risk is present, drawn from the source's own failure conditions (pp. 9, 14, 15):

- The setting has active, recent, or latent armed conflict or "underlying instability," and the framework is being applied without conflict-specific adaptation.
- Community structures are fragmented or contested — "more serious divisions within the community," rival authorities, or no body that can legitimately speak for the whole (p. 9).
- The markers of eroded resilience Twigg names are present: broken social cohesion, destroyed assets and infrastructure, blocked access to water or land, displaced families (p. 15).
- Consensus-dependent Characteristics (shared vision, agreed risk view, collective action) are scored as if achievable, with no safe space for the disagreement they assume away (p. 14).

<a id="mitigation"></a>
## Mitigation

The honest position is that the source mainly **raises the caution** rather than resolving it — there is no field-tested protocol to inherit. Twigg states only that "alterations… will surely be needed" but "exactly what is difficult to say" given the absence of experience, and points the user to Section 3.4 on the Enabling Environment for the external dimension (p. 15). From that the source supports:

- **Do not apply the Characteristics unadapted in a conflict or post-conflict setting.** The consensus assumption is load-bearing, not a detail to work around, and its failure can invalidate the analysis.
- **Treat any conflict-setting application as an explicit human-review decision**, not a routine assessment — the finding behind this page is flagged `human_review_required: true` as a major limitation.
- **If partial adaptation is attempted, lean on the post-disaster guidance as the nearest analogue** — a small set of key or "minimum" Characteristics as first-step milestones rather than the full ideal — while recognising the source offers this for post-disaster, not conflict, contexts (p. 15).
- **Treat the adaptation evidence base as a known gap to fill before promoting the framework for conflict use.** Until corroborating field experience exists, its transferability to conflict settings remains untested.

> **Evidence note.** Single source (Twigg 2009, *Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community*, Section 4.1.2(c), p. 15, with supporting context on pp. 9 and 14), so this page sits at `limited` trust. The claims are grounded directly in the source's own conflict caveat; the source explicitly notes there is no field experience to confirm how the framework should be adapted, so the gap is documented by the author rather than resolved. Corroboration from a second source (e.g. conflict-sensitivity or do-no-harm guidance) would be needed to promote beyond `limited`.
