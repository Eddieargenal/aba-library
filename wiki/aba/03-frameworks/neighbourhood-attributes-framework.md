<a id="decision-scope"></a>
## Decision Scope

The **neighbourhood attributes spectrum** is a checklist for *characterising* a
neighbourhood — not for bounding or governing it. Use it during
`neighbourhood-diagnosis`, when the question is "what kind of place is this, and
along which dimensions does it vary?" rather than "where does it begin and end?"
The Young Foundation presents it (drawing on Galster 2001 and Power et al 2004)
as a way to organise the many physical and social characteristics that "rich
descriptions of what neighbourhood can mean" combine, while stressing that such a
description "is not a definition of what it is" (Young Foundation 2010, p. 11–12).
It belongs alongside boundary-definition work, not in place of it: the spectrum
tells you what to observe inside a unit once you have one.

The spectrum runs **from geographic to social** across nine attribute classes
(p. 12): *environmental* (topography, pollution); *proximity* (location,
transport infrastructure); *buildings* (type, design, material, density, repair);
*infrastructure* (roads, streetscape); *demography* (age profile, class, ethnic
diversity, population mobility); *existence and quality of local services*;
*social-interactive* (friend and family networks, local associations, informal
interactions, social-control mechanisms); *sentiment* (identification with place,
historical significance, local stories); and *political* (local parties,
political networks, resident involvement).

<a id="application-logic"></a>
## Application Logic

Apply the spectrum as an ordered traverse from the physical to the relational.
The ordering is itself the method: the source frames neighbourhood as something
experienced largely through "social proximity" — patterned interactions between
residents — so the early (environmental, proximity, buildings, infrastructure)
classes describe the *container*, and the later (social-interactive, sentiment,
political) classes describe the *life inside it* (p. 12).

- **Inventory each class for the target area.** Walk the nine classes in turn and
  record what is present, absent, or degraded. The middle classes — *demography*
  and *quality of local services* — are the hinge between physical form and social
  function (p. 12).
- **Read the social classes as the harder, more telling signal.** The source warns
  that *social homogeneity* is used as an administrative proxy for local networks
  but is "clearly inadequate to the task" (p. 12). The estate study it cites — 64%
  of residents knew more than five people of the same housing tenure but only 17%
  knew more than five of a different tenure — shows that demographic mixing on
  paper can coexist with "superimposed but seldom-connected" networks that meet
  "largely through friction and conflict" (p. 12). So strong physical/demographic
  attributes do not imply strong social-interactive ones; check them separately.
- **Use the profile to locate where a neighbourhood is weak**, then pair the
  diagnosis with the source's other lenses (the local public realm, the dual-scale
  typology) to decide what to do about it.

<a id="limitations"></a>
## Limitations

- **It describes, it does not define or bound.** The source is explicit that
  combining physical and social characteristics yields description, not a
  definition or a usable unit (p. 11). The spectrum cannot answer "what are the
  units, and where does each begin or end?" (p. 11).
- **It is a borrowed list, lightly presented.** The nine classes are reproduced
  from Galster 2001 and Power et al 2004 (p. 12); the Young Foundation does not
  weight the classes, score them, or specify thresholds, so the framework gives no
  built-in way to say a neighbourhood is "good" or "bad."
- **The social classes resist measurement.** Sentiment, social-interactive ties
  and political involvement are exactly the attributes the source flags as poorly
  captured by available proxies, and as increasingly strained by "increasing
  transience and social diversity" (p. 12) — so the most diagnostic part of the
  spectrum is also the least directly observable.
- **Urban-UK assumptions.** Like the rest of the source, the attribute classes
  assume formal tenure, recognisable building stock, and established services;
  several classes need adaptation for informal-settlement or displaced-population
  contexts.

> **Evidence note.** Single-source page (Young Foundation 2010, p. 11–12), so this
> sits at `limited` trust. The nine-class spectrum is the source's own rendering
> of Galster (2001) and Power et al (2004); claims here are grounded in that p. 12
> passage and its surrounding discussion of social neighbourhoods. A second source
> would be needed to weight or operationalise the classes and to promote to
> `usable`.
