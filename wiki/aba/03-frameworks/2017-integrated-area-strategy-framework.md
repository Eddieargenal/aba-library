# Integrated Area Strategy Framework

## Atomic question
How do diagnosis findings and prioritization outputs translate into a coherent, integrated area-level response strategy?

## Why this decision matters
Without an integration step, diagnosis and prioritization produce sector programmes rather than an area response — each sector team designs independently, and coordination is grafted on afterward rather than built into the logic. The EFL Basics First principle shows that foundational sequencing decisions (what to do first, and why) are not self-evident from a priority list; they require a strategy layer that links problems to interventions and defines who does what in what order. Strategies designed without a coherent theory of change produce uncoordinated activity in the same geography that cannot be evaluated as an integrated programme.

## Prerequisites
- From stage 2 (Neighbourhood Diagnosis): completed area profile across all five diagnostic domains (urban systems, HEVC, services, stakeholders, community dynamics)
- From stage 4 (Joint Prioritization): agreed priority tier list with issue-level scores and actor responsibility assignments
- From stage 3 (Coordination): functioning area coordination platform with sector representation that can review and endorse the strategy
- Minimum evidence standard: at least one completed community consultation informing the priority list; at least one session with the local authority confirming their engagement level and role in the strategy

## Decision domains

1. **Theory of change coherence** — does the strategy have an explicit logic chain from diagnosis findings → prioritized problems → interventions → intended outcomes? The theory of change must be traceable: for each major intervention, the diagnosis evidence that justifies it and the priority tier score that ranked it must be citable. A strategy without this traceability cannot be monitored or adapted.

2. **Sector integration** — are sector responses designed to complement and reinforce each other, or do they operate as parallel programmes sharing only a geographic boundary? Integration means shared timing (sector A's output is sector B's precondition), shared targeting (the same population is reached through coordinated entry points), and shared accountability (gaps and duplications are owned by the platform, not individual sectors).

3. **Local resource leverage** — are local actors, institutions, markets, and social structures integrated as delivery mechanisms, not only as beneficiary targets or consultation objects? Per the Barrio Mio and Katye cases, strategies that engage local actors only as recipients fail to build sustainability and miss the social capital that community networks provide. Local resource mapping must be explicit in the strategy.

4. **DRR alignment** — does the strategy reduce future risk as well as address present need, and are recovery and resilience investments sequenced appropriately? A strategy that addresses acute needs without a risk-reduction layer will recreate the conditions for future loss. Sequencing must follow Basics First logic: life-safety and exposure reduction before complex resilience investment.

5. **Coordination architecture fit** — does the strategy design align with the coordination platform established in stage 3? The strategy must assign responsibilities to named actors and mechanisms already present in the platform TOR. If the strategy requires coordination structures that do not yet exist, this is a gap to resolve before approval.

6. **Phasing and adaptability** — is the strategy sequenced into phases with defined outputs, decision points for adaptation, and explicit conditions under which the next phase begins or a phase is revised? Phases defined by funding cycles rather than programme logic produce strategies that cannot adapt to changing conditions.

## Decision logic

Apply a **readiness gate model** across all six domains before the strategy is approved for implementation. Each domain is scored on a 3-point scale:

| Score | Meaning | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | Explicitly addressed with evidence and actor assignments | Passes gate |
| 2 | Addressed but incomplete — specific gap identified | Conditional pass: named responsible party and deadline required |
| 1 | Not addressed | Gate fails — strategy cannot be approved until resolved |

**Approval rule:** Strategy proceeds only when all six domains score ≥ 2 and no domain scores 1. Domains scoring 2 are documented as conditional approvals with a named responsible party and a resolution deadline no later than 30 days after strategy launch.

**EFL Basics First application:** Within domain 4 (DRR alignment), the strategy must explicitly sequence foundational life-safety and exposure-reduction measures before complex resilience investments. A strategy that places complex resilience activities in phase 1 while life-safety gaps remain unaddressed fails domain 4 regardless of its overall score.

**Barrio Mio integration test:** At least one actor in the strategy must hold a cross-sector convenor role — coordinating across sector activities and maintaining the area theory of change over time. A strategy in which each sector lead is accountable only to their sector fails domain 2 (sector integration) at the gate.

## Red flags / override conditions

- Theory of change was developed by programme staff without community input → require revision incorporating at least one community validation session before approval; community-invisible strategies fail in implementation when local priorities diverge from programme assumptions
- Strategy proposes more than three sectors without an explicit integration mechanism (shared data system, joint coordination session schedule, cross-sector accountability matrix) → flag as high fragmentation risk; require integration mechanism before approval
- No exit or handover plan is referenced in the phasing section → require before approval; strategies without planned exits default to indefinite humanitarian presence and undermine local ownership
- The strategy's geographic scope differs from the boundary used in diagnosis → require reconciliation; diagnosis-to-strategy boundary mismatch produces evidence that does not apply to the operating area
- Actor responsibility assignments in the strategy name actors who have not agreed to those roles → require written confirmation from each named actor before approval

## Failure modes

- **Breaks when strategy design is run by sector teams separately and outputs are merged:** the merge produces a set of sector plans, not an integrated strategy. Integration cannot be achieved by combining independently-designed sector plans after the fact; it requires joint design from the theory-of-change stage.
- **Breaks when the theory of change is built before diagnosis is complete:** strategies designed from incomplete diagnosis embed assumptions that contradict field reality. The strategy must be built from diagnosis, not in parallel with it.
- **Breaks when phasing is defined by funding cycles rather than programme logic:** funding-cycle phases produce artificial milestones that force premature closure of activities and prevent coherent sequencing based on community readiness or risk reduction logic.
- **Breaks when the convenor role exists on paper but is not resourced:** a strategy that designates a convenor without allocating time, budget, and authority for that role will fragment during implementation as sector pressures pull each actor toward their own mandate.
- **Breaks when local resource leverage is treated as cost reduction:** engaging local actors as cheap delivery mechanisms rather than genuine partners produces shallow integration and community resistance that surfaces during scale-up or handover.
