# Risk-Based City / Neighborhood Screening Framework

**Source:** World Bank / GFDRR, *Building Urban Resilience: Principles, Tools, and Practice*  
**Year:** 2013  
**Lifecycle stage:** 1. Area selection and boundary definition  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to identify and prioritize high-risk urban areas for humanitarian response, disaster risk reduction, resilience programming, infrastructure planning, and investment decisions.

## Framework Overview

The **Risk-Based City / Neighborhood Screening Framework** helps teams select priority urban areas by analyzing where hazards, exposure, infrastructure weakness, service dependency, and urban growth patterns combine to create concentrated risk.

The framework is useful when an Area-Based Approach needs to be grounded in city-level resilience logic rather than only immediate humanitarian needs. It connects neighborhood selection with broader questions of urban systems, infrastructure vulnerability, service continuity, land-use change, and future risk.

## Core Framework Question

**Which city areas or neighborhoods face the highest combined risk because of hazard exposure, vulnerable populations, weak infrastructure, service dependency, and patterns of urban growth?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Hazard exposure | Floods, landslides, earthquakes, storms, fire, heat, drought, contamination, conflict, or other shocks and stresses. | Identifies where damaging events are most likely to affect people, assets, services, and infrastructure. |
| Population exposure | Number, density, vulnerability, poverty, displacement, tenure insecurity, age, disability, and social exclusion. | Shows where hazards are likely to affect the largest or most vulnerable groups. |
| Infrastructure weakness | Roads, bridges, drainage, water systems, sanitation, electricity, solid waste, public buildings, and protective infrastructure. | Reveals where physical systems may fail, amplify risk, or block response and recovery. |
| Service dependency | Reliance on specific facilities, networks, routes, markets, utilities, or institutions for basic services. | Helps identify areas where disruption to one service or asset could create wider humanitarian consequences. |
| Urban growth patterns | Informal expansion, densification, settlement on marginal land, peri-urban growth, and unplanned construction. | Identifies where future risk is increasing and where intervention can reduce risk before it becomes more severe. |
| Institutional and planning context | Local government capacity, planning controls, investment priorities, data availability, and coordination systems. | Connects area selection to realistic planning, financing, and implementation pathways. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a layered screening process:

1. **Define the screening area**  
   Identify the city, municipality, district, corridor, settlement cluster, or urban region to be screened.

2. **Identify relevant hazards**  
   List the main shocks and stresses affecting the city or target area. Include both sudden-onset hazards and chronic stresses.

3. **Map exposure**  
   Identify where people, housing, infrastructure, livelihoods, public facilities, and essential services are located in relation to the hazards.

4. **Assess vulnerability and infrastructure weakness**  
   Examine which neighborhoods or systems are least able to withstand, absorb, or recover from shocks.

5. **Analyze service dependency**  
   Identify critical facilities, networks, and routes that people depend on. Look for single points of failure or areas where service disruption would have cascading effects.

6. **Review urban growth trends**  
   Identify where the city is expanding, densifying, or developing informally in ways that increase exposure to hazards or service gaps.

7. **Rank high-risk areas**  
   Compare areas based on combined risk, not one factor alone. Prioritize places where hazard exposure, vulnerability, infrastructure weakness, and service dependency overlap.

8. **Validate findings with local actors**  
   Test the screening results with local authorities, communities, service providers, technical agencies, and humanitarian or development partners.

9. **Translate findings into area selection**  
   Use the risk ranking to select priority neighborhoods, define boundaries, sequence interventions, and connect humanitarian programming with resilience or investment planning.

## Risk Screening Matrix

| Screening question | Strong priority signal |
|---|---|
| Is the area exposed to one or more significant hazards? | The area is located in a floodplain, steep slope, seismic zone, fire-prone area, coastal risk zone, conflict-affected area, or other hazard-prone location. |
| Are many people or vulnerable groups exposed? | The area has high population density, poverty, displacement, informal housing, tenure insecurity, or marginalized groups. |
| Is critical infrastructure weak or failing? | Roads, drainage, WASH systems, electricity, public buildings, or protective infrastructure are damaged, overloaded, or absent. |
| Are services highly interdependent? | Failure of one asset, route, utility, or facility would disrupt multiple services or large population groups. |
| Is urban growth increasing risk? | The area is expanding into unsafe land, densifying without services, or growing faster than planning and infrastructure systems can manage. |
| Would intervention reduce future risk? | Investments or humanitarian actions could reduce exposure, strengthen services, improve access, or support safer development. |
| Can local institutions act on the findings? | Authorities, agencies, or communities can use the screening results for planning, coordination, or investment decisions. |

## Prioritization Logic

Use the framework to compare candidate areas across three levels of concern:

| Priority level | Characteristics | Recommended action |
|---|---|---|
| High priority | Multiple hazards overlap with vulnerable populations, weak infrastructure, service dependency, and rapid or unsafe growth. | Prioritize for detailed assessment, area-based planning, risk reduction, infrastructure support, and coordinated response. |
| Medium priority | Some risk factors are present, but exposure, vulnerability, or service dependency is less severe or less concentrated. | Monitor, conduct targeted analysis, and consider preventive or sector-specific interventions. |
| Low priority | Risk factors are limited, dispersed, or manageable through existing systems. | Do not prioritize for full area-based intervention unless new evidence emerges. |

## Design Implications

If an area is identified as high risk, the next design steps should include:

| Design step | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Conduct detailed area profiling | Understand the specific hazards, vulnerabilities, services, infrastructure, stakeholders, and capacities in the selected area. |
| Define risk-informed boundaries | Draw boundaries that reflect hazard zones, service systems, infrastructure networks, and affected populations. |
| Identify critical infrastructure and services | Locate the assets whose failure would create the greatest humanitarian or resilience consequences. |
| Link humanitarian action with resilience planning | Ensure emergency interventions do not undermine long-term urban planning or future risk reduction. |
| Prioritize no-regret interventions | Select actions that reduce immediate needs while also strengthening future resilience. |
| Coordinate with city authorities and technical actors | Align area-based work with municipal plans, infrastructure investments, DRR strategies, and service-provider priorities. |

## Boundary Selection Principles

**Use risk as a boundary logic.** Boundaries should not rely only on administrative lines. Hazard zones, drainage systems, transport corridors, service catchments, infrastructure networks, and settlement growth patterns may define a more meaningful area.

**Look for overlapping risk.** The highest-priority areas are usually where several risk factors coincide, not where one hazard exists in isolation.

**Connect current needs with future risk.** Area selection should consider both present humanitarian conditions and the likelihood that risk will increase if no action is taken.

**Include infrastructure and services in the analysis.** Urban risk is often shaped by the failure or absence of systems, not only by the hazard itself.

**Validate technical findings locally.** Maps and datasets should be checked against community experience, local authority knowledge, and service-provider information.

## Risk Alerts

**Hazard-only trap:** Do not select areas only because they are exposed to a hazard. Risk depends on the combination of hazard, exposure, vulnerability, infrastructure weakness, and capacity.

**Data-blindness risk:** Available maps may hide informal settlements, marginalized groups, undocumented residents, or service gaps.

**Administrative-boundary trap:** Official boundaries may not match the geography of hazards, infrastructure systems, or service dependency.

**Short-term response bias:** Humanitarian teams may prioritize visible damage while missing slower urban risks linked to growth, infrastructure stress, or service fragility.

**Investment disconnect:** If area selection is not connected to city planning or infrastructure priorities, short-term interventions may fail to support longer-term resilience.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Risk-Based Area Screening Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Screening area | The city, district, neighborhood, settlement, corridor, or urban zone reviewed. |
| Main hazards | The shocks and stresses considered in the screening. |
| Exposed populations | Key population groups, density patterns, vulnerability factors, and affected communities. |
| Infrastructure concerns | Critical weaknesses in roads, drainage, water, sanitation, electricity, public buildings, or other systems. |
| Service dependencies | Key facilities, routes, networks, markets, or institutions people depend on. |
| Growth concerns | Urban expansion, densification, informal settlement, or land-use trends increasing risk. |
| Priority ranking | High, medium, or low priority, with justification. |
| Recommended next step | Detailed assessment, area boundary definition, coordination, investment planning, or monitoring. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when area selection should be driven by **risk concentration**, not only by visible need or agency presence.

Prioritize areas where **hazards, vulnerable populations, weak infrastructure, service dependency, and unsafe urban growth overlap** and where humanitarian action can connect with longer-term resilience, infrastructure, and investment planning.
