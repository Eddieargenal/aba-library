# Urban Service Gap Analysis Framework

**Source:** IRC, *Urban Context Analysis Toolkit: Guidance Note for Humanitarian Practitioners*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 4. Joint risk and needs prioritization  
**Framework purpose:** Identify which services are unavailable, overloaded, unsafe, unaffordable, discriminatory, or physically inaccessible.

## Framework Overview

The **Urban Service Gap Analysis Framework** helps teams understand how affected people access — or fail to access — essential services in an urban area.

It is designed for the joint risk and needs prioritization stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), when teams must decide which service gaps matter most, which groups are most affected, which systems are under pressure, and which interventions should be prioritized.

The framework adapts the IRC Urban Context Analysis Toolkit’s focus on service delivery, infrastructure, stakeholders, power relations, resource distribution, governance, livelihoods, social networks, and access to services into a practical ABA prioritization tool.

It is especially useful for WASH, health, education, markets, transport, waste, electricity, shelter-related services, social protection, legal aid, and other urban systems where access is shaped not only by availability, but also by cost, safety, discrimination, distance, documentation, social networks, and institutional rules.

## Core Framework Question

**Which urban services are unavailable, overloaded, unsafe, unaffordable, discriminatory, physically inaccessible, or poorly coordinated — and which gaps create the greatest risk for affected people?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Availability | Whether the service exists in or near the target area. | A service cannot meet needs if it is absent or geographically out of reach. |
| Capacity | Whether the provider can meet current demand. | Services may exist but be overloaded by displacement, crisis, or urban growth. |
| Physical access | Distance, transport, mobility barriers, road conditions, disability access, safety of routes, and opening hours. | Urban services may be present but inaccessible to certain groups. |
| Affordability | Fees, transport costs, informal payments, debt, opportunity costs, and price increases. | People may be excluded from services because they cannot pay the direct or indirect costs. |
| Quality and safety | Technical quality, reliability, dignity, privacy, protection risks, safety, and user experience. | Poor-quality or unsafe services can increase harm even when coverage exists. |
| Discrimination and exclusion | Barriers linked to gender, age, disability, legal status, displacement status, ethnicity, language, tenure, religion, or social identity. | Service gaps are often unequal across groups. |
| Governance and accountability | Who provides the service, who regulates it, how users complain, and whether users can influence provision. | Service delivery is shaped by institutions, incentives, rules, and accountability. |
| Coordination | Relationships between public, private, community, humanitarian, faith-based, and informal service providers. | Lack of coordination can create duplication, gaps, competition, and unclear referral pathways. |
| Infrastructure dependency | Power, water, roads, drainage, transport, waste systems, buildings, and digital connectivity required for service continuity. | A service may fail because supporting infrastructure fails. |
| Risk sensitivity | How service access affects exposure to hazards, protection risks, disease, violence, or social tension. | Some service gaps create immediate life-safety or do-no-harm risks. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a service-access prioritization process:

1. **Define the target area and population groups**  
   Identify the neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone and the affected groups to be compared, including displaced people, host communities, renters, informal settlers, women, children, older people, people with disabilities, and marginalized groups.

2. **List essential services and systems**  
   Include WASH, health, education, markets, transport, waste, electricity, shelter-related services, legal aid, documentation, protection, social services, financial services, communications, and public spaces.

3. **Map service providers and responsibilities**  
   Identify public, private, humanitarian, faith-based, community, informal, and hybrid providers. Clarify who delivers, regulates, finances, maintains, and coordinates each service.

4. **Assess access barriers for each group**  
   Review whether services are unavailable, overloaded, unsafe, unaffordable, discriminatory, or physically inaccessible.

5. **Assess service capacity and quality**  
   Examine provider capacity, staffing, resources, infrastructure, planning, policy implementation, accountability, and consistency with local service norms.

6. **Identify infrastructure dependencies**  
   Determine whether roads, drainage, water networks, electricity, waste systems, transport, or buildings are enabling or blocking service access.

7. **Analyze coordination and accountability**  
   Check how providers coordinate with one another and whether service users can influence, complain about, or improve service delivery.

8. **Prioritize service gaps by risk and feasibility**  
   Rank gaps based on severity, scale, urgency, inequality, risk, community priority, system dependency, and feasibility of action.

9. **Define response options**  
   Decide whether the program should provide direct support, strengthen existing services, improve access, refer cases, advocate, coordinate, repair infrastructure, subsidize costs, or monitor risks.

10. **Validate priorities with communities and providers**  
   Confirm findings with residents, service users, local authorities, service providers, and community representatives before finalizing priorities.

## Urban Service Gap Analysis Matrix

| Service area | Availability | Capacity | Access barriers | Risk consequences | Priority action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Water supply | Is safe water available in the area? | Can providers meet demand? | Cost, distance, queues, discrimination, unreliable supply, unsafe routes. | Dehydration, disease, tension, household debt, unsafe water sources. | Repair, expand, subsidize, coordinate, improve accountability, strengthen provider capacity. |
| Sanitation | Are toilets, sewers, desludging, or sanitation services available? | Are systems functional and maintained? | Cost, privacy, safety, disability access, overcrowding, tenure restrictions. | Disease, GBV risk, environmental contamination, dignity concerns. | Emergency sanitation, desludging, lighting, maintenance, gender-sensitive design. |
| Solid waste | Is waste collection available and regular? | Can collection systems handle current volume? | Informal areas excluded, fees, blocked roads, unsafe dumping sites. | Flooding, disease vectors, fire risk, environmental hazards. | Collection support, municipal coordination, cleanup campaigns, drainage protection. |
| Health | Are health services available and reachable? | Are facilities staffed, supplied, affordable, and safe? | Fees, documentation, distance, discrimination, language, referral gaps. | Untreated illness, disease outbreaks, maternal/child health risks, mortality. | Referral strengthening, fee support, outreach, public health coordination. |
| Education | Are schools available, safe, and accessible? | Can schools absorb displaced or crisis-affected children? | Fees, documentation, language, transport, overcrowding, safety. | Learning loss, child protection risks, social exclusion. | Enrollment support, school WASH, safe access, learning continuity. |
| Markets | Are food, NFIs, shelter materials, transport, and services available? | Can markets meet demand without harmful price effects? | Prices, supply chains, credit, transport, discrimination, insecurity. | Food insecurity, poor shelter repair, debt, livelihood stress. | Market support, cash feasibility review, supplier engagement, price monitoring. |
| Transport | Are transport options available and safe? | Can transport meet demand and connect people to services? | Cost, safety, routes, disability access, curfews, checkpoints. | Reduced service access, livelihood loss, isolation, protection risks. | Transport vouchers, route coordination, safe access planning. |
| Electricity and energy | Is power or safe energy available? | Is supply reliable and safe? | Connection cost, informal connections, outages, fire risk, landlord restrictions. | Fire, health risks, reduced study/work, insecurity, service failure. | Safe energy support, coordination with providers, risk mitigation. |
| Shelter-related services | Are repair materials, rental options, technical advice, and tenure support available? | Can markets and authorities support safe repair or rental access? | Cost, tenure, permits, landlord control, market shortage, safety. | Unsafe shelter, eviction, overcrowding, exposure to hazards. | Technical support, rental mediation, permit coordination, market support. |
| Protection and social services | Are protection, legal aid, case management, and social support available? | Can providers safely manage demand? | Stigma, confidentiality, referral gaps, documentation, transport, language. | GBV, exploitation, eviction, family separation, exclusion. | Referral mapping, safe pathways, confidential communication, coordination. |

## Service Access Barrier Typology

| Barrier type | Diagnostic question | Example evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Unavailable | Does the service exist in or near the area? | No facility, no network, no provider, no public coverage. |
| Overloaded | Does demand exceed service capacity? | Long queues, rationing, provider complaints, reduced quality, waiting lists. |
| Unsafe | Does accessing or using the service expose people to harm? | Harassment, GBV risk, unsafe routes, poor lighting, conflict, contamination. |
| Unaffordable | Are direct or indirect costs preventing access? | Fees, transport costs, informal payments, debt, opportunity costs. |
| Discriminatory | Are some groups treated differently or excluded? | Denial of service, documentation requirements, language barriers, stigma. |
| Physically inaccessible | Are distance, mobility, transport, disability, terrain, or infrastructure barriers preventing use? | Long travel times, damaged roads, inaccessible buildings, no transport. |
| Poor quality | Is the service unreliable, ineffective, undignified, or technically inadequate? | Stockouts, unsafe water, untrained staff, privacy concerns, breakdowns. |
| Poorly accountable | Can users complain, influence, or receive information about services? | No complaint channel, no response to reports, unclear rules, lack of information. |
| Poorly coordinated | Are providers duplicating, competing, or leaving gaps? | Overlap in one area, no referral pathway, inconsistent standards. |

## Service Provider Mapping Table

| Provider type | What to identify |
|---|---|
| Municipal departments | Mandate, coverage, capacity, budget, maintenance role, coordination channel. |
| Public utilities | Network coverage, service quality, tariffs, repair capacity, expansion plans, accountability mechanisms. |
| Public facilities | Staffing, equipment, access rules, service hours, overload, referral links. |
| Private providers | Price, quality, market power, coverage, regulation, risk of exclusion. |
| Informal providers | Coverage, affordability, quality, safety, legitimacy, community trust. |
| Community groups | Support roles, mutual aid, communication channels, accountability, inclusion. |
| Humanitarian actors | Coverage, eligibility, standards, referrals, coordination, exit plans. |
| Faith-based and civil society actors | Service role, trust, coverage, referral capacity, inclusion risks. |
| Landlords and property actors | Control over housing, utilities, repairs, access, eviction risk, fees. |

## Joint Prioritization Criteria

| Criterion | Question |
|---|---|
| Severity | How serious is the harm caused by the service gap? |
| Scale | How many people or which groups are affected? |
| Inequality | Does the gap disproportionately affect specific groups? |
| Urgency | Does the gap require action now to prevent harm? |
| System dependency | Does this service enable other services or interventions? |
| Risk reduction value | Would closing the gap reduce hazard, health, protection, or social tension risks? |
| Feasibility | Can the gap be addressed through available actors, resources, permissions, and timelines? |
| Local ownership | Is there a responsible provider or authority that can sustain the improvement? |
| Community priority | Do residents identify the gap as a priority? |
| Do-no-harm | Could action worsen tensions, exclusion, market distortion, or parallel service systems? |

## Coordination and Validation Agenda

Use this framework to structure a joint service-prioritization discussion with local authorities, providers, community representatives, and humanitarian actors.

| Agenda item | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Area and population overview | Confirm the geographic area, population groups, and key vulnerabilities. |
| Service mapping | Review which services exist, who provides them, and where access gaps are located. |
| Barrier analysis | Identify whether gaps are due to availability, capacity, cost, safety, discrimination, physical access, quality, accountability, or coordination. |
| Risk consequences | Discuss how each service gap affects health, protection, livelihoods, social cohesion, hazards, and recovery. |
| Provider capacity | Clarify what providers can realistically maintain, expand, or adapt. |
| Infrastructure dependencies | Identify enabling works such as drainage, roads, lighting, transport, WASH systems, or electricity. |
| Prioritization | Rank gaps using severity, scale, urgency, inequality, feasibility, and system dependency. |
| Response options | Decide whether to repair, subsidize, refer, coordinate, advocate, strengthen providers, or provide temporary direct assistance. |
| Accountability | Agree how communities will receive information, report problems, and influence decisions. |
| Follow-up | Assign responsible actors, deadlines, unresolved questions, and coordination actions. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Sector prioritization | Shows which services create the greatest risk or blockage in the area. |
| Targeting | Identifies which groups face the strongest access barriers and why. |
| Modality selection | Helps decide between cash, voucher, in-kind, referral, infrastructure support, advocacy, or service strengthening. |
| Sequencing | Reveals dependencies, such as drainage before shelter repair or road access before waste collection. |
| Coordination | Identifies public, private, humanitarian, community, and informal providers that must be engaged. |
| Risk mitigation | Highlights service gaps that increase protection, disease, fire, flooding, eviction, or social tension risks. |
| Handover | Clarifies which actors can maintain services after project support ends. |
| Monitoring | Provides service-access indicators for baseline, reassessment, and adaptation. |

## Risk Alerts

**Availability illusion:** A service may exist on a map but still be inaccessible because of cost, discrimination, distance, safety, documentation, or poor quality.

**Provider-blind design risk:** Programs can undermine existing service systems if they deliver parallel services without understanding who already provides what.

**Infrastructure dependency risk:** Service gaps may not be solved by sector activities alone if roads, drainage, electricity, waste systems, or transport are failing.

**Equity risk:** Average access figures can hide exclusion of women, people with disabilities, renters, informal settlers, displaced people, undocumented people, minorities, or isolated households.

**Coordination risk:** Multiple providers can produce duplication in one area and neglect in another if service mapping is weak.

**Accountability gap:** If users cannot complain or influence service provision, technical improvements may not address the real access problem.

**Do-no-harm risk:** Changes in service access can affect local power, rents, market prices, social tensions, or perceptions of unfair aid distribution.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Urban Service Gap Prioritization Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | Neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone assessed. |
| Population groups | Affected groups compared in the analysis, including marginalized or high-risk groups. |
| Service map | Main services, providers, coverage areas, access points, and excluded zones. |
| Barrier analysis | Unavailable, overloaded, unsafe, unaffordable, discriminatory, physically inaccessible, poor quality, or poorly coordinated services. |
| Provider capacity | Capacity, constraints, accountability, and coordination status of relevant providers. |
| Risk consequences | Health, WASH, protection, livelihood, infrastructure, social tension, and DRR risks linked to service gaps. |
| Prioritized service gaps | Ranked service gaps with rationale and affected groups. |
| Recommended response options | Direct support, service strengthening, referral, repair, subsidy, advocacy, coordination, or monitoring. |
| Sequencing dependencies | Activities that must happen before others. |
| Accountability actions | How users will receive information, provide feedback, and influence service improvements. |
| Follow-up responsibilities | Responsible actors, deadlines, unresolved questions, and coordination needs. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when area teams need to decide which service gaps are most important to address, coordinate, refer, or monitor.

A strong urban service gap analysis should explain **which services are missing or inaccessible, who is excluded, why access is blocked, which providers are responsible, what risks result from the gap, and what response option is most appropriate for the area-based plan**.
