# Local Market and Service Provider Mapping Framework

**Sources:** IRC, *Urban Context Analysis Toolkit*; ALNAP, *Good Practice Review 12: Humanitarian Protection*  
**Years:** 2017 / 2019  
**Lifecycle stage:** 6. Program design and modality selection  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to identify whether local providers, suppliers, utilities, contractors, landlords, or community organizations can safely and effectively deliver, support, or maintain part of the response.

## Framework Overview

The **Local Market and Service Provider Mapping Framework** helps teams understand which local actors can contribute to an urban humanitarian, recovery, or area-based response.

It is designed for the program design and modality selection stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA). In urban contexts, markets and private providers often remain central even after a shock. People may continue relying on shops, transport operators, landlords, utilities, informal service providers, contractors, financial services, local NGOs, community organizations, and repair workers to meet basic needs.

The framework helps teams decide whether response activities should be delivered directly by humanitarian actors, contracted through local providers, supported through markets, linked to utilities or public services, or implemented through community-based organizations.

## Core Framework Question

**Which local market actors, service providers, utilities, contractors, landlords, or community organizations can safely, fairly, and effectively support delivery of the response?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Market actors | Shops, wholesalers, vendors, traders, suppliers, transporters, warehouses, and financial service providers. | Determines whether cash, vouchers, procurement, or market-based support is feasible. |
| Service providers | Water, sanitation, electricity, waste, health, education, transport, protection, communications, and administrative service actors. | Shows whether existing services can be supported rather than bypassed. |
| Contractors and skilled labor | Builders, engineers, plumbers, electricians, masons, drainage workers, waste workers, and repair teams. | Identifies local capacity for shelter, infrastructure, WASH, and public works. |
| Utilities and public systems | Municipal departments, public utilities, regulated providers, and public-private operators. | Connects response design to service continuity, regulation, maintenance, and sustainability. |
| Landlords and housing actors | Landlords, property owners, rental agents, tenant associations, land offices, and housing committees. | Helps manage rental support, repairs, tenure risk, eviction risk, and housing access. |
| Community organizations | CBOs, women’s groups, youth groups, disability groups, faith groups, mutual aid networks, and neighborhood committees. | Identifies trusted local structures that can support outreach, feedback, monitoring, and implementation. |
| Quality and accountability | Provider reliability, quality standards, pricing, safeguards, complaint mechanisms, and monitoring systems. | Reduces risks of poor delivery, exploitation, exclusion, or aid capture. |
| Protection and do-no-harm | Risks of discrimination, unsafe access, exploitation, GBV, coercion, corruption, gatekeeping, or social tension. | Ensures use of local providers does not create or reinforce harm. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a structured provider-mapping process:

1. **Define the response function to be delivered**  
   Clarify what the program needs: goods, repairs, service delivery, infrastructure works, referrals, outreach, transport, cash transfer services, monitoring, or maintenance.

2. **Identify relevant local actors**  
   Map providers, suppliers, utilities, contractors, landlords, community organizations, and informal actors connected to that function.

3. **Assess capacity and coverage**  
   Determine whether actors can deliver at the required scale, quality, location, speed, and frequency.

4. **Assess market and service functionality**  
   Check whether supplies, staff, transport, infrastructure, pricing, and demand conditions allow the provider to function.

5. **Assess access and inclusion**  
   Identify whether all target groups can safely and affordably access the provider or service.

6. **Assess protection and power risks**  
   Examine whether the actor may discriminate, exploit, capture aid, increase rents, exclude groups, or expose people to unsafe access.

7. **Compare delivery models**  
   Decide whether to use direct implementation, local procurement, service contracts, vouchers, cash, community grants, public works, technical support, or advocacy.

8. **Define safeguards and monitoring**  
   Establish quality standards, pricing controls, complaints mechanisms, referral pathways, contract terms, and performance monitoring.

9. **Decide the provider role**  
   Determine whether the actor should deliver, support, be strengthened, be monitored, be bypassed, or be engaged only through advocacy.

## Provider Mapping Matrix

| Actor type | What to assess | Possible response role |
|---|---|---|
| Retailers and vendors | Availability, prices, quality, stock levels, supply chains, credit practices, and geographic coverage. | Voucher vendor, cash-response market actor, supplier, procurement source, price-monitoring informant. |
| Wholesalers and suppliers | Supply capacity, delivery timelines, transport, bulk pricing, reliability, and quality standards. | Procurement partner, supply-chain support actor, framework agreement supplier. |
| Financial service providers | Coverage, liquidity, fees, identification requirements, user access, data protection, and reliability. | Cash transfer provider, payment agent, mobile money partner, remittance channel. |
| Utilities | Service coverage, reliability, repair capacity, billing, regulation, infrastructure condition, and maintenance systems. | Service restoration partner, infrastructure owner, technical counterpart, maintenance actor. |
| Contractors | Licensing, technical capacity, cost, safety practices, labor conditions, quality control, and past performance. | Shelter repair contractor, infrastructure works provider, WASH repair team, public works supervisor. |
| Skilled workers | Availability, skills, tools, wage rates, safety, quality, and local acceptance. | Repair teams, training participants, cash-for-work supervisors, technical support providers. |
| Landlords and housing actors | Rental terms, eviction behavior, repair willingness, tenant relations, tenure security, and rent-setting practices. | Rental support counterpart, repair agreement party, tenure-risk actor requiring safeguards. |
| Community organizations | Trust, representation, inclusion, capacity, governance, safeguarding, and reach. | Outreach partner, feedback channel, monitoring actor, community grant recipient, referral actor. |
| Local NGOs and CBOs | Mandate, capacity, legitimacy, access, safeguarding, technical skills, and accountability systems. | Implementing partner, referral partner, community engagement partner, monitoring partner. |
| Informal service providers | Coverage, affordability, quality, safety, legitimacy, and risk of exploitation. | Temporary service actor, access channel, risk to monitor, actor requiring standards or safeguards. |

## Provider Assessment Criteria

| Criterion | Guiding question |
|---|---|
| Relevance | Does the provider deliver goods or services linked to the priority need? |
| Capacity | Can the provider deliver at the required scale, quality, speed, and location? |
| Reliability | Has the provider shown consistent performance and continuity? |
| Accessibility | Can target groups safely and affordably use the provider? |
| Inclusion | Does the provider serve marginalized groups, displaced people, renters, undocumented people, women, older people, and people with disabilities? |
| Quality | Are goods, works, or services safe, appropriate, and technically acceptable? |
| Cost | Are prices reasonable, transparent, and stable enough for the selected modality? |
| Accountability | Can complaints, disputes, quality problems, and abuse be reported and resolved? |
| Protection safety | Could engagement increase exploitation, eviction, GBV, coercion, discrimination, or unsafe access? |
| Sustainability | Can the provider continue operating after the project ends? |
| Coordination value | Does working with this provider strengthen local systems rather than bypass them? |

## Delivery Model Decision Matrix

| Delivery model | When it may be appropriate |
|---|---|
| Direct implementation | Local providers are unavailable, unsafe, too weak, or cannot meet quality and protection standards. |
| Local procurement | Markets function and suppliers can provide goods at acceptable quality, price, and scale. |
| Service contract | A provider can deliver a defined service with clear standards, monitoring, and accountability. |
| Voucher model | Specific goods or services are available locally and quality or spending needs to be controlled. |
| Cash assistance | Markets are functional, households can safely choose providers, and access barriers are manageable. |
| Community grant | A trusted community organization can implement or manage a local priority with safeguards. |
| Public works | Labor-based activities can meet community needs while providing income safely and fairly. |
| Technical assistance | Local actors can deliver but need standards, training, design support, or quality assurance. |
| Advocacy | The main barrier is policy, regulation, exclusion, service neglect, or institutional decision-making. |
| Provider strengthening | A local service or market actor can meet needs if supported with capacity, supplies, coordination, or infrastructure. |

## Protection and Do-No-Harm Checks

| Risk | What to check |
|---|---|
| Exploitation | Are providers charging unfair prices, demanding favors, or abusing dependency? |
| Exclusion | Do providers refuse or underserve certain groups because of status, identity, documentation, disability, gender, or poverty? |
| Aid capture | Can powerful actors control access to provider lists, contracts, referrals, or benefits? |
| Unsafe access | Do people need to travel through unsafe areas, wait in unsafe places, or disclose sensitive information? |
| Eviction and rent pressure | Could landlord engagement, repairs, or rental support increase rents or eviction risk? |
| GBV and safeguarding | Could interactions with providers expose people to harassment, abuse, coercion, or unsafe reporting conditions? |
| Market distortion | Could the program inflate prices, favor one provider unfairly, or undermine local competition? |
| Labor risk | Are workers exposed to unsafe conditions, underpayment, coercion, or child labor? |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Modality selection | Helps determine whether to use cash, vouchers, contracts, direct works, grants, public works, technical assistance, or advocacy. |
| Partner selection | Identifies which local actors are capable, trusted, safe, and accountable. |
| Market-based programming | Tests whether markets can support cash, vouchers, procurement, or supplier engagement. |
| Service-system strengthening | Identifies providers or utilities that can be supported to improve access and continuity. |
| Shelter and infrastructure programming | Identifies contractors, landlords, skilled workers, and maintenance actors. |
| Safeguarding | Screens provider-related risks before contracts or delivery begin. |
| Monitoring | Defines what to track: price, quality, access, complaints, exclusion, performance, and protection risks. |
| Sustainability | Connects response activities to local capacity and service continuity. |

## Risk Alerts

**Market-assumption risk:** Do not assume markets are working because shops are open. Check prices, supply chains, quality, affordability, and access.

**Provider-capacity risk:** Local providers may be present but unable to deliver at the required scale or quality.

**Private-provider risk:** Private actors may remain central after a shock, but they may also exclude, overcharge, exploit, or discriminate.

**Landlord-risk alert:** Engaging landlords without safeguards can increase rent pressure, eviction, or tenant dependency.

**Community-organization risk:** Local organizations may be trusted, but they may also reflect local power imbalances or exclude marginalized groups.

**Contracting-without-accountability risk:** Service contracts can fail if standards, complaint channels, monitoring, and consequences are unclear.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Local Market and Service Provider Mapping Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Response function | The goods, services, works, referrals, outreach, or support needed. |
| Provider map | Local providers, suppliers, utilities, contractors, landlords, community organizations, and informal actors. |
| Capacity assessment | Provider scale, quality, coverage, reliability, staffing, supply, and delivery constraints. |
| Access and inclusion analysis | Which groups can or cannot safely and affordably access providers. |
| Protection risk analysis | Exploitation, exclusion, aid capture, GBV, eviction, unsafe access, labor, and data risks. |
| Delivery model recommendation | Direct implementation, procurement, service contract, voucher, cash, community grant, public works, technical assistance, advocacy, or provider strengthening. |
| Safeguards | Contract terms, quality standards, price checks, complaints channels, monitoring, referral pathways, and safeguarding measures. |
| Monitoring indicators | Price, availability, quality, access, complaints, provider performance, exclusion, and protection incidents. |
| Decision | Engage, support, contract, monitor, bypass, or advocate. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when program design depends on whether local actors can safely and effectively deliver part of the response.

A strong local market and service provider mapping process should identify **who can deliver what, at what quality and scale, for whom, under what safeguards, and with what risks to access, protection, equity, and sustainability**.
