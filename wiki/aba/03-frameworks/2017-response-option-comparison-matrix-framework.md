# Response Option Comparison Matrix Framework

**Source:** URAF, *Urban Response Analysis Framework*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 6. Program design and modality selection  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to compare in-kind assistance, cash, vouchers, service contracts, direct works, community grants, public works, technical assistance, and advocacy in order to justify the most appropriate modality or package for a specific neighborhood.

## Framework Overview

The **Response Option Comparison Matrix Framework** helps teams select the most appropriate response modality or combination of modalities for an urban Area-Based Approach (ABA).

It is designed for the program design and modality selection stage, after the team has identified priority needs, risks, market conditions, service gaps, stakeholder dynamics, protection concerns, and area-level objectives.

The framework prevents teams from choosing a modality based only on institutional preference, speed, donor habit, or sector tradition. Instead, it compares options against relevance, feasibility, market and service functionality, protection risks, cost-effectiveness, accountability, sustainability, and neighborhood-specific conditions.

## Core Framework Question

**Which response option, or combination of response options, is most appropriate for this neighborhood given the needs, risks, markets, services, protection concerns, operational capacity, and desired outcomes?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Response objective | The specific outcome the modality is meant to achieve. | A modality should be selected because it helps achieve a clearly defined result. |
| Market functionality | Whether goods, services, suppliers, labor, and financial systems are available and accessible. | Determines whether cash, vouchers, procurement, or market-based support is feasible. |
| Service-system capacity | Whether public, private, NGO, or community services can deliver the required support. | Helps determine whether direct delivery, service contracts, or system support is needed. |
| Household and community preference | What affected people consider useful, safe, dignified, and accessible. | Improves relevance, uptake, accountability, and appropriateness. |
| Protection and do-no-harm risk | Whether the option could increase exclusion, violence, eviction, GBV, aid capture, unsafe access, or tension. | Ensures modality selection does not create new harm. |
| Operational feasibility | Staffing, logistics, procurement, access, permissions, partner capacity, timing, and quality control. | Keeps design realistic and implementable. |
| Cost and efficiency | Total cost, delivery cost, value for money, speed, and administrative burden. | Supports transparent comparison between options. |
| Sustainability and systems fit | Whether the option strengthens or bypasses local systems, markets, governance, or community capacity. | Helps avoid short-term fixes that weaken longer-term recovery. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a structured modality comparison process:

1. **Define the problem and objective**  
   Clarify the need, risk, or service gap the program is trying to address, and define the intended outcome.

2. **List feasible response options**  
   Include all realistic options before choosing a preferred modality: in-kind, cash, vouchers, service contracts, direct works, community grants, public works, technical assistance, advocacy, or combinations.

3. **Set comparison criteria**  
   Agree on the criteria that matter for the decision, including relevance, feasibility, safety, equity, cost, market fit, service fit, speed, quality, and sustainability.

4. **Assess each option against evidence**  
   Use assessment findings, market analysis, service mapping, protection screening, stakeholder analysis, community feedback, and operational constraints.

5. **Identify complementary options**  
   Determine whether one modality alone is insufficient and whether a package is needed.

6. **Document trade-offs**  
   Explain the strengths, limitations, risks, costs, and assumptions behind each option.

7. **Select the preferred modality or package**  
   Choose the option or combination that best fits the area-level objective and context.

8. **Define mitigation and monitoring measures**  
   Identify safeguards, quality controls, feedback systems, and adaptation triggers.

## Response Option Matrix

| Response option | When it may be appropriate | Main risks or limitations |
|---|---|---|
| In-kind assistance | Goods are unavailable, unaffordable, unsafe to purchase, or quality-controlled delivery is required. | Can be slow, costly, supply-driven, logistically heavy, or poorly matched to preferences. |
| Multipurpose cash | Markets are functioning, goods and services are available, and people can safely use cash to meet diverse needs. | May not solve weak services, infrastructure gaps, protection risks, or market failures. |
| Sector-specific cash | A specific sector outcome is needed but households can purchase goods or services themselves. | Requires strong market availability, quality monitoring, and risk controls. |
| Vouchers | Markets function but spending needs to be linked to approved suppliers, items, or services. | Can restrict choice, distort markets, exclude smaller vendors, or create access barriers. |
| Service contracts | Existing providers can deliver services if contracted, subsidized, or supported. | Requires provider capacity, quality assurance, accountability, and contract management. |
| Direct works | Physical works are needed and must be implemented directly by the agency or contractor. | Requires technical design, permits, safeguards, maintenance planning, and quality control. |
| Community grants | Community groups can identify, manage, and implement local priorities. | Requires inclusion safeguards, financial controls, technical support, and elite-capture prevention. |
| Public works | Labor-based activities can provide income while improving community assets or services. | May exclude people unable to work, create safety risks, or produce poor-quality assets if poorly designed. |
| Technical assistance | Local actors need guidance, standards, training, engineering, planning, or management support. | May have limited impact without resources, authority, or incentives to act. |
| Advocacy | Barriers are caused by policy, rights, access restrictions, exclusion, tenure insecurity, or institutional decisions. | May be slow, politically sensitive, hard to attribute, or risky without strong evidence and alliances. |

## Comparison Criteria

| Criterion | Guiding question |
|---|---|
| Relevance | Does the option address the specific need or risk identified in the neighborhood? |
| Effectiveness | Is the option likely to achieve the intended outcome? |
| Timeliness | Can the option be delivered quickly enough for the problem? |
| Feasibility | Can the team implement the option with available capacity, access, permissions, and resources? |
| Market fit | Are goods, services, suppliers, labor, and payment systems available and reliable? |
| Service fit | Can local providers or systems support delivery or sustain results? |
| Protection safety | Could the option increase eviction, exclusion, violence, GBV, aid capture, tension, or unsafe access? |
| Equity | Will the option reach people facing the greatest needs and barriers? |
| Dignity and preference | Does the option respect affected people’s preferences and decision-making? |
| Quality control | Can the team verify quality, standards, safety, and appropriate use? |
| Cost-effectiveness | Does the option achieve the result efficiently compared with alternatives? |
| Sustainability | Does the option strengthen local systems, markets, services, or capacities? |
| Adaptability | Can the option be adjusted if prices, access, security, services, or needs change? |

## Scoring Approach

Use a simple scoring method to compare options.

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Weak fit or high concern |
| 2 | Partial fit with significant limitations |
| 3 | Acceptable fit with manageable limitations |
| 4 | Strong fit |
| 5 | Very strong fit and preferred option |

Teams can score each modality against the agreed criteria, then discuss trade-offs rather than relying only on the total score. A lower-scoring option may still be selected if it is the safest or only feasible option.

## Modality Package Logic

In urban areas, the best solution is often a package rather than a single modality.

| Package type | Example |
|---|---|
| Cash + technical assistance | Cash for shelter repairs combined with technical guidance and quality checks. |
| Cash + service support | Multipurpose cash combined with support to water, sanitation, health, or protection services. |
| Vouchers + market support | Vouchers for essential items combined with support to local suppliers. |
| Direct works + community grants | Contractor-led infrastructure works combined with community-managed small improvements. |
| Public works + DRR | Labor-based drainage clearing, access improvement, or debris removal linked to risk reduction. |
| Advocacy + legal support | Advocacy on tenure or documentation combined with referrals or case support. |
| Service contracts + accountability | Contracted service delivery combined with community feedback and quality monitoring. |

## Decision Documentation

For each selected option, the team should document:

| Decision element | Description |
|---|---|
| Selected modality or package | The response option or combination chosen. |
| Objective | The outcome the option is expected to achieve. |
| Alternatives considered | Other options reviewed and why they were rejected or deprioritized. |
| Evidence used | Assessment, market analysis, service mapping, protection screening, community feedback, or cost analysis. |
| Key trade-offs | Strengths, limitations, costs, risks, and assumptions. |
| Risk mitigation | Safeguards for protection, exclusion, quality, access, accountability, and do-no-harm. |
| Monitoring triggers | Conditions that would require adaptation, suspension, or modality change. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Modality selection | Provides a transparent basis for choosing cash, in-kind, vouchers, services, works, grants, technical support, or advocacy. |
| Response package design | Helps combine modalities when one option alone cannot solve the problem. |
| Budget planning | Clarifies cost drivers, delivery costs, and value-for-money considerations. |
| Procurement and partnerships | Identifies when suppliers, contractors, service providers, or community groups are needed. |
| Protection mainstreaming | Screens modalities for risks before implementation. |
| Monitoring and adaptation | Defines indicators and triggers for changing the modality if context shifts. |
| Donor justification | Provides a clear rationale for why a modality or package is appropriate in a specific neighborhood. |

## Risk Alerts

**Modality-first trap:** Do not start with “we will do cash” or “we will distribute kits.” Start with the objective and context.

**Cash-assumption risk:** Cash is not automatically appropriate because markets exist. Services, protection risks, access, prices, and quality still matter.

**In-kind-default risk:** In-kind assistance may be familiar but can undermine choice, markets, and efficiency if not justified.

**Voucher-restriction risk:** Vouchers can reduce flexibility and exclude people who cannot access selected vendors.

**Community-grant capture risk:** Community grants can be captured by local elites unless representation, transparency, and controls are strong.

**Public-works exclusion risk:** Public works may exclude people unable to work and should not replace assistance for households with high dependency or protection risks.

**Advocacy-underuse risk:** Some urban problems require policy, tenure, access, or governance change and cannot be solved through material assistance alone.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Response Option Comparison and Modality Selection Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Neighborhood or target area | The area where the modality decision applies. |
| Priority problem | The need, risk, or service gap being addressed. |
| Response objective | The intended outcome. |
| Options compared | In-kind, cash, vouchers, service contracts, direct works, community grants, public works, technical assistance, advocacy, or packages. |
| Comparison criteria | Criteria used to judge appropriateness. |
| Option scoring or narrative comparison | Strengths, weaknesses, risks, and trade-offs for each option. |
| Selected modality or package | The preferred option or combination. |
| Rationale | Why this option is better than alternatives in this neighborhood. |
| Risk mitigation | Measures to reduce protection, exclusion, quality, access, or do-no-harm risks. |
| Monitoring triggers | Signals that the modality should be adapted or changed. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the team must justify **why one modality or package is better than another in a specific neighborhood**.

A strong modality decision should explain **what problem is being solved, which options were compared, what evidence was used, what trade-offs were accepted, what risks will be mitigated, and how the team will know if the modality needs to change**.
