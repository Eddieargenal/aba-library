# Urban DRR Response Design Framework

## Atomic question
How should hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and capacity analysis shape the architecture of an urban DRR response?

## Why this decision matters
Urban DRR response fails in two characteristic ways: it is designed from sector mandates without integrating risk analysis (producing programmes that address present need but increase future exposure), or it is designed from technical risk analysis without community validation (producing risk maps that communities do not recognise or trust, and therefore do not act on). This framework ensures that HEVC analysis is complete and validated before strategy design begins, and that the four Sendai Framework priorities are used as simultaneous design lenses — not as a rigid sequence — across each stage of the response. Twigg's Basics First principle applies here: foundational life-safety and exposure-reduction measures must be addressed before more complex resilience investments, using the same urgency-and-role-scoping logic the Twigg framework describes.

## Prerequisites
- From stage 1 (Area Selection): confirmed operational area with documented hazard profile
- From stage 2 (Neighbourhood Diagnosis): HEVC analysis initiated (may be incomplete — this framework defines the minimum completion standard before strategy design)
- Minimum evidence standard before Stage 1 of this framework: at least one hazard source and one exposure source, even if preliminary; full HEVC completeness is assessed within this framework

## Decision domains

1. **HEVC integration** — are hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and capacity all assessed and integrated into response design, or is the response addressing only one dimension? Single-dimension responses (hazard-only or vulnerability-only) systematically miss the risk drivers they cannot see.

2. **Sendai priority alignment** — does the response design engage all four Sendai priorities (understand risk, strengthen governance, invest in resilience, improve preparedness and BBB)? Each priority corresponds to a design requirement, not a phase. All four must be addressed simultaneously at different intensities depending on context.

3. **System-level intervention logic** — does the response address root causes in urban systems (governance, infrastructure, land tenure, markets, mobility) or only immediate symptoms? Urban DRR requires systems thinking: a damaged water system is not only an infrastructure problem — it is a governance, tenure, and economic problem that requires a multi-system response.

4. **Resilience investment sequencing** — are foundational life-safety and exposure-reduction measures addressed before complex resilience investments? Per Twigg's Basics First logic: rank problem domains by urgency first, then by actor responsibility. No-regret measures (World Bank framing) — interventions that are beneficial across a range of future scenarios — should be prioritised where uncertainty is high.

5. **Enabling environment requirements** — does the response design account for the institutional, policy, and financial conditions required to sustain DRR gains? Twigg's enabling environment concept and the UNDRR Ten Essentials framework both identify governance conditions as prerequisites for community-level resilience, not optional add-ons.

## Decision logic

This framework operates in two sequential stages. Stage 1 is a completeness gate — strategy design does not begin until it passes. Stage 2 is a quality check run at strategy approval.

---

### Stage 1 — HEVC Completeness Check
*(Run before strategy design begins — at end of stage 2, Neighbourhood Diagnosis)*

| HEVC dimension | Minimum standard | If not met |
|---|---|---|
| **Hazard** | ≥2 independent sources (e.g., technical map + community hazard mapping) | Collect before proceeding to strategy design |
| **Exposure** | Geographically specific to the operational area, not regional or national averages | Collect before proceeding — regional data cannot drive area-level design decisions |
| **Vulnerability** | Disaggregated by at least gender, age, disability, and tenure/legal status | Flag as gap; proceed with explicit gap disclosure; design must address gap in data collection during implementation |
| **Capacity** | Local institutional and community capacities documented (formal + informal) | Flag as gap; proceed with explicit gap disclosure; capacity assessment must be integrated into stage 3 community engagement |

**Rule:** Hazard and Exposure gaps block strategy design. Vulnerability and Capacity gaps are flagged but do not block — the strategy must explicitly address how gaps will be filled during implementation.

---

### Stage 2 — Sendai Alignment Quality Check
*(Run at strategy approval — stage 5, Integrated Area Strategy)*

Score each Sendai priority dimension 0–3:
- **3** = explicitly and specifically addressed with evidence, actor assignments, and measurable indicators
- **2** = addressed but partially — some elements present, others missing or implicit
- **1** = mentioned but not operationalised — language present, no implementation design
- **0** = absent

| Sendai Priority | Design question | Minimum score |
|---|---|---|
| **Priority 1: Understand risk** | Does the response build local risk knowledge and risk literacy, not only import external analysis? Are communities co-producers of risk data? | ≥2 |
| **Priority 2: Strengthen governance** | Does the response strengthen local DRR governance structures, not bypass them? Are municipal and community governance actors integrated into delivery? | ≥2 |
| **Priority 3: Invest in resilience** | Does the response use DRR investment logic — no-regret measures, resilient infrastructure standards, Build Back Better — not only emergency spend? | ≥2 |
| **Priority 4: Preparedness and BBB** | Does the response integrate preparedness as a programme output (early warning, contingency planning, response capacity), not only address it as a risk? | ≥2 |

**Rule:** All four dimensions must score ≥2 before strategy is approved. Any dimension scoring 1 requires a design revision addressing the gap before approval. A dimension scoring 0 blocks approval entirely until addressed.

**Twigg Basics First application:** Use the five Thematic Areas (Governance; Risk Assessment; Knowledge and Education; Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction; Disaster Preparedness and Response) as a coverage check. Map each planned intervention to at least one Thematic Area. If any Thematic Area has zero interventions, flag for review — not every area needs direct programming, but gaps must be conscious decisions, not omissions.

**UNDRR Ten Essentials check (for programmes engaging municipal actors):** Map the response design against the Ten Essentials for Making Cities Resilient. Any Essential directly within the programme's scope that scores below the municipal baseline should be flagged as a design integration opportunity.

## Red flags / override conditions

- **Response design is driven by sectoral mandates without completed HEVC analysis** — halt design process; return to Stage 1. Sector-first design systematically excludes the risk dimensions that sectors cannot see from within their mandate.
- **Recovery investment does not include a Build Back Better standard** — flag for design revision. Per Sendai Priority 3 and the World Bank multi-pillar DRM framework, reconstruction that restores pre-disaster conditions without reducing future risk is a missed investment. Require a BBB commitment — minimum: construction standards that reduce exposure, not only restore function.
- **Enabling environment is ignored and all DRR responsibility is placed on community** — flag as unsustainable design. Communities cannot sustain DRR gains without enabling policy, governance, and financing conditions. If enabling environment factors are entirely absent from the design, gains will reverse when programme support ends.
- **HEVC analysis is used directly as-is from a national or regional source without area-level validation** — flag as inadequate evidence. National risk atlases cannot substitute for area-level HEVC analysis; they can inform it. Require at least one area-level validation method (community hazard mapping, KI interview, local authority consultation) before proceeding.

## Failure modes

- **Breaks when HEVC analysis is conducted by technical experts without community validation.** A technically accurate risk map that communities do not recognise produces compliance at best and rejection at worst. Community co-production of risk data is not a participation checkbox — it is the mechanism by which risk knowledge becomes actionable at the household and neighbourhood level.
- **Breaks when the four Sendai priorities are applied as a rigid sequential order** (understand risk → then governance → then invest → then preparedness) rather than as simultaneously active lenses applied at different intensities. All four are always relevant; their relative weight shifts by programme stage and context.
- **Breaks when urban systems complexity leads to analysis paralysis.** HEVC analysis in a dense urban area can theoretically expand indefinitely. A practical scoping limit must be set — define the systems to be analysed, the evidence threshold at which analysis is sufficient, and the point at which additional data collection yields diminishing returns for design decisions.
- **Breaks when DRR framing is applied only to new programme design and not to recovery investments.** In post-disaster contexts, recovery investment is where BBB either happens or is missed. If this framework is applied only at the beginning of a programme and not at each recovery investment decision point, the DRR framing will be lost under the pressure of reconstruction timelines.

## Concept anchoring

- [[../02-concepts/disaster-risk-reduction]] — the foundational concept underlying all five decision domains; defines DRR as systematic risk reduction across development and relief, not an add-on
- [[../02-concepts/risk-calculation]] — informs domain 1 (HEVC integration); hazard × exposure × vulnerability is the analytical core of Stage 1; risk = this calculation, not a synonym for vulnerability alone
- [[../02-concepts/resilience]] — informs domain 3 (system-level intervention) and domain 4 (resilience investment sequencing); resilience is the target state this framework is designed to achieve
- [[../02-concepts/build-back-better]] — informs domain 4 and Sendai Priority 3; BBB requires pre-disaster planning and investment standards — it cannot be retrofitted after reconstruction is complete
- [[../02-concepts/enabling-environment]] — informs domain 5; Twigg's enabling environment concept identifies the institutional, policy, and financial conditions that make community-level resilience sustainable
- [[../02-concepts/urban-systems-thinking]] — informs domain 3; urban DRR requires understanding how systems (infrastructure, governance, markets, land, mobility) interact and fail, not only how individual sector components perform
