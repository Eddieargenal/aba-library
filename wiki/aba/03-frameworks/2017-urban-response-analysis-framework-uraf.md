# Urban Response Analysis Framework / URAF

**Source:** Mohiddin, Smith & Phelps, *Urban Response Analysis Framework*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 5. Integrated area strategy  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to compare multi-sector response options and select an appropriate combination of cash, sector assistance, advocacy, technical support, and service-system support.

## Framework Overview

The **Urban Response Analysis Framework / URAF** helps teams move from assessment findings to an integrated urban response strategy.

It is designed for the integrated area strategy stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), after the team has completed area profiling, systems diagnosis, stakeholder analysis, risk mapping, and needs prioritization. The framework supports structured comparison of response options across sectors and systems so that teams do not automatically default to one modality, one sector, or one agency-driven activity.

URAF is especially useful in urban contexts where household needs, markets, services, infrastructure, governance, land, protection risks, and livelihoods are interdependent. It helps teams identify whether the best response is cash, in-kind assistance, sector-specific support, market support, technical assistance, advocacy, service-system strengthening, or a combination of these.

## Core Framework Question

**What combination of response options is most appropriate for addressing priority needs and risks in this urban area, given markets, services, protection risks, local systems, feasibility, and affected people’s preferences?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Priority needs and risks | The main needs, risks, and vulnerabilities identified through assessment and prioritization. | Ensures response options are grounded in evidence rather than agency preference. |
| Response objectives | The outcomes the response should achieve for households, communities, services, markets, or institutions. | Clarifies what success should look like before selecting activities. |
| Market functionality | Availability, prices, supply chains, traders, financial services, and purchasing power. | Determines whether cash or market-based support is feasible and safe. |
| Service-system capacity | Functionality of water, sanitation, health, education, shelter, transport, waste, protection, and administrative services. | Helps decide whether to support households directly, strengthen services, or do both. |
| Household and community preferences | What affected people prefer, can access safely, and consider appropriate. | Improves relevance, dignity, uptake, and accountability. |
| Protection and do-no-harm risks | Risks of exclusion, eviction, violence, GBV, aid capture, unsafe access, or market distortion. | Ensures response options do not create new harm. |
| Feasibility and operational capacity | Access, staffing, funding, logistics, partner capacity, permissions, timeframes, and implementation complexity. | Keeps the strategy realistic and implementable. |
| Complementarity and sequencing | How different activities work together over time. | Helps combine cash, services, advocacy, and technical support into a coherent strategy. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a response analysis process:

1. **Confirm priority problems**  
   Start with the prioritized needs, risks, service gaps, and system failures identified during assessment.

2. **Define response objectives**  
   Translate problems into clear objectives. For example: restore access to safe shelter, reduce WASH-related disease risk, improve access to services, stabilize household purchasing power, or reduce eviction risk.

3. **Identify possible response options**  
   List all plausible options before selecting a modality. Include cash, vouchers, in-kind assistance, service support, infrastructure repair, technical support, legal aid, advocacy, referral pathways, market support, and community-led actions.

4. **Analyze markets and services**  
   Check whether markets and service systems can meet demand safely and equitably if households receive cash or other support.

5. **Assess protection and do-no-harm risks**  
   Screen each option for risks related to exclusion, unsafe access, eviction, aid capture, gender-based violence, social tension, or unintended consequences.

6. **Compare feasibility and effectiveness**  
   Review each option against cost, speed, quality, coverage, operational capacity, sustainability, and alignment with local systems.

7. **Select a response package**  
   Choose a combination of options rather than assuming one modality is sufficient. The package may include multipurpose cash plus complementary sector-specific or system-level support.

8. **Sequence the response**  
   Decide what should happen first, what should happen in parallel, and what can follow later as conditions improve.

9. **Define monitoring and adaptation triggers**  
   Identify indicators that show whether the response remains appropriate, such as price changes, service access, protection incidents, exclusion patterns, or market disruption.

## Response Option Comparison Matrix

| Response option | When it may be appropriate | Key checks |
|---|---|---|
| Multipurpose cash assistance | Households can safely access functioning markets and use cash to meet diverse needs. | Market availability, price stability, safe access, financial service access, protection risks, and household preferences. |
| Sector-specific cash or vouchers | A specific need can be met through markets, but spending may need to be linked to a sector outcome. | Commodity availability, supplier capacity, price risk, quality standards, and monitoring feasibility. |
| In-kind assistance | Markets are not functioning, items are unavailable, prices are unstable, or cash would create serious risk. | Logistics, quality, appropriateness, storage, distribution safety, and duplication risk. |
| Service-system support | The main barrier is weak or overloaded services rather than only household purchasing power. | Service provider capacity, governance, equity, sustainability, and maintenance. |
| Infrastructure repair or upgrading | Critical infrastructure failure is driving risk or blocking access to services. | Technical feasibility, land/tenure issues, maintenance responsibility, environmental risk, and authority approval. |
| Technical assistance | Local actors or households need skills, guidance, standards, engineering support, or planning support. | Local capacity, uptake, quality control, safeguarding, and follow-up. |
| Advocacy | Needs are caused by policy, exclusion, access restrictions, legal barriers, service neglect, or institutional decisions. | Advocacy target, evidence, risk, political sensitivity, and coalition support. |
| Referral and case management | Individuals or households require specialized support beyond area-level activities. | Referral availability, confidentiality, safety, service quality, and follow-up. |
| Market support | Local markets or suppliers need support to restore supply, reduce bottlenecks, or meet demand. | Market actors, competition, supply chains, price effects, and risk of unfair advantage. |
| Community-led action | Local groups can identify and implement practical improvements. | Inclusion, representation, resources, safeguards, technical support, and accountability. |

## Response Analysis Criteria

| Criterion | Guiding question |
|---|---|
| Relevance | Does the option address the priority need or risk identified in the assessment? |
| Appropriateness | Is the option acceptable, culturally appropriate, and aligned with affected people’s preferences? |
| Feasibility | Can the option be delivered safely, on time, and with available resources and capacity? |
| Market and service fit | Are markets or services able to support the option without creating harm or distortion? |
| Protection safety | Could the option increase exclusion, violence, GBV, eviction, aid capture, or unsafe access? |
| Equity | Does the option reach groups facing the greatest barriers or risks? |
| Complementarity | Does the option work well with other sector, cash, service, or advocacy activities? |
| Sustainability | Does the option strengthen local systems or create dependency and parallel structures? |
| Scalability | Can the option be expanded, replicated, or adapted if conditions change? |
| Cost-effectiveness | Does the option achieve the intended outcome efficiently without sacrificing quality or safety? |

## Integrated Response Package Logic

URAF supports selecting response packages rather than isolated interventions.

| Package type | Example |
|---|---|
| Cash plus sector support | Multipurpose cash for household needs combined with shelter technical guidance or WASH hygiene support. |
| Cash plus service strengthening | Cash assistance combined with support to water providers, waste collection, health referrals, or administrative services. |
| Infrastructure plus community engagement | Drainage repair combined with community maintenance planning and risk communication. |
| Advocacy plus direct assistance | Legal or policy advocacy combined with temporary support for households facing eviction or exclusion. |
| Market support plus household assistance | Support to local vendors or supply chains combined with vouchers or cash for affected households. |
| Protection referrals plus area-level action | Individual referrals combined with safer access routes, feedback mechanisms, and community-level risk mitigation. |

## Sequencing Logic

| Sequence question | Design implication |
|---|---|
| What must be done immediately to reduce harm? | Prioritize life-saving, protection-sensitive, and access-restoring actions. |
| What systems must function for household support to work? | Strengthen services, markets, infrastructure, or referral pathways before or alongside cash or household assistance. |
| What can be done through local systems? | Support existing providers, authorities, markets, and community structures where safe and appropriate. |
| What requires advocacy or policy change? | Identify issues that cannot be solved through direct assistance alone. |
| What should be monitored for adaptation? | Track prices, service functionality, access, protection risks, feedback, and unintended consequences. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Modality selection | Helps decide between cash, vouchers, in-kind support, services, infrastructure, advocacy, or technical assistance. |
| Multi-sector strategy | Builds a coherent package across shelter, WASH, protection, livelihoods, services, and governance. |
| Cash feasibility | Tests whether multipurpose or sector-specific cash is appropriate and what complementary support is needed. |
| Service-system support | Identifies when household assistance is insufficient without strengthening services or infrastructure. |
| Protection mainstreaming | Screens response options for harm before implementation. |
| Sequencing | Clarifies which activities must happen first or together. |
| Coordination | Identifies which sectors, actors, authorities, markets, and service providers need alignment. |
| Monitoring | Defines indicators and triggers to adapt the response as conditions change. |

## Risk Alerts

**Modality-first trap:** Do not start by deciding “cash,” “in-kind,” or “infrastructure.” Start with the problem, objective, context, and feasibility.

**Cash-only risk:** Multipurpose cash may help households meet diverse needs, but it may not solve weak services, damaged infrastructure, protection risks, or market failures.

**Sector-silo risk:** Sector-specific activities may miss the way urban needs interact across housing, services, livelihoods, markets, and governance.

**Service-blind risk:** Supporting households without addressing broken services may leave core problems unresolved.

**Protection-blind risk:** A response that is technically appropriate can still increase eviction, exclusion, violence, GBV, or aid capture if risk analysis is weak.

**One-time-analysis risk:** Response appropriateness can change as prices, access, services, security, displacement, and local politics shift.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Urban Response Analysis and Integrated Strategy Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Priority problems | Main needs, risks, service gaps, and system failures to address. |
| Response objectives | Intended outcomes for households, communities, services, markets, or institutions. |
| Options considered | Cash, vouchers, in-kind, services, infrastructure, advocacy, technical support, market support, referrals, and community-led actions. |
| Market and service analysis | Evidence on whether markets and services can support each option. |
| Protection and do-no-harm analysis | Key risks, mitigation measures, and residual risk. |
| Selected response package | The integrated combination of response options chosen. |
| Rationale | Why this package is more appropriate than alternatives. |
| Sequencing plan | What happens first, what happens in parallel, and what follows later. |
| Coordination requirements | Actors, sectors, authorities, service providers, and market actors that must be aligned. |
| Monitoring and adaptation triggers | Indicators that show whether the response should continue, adapt, pause, or change modality. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework after assessment and prioritization, before finalizing the integrated area strategy.

A strong URAF-informed strategy should explain **why each response option was selected or rejected, how the selected package combines household support with sector and system-level action, what protection risks remain, and how the strategy will adapt as urban conditions change**.
