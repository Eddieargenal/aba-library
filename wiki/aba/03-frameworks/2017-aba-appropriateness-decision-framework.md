# ABA Appropriateness Decision Framework

## Atomic question
Is an area-based approach needed, feasible, and justified for this crisis, in this area, at this time?

## Why this decision matters
A programme is NOT area-based simply because it is located in a neighbourhood — the three core ABA conditions (geographic targeting, multi-sectorality, and participation) must all be independently satisfied and designed into the programme logic. Without this decision gate, programmes adopt ABA framing for coordination or donor-positioning reasons, generating coordination complexity without the integrated outcomes that justify it. Poorly justified ABA is worse than a well-designed single-sector response: it adds overhead, diffuses accountability, and crowds out simpler approaches that would have been more effective.

## Prerequisites
- A defined crisis or shock affecting a locatable population in an identifiable area
- Sufficient initial context information to assess all 9 domains (secondary data plus at least 2 key informant types)
- Domain 1 (Crisis and Area Definition) must pass as a prerequisite gate before scoring proceeds — if no coherent operational boundary can be drawn, stop here

**Minimum evidence standard:** Each domain score must be supported by at least 2 independent source types from:
- Direct observation
- Key informant interviews (disaggregated by actor type)
- Community consultation
- Household mini-survey
- Service provider data
- Secondary data / maps
- 4W / 5W coordination data

Flag any domain score based on only 1 source type as **weak evidence**.

## Decision domains

1. **Crisis and Area Definition** — Is the crisis and affected area clearly defined? Can a coherent operational boundary be drawn? *(Prerequisite gate — not scored; scoring does not proceed if this fails)*
2. **Geographic Concentration** — Are needs, hazards, and vulnerabilities geographically concentrated enough to justify area targeting rather than dispersed or household-only response?
3. **Multi-Sector Needs** — Are there multiple interdependent sector needs that cannot be effectively addressed through single-sector programmes?
4. **Urban Systems Interdependence** — Are urban systems (infrastructure, services, markets, governance) driving the problem in ways that require systems-level response?
5. **Stakeholder and Coordination Complexity** — Is there a complex actor environment requiring structured area coordination to prevent duplication and fill gaps?
6. **Local Capacity and Resource Leverage** — Are there local actors, institutions, markets, or resources that can be identified, engaged, and leveraged?
7. **DRR and Future Risk Relevance** — Is there a meaningful DRR opportunity or future risk dimension that should shape the response design?
8. **Community Participation Feasibility** — Is meaningful community participation feasible within the operational context, timelines, and safety constraints?
9. **Operational Feasibility and Safeguards** — Does the team have the access, staffing, time, data, systems, and protection safeguards to implement responsibly?

## Decision logic

Score domains 2–9 on a 0–4 scale (0 = absent, 4 = strong), multiply by weight, and sum. Maximum = 100.

| Domain | Weight |
|---|---:|
| Geographic concentration | 15% |
| Multi-sector need | 15% |
| Urban systems interdependence | 15% |
| Stakeholder/coordination complexity | 15% |
| Local capacity/resource leverage | 10% |
| DRR/future risk relevance | 15% |
| Participation feasibility | 10% |
| Operational feasibility/safeguards | 5% |

| Score | Decision |
|---:|---|
| 80–100 | Full area-based approach justified |
| 65–79 | Area-based approach justified with conditions |
| 50–64 | Hybrid model recommended |
| 35–49 | Sector/institution/household approach preferred |
| 0–34 | Do not use area-based approach |

## Red flags / override conditions

Even a high weighted score does NOT justify proceeding if any of the following apply:
- Operational access is insufficient to cover the full area safely
- No viable community consultation mechanism exists (participation will be tokenistic)
- Protection risks from area-based targeting are not mitigated
- No exit or handover plan is feasible within the programme timeframe
- Data quality is too weak to support defensible area selection
- Key authorities refuse to engage or actively obstruct the programme

## Failure modes

- **Breaks when ABA is selected for positioning reasons** — if the decision is made because the team is "working in a neighbourhood" or to satisfy a donor preference for area-based framing, the three-condition test will not be honestly applied; domain scores will be inflated to justify a pre-made decision
- **Breaks when feasibility dominates informally** — operational feasibility carries only 5% weight by design; when teams apply it as a primary criterion outside the scoring model, programmes end up in accessible, well-resourced areas rather than highest-need areas
- **Breaks in dispersed slow-onset crises** — geographic concentration (domain 2) will correctly score low when needs are city-wide rather than concentrated; practitioners may bypass the framework rather than accept a low score, producing an undocumented decision
- **Breaks when assessed without community or partner input** — a single team can produce internally consistent scores that do not reflect local reality; the 2+ source type minimum per domain is the structural safeguard against this and must not be waived
