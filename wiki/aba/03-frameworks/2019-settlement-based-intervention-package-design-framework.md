# Settlement-Based Intervention Package Design Framework

**Source:** Global Shelter Cluster / Urban Settlements Working Group, *Area-Based Approaches in Urban Settings: Compendium of Case Studies*  
**Year:** 2019  
**Lifecycle stage:** 7. Detailed technical design  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to design linked settlement-based intervention packages that combine household shelter repair, communal infrastructure, drainage, WASH, access, tenure support, preparedness, and local governance.

## Framework Overview

The **Settlement-Based Intervention Package Design Framework** helps teams turn area-level priorities into a coherent package of mutually reinforcing interventions.

It is designed for the detailed technical design stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), when teams need to move from broad strategy to practical implementation packages. The framework focuses on how different activities should be combined and sequenced within a defined settlement, neighborhood, or urban area.

The framework recognizes that settlement problems are rarely solved through one activity alone. Household shelter repair may depend on drainage. WASH improvements may depend on access routes and waste management. Infrastructure improvements may require tenure safeguards. Preparedness may depend on local governance and community organization.

## Core Framework Question

**What combination of household, community, infrastructure, service, tenure, preparedness, and governance interventions should be packaged together to improve conditions in the target settlement?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Household shelter repair | Repairs, retrofitting, habitability, safety, accessibility, privacy, and household-level recovery. | Improves living conditions and safety for affected households. |
| Communal infrastructure | Shared pathways, lighting, drainage, public spaces, communal facilities, retaining walls, and service points. | Addresses area-level problems that individual households cannot solve alone. |
| Drainage and environmental risk | Surface water, blocked drains, flooding, erosion, waste accumulation, slopes, and stagnant water. | Reduces disaster risk, disease risk, shelter damage, and access problems. |
| WASH systems | Water supply, sanitation, hygiene, solid waste, wastewater, and public health risks. | Strengthens essential services and reduces health risks across the settlement. |
| Access and mobility | Roads, footpaths, bridges, stairs, ramps, transport links, emergency access, and safe routes. | Enables access to services, livelihoods, markets, schools, health care, and emergency response. |
| Tenure and occupancy support | Tenure security, rental arrangements, occupancy agreements, eviction risk, land disputes, and housing rights. | Prevents shelter and infrastructure assistance from increasing eviction or exploitation risks. |
| Preparedness and DRR | Risk mapping, early warning, evacuation routes, contingency planning, community response, and hazard mitigation. | Reduces future losses and strengthens local capacity to manage shocks. |
| Local governance and maintenance | Community committees, municipal roles, service-provider coordination, maintenance systems, accountability, and financing. | Helps sustain improvements after project completion. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a linked-package design process:

1. **Start from the settlement diagnosis**  
   Use area profiling, systems mapping, risk mapping, stakeholder analysis, and protection screening to identify priority problems.

2. **Identify problems that must be solved together**  
   Determine which needs are interdependent. For example, shelter repair may fail if drainage is not improved, or sanitation upgrades may fail if water access is unreliable.

3. **Define the package objective**  
   Set a clear objective for the intervention package, such as safer living conditions, improved service access, reduced flood risk, or strengthened settlement resilience.

4. **Select complementary interventions**  
   Choose household, community, infrastructure, WASH, access, tenure, preparedness, and governance activities that reinforce one another.

5. **Sequence the package**  
   Decide what must happen first, what can happen in parallel, and what should follow after initial improvements.

6. **Check technical standards and safeguards**  
   Ensure each component meets sector standards and includes protection, environmental, accessibility, and do-no-harm safeguards.

7. **Define roles and responsibilities**  
   Identify what will be done by households, communities, contractors, local authorities, service providers, NGOs, and technical specialists.

8. **Plan maintenance and governance**  
   Clarify ownership, operation, maintenance, financing, and accountability for shared infrastructure and services.

9. **Monitor package-level outcomes**  
   Track whether the package improves settlement conditions, not only whether individual activities are completed.

## Intervention Package Matrix

| Package component | Design questions |
|---|---|
| Household shelter repair | Which households require repair, what standards apply, what support modality is appropriate, and how will quality be verified? |
| Communal infrastructure | Which shared assets are needed to improve safety, access, services, or risk reduction? |
| Drainage | Where does water flow, where are blockages, what upstream or downstream effects exist, and who will maintain the system? |
| WASH | What water, sanitation, hygiene, solid waste, and public health improvements are required at household and community levels? |
| Access | Which routes are critical for services, livelihoods, emergency response, inclusion, and safe movement? |
| Tenure support | Could assistance increase eviction, rent, land disputes, or exclusion, and what safeguards are needed? |
| Preparedness | What hazards affect the settlement, what early warning or evacuation measures exist, and what local capacity can be strengthened? |
| Local governance | Who will coordinate, decide, maintain, monitor, and resolve disputes after implementation? |

## Package Typologies

| Package type | Typical combination |
|---|---|
| Shelter safety package | Household shelter repair, technical guidance, tenure screening, accessibility adaptations, and referral support. |
| Flood-risk reduction package | Drainage clearing or upgrading, solid waste management, pathway improvement, community maintenance, and risk communication. |
| WASH and public health package | Water access, sanitation improvements, hygiene promotion, waste management, drainage, and service-provider coordination. |
| Access and mobility package | Footpaths, stairs, ramps, lighting, road repair, emergency access, and safe-route planning. |
| Tenure-sensitive recovery package | Shelter support, occupancy agreements, landlord engagement, legal referral, and eviction-risk monitoring. |
| Preparedness package | Hazard mapping, early warning links, evacuation routes, community response planning, and contingency coordination. |
| Governance and maintenance package | Community committees, municipal coordination, service agreements, maintenance plans, feedback systems, and accountability mechanisms. |
| Integrated settlement upgrade package | Combined shelter, WASH, drainage, access, public space, tenure, preparedness, and governance interventions. |

## Sequencing Logic

| Sequence question | Design implication |
|---|---|
| What must be addressed before household improvements are durable? | Drainage, tenure risk, access, structural safety, or service connection may need to come first. |
| What can be done quickly to reduce immediate harm? | Emergency repairs, drainage clearing, waste removal, safe access, lighting, or temporary WASH improvements. |
| What requires technical design or permits? | Infrastructure works, drainage systems, road improvements, water connections, sanitation facilities, or public works. |
| What requires community agreement? | Shared infrastructure, maintenance systems, public space improvements, access routes, or local governance arrangements. |
| What requires municipal or service-provider involvement? | Roads, utilities, drainage, waste collection, land issues, public services, or formal infrastructure. |
| What must be monitored after completion? | Maintenance, use, safety, access, exclusion, rent increases, service reliability, and protection risks. |

## Design Principles

**Package linked problems together.** Do not design household, infrastructure, WASH, access, tenure, and preparedness activities as isolated outputs if they depend on one another.

**Use the settlement as the organizing unit.** The package should respond to how people live, move, access services, manage risk, and interact with local systems.

**Combine household and area-level interventions.** Household assistance may stabilize families, but shared infrastructure and service improvements often determine whether recovery is sustainable.

**Design for sequence and dependency.** Some interventions only work if another system is repaired, protected, or governed first.

**Build in tenure safeguards.** Settlement improvements can increase land or rental value and may increase eviction risk if tenure issues are ignored.

**Plan maintenance from the start.** Shared infrastructure must have clear ownership, maintenance responsibilities, and financing arrangements.

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Activity selection | Identifies which activities need to be combined to solve area-level problems. |
| Technical design | Links sector standards to settlement-level conditions and dependencies. |
| Sequencing | Clarifies what should happen first, in parallel, or later. |
| Partner roles | Defines who should implement, manage, supervise, maintain, or govern each component. |
| Risk mitigation | Identifies tenure, protection, environmental, technical, and social risks across the package. |
| Community engagement | Shows where community input, agreement, labor, monitoring, or governance is required. |
| Municipal coordination | Identifies where local authority or service-provider involvement is necessary. |
| Monitoring | Tracks package-level outcomes rather than only activity completion. |

## Risk Alerts

**Single-activity trap:** One intervention may fail if linked settlement problems are not addressed.

**Infrastructure-without-governance risk:** Communal infrastructure may deteriorate quickly without maintenance arrangements and local ownership.

**Shelter-without-drainage risk:** Household repairs may be damaged again if flooding, drainage, or waste problems remain unresolved.

**WASH-without-access risk:** WASH facilities may be underused or unsafe if access routes, lighting, privacy, or protection risks are ignored.

**Tenure-blind risk:** Improvements can increase eviction, rent pressure, landlord control, or land disputes.

**Over-complexity risk:** Packages should be integrated but still implementable within available time, capacity, and budget.

**Case-study-copying risk:** Use case studies for inspiration, but adapt packages to the specific settlement, risks, systems, and capacities.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Settlement-Based Intervention Package Design Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target settlement | The neighborhood, settlement, block, corridor, or urban area where the package will be implemented. |
| Priority problems | The linked shelter, WASH, infrastructure, access, tenure, preparedness, governance, or service issues being addressed. |
| Package objective | The main settlement-level outcome the package is designed to achieve. |
| Intervention components | Household, community, infrastructure, WASH, access, tenure, preparedness, and governance activities included. |
| Sequencing plan | What happens first, what happens in parallel, and what follows later. |
| Technical standards | Sector, national, municipal, engineering, humanitarian, and protection standards applied. |
| Roles and responsibilities | Households, community groups, contractors, local authorities, service providers, NGOs, and technical specialists. |
| Risk and safeguard analysis | Tenure, protection, environmental, safety, accessibility, and do-no-harm risks with mitigation. |
| Maintenance and governance plan | Ownership, operations, maintenance, financing, community monitoring, and accountability arrangements. |
| Monitoring indicators | Output, outcome, quality, safety, inclusion, maintenance, service access, and risk-reduction indicators. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when detailed design requires a **linked package of interventions** rather than a single sector activity.

A strong settlement-based intervention package should explain **which problems are connected, which activities must be combined, how they should be sequenced, who is responsible, what safeguards are required, and how the package will be maintained and monitored over time**.
