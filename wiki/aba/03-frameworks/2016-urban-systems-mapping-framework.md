# Urban Systems Mapping Framework

**Source:** Campbell, *Stepping Back: Understanding Cities and Their Systems*  
**Year:** 2016  
**Lifecycle stage:** 2. Area profile and systems diagnosis  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to map how infrastructure, services, markets, governance, mobility, land, and social systems interact within an urban area.

## Framework Overview

The **Urban Systems Mapping Framework** helps teams understand a city or neighborhood as a set of connected systems rather than as a collection of separate sector problems.

It is designed for the area profile and systems diagnosis stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA). The framework helps teams identify how different urban systems interact, where failures cascade across sectors, and where interventions can create wider benefits or unintended consequences.

This framework is especially useful when humanitarian, recovery, or disaster risk reduction teams need to understand the relationships between physical infrastructure, service delivery, markets, governance, land use, mobility, social networks, and risk.

## Core Framework Question

**How do the major urban systems in the target area interact, and where are the critical dependencies, bottlenecks, risks, and leverage points?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Infrastructure systems | Roads, drainage, electricity, water networks, sanitation systems, solid waste, public buildings, and protective infrastructure. | Infrastructure shapes access, safety, service continuity, economic activity, and disaster risk. |
| Service systems | Health, education, WASH, protection, transport, waste collection, shelter support, social services, and administrative services. | Shows how people meet basic needs and where service gaps or failures are concentrated. |
| Market and livelihood systems | Markets, supply chains, labor, credit, transport, vendors, employers, and informal economic activity. | Explains how people earn income, access goods, recover after shocks, and cope with disruption. |
| Governance systems | Municipal authorities, line ministries, local committees, informal leaders, regulations, plans, budgets, and decision-making structures. | Reveals who manages the city, who controls resources, and how decisions affect services and risk. |
| Mobility and access systems | Roads, public transport, walking routes, checkpoints, safety barriers, cost of movement, and travel time. | Shows how people reach services, jobs, markets, schools, aid, and social support. |
| Land and housing systems | Land tenure, rental markets, informal settlements, land-use planning, building quality, eviction risk, and property relations. | Helps explain settlement patterns, vulnerability, investment incentives, and displacement risk. |
| Social systems | Social networks, identity groups, community organizations, mutual aid, trust, conflict, and exclusion. | Shows how people access information, protection, support, representation, and collective action. |
| Environmental and risk systems | Hazards, drainage basins, slopes, flood zones, heat, pollution, waste, green space, and ecological services. | Connects urban systems to current and future disaster risk. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a systems-mapping process:

1. **Define the urban area and purpose**  
   Clarify the geographic area and why systems mapping is being done. The purpose may be area profiling, program design, coordination, risk reduction, service strengthening, or recovery planning.

2. **Identify major systems**  
   List the key systems that shape conditions in the area, including infrastructure, services, markets, governance, mobility, land, housing, social networks, and environmental risks.

3. **Map system components**  
   Identify the main assets, actors, rules, flows, services, institutions, networks, and constraints within each system.

4. **Map relationships between systems**  
   Analyze how one system affects another. For example, drainage failure may damage roads, restrict access to markets, increase disease risk, and reduce school attendance.

5. **Identify dependencies and bottlenecks**  
   Determine where people, services, or institutions depend on a specific facility, route, actor, market, regulation, or infrastructure asset.

6. **Analyze cascading effects**  
   Examine how failure in one system could trigger wider problems across the area.

7. **Identify leverage points**  
   Find places where targeted action could produce wider benefits across multiple systems.

8. **Validate with local actors**  
   Test the systems map with communities, municipal authorities, service providers, market actors, civil society, and technical experts.

9. **Translate findings into design decisions**  
   Use the map to prioritize interventions, sequence activities, coordinate actors, reduce risk, and avoid unintended harm.

## Urban Systems Diagnostic Matrix

| Diagnostic question | What to look for |
|---|---|
| Which systems are most important for daily life in the area? | Water, sanitation, electricity, markets, transport, health, schools, livelihoods, housing, social support, and local governance. |
| Which systems are failing or under stress? | Overloaded infrastructure, unreliable services, damaged networks, unaffordable access, market disruption, weak governance, or social tension. |
| How do systems depend on each other? | Service delivery depending on roads, markets depending on transport, health depending on water, livelihoods depending on electricity, or housing depending on tenure security. |
| Where are the bottlenecks? | Critical routes, facilities, actors, permissions, infrastructure assets, supply chains, or decision points that limit system performance. |
| Where could failure cascade? | Drainage failure causing road blockage, market disruption, disease risk, school closure, income loss, or protection concerns. |
| Who manages or controls each system? | Municipal departments, utilities, private providers, landlords, transport operators, market actors, informal committees, or community groups. |
| Who is excluded from system access? | Renters, informal settlers, displaced people, undocumented residents, people with disabilities, women, older people, informal workers, or marginalized groups. |
| What local capacities can strengthen systems? | Skilled workers, community groups, service providers, local businesses, municipal staff, civil society, and mutual aid networks. |

## System Interaction Examples

| Interaction | Example implication for ABA design |
|---|---|
| Infrastructure ↔ Services | Damaged roads may prevent water trucking, waste collection, school access, health referrals, and emergency response. |
| Land ↔ Risk | Informal settlements on unsafe land may face flood, landslide, eviction, and service exclusion risks. |
| Markets ↔ Livelihoods | Market disruption may reduce income, increase prices, weaken food access, and increase debt. |
| Governance ↔ Services | Weak coordination between municipal departments may create service gaps, duplication, or delayed repairs. |
| Mobility ↔ Protection | Unsafe routes or checkpoints may restrict access to services, work, aid, and social networks. |
| Social systems ↔ Accountability | Trusted local networks can improve communication, feedback, targeting, and community engagement. |
| Environment ↔ Infrastructure | Blocked drainage, waste accumulation, or loss of green space can increase flooding, disease, and infrastructure damage. |

## Leverage Point Analysis

Use the framework to identify where targeted action can improve multiple systems at once.

| Leverage point type | What it means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Physical leverage point | A place or asset whose repair improves multiple services. | Drainage repair that reduces flooding, improves road access, lowers disease risk, and protects homes. |
| Institutional leverage point | A decision-making or coordination mechanism that improves system performance. | Municipal coordination group aligning WASH, shelter, roads, and waste services. |
| Market leverage point | A market actor, supply chain, or service provider that affects household recovery. | Supporting local vendors to restore access to shelter materials or hygiene items. |
| Social leverage point | A trusted network that improves communication, inclusion, or accountability. | Women’s groups or community committees supporting feedback and risk identification. |
| Information leverage point | A data gap or shared analysis that improves collective decision-making. | Shared service map showing gaps, overlaps, and priority infrastructure dependencies. |

## Analysis Principles

**Step back before designing.** Do not jump directly from needs to activities. First understand how the city system produces or reinforces those needs.

**Map relationships, not only assets.** The value of systems mapping is in showing connections, dependencies, and consequences.

**Look for cascading effects.** Urban problems often spread through linked systems. A small failure in one system can produce major impacts elsewhere.

**Combine technical and local knowledge.** Maps, engineering data, service records, and satellite imagery should be tested against community experience and local expertise.

**Use the map to prioritize.** Systems mapping should help teams identify where action will have the greatest practical effect.

**Update the map over time.** Urban systems change as people move, markets shift, services recover, infrastructure fails, or new actors enter the area.

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Area profiling | Shows the systems that shape needs, risks, capacities, and opportunities. |
| Activity prioritization | Identifies interventions that address root causes or multiple connected problems. |
| Sequencing | Clarifies which systems must be addressed first to enable other activities. |
| Coordination | Identifies which actors and sectors need to work together around shared dependencies. |
| Risk mitigation | Reveals cascading effects, fragile systems, and unintended consequences. |
| Local partnerships | Identifies system owners, service providers, technical actors, and community networks. |
| Monitoring | Tracks changes in system function, not only activity completion. |

## Risk Alerts

**Sector-silo trap:** Treating shelter, WASH, livelihoods, protection, and infrastructure as separate problems can miss the causes and interactions that shape urban vulnerability.

**Map-without-analysis risk:** Drawing assets on a map is not enough. Teams must analyze flows, dependencies, relationships, bottlenecks, and power.

**Technical-only trap:** Infrastructure data may not show affordability, safety, trust, informal access, or social exclusion.

**Over-complexity risk:** Systems mapping can become too broad. Focus on systems that affect the response problem and decision-making.

**Static-map risk:** A one-time map can become outdated quickly in fast-changing urban contexts.

**Unintended-consequence risk:** Intervening in one system without understanding dependencies can shift risk, create bottlenecks, or worsen exclusion elsewhere.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Urban Systems Map and Diagnosis Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | The neighborhood, settlement, district, corridor, or urban zone being analyzed. |
| Priority systems | The main infrastructure, service, market, governance, mobility, land, social, and risk systems relevant to the area. |
| System components | Key assets, actors, institutions, rules, flows, and constraints within each system. |
| Interdependencies | How systems affect and depend on one another. |
| Bottlenecks | Critical constraints, weak points, or single points of failure. |
| Cascading risks | How failure in one system could affect others. |
| Excluded groups | Groups with limited access to systems, services, resources, or decision-making. |
| Leverage points | Places, actors, mechanisms, or investments that could create wider benefits. |
| Program implications | Recommended priorities, sequencing, partnerships, coordination needs, and monitoring focus. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the team needs to understand **how urban systems interact before deciding what to do**.

A strong urban systems map should show **what systems matter, how they connect, where they fail, who controls them, who is excluded, and where targeted action can create wider benefits**.
