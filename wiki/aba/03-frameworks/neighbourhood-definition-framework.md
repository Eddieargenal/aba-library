<a id="decision-scope"></a>
## Decision Scope

Use this framework when you must fix a **working neighbourhood unit** for governance or
area-based programming and the obvious candidate boundary — a ward, an estate, a
statistical zone — does not by itself settle the question. The Young Foundation's
starting premise is that there is **no consensus answer to "What is a neighbourhood?"**:
the unit in play might be "a street of 100 people or a ward of 10,000" (Young Foundation
2010, p. 9). Because "rich descriptions of what neighbourhood can mean… [are] not a
definition of what it is" (p. 9), the source treats neighbourhood definition as the
central design problem that any targeted intervention must solve before it can act —
otherwise programmes "run the risk of public disappointment and policy failure" (p. 9).

Apply the framework at **area selection** and **neighbourhood diagnosis**: when you are
deciding where a programme's boundary should fall, and when administrative geography and
residents' lived sense of place diverge. The source frames the choice as a contest
between "two models… one based on top-down administrative geography, the other on mental
maps and subjective identifications" (p. 9–10), and its core move is to refuse the
either/or — combining three lenses rather than ranking them. Where "quasi-'natural'
boundaries of geography or community already exist" (p. 9), use them as a starting point
rather than imposing a new definition.

<a id="application-logic"></a>
## Application Logic

Combine **three lenses** to triangulate a working boundary, treating them as
complementary rather than competing (Young Foundation 2010, p. 9–10, 14):

1. **Administrative geography** — wards and Super Output Areas. This lens is "simple,"
   gives a "clear patch" for accountability, and ensures "no-one is left out," but it is
   "less rooted in lived experience [and] may not correspond to local realities"
   (p. 10–11). Use it for the complete, data-ready, non-overlapping frame.
2. **Personal identifications** — residents' mental maps and self-defined communities.
   This lens grows "from popular demand and local challenges" and is "likelier to be
   cohesive," but is complex, generates "competing claims," and risks leaving "citizens
   on the margins" out (p. 10). Use it to test whether the administrative unit matches
   how people actually experience the area.
3. **Local public realm** — shared public spaces, public buildings, and public services
   (parks, schools, GP surgeries, libraries). The source proposes this as a "third
   framework for understanding and anchoring neighbourhood" that can "build a bridge
   between the lenses of administrative geography and personal identifications," because
   mental maps "are often based around these common landmarks" (p. 13–14).

The synthesis: "it would be inappropriate to impose a single meaning of neighbourhood,
but our working definition for neighbourhood governance structures could combine
geographic and social characteristics, [and] be drawn together by the local public
realm" (p. 17). In practice, cross-reference the administrative zone against
resident-drawn maps and the location of shared service and civic anchors; where they
converge, you have a defensible unit; where they diverge, surface the gap for a
stakeholder decision rather than papering over it.

<a id="limitations"></a>
## Limitations

The framework **bridges but does not resolve** the underlying tension between boundary
*validity* (matching lived reality) and *completeness* (leaving no one out): the source
offers the public realm as a bridge while conceding "there is no perfect answer to the
question of how we should define neighbourhoods" (p. 13). Any operational boundary
therefore remains a trade-off, not a correct answer.

Definitions are also **contested and scale-dependent**. Neighbourhoods "vary in size,"
and "large and small conceptions… can be considered as complementary," so a single fixed
unit may misrepresent overlapping realities (p. 16–17). Self-defined units carry their
own risk: they can produce "more bonding than bridging capital, reinforcing social
divides" (p. 10) — a real hazard in diverse or contested urban areas, illustrated by the
source's own example of adjoining Asian- and white-majority estates (p. 9). Bottom-up,
mental-map approaches may prove "complex, overlapping and fragmented… [rather than]
capable of being gathered into any coherent neighbourhood governance framework" (p. 13).

Finally, the framework is **provisional and context-bound**. The Young Foundation labels
this proposal a "hypothesis" (p. 17), and the evidence base assumes formal housing
tenure, residential stability, and accessible local government — assumptions that may not
hold in informal-settlement, displaced-population, or weak-governance contexts.
