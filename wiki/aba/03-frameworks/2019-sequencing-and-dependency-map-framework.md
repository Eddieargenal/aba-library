# Sequencing and Dependency Map Framework

**Source:** ALNAP / ODI HPN, *Urban Humanitarian Response: Good Practice Review 12*  
**Year:** 2019  
**Lifecycle stage:** 8. Area-based implementation  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to identify which area-based activities must happen first, which actions depend on other actions, and how to sequence access, debris, drainage, WASH, protection mitigation, permits, market support, shelter repair, preparedness, and recovery activities so that implementation is coherent rather than fragmented.

## Framework Overview

The **Sequencing and Dependency Map Framework** helps teams move from an integrated area-based plan to an implementable sequence of activities.

It is designed for the implementation stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), when multiple sectors, actors, permits, community processes, infrastructure works, service systems, and protection risks must be coordinated in the same neighborhood, settlement, or urban area.

Urban humanitarian action is rarely a set of independent sector activities. Shelter repair may depend on debris clearance, drainage improvements, tenure clarification, building permissions, market access, and household-level protection checks. WASH improvements may depend on road access, utility coordination, drainage flows, solid waste management, and community maintenance arrangements. Preparedness activities may depend on prior risk mapping, safe access, public communication, early warning links, and agreement with local authorities.

This framework translates the logic of urban area-based programming into a practical sequencing tool. It helps teams ask: **what must happen before what, who is responsible, what risks block progress, and what can safely happen in parallel?**

## Core Framework Question

**What is the correct order of implementation for area-based activities, and what dependencies must be resolved before each sector or actor moves forward?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Physical access | Roads, paths, bridges, rubble, checkpoints, damaged structures, unsafe routes, and access to priority sites. | Many activities cannot begin until people, materials, service providers, and equipment can safely reach the area. |
| Debris and disaster waste | Rubble, damaged materials, hazardous waste, recyclable materials, UXO risk, disposal routes, and ownership of materials. | Debris can block access, drainage, shelter repair, markets, schools, health facilities, and safe movement. |
| Drainage and flood risk | Blocked drains, canals, culverts, solid waste, standing water, erosion, slope runoff, and flood-prone pathways. | Drainage works may need to precede shelter repair, road rehabilitation, WASH upgrades, or public-space improvements. |
| WASH and public health | Water supply, sanitation, waste collection, hygiene risks, vector risks, service continuity, and utility links. | WASH actions often depend on access, drainage, municipal services, infrastructure ownership, and public health priorities. |
| Protection mitigation | GBV risks, unsafe routes, eviction risk, tenure insecurity, family separation, access barriers, exclusion, and aid capture. | Protection risks may determine where, when, and how implementation can safely proceed. |
| Permits and approvals | Municipal permissions, land-use clearance, service-provider approval, building permits, environmental requirements, and security coordination. | Work can be delayed or reversed if permissions and mandates are not clarified before implementation. |
| Market and supply capacity | Availability of labor, materials, transport, suppliers, financial service providers, prices, and procurement routes. | Shelter, WASH, livelihoods, and cash-based activities depend on functioning markets and supply chains. |
| Shelter and settlement repair | Housing repair, safer construction, public infrastructure, building regulations, technical assistance, and household engagement. | Shelter work must be sequenced with access, HLP, WASH, drainage, safety, and technical quality controls. |
| Preparedness and response readiness | Early warning, evacuation routes, community response teams, emergency access, drills, communication, and local response roles. | Preparedness systems depend on risk mapping, safe routes, local actors, public communication, and municipal coordination. |
| Coordination and adaptive management | Actor roles, handoff points, shared workplans, dependency tracking, learning loops, and adjustment decisions. | Urban implementation changes quickly; teams need mechanisms to adjust sequence as constraints change. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a dependency-first implementation process:

1. **Define the implementation area**  
   Confirm the neighborhood, settlement, corridor, service catchment, drainage basin, market area, or administrative zone where implementation will occur.

2. **List all planned activities**  
   Include all sector and enabling activities: access, debris removal, drainage, WASH, shelter repair, HLP checks, protection mitigation, permits, market support, preparedness, livelihoods, community engagement, monitoring, and handover.

3. **Identify hard prerequisites**  
   For each activity, ask what must be true before it can begin. Examples include access, permission, technical design, community agreement, debris clearance, drainage correction, verified supplier capacity, or protection safeguards.

4. **Identify soft dependencies**  
   Identify activities that can start before another activity is fully complete but require coordination. For example, household shelter assessments may begin while drainage designs are being finalized, but repairs should wait until flood-risk constraints are understood.

5. **Map blockers and risks**  
   Record what could block the activity: unsafe access, lack of permits, contaminated debris, UXO, market disruption, landlord resistance, tenure disputes, drainage failure, community opposition, exclusion risks, or insufficient funding.

6. **Define what can run in parallel**  
   Separate tasks that are sequential from tasks that can proceed simultaneously. Community engagement, risk communication, supplier mapping, and permit preparation can often start early while technical assessments continue.

7. **Create the dependency map**  
   Draw the chain of activities using dependency arrows. Mark the critical path, parallel tracks, decision points, and required coordination handoffs.

8. **Validate with local actors**  
   Review the map with community representatives, municipal departments, utilities, service providers, protection actors, engineers, suppliers, and implementing partners.

9. **Convert the map into a workplan**  
   Translate the dependency map into phased implementation: immediate actions, enabling works, main works, household or community-level interventions, preparedness actions, monitoring, and handover.

10. **Review and adapt regularly**  
   Update the sequence as new risks, delays, community feedback, market changes, weather conditions, security constraints, or municipal decisions emerge.

## Sequencing and Dependency Design Table

| Activity area | Typical dependencies | Key implementation questions |
|---|---|---|
| Community engagement | Stakeholder mapping, trusted entry points, language access, inclusion plan, coordination with local leaders. | Who needs to understand and validate the sequence before work starts? |
| Access clearance | Security clearance, debris assessment, road/path condition, heavy equipment access, permissions, protection risk review. | Can teams, residents, suppliers, emergency responders, and equipment safely reach the area? |
| Debris clearance | Ownership of debris, HLP concerns, hazardous material screening, UXO check where relevant, disposal site, labor safety. | What rubble must be removed first to unlock access, drainage, markets, shelter repair, and services? |
| Drainage works | Topographic understanding, flood pathways, solid waste removal, municipal/public works involvement, maintenance plan. | Does drainage need to be cleared or repaired before shelter, WASH, or road works proceed? |
| WASH restoration | Access to infrastructure, utility permissions, drainage status, water-quality testing, public health priorities, maintenance responsibility. | Which WASH actions are life-saving and which depend on infrastructure or authority decisions? |
| Protection mitigation | Protection analysis, safe access routes, privacy, GBV risk review, tenure review, referral pathways, complaint channels. | What safeguards must be in place before works expose people to additional harm? |
| Permit and authority approval | Municipal mandate mapping, technical drawings, land-use review, building regulations, utility approval, environmental clearance. | Which activities cannot legally or practically proceed without approval? |
| Market support | Market assessment, supplier capacity, price monitoring, transport access, financial service providers, procurement rules. | Can local markets supply materials and services without creating harmful price or access effects? |
| Shelter repair | Household assessment, tenure/HLP review, technical standards, drainage status, material availability, trained labor, quality control. | What must happen before household or block-level repair is safe and technically sound? |
| Preparedness actions | Risk mapping, early warning links, safe routes, community roles, municipal response coordination, communication plan. | What preparedness systems should be activated before, during, and after physical works? |
| Handover and maintenance | Owner identification, maintenance roles, municipal/service-provider commitment, community monitoring, budget or resource plan. | Who will maintain the works, systems, routes, drains, facilities, or preparedness arrangements after the project? |

## Dependency Mapping Template

| Activity | Must happen before this | Can happen in parallel | Main blocker | Responsible actor | Decision point |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Access route opening | Security/access clearance; debris risk screening; municipal or landowner permission. | Community communication; supplier mapping; protection route review. | Blocked road, unsafe structures, checkpoint, hazardous debris. | Public works, implementing partner, community committee. | Route confirmed safe for workers, residents, and materials. |
| Debris clearance | Access agreement; hazardous material/UXO screening; disposal or reuse plan. | Cash-for-work planning; recyclable material mapping; HLP documentation. | Ownership disputes, unsafe rubble, lack of equipment, disposal constraints. | Shelter/public works/WASH actors with local authority. | Clearance unlocks drainage, shelter repair, market access, or service restoration. |
| Drainage clearing | Debris removed from key channels; public works approval; solid waste coordination. | Hygiene promotion; flood-risk communication; community maintenance planning. | Blocked culverts, lack of disposal route, downstream effects. | WASH, public works, community maintenance groups. | Drainage no longer undermines shelter or road works. |
| WASH service repair | Access to pipes/points/facilities; utility approval; drainage and waste risks reviewed. | Hygiene promotion; water-quality monitoring; feedback mechanisms. | Utility restrictions, damaged network, contamination, lack of spare parts. | WASH actor, utility, municipality. | Safe water/sanitation service restored or interim option approved. |
| Shelter repair | HLP check; technical assessment; drainage risk reviewed; material/labor availability. | Training of masons; procurement; household communication. | Unsafe site, unresolved tenure, flood risk, unaffordable materials. | Shelter actor, household, technical team, municipality. | Repair package approved for each household/block. |
| Market or cash support | Market functionality check; supply chain access; protection and fraud risk review. | Supplier registration; price monitoring; community orientation. | Inflation, weak supply, exploitation, access barriers. | CVA/livelihoods team, finance, suppliers. | Market support will not undermine access or inflate critical goods. |
| Preparedness and evacuation | Risk map; safe routes; warning channels; municipal role agreement. | Community drills; route signage; household preparedness. | Unsafe routes, inaccessible shelters, unclear authority roles. | DRR actor, municipality, community groups. | Preparedness system tested and revised. |

## Critical Path Examples

| Scenario | Suggested critical path |
|---|---|
| Flood-prone neighborhood with damaged shelters | Community entry and risk mapping → drainage and solid waste assessment → debris removal from drains/access routes → drainage clearing/repair → WASH risk mitigation → shelter repair with flood-safe guidance → preparedness and maintenance plan. |
| Earthquake-affected dense settlement | Access and safety screening → hazardous debris/UXO check if relevant → main route clearance → rubble ownership/HLP documentation → priority service restoration → shelter technical assessment → staged repair/reconstruction → public-space and evacuation planning. |
| Informal settlement with tenure risk | Stakeholder and HLP analysis → protection and eviction-risk mitigation → authority negotiation → minimum safe access/WASH works → household-level assistance with safeguards → longer-term settlement upgrading or referral pathway. |
| Market-dependent shelter recovery | Access and supplier mapping → market capacity and price monitoring → technical standards and labor training → phased cash/material support → quality checks → supplier accountability and household feedback. |
| High protection-risk urban area | Protection analysis → safe engagement plan → route and timing review → confidential referral pathways → targeted service and shelter support → monitoring for backlash, exclusion, GBV, or aid capture. |

## Implementation Sequencing Checklist

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| What is the first activity that unlocks the greatest number of other activities? | Helps identify the critical path and avoid scattered implementation. |
| Which activities are blocked by physical access? | Prevents planning shelter, WASH, or market support before materials and services can reach the area. |
| Which activities are blocked by debris, rubble, waste, or hazardous materials? | Avoids unsafe works and identifies where clearance must precede repair or service restoration. |
| Which activities are blocked by drainage or flood risk? | Prevents repairing shelters or roads that will be damaged again by unresolved water flow. |
| Which activities require municipal, utility, landowner, or service-provider permission? | Reduces delay, conflict, duplication, or removal of works after implementation. |
| Which activities create protection risks if done too early? | Ensures that implementation does not increase exposure to violence, eviction, exclusion, or exploitation. |
| Which activities depend on functioning markets or suppliers? | Protects program quality and avoids market distortion, inflation, or procurement failure. |
| Which activities can be done in parallel to save time? | Enables efficient implementation without forcing false sequencing. |
| Which activities require community validation before execution? | Supports legitimacy, transparency, and safer implementation. |
| Which handover and maintenance arrangements must be agreed before construction or service work starts? | Prevents infrastructure, drains, WASH systems, or preparedness tools from failing after project exit. |

## Coordination Meeting Agenda

Use the framework to structure weekly or biweekly area-based implementation meetings.

| Agenda item | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Review area-level objective | Confirm the shared outcome for the neighborhood, settlement, or zone. |
| Update dependency map | Identify activities completed, delayed, blocked, or newly dependent on other actions. |
| Confirm critical path | Agree on the next actions that unlock the most progress. |
| Resolve blockers | Address permits, access, security, procurement, HLP, protection, drainage, or coordination constraints. |
| Check sector interactions | Review how shelter, WASH, protection, livelihoods, health, markets, and preparedness affect each other. |
| Validate community feedback | Integrate resident concerns, complaints, exclusion signals, and implementation observations. |
| Adjust sequencing | Move activities forward, delay activities, run parallel workstreams, or revise assumptions. |
| Assign action points | Name responsible actors, deadlines, decision points, and follow-up evidence. |
| Review handover risks | Confirm maintenance, ownership, local authority roles, and post-project monitoring. |

## Design and Management Principles

**Sequence enabling works first.** Access, debris, drainage, permissions, market capacity, protection safeguards, and community agreement often determine whether sector activities can proceed safely.

**Avoid false sector independence.** In dense urban areas, shelter, WASH, drainage, protection, markets, infrastructure, and preparedness are physically and socially linked.

**Work with existing structures.** Engage municipal departments, utilities, community committees, local suppliers, service providers, and informal systems instead of creating isolated implementation tracks.

**Use adaptive management.** Treat the sequence as a live tool. Urban conditions change quickly, and the workplan should be reviewed as new information emerges.

**Keep visible outputs incremental.** Early tangible improvements can build confidence, but they should not undermine the longer sequence or create downstream risks.

**Protect the critical path.** The critical path is the chain of dependencies that determines whether the overall area plan can progress. It should be reviewed at every coordination meeting.

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Workplan structure | Converts a sector package into a phased, dependency-aware implementation plan. |
| Coordination model | Identifies which actors must coordinate around each dependency and decision point. |
| Procurement planning | Shows when materials, labor, suppliers, equipment, and service contracts are needed. |
| Engineering design | Ensures drainage, debris, access, WASH, and shelter decisions are technically sequenced. |
| Protection mainstreaming | Identifies when safeguards must precede or modify implementation. |
| Municipal engagement | Clarifies which permits, approvals, public works links, and maintenance roles are needed before works begin. |
| Market support | Links cash, vouchers, procurement, and livelihoods to functioning supply chains and access. |
| Preparedness planning | Ensures evacuation, early warning, and emergency access are integrated into implementation rather than added at the end. |
| Monitoring | Tracks whether dependencies are resolved and whether the sequence remains appropriate. |

## Risk Alerts

**Fragmentation risk:** Sector teams may implement independently and create avoidable failures, such as repairing shelters before drainage is cleared.

**Access-blind risk:** Activities may be scheduled before roads, pathways, permits, or safety conditions allow materials and people to move.

**Debris/HLP risk:** Clearing rubble without checking ownership, personal belongings, hazardous materials, or tenure evidence may cause harm or disputes.

**Drainage-before-shelter risk:** In flood-prone areas, shelter repair may be wasted if water flow, waste blockage, or drainage capacity is not addressed first.

**Permit-delay risk:** Activities may stall if municipal approvals, utility permissions, landowner consent, or environmental requirements are treated as administrative details rather than dependencies.

**Protection-sequencing risk:** Early works can expose people to GBV, eviction, retaliation, exclusion, theft, or unsafe movement if safeguards are not in place.

**Market distortion risk:** Cash or material programs can raise prices or exclude poorer households if market capacity and access are not checked before scale-up.

**Over-planning risk:** A dependency map should not become a static plan. It must be updated as conditions change.

**Parallel-work confusion risk:** Running activities in parallel without clear interfaces can create duplication, unsafe overlaps, or unresolved handoffs.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Sequencing and Dependency Map Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | The neighborhood, settlement, corridor, catchment, or zone covered by the implementation sequence. |
| Area objective | The integrated outcome the sequence is designed to achieve. |
| Activity inventory | All planned sector, enabling, protection, coordination, and handover activities. |
| Dependency map | A visual or tabular map showing what must happen before what. |
| Critical path | The minimum sequence of actions that unlocks the main area-based intervention package. |
| Parallel tracks | Activities that can safely proceed at the same time. |
| Blockers and risks | Access, debris, drainage, WASH, HLP, protection, permit, market, security, or capacity constraints. |
| Decision points | Moments when implementation should pause, adapt, or move to the next phase. |
| Responsible actors | Named municipal, community, humanitarian, utility, supplier, or service-provider roles. |
| Monitoring triggers | Signals that sequencing needs revision, including delays, new hazards, complaints, market changes, weather, or safety concerns. |
| Handover dependencies | Ownership, maintenance, budget, authority, and community arrangements needed before exit. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework before implementation starts and update it throughout delivery.

A strong sequencing and dependency map should explain **what must happen first, what can happen in parallel, what is blocked, who must act, what decisions are needed, and how the sequence prevents fragmented sector delivery**.

For flood-prone areas, for example, the framework should make visible whether **drainage clearing, debris removal, WASH risk mitigation, and access improvements must precede shelter repair**. For dense urban settlements, it should show how physical works, protection safeguards, market support, permits, community engagement, and preparedness activities depend on each other.
