# Area Selection Framework

## Atomic question
Given a candidate pool of urban areas, which should be prioritized for ABA programming?

## Why this decision matters
Area selection is the foundational geographic commitment of an ABA programme — it determines who is reached and who is not, and it cannot be easily reversed once programming begins. Selection driven by donor geography, operational convenience, or administrative boundaries rather than severity and risk produces programmes that miss the most vulnerable and undermine the evidence base for ABA as an approach. The EFL quick-scan model shows that without a structured multi-criteria comparison across candidate areas, selection defaults to whichever area is most legible to external actors, not most in need.

## Prerequisites
- From prior stage (ABA Appropriateness Decision): confirmed that an area-based approach is warranted for this crisis context (score ≥50 on appropriateness framework)
- A candidate pool of at least 2 areas to compare — if only one area is under consideration, apply the red-flag checklist only, not the scoring model
- Minimum baseline data: population estimate, at least one hazard type identified, at least one administrative or social boundary candidate for each area
- If no baseline data exists for any candidate area: conduct rapid key-informant profiling before applying this framework (see REACH/UNHCR practical guide)

## Decision domains

1. **Humanitarian severity and risk concentration** (30%) — Are acute humanitarian needs or disaster risk geographically concentrated in this area at a level that justifies spatial targeting over dispersed or household-only response? Assess: severity of ongoing need (mortality, morbidity, displacement, food insecurity, shelter deprivation); disaster risk profile (hazard type, exposure, recurrence); and whether need/risk is distributed area-wide or concentrated in specific pockets that may require sub-area targeting.

2. **DRR and future-risk relevance** (25%) — Does the area face significant future risk that an ABA programme can plausibly reduce? Assess: hazard probability and projected intensification (climate variability, urbanization dynamics); community exposure trends (is in-migration increasing exposure?); existing risk governance capacity that programming could strengthen. World Bank no-regret logic applies: interventions that reduce risk regardless of future hazard scenario should score higher.

3. **Operational access and feasibility** (20%) — Can the programme team safely, consistently, and legally operate throughout the area for the programme duration? Assess: physical access (roads, flooding, security perimeter); legal and administrative access (local government permission, host-community acceptance); staff safety and protection protocols; consistency of access across seasons or conflict cycles.

4. **Strategic leverage and actor landscape** (15%) — Are there local actors, institutions, markets, or resources present that ABA can identify, engage, and leverage? Assess: presence of local NGOs, community-based organizations, service providers, and markets; gaps and duplications in existing actor coverage (4W/5W); municipal or government entry points; potential for co-delivery that amplifies programme reach beyond direct spend.

5. **Boundary definability** (10%) — Can a coherent operational boundary be drawn for this area that is agreed by key actors and reflects the social and risk geography of the community? Assess: whether social neighbourhood boundaries (community-identified) align sufficiently with administrative boundaries to enable data collection and coordination; whether the area can be described precisely enough for 4W reporting and inter-agency coordination; whether boundary disputes (land tenure, inter-community conflict) would destabilize the programme.

## Decision logic

Score each candidate area 0–4 per domain, multiply by weight, and sum:

| Domain | Weight | Score 0 | Score 2 | Score 4 |
|---|---:|---|---|---|
| Humanitarian severity/risk | 30% | No concentration | Moderate concentration | Severe, clearly area-wide |
| DRR relevance | 25% | Minimal future risk | Moderate risk, some reduction potential | High risk, strong reduction potential |
| Operational access | 20% | No safe access | Partial or seasonal access | Full, consistent, legal access |
| Strategic leverage | 15% | No local actors or resources | Some actors, limited leverage | Strong local ecosystem, clear leverage points |
| Boundary definability | 10% | Boundary not agreeable | Partial agreement, disputes present | Clear, agreed, stable boundary |

**Maximum score: 100.**

**EFL two-stage application:** Apply this scoring as a quick-scan first across all candidate areas, then conduct in-depth analysis only for areas scoring ≥55. This prevents in-depth resources being spent on areas that fail basic thresholds.

**Priority tiers:**

| Score | Recommendation |
|---:|---|
| 75–100 | Prioritize — proceed to boundary definition and in-depth diagnosis |
| 55–74 | Conditional — proceed with documented conditions and monitoring |
| 35–54 | Deprioritize — do not select unless higher-scoring areas are unavailable |
| 0–34 | Do not select |

**Multi-area comparison rule:** When comparing multiple candidate areas, the highest-scoring area is selected first. If two areas are within 5 points of each other, escalate to a joint team decision session with community and partner input before finalizing.

## Red flags / override conditions

Even a high score does NOT justify selecting an area if any of the following apply:

- **No safe or consistent access** — operational access score = 0; programme cannot proceed regardless of severity score; escalate to security and access team before any decision
- **Boundary cannot be agreed** — if key actors (including local government and community leaders) cannot agree on an operational boundary within a reasonable scoping period, do not select; boundary disputes will fragment all subsequent coordination
- **Active inter-community conflict within the area** — protection review required before selection; targeting an area in active internal conflict can exacerbate harm regardless of need concentration
- **Selection is driven by a pre-assigned donor geography** — flag this as a constraint to be documented and disclosed; apply the framework anyway; if the donor area scores below 35, escalate to management before accepting the geographic constraint
- **Population in the area is primarily mobile or transient** — area-based approach may not be appropriate for this population; revisit appropriateness decision before proceeding

## Failure modes

- **Breaks when administrative boundaries are used as area proxies without validation** — municipal ward or census zone boundaries often do not match the socially experienced neighbourhood (Young Foundation 2010 finding); using them unvalidated produces a programme area that communities do not recognize as coherent, undermining participation and coordination
- **Breaks when selection is de facto made before the framework is applied** — if field teams have already negotiated access and begun community engagement in a specific area, this framework becomes post-hoc justification rather than a decision tool; apply before any community-facing activities begin
- **Breaks when only one source type informs severity scoring** — a single secondary data source (e.g., satellite imagery alone) can misrepresent actual community need; minimum two independent source types required per domain before scores are accepted as valid
- **Breaks when the framework is applied by a single sector lead** — area selection must reflect multi-sector need, not only the lead sector's mandate; apply in a joint session with at least shelter, WASH, and protection representation
- **Breaks in contexts of rapid population movement** — in displacement or return contexts, severity and boundary scores can change faster than the programme cycle; build in a re-assessment trigger at 6 weeks post-selection
