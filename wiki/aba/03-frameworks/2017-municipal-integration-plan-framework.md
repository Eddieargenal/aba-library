# Municipal Integration Plan Framework

**Source:** UNDRR, *How to Make Cities More Resilient: A Handbook for Local Government Leaders*; World Bank/GFDRR, *Building Urban Resilience: Principles, Tools, and Practice*  
**Years:** 2017 / 2013  
**Lifecycle stage:** 10. Transition, handover, and scaling  
**Framework purpose:** Connect neighborhood-level outputs to municipal plans, budgets, service providers, risk management offices, and public works schedules so area-based gains can be maintained and expanded.

## Framework Overview

The **Municipal Integration Plan Framework** helps area-based teams move from project outputs to municipal ownership, planning, budgeting, and service management.

It is designed for the transition, handover, and scaling stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), especially when outputs involve infrastructure, drainage, roads, waste, water networks, public spaces, evacuation systems, risk information, or preparedness arrangements that require long-term public management.

The framework adapts city resilience and urban risk management guidance into a practical tool for linking neighborhood-level interventions to municipal systems. It ensures that outputs are not left as isolated project assets, but are incorporated into official plans, budgets, maintenance schedules, service-provider workflows, and disaster risk management arrangements.

## Core Framework Question

**How will neighborhood-level outputs be integrated into municipal plans, budgets, public works systems, service-provider responsibilities, and risk management structures so they remain functional after the project ends?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Municipal planning alignment | Whether outputs connect to city development plans, land-use plans, DRR strategies, public works plans, or service plans. | Integration is stronger when outputs support official priorities. |
| Budget pathway | How recurrent costs, repairs, upgrades, or expansion will be financed. | Municipal ownership requires a financing route, not only technical approval. |
| Public works scheduling | Whether infrastructure works are included in maintenance, inspection, repair, or upgrade schedules. | Drainage, roads, access, and public spaces need routine management. |
| Service-provider linkage | Water, sanitation, waste, electricity, transport, health, education, or emergency service actors linked to the output. | Many area outputs depend on providers outside the humanitarian project. |
| Risk-management office linkage | Connection to DRR committees, emergency management offices, early warning systems, preparedness plans, and evacuation arrangements. | Area-based outputs should strengthen city risk management. |
| Technical standards and records | Drawings, design files, specifications, permits, quality records, and asset inventories. | Municipal actors need usable technical documentation to maintain assets. |
| Community interface | How residents remain connected to municipal systems after project closure. | Integration must preserve community voice and accountability. |
| Governance and accountability | Roles, responsibilities, public commitments, feedback channels, and performance review. | Prevents outputs from becoming unowned or politically captured. |
| Scaling potential | Whether the model can be replicated or institutionalized in other neighborhoods. | Municipal integration can turn a pilot into a city-level approach. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a municipal embedding process:

1. **List neighborhood outputs requiring long-term management**  
   Include infrastructure, public works, WASH systems, drainage, access improvements, waste systems, public spaces, evacuation routes, preparedness systems, service referral pathways, and community coordination mechanisms.

2. **Map each output to a municipal function**  
   Identify whether it belongs to planning, public works, water, sanitation, waste, roads, risk management, health, education, social services, environment, protection, or local governance.

3. **Identify the municipal plan or schedule where the output should sit**  
   Link the output to a municipal development plan, risk reduction strategy, land-use plan, public investment plan, maintenance schedule, emergency plan, or service-provider workplan.

4. **Confirm technical acceptance**  
   Check that municipal or service-provider technical staff approve the standards, design, safety, location, and maintenance requirements.

5. **Define the budget/resource route**  
   Identify whether the output will be funded through municipal budget, service-provider resources, community contributions, donor continuation, private-sector partnership, or phased public investment.

6. **Prepare the technical handover package**  
   Provide drawings, maps, asset inventories, bills of quantities, inspection records, maintenance schedules, risk maps, contact lists, and operating procedures.

7. **Agree coordination and accountability arrangements**  
   Define how residents will report problems, how municipal actors will respond, and how progress will be reviewed.

8. **Identify scaling or institutionalization options**  
   Determine whether the approach can be incorporated into citywide programming, public investment cycles, DRR planning, or service delivery models.

## Municipal Integration Matrix

| Output type | Municipal linkage required | Integration action |
|---|---|---|
| Drainage improvements | Public works, water authority, environmental unit, flood risk office. | Add to inspection and cleaning schedule; assign maintenance actor; budget recurrent clearing. |
| Road or access works | Roads/public works department, transport authority, emergency services. | Record in road inventory; confirm repair responsibility; align with emergency access planning. |
| WASH systems | Water utility, sanitation office, waste department, health authority. | Transfer technical records; define O&M tasks; link to tariff/subsidy or service budget. |
| Waste management | Municipal waste service, community committees, private contractors. | Clarify collection routes, bins, payment arrangements, cleaning responsibilities, and complaint pathway. |
| Shelter repair standards | Building control office, housing department, DRR office. | Align with safer construction guidance and permit requirements. |
| Public facilities | Education, health, social services, public works, facility owner. | Confirm facility management plan, maintenance budget, safety standards, and service continuity. |
| Evacuation routes and safe areas | DRM office, public works, police/security, schools, community committees. | Add to contingency plans, signage, drills, and route maintenance schedules. |
| Early warning systems | DRM office, meteorological/hydrological services, community focal points. | Define triggers, communication chain, equipment maintenance, and update cycle. |
| Community committees | Municipal community development office, DRM committee, local leadership structures. | Integrate into recognized coordination or accountability mechanisms. |
| Risk maps and assessment data | Planning office, DRM office, public works, service providers. | Archive in municipal data systems and use for future investment decisions. |

## Municipal Integration Checklist

| Checklist area | Key question |
|---|---|
| Plan alignment | Which municipal plan, strategy, or schedule will include this output? |
| Mandate | Which municipal department, utility, or public institution has responsibility? |
| Technical acceptance | Has the responsible technical actor reviewed and accepted the design or output? |
| Asset registration | Has the output been added to the relevant municipal or service-provider asset inventory? |
| Maintenance | Is there a clear inspection, cleaning, repair, or operating schedule? |
| Budget | What line, fund, contribution, or resource source will cover recurrent costs? |
| Community reporting | How will residents report damage, blockage, service failure, or misuse? |
| Emergency function | Does the output support evacuation, access, early warning, response, or recovery? |
| Risk reduction | Does the output reduce risk rather than relocate or reproduce it? |
| Data transfer | Have maps, drawings, records, and technical files been handed over in usable formats? |
| Accountability | Who will report back to residents on follow-up actions? |
| Scaling | What would be required to apply this model in another area? |

## Municipal Coordination Agenda

| Agenda item | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Neighborhood output review | Present what was built, repaired, organized, or strengthened. |
| Municipal mandate mapping | Confirm which department or service provider owns each output. |
| Technical and safety review | Validate technical standards, defects, permits, and quality records. |
| Budget and maintenance review | Confirm resource source, maintenance frequency, and responsible actor. |
| Public works scheduling | Add infrastructure and service outputs to municipal schedules. |
| DRR and preparedness integration | Link outputs to risk maps, evacuation plans, early warning, and contingency plans. |
| Community accountability | Agree how residents will communicate problems and receive responses. |
| Scaling discussion | Identify what can be replicated, institutionalized, or included in future investment plans. |
| Action plan | Record responsible actors, deadlines, documents required, and unresolved decisions. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Handover design | Ensures outputs are transferred into specific municipal systems. |
| Infrastructure design | Forces early attention to ownership, standards, maintenance, and recurrent cost. |
| Municipal budgeting | Identifies which outputs require public financing or investment planning. |
| Service continuity | Links area-level improvements to service-provider operations. |
| DRR integration | Connects physical works and preparedness systems to city risk management. |
| Scaling | Helps convert a neighborhood project into a model that can inform municipal practice. |
| Accountability | Maintains a resident-to-municipality feedback pathway after project exit. |

## Risk Alerts

**Project-island risk:** Outputs may remain disconnected from municipal systems and fail after project closure.

**Unbudgeted asset risk:** Municipal actors may accept assets without funds for inspection, repair, operation, or replacement.

**Technical-records gap:** Municipal teams cannot maintain what they cannot locate, inspect, or understand.

**Mandate confusion risk:** Public works, utilities, service providers, and community structures may have overlapping or unclear roles.

**Political capture risk:** Municipal integration can be used to prioritize visible assets over the highest-risk areas unless risk and vulnerability evidence remain central.

**Community disconnect risk:** Integration into government systems should not eliminate resident feedback and accountability.

**Scale-up without adaptation risk:** A successful output in one neighborhood may fail elsewhere if service systems, hazards, tenure, or governance differ.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Municipal Integration Plan** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | Neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone. |
| Outputs requiring integration | Infrastructure, services, preparedness systems, committees, data products, or public assets. |
| Municipal responsibility map | Department, service provider, utility, DRM office, or public institution linked to each output. |
| Plan/budget/schedule linkage | Municipal plan, budget line, public works schedule, investment plan, or service-provider workplan. |
| Technical handover package | Drawings, maps, asset list, specifications, inspection records, permits, and maintenance instructions. |
| Maintenance arrangement | Tasks, frequency, responsible actor, tools, resources, and escalation route. |
| Community accountability pathway | How residents report issues and receive responses. |
| DRR and preparedness linkage | How outputs support risk reduction, evacuation, early warning, response, or recovery. |
| Unresolved issues | Pending approvals, budget gaps, technical risks, or coordination concerns. |
| Scale-up potential | Conditions, costs, adaptations, and decisions required for replication. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when an area-based output will require long-term public management or service-provider responsibility.

A strong municipal integration plan should show **where each output sits inside municipal systems, who owns it, how it will be maintained, how it will be funded, how residents will hold actors accountable, and how the output contributes to city-level resilience rather than remaining an isolated project result**.
