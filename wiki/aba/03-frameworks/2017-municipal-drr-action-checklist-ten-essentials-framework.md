# Municipal DRR Action Checklist / Ten Essentials Framework

**Source:** UNDRR, *How to Make Cities More Resilient: A Handbook for Local Government Leaders*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 7. Detailed technical design  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to ensure an area-based plan links with local government responsibilities for organization, finance, risk-informed planning, infrastructure, schools, health, preparedness, and recovery.

## Framework Overview

The **Municipal DRR Action Checklist / Ten Essentials Framework** helps teams connect detailed area-based technical design with municipal disaster risk reduction (DRR) responsibilities.

It is designed for the detailed technical design stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), especially when teams need to prepare municipal coordination meetings, align with city resilience priorities, and identify where humanitarian action should support local governance rather than substitute for it.

The framework adapts the logic of the UNDRR “Ten Essentials” into a practical design and coordination checklist for humanitarian, recovery, and resilience teams working at neighborhood, settlement, or district level.

## Core Framework Question

**How should the area plan connect to municipal responsibilities, systems, budgets, infrastructure, preparedness, and recovery so that humanitarian action strengthens local governance instead of replacing it?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Organization and coordination | Municipal leadership, DRR platforms, departments, committees, service providers, and community structures. | Ensures the area plan has clear institutional links and does not operate in isolation. |
| Finance and resources | Municipal budgets, contingency funds, donor resources, public investment plans, and maintenance financing. | Connects area activities to realistic financing and sustainability pathways. |
| Risk-informed planning | Hazard maps, exposure analysis, land-use planning, zoning, building controls, and settlement growth. | Ensures area-level action reduces future risk rather than rebuilding vulnerability. |
| Infrastructure resilience | Roads, drainage, water, sanitation, electricity, waste, transport, public buildings, and protective infrastructure. | Links technical activities to systems that affect safety, access, and service continuity. |
| Schools and education | School safety, continuity of education, evacuation, risk education, and use of schools as community assets. | Protects children and connects preparedness to local institutions. |
| Health and public safety | Health facilities, emergency medical systems, disease risks, WASH, public health surveillance, and referral pathways. | Connects area-level risk reduction with life safety and public health systems. |
| Preparedness and response | Early warning, contingency planning, evacuation, emergency access, local response roles, and communication systems. | Ensures the area can act before and during shocks. |
| Recovery and reconstruction | Build-back-better planning, damage assessment, service restoration, safer reconstruction, and recovery coordination. | Links immediate recovery with longer-term resilience. |
| Community participation | Residents, civil society, vulnerable groups, informal leaders, and community-based organizations. | Ensures municipal coordination reflects local realities and affected people’s priorities. |
| Accountability and learning | Monitoring, feedback, public reporting, review meetings, after-action learning, and plan updates. | Helps municipal and humanitarian actors adapt and remain accountable. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a municipal alignment and action-planning process:

1. **Start from the area plan**  
   Review the proposed technical design, intervention package, theory of change, and priority activities.

2. **Identify relevant municipal responsibilities**  
   Determine which municipal departments, public services, utilities, schools, health systems, emergency services, and planning bodies are connected to the area plan.

3. **Map the plan against the Ten Essentials logic**  
   Check whether the area plan addresses organization, finance, risk knowledge, planning, infrastructure, services, preparedness, and recovery.

4. **Identify where humanitarian action should support local systems**  
   Clarify what the program can strengthen, facilitate, finance, or coordinate without replacing government responsibility.

5. **Identify municipal gaps and constraints**  
   Document gaps in authority, budget, data, coordination, technical capacity, maintenance, enforcement, or service coverage.

6. **Prepare coordination decisions**  
   Convert findings into specific agenda items for municipal coordination meetings.

7. **Assign responsibilities**  
   Clarify which actions belong to humanitarian actors, municipal departments, service providers, community structures, donors, and technical specialists.

8. **Define financing and maintenance arrangements**  
   Identify what can be funded immediately and what requires municipal budget, donor investment, or longer-term financing.

9. **Build monitoring and review into the plan**  
   Track whether municipal linkages are functioning and whether the area plan is strengthening local systems.

## Municipal DRR Action Checklist

| Action area | Design question |
|---|---|
| Organization | Which municipal department or local authority has responsibility for this issue, and how will they be involved? |
| Coordination | What coordination mechanism will connect humanitarian actors, municipal actors, service providers, and communities? |
| Finance | What resources are available now, and what budget or investment pathway is needed for sustainability? |
| Risk knowledge | What hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and capacity information should inform the design? |
| Planning and regulation | Does the intervention align with land-use plans, building rules, settlement plans, or DRR strategies? |
| Infrastructure | Which critical infrastructure systems are affected, and who owns or maintains them? |
| Schools | Are schools exposed, used as community assets, or needed for preparedness, evacuation, or risk education? |
| Health | Are health facilities, disease risks, WASH conditions, or emergency medical systems connected to the plan? |
| Preparedness | Does the area have early warning, evacuation routes, contingency plans, response roles, and communication channels? |
| Recovery | Does the design support safer reconstruction, service restoration, and build-back-better principles? |
| Inclusion | Are vulnerable and marginalized groups represented in municipal planning and area-level decisions? |
| Accountability | How will residents provide feedback and how will municipal and humanitarian actors respond? |

## Ten Essentials Alignment Matrix

| Essential area | What the area plan should clarify |
|---|---|
| Organize for resilience | Roles, responsibilities, coordination structures, and leadership arrangements. |
| Identify and understand risk | Hazards, exposure, vulnerability, capacity, risk hotspots, and data gaps. |
| Strengthen financial capacity | Budget needs, financing sources, donor links, maintenance costs, and investment pathways. |
| Pursue resilient urban development | Land-use, settlement planning, building safety, growth patterns, and risk-informed design. |
| Safeguard natural buffers | Drainage, slopes, wetlands, green space, watersheds, erosion control, and environmental protection. |
| Strengthen institutional capacity | Training, technical support, municipal systems, service-provider capacity, and community capacity. |
| Understand and strengthen societal capacity | Community participation, social networks, local organizations, inclusion, and accountability. |
| Increase infrastructure resilience | Roads, drainage, WASH, electricity, schools, health facilities, public buildings, and service networks. |
| Ensure effective preparedness and response | Early warning, evacuation, contingency planning, emergency access, communication, and response roles. |
| Expedite recovery and build back better | Recovery planning, safer reconstruction, damage assessment, service restoration, and lessons learned. |

## Municipal Coordination Meeting Preparation

Use the framework to prepare structured coordination meetings with local government.

| Meeting item | What to prepare |
|---|---|
| Area priority | The problem the area plan is trying to solve. |
| Evidence | Assessment findings, risk maps, service gaps, stakeholder input, and community priorities. |
| Municipal responsibility | Department, service provider, authority, or committee linked to the issue. |
| Proposed humanitarian support | What the program can contribute without replacing municipal responsibility. |
| Required municipal action | Decision, approval, coordination, technical input, budget link, maintenance role, or policy support needed. |
| Community role | How residents, local groups, or civil society will participate. |
| Risks | Protection, exclusion, tenure, infrastructure, environmental, financial, or governance risks. |
| Follow-up action | Named responsible actor, deadline, resource need, and next coordination step. |

## Support-Not-Substitute Logic

| Humanitarian action should support | Humanitarian action should avoid |
|---|---|
| Municipal planning with evidence and technical analysis. | Creating parallel plans disconnected from official systems. |
| Service providers with capacity, coordination, or temporary resources. | Permanently replacing public or local service providers. |
| Community participation in municipal decisions. | Treating community engagement as separate from local governance. |
| Maintenance planning and budget advocacy. | Building assets without an owner or maintenance pathway. |
| Risk-informed recovery and safer reconstruction. | Rebuilding the same risk conditions after a crisis. |
| Coordination between sectors and authorities. | Delivering isolated activities without municipal alignment. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Technical design | Ensures sector designs align with municipal systems, standards, permissions, and responsibilities. |
| Institutional roles | Clarifies what local government, service providers, communities, and humanitarian actors should each do. |
| Budget planning | Identifies immediate financing and longer-term municipal or donor investment needs. |
| Infrastructure planning | Links area works to ownership, maintenance, and citywide systems. |
| Preparedness | Connects community preparedness to municipal early warning, evacuation, and response systems. |
| Recovery | Aligns recovery activities with build-back-better and risk-informed planning. |
| Coordination | Provides concrete agenda items for municipal coordination meetings. |
| Sustainability | Reduces the risk that area-level improvements disappear after the project ends. |

## Risk Alerts

**Substitution risk:** Humanitarian actors may unintentionally replace local government functions instead of strengthening them.

**Coordination-only risk:** Meetings without decisions, responsibilities, budgets, or follow-up do not create municipal alignment.

**Plan-disconnect risk:** Area plans may remain isolated if not linked to city DRR, infrastructure, service, and recovery systems.

**Maintenance gap risk:** Infrastructure or service improvements may fail if municipal or community maintenance roles are not agreed.

**Authority-only risk:** Municipal alignment should not bypass residents, informal settlements, excluded groups, or civil society.

**Budget realism risk:** Municipal responsibilities without financing pathways may remain aspirational.

**Governance-blind technical design risk:** A technically strong intervention can fail if ownership, permissions, maintenance, and accountability are unclear.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Municipal DRR Action and Coordination Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | The neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone covered by the area plan. |
| Area priority | The main needs, risks, infrastructure gaps, service issues, or preparedness problems being addressed. |
| Municipal responsibility map | Local government departments, utilities, service providers, schools, health actors, emergency services, and planning bodies linked to the issue. |
| Ten Essentials alignment | Summary of how the area plan supports organization, finance, risk-informed planning, infrastructure, preparedness, and recovery. |
| Required municipal decisions | Approvals, technical inputs, coordination actions, maintenance commitments, budget links, or policy decisions needed. |
| Humanitarian support role | What the program will provide to support local systems without substituting for them. |
| Community participation role | How residents and local groups will engage in planning, implementation, monitoring, and accountability. |
| Financing and maintenance plan | Immediate resources, long-term financing needs, maintenance roles, and sustainability arrangements. |
| Risks and safeguards | Governance, protection, tenure, environmental, technical, financial, and do-no-harm risks with mitigation. |
| Follow-up actions | Named responsibilities, deadlines, next meetings, and monitoring points. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework before finalizing detailed technical designs that require municipal coordination, approval, ownership, service integration, or maintenance.

A strong municipal DRR action plan should explain **which local government responsibilities are involved, what humanitarian actors will support, what municipal decisions are needed, how communities will participate, how financing and maintenance will work, and how the area plan strengthens local governance rather than substituting for it**.
