# Area Theory of Change / Results Framework

**Sources:** Sanderson & Sitko; Global Shelter Cluster, *Area-Based Approaches in Urban Settings: Compendium of Case Studies*; ALNAP, *Good Practice Review 12: Humanitarian Protection*  
**Years:** 2017 / 2019  
**Lifecycle stage:** 5. Integrated area strategy  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to convert prioritized area-level needs and risks into a coherent goal, outcomes, sector packages, risk reduction outcomes, and measurable indicators.

## Framework Overview

The **Area Theory of Change / Results Framework** helps teams translate assessment and prioritization findings into an integrated area strategy.

It is designed for the integrated area strategy stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA). The framework shows how household support, community infrastructure, services, governance, preparedness, protection, and risk reduction activities interact to produce area-level change.

This framework is useful when teams need to move beyond a list of activities and explain how different interventions will work together to improve conditions in a defined neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban area.

## Core Framework Question

**How will the selected package of household, community, service, governance, and risk reduction interventions produce measurable improvement in the target area?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it defines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Area-level goal | The overall change the response seeks to achieve in the target area. | Provides a shared strategic direction across sectors and actors. |
| Area-level outcomes | The main changes expected in people’s wellbeing, access, safety, services, resilience, and local systems. | Connects sector activities to integrated area results. |
| Sector packages | The grouped interventions across shelter, WASH, protection, livelihoods, infrastructure, services, governance, preparedness, and other sectors. | Shows how activities complement each other rather than operating as isolated projects. |
| Risk reduction outcomes | Expected changes in hazard exposure, vulnerability, preparedness, service continuity, and local capacity. | Links humanitarian action with disaster risk reduction and recovery. |
| Assumptions | Conditions that must hold true for the strategy to work. | Makes hidden dependencies visible and testable. |
| Risks and mitigation | Factors that could block, distort, or harm implementation. | Supports adaptive management and do-no-harm. |
| Indicators | Measures used to track outputs, outcomes, risk reduction, access, quality, and accountability. | Allows teams to monitor whether area-level change is happening. |
| Learning and adaptation | Mechanisms for reviewing evidence and adjusting the strategy. | Keeps the area strategy responsive to changing urban conditions. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a results-chain process:

1. **Start from jointly prioritized problems**  
   Use the outputs of area profiling, systems diagnosis, stakeholder mapping, risk mapping, and protection screening.

2. **Define the area-level goal**  
   State the desired change for the target area in clear, people-centered, place-based terms.

3. **Define outcome pathways**  
   Identify the main changes needed across households, services, infrastructure, governance, preparedness, and protection.

4. **Group activities into sector packages**  
   Organize interventions into complementary packages rather than separate sector lists.

5. **Map how packages interact**  
   Show how household assistance depends on services, how infrastructure affects safety and access, how governance affects sustainability, and how preparedness reduces future risk.

6. **Define assumptions and risks**  
   Identify the conditions required for success and the risks that could undermine or distort outcomes.

7. **Select indicators**  
   Choose practical indicators for outputs, outcomes, risk reduction, access, inclusion, quality, accountability, and systems strengthening.

8. **Define review and adaptation points**  
   Set moments when evidence will be reviewed and the strategy adjusted.

## Results Chain

| Level | Guiding question | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | What area-level change should the response contribute to? | Crisis-affected households in the target area live in safer conditions, access essential services, and are better prepared for future shocks. |
| Outcomes | What changes must happen for the goal to be achieved? | Improved safe shelter, restored WASH access, reduced protection risks, strengthened service coordination, and improved preparedness. |
| Outputs | What direct products or services will the program deliver? | Repaired shelters, rehabilitated drainage, cash assistance, service referrals, community risk plans, and functioning feedback mechanisms. |
| Activities | What actions will teams implement? | Assessments, repairs, distributions, training, coordination meetings, infrastructure works, referrals, and community engagement. |
| Inputs | What resources are required? | Funding, staff, partners, materials, data, technical expertise, permissions, and local participation. |

## Outcome Pathway Matrix

| Outcome area | What the framework should show |
|---|---|
| Household support | How assistance improves household safety, dignity, basic needs, access, or recovery. |
| Community infrastructure | How shared infrastructure reduces risk, improves access, or restores critical area functions. |
| Service systems | How water, sanitation, health, education, transport, waste, protection, or administrative services become more accessible or functional. |
| Governance and coordination | How local authorities, service providers, communities, and humanitarian actors coordinate decisions and responsibilities. |
| Protection and inclusion | How the strategy reduces exclusion, unsafe access, eviction risk, violence, aid capture, or other protection concerns. |
| Risk reduction and preparedness | How the area becomes better prepared for hazards, service disruption, displacement, or future shocks. |
| Livelihoods and markets | How households and local markets recover income, access goods, and reduce economic vulnerability. |
| Accountability and participation | How affected people influence priorities, provide feedback, and monitor whether assistance is fair and safe. |

## Integrated Strategy Logic

The framework should show how different packages reinforce one another.

| Intervention package | Contribution to area-level change |
|---|---|
| Household assistance | Stabilizes affected households and helps them meet urgent needs. |
| Shelter and settlement support | Improves safety, habitability, tenure-sensitive recovery, and dignity. |
| WASH and public health support | Reduces disease risk and improves service access. |
| Community infrastructure | Restores shared assets, access routes, drainage, public spaces, or service points. |
| Protection and legal support | Reduces risks related to eviction, exclusion, violence, exploitation, and unsafe access. |
| Livelihoods and market support | Strengthens household recovery and local economic functioning. |
| Governance and coordination support | Improves local decision-making, service alignment, and sustainability. |
| Preparedness and DRR | Reduces future risk and strengthens local response capacity. |

## Indicator Framework

| Result area | Example indicator type |
|---|---|
| Area-level goal | Percentage of target population reporting improved safety, service access, or ability to recover. |
| Shelter outcome | Number or percentage of households living in safer, more habitable shelter. |
| WASH outcome | Percentage of households with reliable access to safe water, sanitation, or hygiene services. |
| Protection outcome | Number of identified protection risks mitigated or referred through safe pathways. |
| Infrastructure outcome | Number of critical community infrastructure assets restored, upgraded, or maintained. |
| Governance outcome | Number of functioning coordination mechanisms involving authorities, communities, and service providers. |
| Preparedness outcome | Number of communities with updated risk maps, early warning links, evacuation plans, or contingency measures. |
| Inclusion outcome | Percentage of marginalized groups reporting safe and meaningful access to assistance and decision-making. |
| Accountability outcome | Percentage of complaints or feedback cases resolved within agreed timelines. |
| Adaptation indicator | Number of strategy adjustments made based on monitoring, feedback, or changed risk conditions. |

## Assumptions to Test

| Assumption | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Affected people can safely access assistance and services. | If access is unsafe, outcomes may not be achieved and harm may increase. |
| Local authorities and service providers are willing to coordinate. | Governance and service outcomes depend on institutional engagement. |
| Markets or supply chains can support selected modalities. | Cash, vouchers, repairs, and procurement depend on market functionality. |
| Land and tenure issues can be managed safely. | Shelter, infrastructure, and settlement activities may increase eviction or conflict risks. |
| Community representatives are sufficiently inclusive. | Participation and accountability can fail if powerful groups dominate. |
| Infrastructure improvements can be maintained. | Short-term works may fail without ownership, maintenance, or financing. |
| Security and access conditions remain workable. | Implementation and monitoring depend on continued access. |

## Risk Alerts

**Activity-list trap:** A theory of change is not a list of activities. It must explain how activities combine to produce outcomes.

**Sector-silo risk:** Sector outcomes should connect to the area-level goal, not remain disconnected project outputs.

**Overclaiming risk:** Do not claim that one project can solve citywide or structural problems unless the strategy and indicators support that claim.

**Indicator overload risk:** Too many indicators can make monitoring unmanageable. Select indicators that support decision-making.

**Weak-assumption risk:** If assumptions are not tested, the strategy may fail silently.

**Protection-blind risk:** Results frameworks that ignore eviction, exclusion, GBV, unsafe access, aid capture, or social tension may incentivize harmful implementation.

**Systems-blind risk:** Household-level results may not be sustainable if services, infrastructure, governance, or markets remain weak.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Area Theory of Change and Results Framework Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Area-level goal | The overall change sought in the target area. |
| Priority problems | The key needs, risks, service gaps, and system failures the strategy addresses. |
| Outcome pathways | The main changes expected across households, services, infrastructure, governance, protection, and preparedness. |
| Intervention packages | The grouped sector and system-level activities that will contribute to outcomes. |
| Theory of change narrative | A concise explanation of how activities will lead to outputs, outcomes, and the area-level goal. |
| Assumptions | Conditions required for the strategy to work. |
| Risks and mitigation | Key risks that may undermine results and how they will be managed. |
| Indicators | Output, outcome, risk reduction, inclusion, accountability, and adaptation indicators. |
| Coordination requirements | Actors, sectors, institutions, and community structures that must work together. |
| Review points | When and how the strategy will be updated based on evidence and feedback. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the team needs to convert priorities into a coherent integrated area strategy.

A strong area theory of change should explain **what will change, for whom, through which intervention packages, under what assumptions, with what safeguards, and how progress will be measured and adapted over time**.
