# Neighbourhood Diagnosis Framework

## Atomic question
What do we need to know about a selected area before designing an ABA response?

## Why this decision matters
Neighbourhood diagnosis is what separates an area-based approach from a sector programme delivered in a geographic location. Without systematic diagnosis across urban systems, risk profiles, services, actors, and community dynamics, programme design defaults to replicating prior sector templates regardless of local conditions. The IRC/IIED context analysis toolkit establishes that urban context analysis is categorically distinct from a needs assessment — it examines the system producing the need, not only the need itself. Diagnosis is also the evidence base that all subsequent decisions in the ABA lifecycle draw on: area selection is validated, coordination architecture is designed, and prioritization is conducted using diagnosis outputs.

## Prerequisites
- From stage 1 (Area Selection): confirmed area with agreed operational boundary
- Area selection score ≥55 and boundary documented and agreed by at least local government and community leadership
- A dedicated diagnosis team that includes at minimum: one urban systems analyst, one protection/community engagement officer, and one sector technical specialist
- Minimum timeline: the IRC/IIED toolkit baseline assumes roughly two weeks in-country data collection; adjust upward for complex multi-hazard or multi-community areas, downward only with documented rationale

## Decision domains

1. **Urban systems status** — Which urban systems — infrastructure (water, sanitation, energy, roads), services (health, education, protection), markets (food, labour, housing, finance), governance (local authority, land tenure, civil registration), and mobility (transport, migration flows) — are functional, stressed, or broken in this area? Campbell's five-system urban typology (physical, economic, social, institutional, environmental) provides the analytical scaffold. The purpose is not to describe each system exhaustively, but to identify which systems are driving vulnerability and which are leverage points for intervention.

2. **Hazard-exposure-vulnerability-capacity (HEVC) profile** — What hazards are present (type, magnitude, frequency, spatial distribution)? Which population groups are most exposed? What drives vulnerability for each group (tenure insecurity, income precarity, disability, legal status, gender, age)? What capacities — community, institutional, infrastructure — already exist to reduce risk or absorb shocks? Twigg's framework maps this to five Thematic Areas; the World Bank formula (risk = hazard × exposure × vulnerability) provides the analytic structure. Both quantitative indicators and qualitative community knowledge must be used — neither alone is sufficient.

3. **Service functionality and gap mapping** — Which services are operating below the threshold required to protect minimum standards for the area population, and for which sub-populations? Service functionality is not binary (present/absent) but a continuum: a health facility may be physically present but understaffed, underequipped, or inaccessible to specific groups. Map each key service against: physical availability, quality, affordability, and accessibility (including protection risks at point of access). REACH key-informant methodology provides a structured way to gather this data across service types.

4. **Stakeholder and actor landscape** — Who is present in the area, what are their mandates and capacities, and what gaps and duplications exist in current coverage? This domain produces the actor map that drives coordination architecture design in stage 3. Include: humanitarian actors (UN, INGO, LNGO); local government and municipal authorities; community-based organizations and informal leadership structures; private sector providers (markets, landlords, transport); and civil society. For each actor, document: geographic coverage, population reach, sector focus, coordination participation (4W/5W), and known constraints or conflicts of interest.

5. **Community dynamics and social structure** — How is the community organized? What are the intra-community power dynamics, social cohesion patterns, and existing collective action mechanisms? Who are the legitimate local leaders and how is legitimacy determined? What are the principal axes of social differentiation (ethnicity, religion, tenure, displacement status, gender)? What participation entry points exist that do not require creating parallel structures? This domain is essential for participation design in stage 3 and for protection risk assessment throughout the programme.

## Decision logic

This framework uses a **completeness threshold model**, not a scoring model. Each domain must meet a minimum evidence standard before the diagnosis is considered complete and strategy design can proceed.

**Minimum evidence standard per domain** (adapted from the gold standard's evidence taxonomy):

| Domain | Minimum source types required | Acceptable sources |
|---|---|---|
| Urban systems status | 2 independent | Direct observation; KII with service providers; secondary data/maps; technical assessments |
| HEVC profile | 3 independent (must include community voice) | Direct observation; KII with community members; household mini-survey; secondary/scientific hazard data; service provider data |
| Service functionality | 2 independent (must include service provider data) | KII with service providers; direct observation; household data; 4W/5W coordination data |
| Stakeholder landscape | 2 independent | 4W/5W data; KII with coordination leads; direct observation; programme documentation review |
| Community dynamics | 2 independent (must include community-direct method) | Community consultation; KII with community leaders (disaggregated); focus groups; observation |

**Completeness gate:**

| Domain coverage | Status | Action |
|---|---|---|
| All 5 domains meet minimum standard | Complete | Proceed to joint prioritization (stage 4) |
| 1–2 domains below minimum | Partial — targeted gap-fill | Conduct targeted additional collection for flagged domains; do not proceed to strategy design |
| 3+ domains below minimum | Incomplete — return to collection | Halt diagnosis; redesign data collection plan before proceeding |

**Domain minimum must be met independently** — a single rich source (e.g., a comprehensive secondary report) does not satisfy the minimum even if it covers multiple domains, because independent triangulation is the quality standard, not data volume.

## Red flags / override conditions

- **HEVC profile based solely on secondary data with no community validation** — do not accept the profile as complete; secondary hazard data frequently misrepresents actual community exposure and coping mechanisms; flag all HEVC-derived scores as provisional until at least one community consultation validates the risk narrative
- **Stakeholder mapping reveals active conflict between key actors** — flag for protection review before proceeding to coordination design in stage 3; an actor landscape with active disputes requires explicit conflict-sensitive programming adjustments before a coordination platform can be established
- **Community dynamics domain reveals significant internal displacement or population flux** — area boundaries and population assumptions must be revised; high mobility invalidates static community profile data within weeks
- **No access to the area for community-direct data collection methods** — diagnosis cannot be completed without direct community engagement; remote or KII-only approaches fail the minimum for domains 2 and 5; do not proceed to strategy design; flag access constraint for management escalation

## Failure modes

- **Breaks when diagnosis replicates a standard needs assessment** — a needs assessment asks "what do people need?"; a neighbourhood diagnosis asks "what is the system producing this need and what leverage points exist?"; conflating the two produces a problem list with no strategic entry points for a systems-level response
- **Breaks when community dynamics are inferred from service data or KII with external actors** — service utilization and provider reports systematically under-represent the most marginalized sub-populations; direct community engagement is required, not optional
- **Breaks when the boundary drawn for diagnosis differs from the boundary used for response design** — data collected within one boundary cannot be used to design programming for a different boundary without explicit re-scoping; boundary consistency is a quality requirement throughout the lifecycle
- **Breaks when diagnosis is conducted by a single sector team** — an urban systems diagnosis requires multi-sector perspective at data collection, not only at analysis; a WASH team conducting diagnosis will produce a water-infrastructure-centric system map that misses economic, governance, and social system dynamics
- **Breaks when diagnosis is treated as a one-time baseline** — urban systems change rapidly; for programmes longer than 6 months, diagnosis outputs require structured reassessment at programme midpoint; build in a reassessment trigger from the start
