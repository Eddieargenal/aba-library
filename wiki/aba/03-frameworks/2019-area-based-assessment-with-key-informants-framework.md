# Area-Based Assessment with Key Informants Framework

**Source:** REACH / UNHCR, *Area-Based Assessment with Key Informants: A Practical Guide*  
**Year:** 2019  
**Lifecycle stage:** 2. Area profile and systems diagnosis  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to collect rapid area-level information when household surveys are too slow, costly, unsafe, incomplete, or operationally unrealistic.

## Framework Overview

The **Area-Based Assessment with Key Informants Framework** helps teams generate a rapid, structured understanding of conditions across a defined area by interviewing people who have knowledge of the area, population groups, services, risks, needs, capacities, and changes over time.

It is designed for the area profile and systems diagnosis stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA). The framework is especially useful when direct household-level data collection is not feasible at scale, when access is limited, when time is short, or when teams need a broad overview before deciding where to conduct deeper assessments.

This framework does not replace household surveys when representative household-level data is required. Instead, it provides a practical approach for collecting area-level information from knowledgeable informants and triangulating their perspectives.

## Core Framework Question

**What can knowledgeable local informants tell us about area-level needs, risks, services, population groups, access constraints, capacities, and priority changes when household surveys are not feasible?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Area-level conditions | Overall situation in the target area, including needs, risks, service access, displacement, and coping. | Provides a rapid profile of the area as a whole. |
| Key informant knowledge | Information held by local leaders, service providers, teachers, health workers, shopkeepers, civil society, community representatives, and other informed actors. | Uses people with broad visibility to understand conditions across groups or locations. |
| Population groups | Residents, displaced people, returnees, refugees, renters, squatters, host communities, marginalized groups, and other sub-groups. | Helps identify whether conditions differ across groups within the same area. |
| Services and infrastructure | Water, sanitation, health, education, shelter, markets, transport, electricity, waste, protection, and administrative services. | Shows which services are available, accessible, functional, overloaded, or disrupted. |
| Access and constraints | Physical access, safety, documentation, affordability, discrimination, mobility, information, and service barriers. | Explains why people may or may not be able to use services or assistance. |
| Needs and priorities | Reported priority needs by area, group, sector, or location. | Supports rapid prioritization and targeting. |
| Risks and protection concerns | Safety, violence, eviction, exploitation, social tension, exclusion, hazards, or service-related risks. | Identifies issues that require safeguards, referrals, or deeper investigation. |
| Triangulation and confidence | Comparison of information across informants, locations, sectors, and secondary data. | Helps prevent over-reliance on one informant or biased perspective. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a structured rapid assessment process:

1. **Define the assessment area**  
   Clarify the neighborhood, settlement, district, service catchment, or urban zone being assessed.

2. **Define the information needs**  
   Identify what the team needs to know for decision-making: population groups, services, needs, access barriers, risks, capacities, or priority locations.

3. **Identify key informant categories**  
   Select informants who have relevant knowledge of different sectors, locations, population groups, institutions, or community networks.

4. **Develop a structured interview tool**  
   Use a short, focused questionnaire that captures area-level information, not only the personal experience of the informant.

5. **Sample for coverage and diversity**  
   Include informants from different locations, roles, genders, population groups, service systems, and perspectives.

6. **Conduct key informant interviews**  
   Collect information systematically, noting the informant’s role, knowledge area, geographic coverage, and confidence level.

7. **Triangulate findings**  
   Compare answers across multiple informants and against secondary data, observation, service records, maps, and partner knowledge.

8. **Identify confidence and gaps**  
   Distinguish strong findings from uncertain findings. Flag areas, groups, or topics requiring follow-up.

9. **Translate findings into area profile decisions**  
   Use the results to prioritize locations, sectors, population groups, coordination needs, and deeper assessment requirements.

## Key Informant Selection Matrix

| Informant type | Possible knowledge contribution |
|---|---|
| Local authorities | Administrative boundaries, public services, infrastructure, governance, official response, and population estimates. |
| Community leaders | Local priorities, social networks, vulnerable groups, conflict dynamics, access issues, and community capacities. |
| Service providers | Functionality, coverage, demand, quality, constraints, and gaps in health, WASH, education, protection, shelter, or other services. |
| Civil society and local NGOs | Marginalized groups, local capacities, protection concerns, community trust, and service gaps. |
| Teachers and school staff | Children’s needs, displacement patterns, family pressures, service access, and social concerns. |
| Health workers | Health trends, access barriers, disease risks, vulnerable groups, and referral constraints. |
| Market actors and shopkeepers | Price changes, supply chains, purchasing power, availability of goods, livelihoods, and market disruption. |
| Landlords and housing actors | Rental pressures, housing conditions, eviction risks, occupancy patterns, and tenure issues. |
| Women’s groups and youth groups | Gendered risks, safety, mobility, access, exclusion, and social priorities. |
| Representatives of marginalized groups | Barriers, discrimination, protection concerns, unmet needs, and underreported issues. |

## Area-Based Assessment Matrix

| Assessment question | What to capture |
|---|---|
| What are the main population groups in the area? | Residents, displaced people, returnees, refugees, migrants, renters, informal settlers, host communities, and marginalized groups. |
| What are the main priority needs? | Shelter, WASH, food, health, livelihoods, protection, education, documentation, transport, or other urgent needs. |
| Which services are available and functional? | Service presence, coverage, reliability, quality, affordability, and capacity. |
| Who cannot access services? | Groups excluded by cost, distance, discrimination, safety, documentation, disability, gender, age, or social status. |
| What risks affect the area? | Hazards, violence, eviction, exploitation, social tension, public health concerns, infrastructure failure, or protection threats. |
| What has changed recently? | Population movement, service disruption, price changes, security shifts, seasonal risks, or new assistance. |
| What coping strategies are people using? | Debt, reduced consumption, informal work, migration, asset sales, unsafe shelter, sharing resources, or reliance on networks. |
| What local capacities exist? | Community groups, service providers, local businesses, volunteers, authorities, civil society, and informal support systems. |
| What information is uncertain? | Conflicting reports, weak knowledge areas, missing groups, inaccessible locations, or topics needing follow-up. |

## Data Quality and Triangulation Principles

**Do not rely on one informant.** Key informants provide perspectives, not automatically verified facts.

**Record the basis of knowledge.** Distinguish whether the informant has direct observation, professional knowledge, community knowledge, second-hand information, or assumptions.

**Use diverse informants.** Include different roles, groups, genders, neighborhoods, and sectors to reduce bias.

**Triangulate systematically.** Compare information across informants and with secondary data, observation, service records, maps, and partner input.

**Track confidence levels.** Mark findings as high, medium, or low confidence depending on consistency, source quality, and corroboration.

**Protect sensitive information.** Avoid exposing informants or vulnerable groups, especially when discussing protection concerns, armed actors, political dynamics, or illegal status.

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Area profiling | Provides rapid area-level information on needs, services, population groups, risks, and capacities. |
| Targeting | Identifies priority groups, underserved locations, and access barriers. |
| Sector prioritization | Shows which sectors appear most urgent or disrupted across the area. |
| Follow-up assessment | Identifies where household surveys, technical assessments, protection analysis, or service mapping are needed. |
| Coordination | Highlights relevant actors, service gaps, duplication risks, and referral needs. |
| Risk mitigation | Flags protection concerns, social tension, access constraints, and data confidence limits. |
| Adaptive programming | Provides a rapid evidence base that can be updated as more information becomes available. |

## Risk Alerts

**Informant-bias risk:** Key informants may overstate, understate, politicize, or selectively report needs based on their position, incentives, or limited visibility.

**Elite-capture risk:** Relying only on leaders, authorities, or service providers can exclude marginalized groups and reinforce existing power imbalances.

**Representation risk:** Key informant data should not be presented as statistically representative household-level data unless the methodology supports that claim.

**False-confidence risk:** Consistent answers may still be wrong if informants share the same assumptions or information sources.

**Protection risk:** Asking sensitive questions may put informants or communities at risk if confidentiality and data protection are weak.

**Area-averaging risk:** Area-level findings can hide differences between groups, blocks, settlements, or service catchments.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Area-Based Key Informant Assessment Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | The neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone assessed. |
| Assessment purpose | The decision the assessment is intended to support. |
| Key informant profile | Number and types of informants, their roles, geographic knowledge, and coverage. |
| Main findings | Area-level needs, risks, service gaps, access barriers, population groups, and capacities. |
| Group differences | Differences in conditions or access between population groups. |
| Service and infrastructure concerns | Key issues affecting service functionality, coverage, reliability, and access. |
| Protection and risk concerns | Safety, exclusion, eviction, violence, exploitation, or other sensitive issues. |
| Confidence level | High, medium, or low confidence findings, with explanation. |
| Information gaps | Topics, groups, or locations requiring further assessment. |
| Program implications | Recommended priorities, follow-up assessments, coordination actions, and risk mitigation steps. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the team needs **rapid area-level evidence** and household surveys are too slow, costly, unsafe, incomplete, or operationally unrealistic.

A strong area-based key informant assessment should combine **diverse informants, structured questions, triangulation, confidence ratings, and clear limits on what the data can and cannot prove**.
