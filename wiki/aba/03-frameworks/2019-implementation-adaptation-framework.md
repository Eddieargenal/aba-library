# Implementation Adaptation Framework

## Atomic question
During implementation, when is a change in plan justified and how should it be made accountable?

## Why this decision matters
Urban ABA programmes operate in dynamic environments where conditions change faster than planning cycles. Without a structured adaptation framework, programmes either rigidly follow an outdated plan (producing irrelevant outputs) or adapt informally (producing accountability gaps with donors and communities). The key distinction is between adaptation that improves programme relevance and drift that erodes programme coherence — only the former is justified. Sanderson and Sitko (2017) identify adaptive management as an explicit design requirement in post-disaster ABA, not an optional response to unexpected events. Without predefined adaptation criteria, any change can be framed as adaptation; with them, changes require evidence and trigger accountability processes.

## Prerequisites
- From stage 5 (Integrated Area Strategy): approved area strategy with defined phases, decision points, and theory of change
- From stage 3 (Coordination): functioning area coordination platform as the body that reviews Tier 2 and Tier 3 adaptations
- Minimum evidence standard before any adaptation is approved: at least 1 independent source type confirming the triggering condition (Tier 1); 2 for Tier 2; 3+ for Tier 3

## Decision domains

1. **Adaptation trigger classification** — is the proposed change a minor adjustment, a significant modification, or a fundamental redesign? Classification determines the approval pathway and evidence requirement. Misclassifying a Tier 3 change as Tier 1 (to avoid review) is the most common adaptation accountability failure.

2. **Evidence threshold** — is there sufficient evidence to justify the change, or is the trigger driven by anecdote, single-actor pressure, or operational convenience? Evidence type matters: direct community feedback, monitoring data, and key informant triangulation each count as independent source types. Donor preference or staff opinion are not evidence.

3. **Accountability floor** — what commitments to communities, donors, and partners cannot be changed regardless of the evidence? Commitments that reduce services to the highest-risk populations, or that violate agreed protection standards, require renegotiation not just notification. The accountability floor defines the non-negotiable minimum.

4. **Partner and community notification** — who must be informed before the change is implemented? At what level is community re-consent required versus notification? The coordination platform must be informed of all Tier 2 and Tier 3 changes before implementation, not after.

5. **Learning capture** — how is the adaptation documented so it feeds back into the area strategy, updates the theory of change, and informs future programme cycles? Adaptations that are not documented repeat as crises rather than producing institutional learning.

## Decision logic

Classify every proposed programme change using the following three-tier model before applying any adaptation:

| Tier | Conditions | Approval authority | Minimum evidence | Timeline | Documentation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Minor adjustment** | Within original scope; budget shift <10%; no change to target population or targeting criteria; no change to protection commitments | Programme manager decision | 1 source type confirming trigger | Implement immediately; notify coordination platform within 5 working days | Log entry in implementation tracker; note trigger and evidence |
| **Tier 2 — Significant modification** | Scope change within the area; budget shift 10–25%; change to targeting criteria or population; change to sector mix that does not alter the theory of change | Coordination platform deliberation required; community notification before implementation | 2 independent source types (e.g., monitoring data + KI interviews) | 2-week decision window from trigger identification to platform review | Updated strategy section; named responsible party; implementation tracker entry |
| **Tier 3 — Fundamental redesign** | Budget shift >25%; change to programme area or geographic scope; revision of theory of change; change that affects protection commitments or community agreements | Full stakeholder review; community re-engagement; donor notification; new sign-off cycle from programme management | 3+ source types including at least 1 qualitative community source and 1 quantitative monitoring source | Full sign-off cycle before implementation (minimum 4 weeks) | Revised strategy document; updated ToC; learning note capturing what triggered the redesign and what was learned |

**Boundary between Tier 1 and Tier 2:** If a series of Tier 1 adjustments within a 3-month period cumulatively produces a budget shift >15% or a de facto change to targeting criteria, treat the cumulative change as Tier 2 and conduct a retrospective platform review. Tier 1 accumulation that constitutes an unacknowledged Tier 2 is the second most common adaptation accountability failure.

**Positive adaptation trigger:** The framework applies equally to positive surprises. If a component is performing significantly better than expected, a Tier 2 or Tier 3 adaptation may be warranted to scale it or reallocate resources toward it. Adaptation triggers must be defined for both underperformance and overperformance at strategy design stage.

## Red flags / override conditions

- An adaptation is proposed that reduces programming in the highest-risk or most vulnerable population groups in order to improve operational convenience or reduce costs → classify as Tier 3 minimum regardless of budget shift; require protection review and community re-consent before approval
- A Tier 2 or Tier 3 adaptation is proposed in response to single donor feedback without corroborating community or monitoring evidence → reject the adaptation trigger; document the donor feedback as an input for next review cycle, not as an adaptation trigger in its own right
- No learning capture mechanism is in place before a Tier 2 or Tier 3 adaptation is approved → require documentation plan before approving; adaptations that are not documented will not produce organizational learning
- An adaptation is proposed that changes the geographic scope of the programme without a new area selection and boundary definition process → require stages 1 and 3 to be rerun for the new area before approving the adaptation

## Failure modes

- **Breaks when adaptation triggers are defined only for negative conditions:** a framework that only monitors what goes wrong misses the opportunity to scale what goes right. Positive adaptation — reallocating resources toward overperforming components — is equally important in resource-constrained ABA contexts.
- **Breaks when Tier 1 adjustments accumulate without review:** each individual Tier 1 change passes the threshold, but their cumulative effect constitutes an unacknowledged Tier 2 or Tier 3 redesign. The 3-month cumulative review is the safeguard; skipping it is the failure mode.
- **Breaks in contexts with high staff turnover:** if adaptation logic lives in individual knowledge rather than documented process, new staff cannot reconstruct the rationale for past changes. Each adaptation must be documented at the time it is made, not reconstructed later from memory.
- **Breaks when the coordination platform is informed after the fact:** notification after implementation converts a governance process into a reporting ritual. Platform deliberation requires advance notice with enough lead time for members to consult their own stakeholders before the decision is made.
- **Breaks when contribution-vs-attribution logic is absent from the evaluation design:** in multi-actor ABA contexts, evidence of what is causing observed change is inherently contested. Adaptations based on attribution assumptions (this change happened because of our intervention) rather than contribution evidence (our intervention contributed to this change alongside others) will misidentify causes and produce adaptations that address the wrong variables.
