# Municipal “Ten Essentials” Diagnostic Framework

**Source:** UNDRR, *How to Make Cities More Resilient: A Handbook for Local Government Leaders*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 1. Area selection and boundary definition  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to screen whether a proposed Area-Based Approach (ABA) target area is connected to wider municipal disaster risk reduction (DRR), governance, budget, infrastructure, preparedness, and recovery systems.

## Framework Overview

The **Municipal “Ten Essentials” Diagnostic Framework** helps teams assess whether a proposed target area can be linked to municipal resilience systems rather than treated as an isolated humanitarian intervention area.

It is especially useful when an area-based response needs to align with local government priorities, city resilience plans, infrastructure investments, preparedness systems, recovery planning, and disaster risk governance.

The framework adapts the logic of the UNDRR “Ten Essentials” for making cities resilient into a practical screening tool for area selection and boundary definition.

## Core Framework Question

**Does the proposed target area connect meaningfully to wider municipal DRR priorities, governance systems, infrastructure risks, preparedness capacities, budgets, and recovery pathways?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Governance and coordination | Whether the target area connects to municipal leadership, local authorities, DRR platforms, and coordination structures. | Ensures the response can align with official systems and avoid parallel planning. |
| Risk knowledge | Whether municipal actors understand the hazards, exposure, vulnerability, and risk drivers affecting the area. | Helps connect area selection to evidence-based DRR and resilience planning. |
| Financial capacity and budgeting | Whether municipal budgets, contingency funds, investment plans, or external financing can support risk reduction in the area. | Links humanitarian or recovery work to realistic financing and sustainability. |
| Urban planning and land use | Whether the area is shaped by zoning, settlement growth, land tenure, building controls, or unsafe development patterns. | Helps identify whether risk is being created or reduced through city planning decisions. |
| Infrastructure resilience | Whether critical infrastructure and services in the area are exposed, weak, overloaded, or poorly maintained. | Connects area selection to systems that affect life safety, access, service continuity, and recovery. |
| Preparedness and response capacity | Whether local systems exist for early warning, evacuation, emergency response, contingency planning, and public communication. | Shows whether the area can be supported through stronger preparedness and local response systems. |
| Ecosystems and protective services | Whether natural systems, drainage, slopes, wetlands, watersheds, or green infrastructure affect risk in the area. | Highlights environmental systems that may reduce or increase disaster risk. |
| Recovery and reconstruction | Whether the area can be integrated into recovery planning, safer reconstruction, and build-back-better strategies. | Ensures short-term action contributes to longer-term resilience. |
| Social inclusion and community capacity | Whether vulnerable groups, local organizations, communities, and civil society are included in risk reduction and recovery planning. | Strengthens equity, accountability, and locally owned resilience. |
| Institutional learning and continuity | Whether municipal systems can monitor risk, learn from past events, and sustain improvements over time. | Helps avoid one-off interventions that disappear after the project ends. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a municipal alignment screening process:

1. **Define the proposed target area**  
   Identify the neighborhood, settlement, district, corridor, or urban zone under consideration.

2. **Identify relevant municipal DRR priorities**  
   Review whether the area appears in city resilience plans, DRR strategies, hazard maps, development plans, infrastructure plans, or recovery frameworks.

3. **Map governance connections**  
   Identify which municipal departments, local authorities, service providers, DRR committees, civil society groups, and community structures have roles in the area.

4. **Assess risk and infrastructure relevance**  
   Examine whether the area contains high-risk infrastructure, exposed populations, weak services, unsafe land use, or recurrent disaster impacts.

5. **Review budget and investment linkages**  
   Determine whether municipal or external resources could support risk reduction, preparedness, infrastructure repair, or recovery in the area.

6. **Check preparedness and recovery systems**  
   Assess whether the area is included in early warning, evacuation, contingency planning, emergency access, debris management, shelter, recovery, or reconstruction systems.

7. **Evaluate inclusion and community capacity**  
   Identify whether vulnerable groups and local organizations are represented in municipal planning and response structures.

8. **Decide whether the area is municipally strategic**  
   Determine whether selecting this area would strengthen wider city resilience or remain an isolated project geography.

## Municipal Diagnostic Matrix

| Diagnostic question | Strong alignment signal |
|---|---|
| Is the area recognized in municipal risk or resilience plans? | The area appears in DRR plans, hazard maps, recovery plans, investment priorities, or municipal development strategies. |
| Are local authorities responsible and engaged? | Municipal departments, local leaders, or service providers have clear mandates and interest in the area. |
| Are risks known and documented? | Hazards, exposure, vulnerabilities, and infrastructure risks have been mapped or described. |
| Does the area contain critical infrastructure or services? | Roads, drainage, water, sanitation, electricity, schools, health facilities, markets, or public buildings are important to wider urban functioning. |
| Are budget or investment pathways possible? | There are municipal, donor, private-sector, or community resources that could support risk reduction or recovery. |
| Is the area connected to preparedness systems? | Early warning, evacuation routes, contingency plans, emergency access, or response roles include the area. |
| Would intervention strengthen citywide resilience? | Actions in the area could reduce wider risk, protect services, support recovery, or inform replication elsewhere. |
| Are vulnerable groups included in planning? | Community organizations, informal leaders, women, older people, people with disabilities, displaced people, and other groups can influence decisions. |

## Prioritization Logic

Use this framework to classify candidate areas by their level of municipal alignment.

| Alignment level | Characteristics | Recommended action |
|---|---|---|
| Strong municipal alignment | The area is clearly connected to municipal DRR priorities, infrastructure systems, budgets, preparedness, and recovery planning. | Prioritize for area-based planning, municipal coordination, resilience investment, and integrated humanitarian-recovery action. |
| Partial municipal alignment | The area has relevant risks or needs, but links to municipal systems are weak or incomplete. | Proceed only if the program can strengthen coordination, planning, and institutional linkages. |
| Weak municipal alignment | The area is not visible in municipal systems, lacks authority engagement, or cannot be linked to planning, preparedness, or recovery pathways. | Reconsider selection or use a narrower humanitarian intervention while advocating for municipal inclusion. |

## Design Implications

If the diagnostic shows strong or partial municipal alignment, the next design steps should include:

| Design step | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Engage municipal leadership early | Align the area-based response with official DRR, recovery, infrastructure, and service priorities. |
| Link area analysis to city plans | Ensure neighborhood-level findings inform municipal planning, investment, and coordination. |
| Identify critical services and infrastructure | Focus on systems whose failure would increase disaster risk or slow recovery. |
| Strengthen preparedness mechanisms | Connect the area to early warning, evacuation, contingency planning, public communication, and emergency access systems. |
| Include recovery and build-back-better logic | Design interventions that reduce future risk, not only immediate needs. |
| Support local ownership | Work with community groups, civil society, and local authorities to sustain improvements beyond the project period. |

## Boundary Selection Principles

**Select areas that matter to municipal systems.** A strong ABA target area should connect to citywide risk, infrastructure, services, or recovery priorities.

**Use municipal plans as evidence, not as the only evidence.** Official plans may miss informal settlements, undocumented populations, or marginalized neighborhoods.

**Connect neighborhood action to city resilience.** Area-based work should create lessons, models, or investments that can inform broader municipal DRR practice.

**Assess both authority capacity and community capacity.** Resilience depends on formal institutions and local social systems working together.

**Treat municipal alignment as a design opportunity.** If the area is poorly integrated into municipal systems, the program may help create that connection.

## Risk Alerts

**Plan-only trap:** Do not assume an area is low priority because it is absent from official plans. Informal or marginalized areas may be underrepresented.

**Authority-only trap:** Do not align only with municipal leadership while ignoring communities, informal actors, and civil society.

**Project-isolation risk:** If the area is not connected to municipal systems, improvements may not be sustained after the project ends.

**Budget disconnect:** DRR priorities without financing pathways may remain aspirational unless the program identifies realistic funding or investment options.

**Infrastructure blind spot:** Focusing only on community needs without analyzing infrastructure and service systems can miss major drivers of urban risk.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Municipal DRR Alignment Diagnostic Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Proposed target area | The neighborhood, settlement, district, corridor, or urban zone being screened. |
| Municipal relevance | How the area connects to city DRR, resilience, development, infrastructure, or recovery priorities. |
| Governance actors | Municipal departments, authorities, service providers, DRR platforms, and community actors relevant to the area. |
| Key risks | Main hazards, vulnerabilities, infrastructure weaknesses, and service dependencies. |
| Preparedness linkages | Early warning, evacuation, contingency planning, emergency access, or response systems connected to the area. |
| Budget or investment pathways | Possible municipal, donor, private-sector, or community financing opportunities. |
| Inclusion concerns | Groups or neighborhoods that may be underrepresented in municipal systems. |
| Alignment rating | Strong, partial, or weak municipal alignment. |
| Recommended next step | Proceed, adapt, advocate for inclusion, or reconsider the target area. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when area selection should be connected to **municipal disaster risk reduction and resilience systems**, not only immediate humanitarian need.

Prioritize areas where neighborhood-level action can strengthen or connect to wider **governance, budgeting, infrastructure, preparedness, recovery, and city resilience priorities**.
