# Handover Readiness Checklist Framework

**Source:** Sanderson & Sitko, *Urban area-based approaches in post-disaster contexts*; UNDRR city resilience logic; IFRC urban resilience practice  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 10. Transition, handover, and scaling  
**Framework purpose:** Confirm whether local actors can maintain services, infrastructure, preparedness systems, accountability mechanisms, and coordination arrangements after humanitarian exit.

## Framework Overview

The **Handover Readiness Checklist Framework** helps area-based teams determine whether an intervention is ready to transition from humanitarian delivery to local ownership.

It is designed for the transition, handover, and scaling stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), when teams must verify that improvements in services, infrastructure, preparedness, coordination, and accountability can continue after external support reduces or ends.

The framework is grounded in the urban ABA principle that effective area-based action should be people-centred, location-focused, connected to existing structures, collaborative across sectors, realistic about timeframes, and able to move from relief toward recovery. It treats handover as a managed process, not a final meeting or asset transfer.

## Core Framework Question

**Can local actors realistically maintain the services, infrastructure, preparedness systems, accountability mechanisms, and coordination relationships created or strengthened by the area-based intervention after external actors exit?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Responsible actor | The institution, group, department, service provider, committee, or community structure that will own or manage each output. | Handover fails when assets or functions have no clear owner. |
| Maintenance capacity | Skills, tools, staffing, operating procedures, spare parts, supervision, and technical support needed to keep the output functional. | Infrastructure and services deteriorate quickly if maintenance is not planned and resourced. |
| Budget and resource source | Municipal budget line, service-provider budget, community contribution, donor follow-on support, user fee, contingency fund, or in-kind arrangement. | Sustainability requires predictable resources, not only goodwill. |
| Coordination continuity | Mechanisms that will continue connecting communities, authorities, service providers, NGOs, and sector actors. | Area-based gains can fragment after exit if coordination stops. |
| Accountability mechanism | Feedback channels, complaint pathways, public commitments, response protocols, and community review processes. | Residents need a way to raise problems after the project closes. |
| Preparedness continuity | Early warning, evacuation, response roles, drills, supplies, and local emergency contacts that remain active. | Preparedness systems must function during a shock, not only during project implementation. |
| Referral options | Pathways for unresolved protection, shelter, WASH, health, legal, livelihood, or social service needs. | Handover should not abandon unresolved vulnerabilities. |
| Unresolved risks | Technical, protection, tenure, environmental, political, financial, or operational issues that remain open. | Exit decisions should make residual risks explicit. |
| Community communication | What residents know about ownership, maintenance, responsibilities, feedback options, and what support will stop or continue. | Clear communication reduces false expectations and accountability gaps. |
| Monitoring after exit | Light-touch follow-up, municipal review, community monitoring, service tracking, or post-handover check-ins. | Handover quality should be verified after responsibilities transfer. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a readiness-gate process:

1. **List all outputs requiring handover**  
   Include infrastructure, drainage, WASH facilities, shelter repair support systems, community preparedness structures, local committees, referral pathways, information systems, equipment, public spaces, and coordination arrangements.

2. **Identify the owner or responsible actor for each output**  
   Confirm the person, office, institution, utility, community structure, or service provider that will manage each output after exit.

3. **Test the maintenance pathway**  
   Check whether the responsible actor has the authority, staffing, technical skills, tools, spare parts, operating procedures, and supervision needed.

4. **Confirm the resource pathway**  
   Identify the budget, resource stream, in-kind arrangement, or financing source that will cover recurrent costs.

5. **Review unresolved risks**  
   Document technical defects, legal issues, land tenure risks, protection concerns, environmental risks, community disputes, or political constraints.

6. **Verify accountability and communication**  
   Confirm that residents know who is responsible after exit and how to report problems.

7. **Check referral and residual caseload arrangements**  
   Identify what happens to unresolved household, protection, shelter, WASH, legal, or livelihood needs.

8. **Agree the handover package**  
   Prepare documents, drawings, inventories, contact lists, maintenance schedules, operating procedures, training records, and action points.

9. **Define post-handover monitoring**  
   Establish what will be checked, by whom, how often, and what triggers corrective action.

## Handover Readiness Checklist

| Checklist area | Readiness question | Evidence required |
|---|---|---|
| Ownership | Who is formally responsible for this output after exit? | Named actor, written agreement, meeting minutes, mandate confirmation, or public commitment. |
| Authority | Does the actor have legal, administrative, or social authority to manage the output? | Mandate, permit, municipal decision, committee terms of reference, or service-provider approval. |
| Maintenance | Is there a maintenance plan with tasks, frequency, tools, and responsible persons? | Maintenance schedule, task list, trained focal points, asset inventory, or O&M plan. |
| Budget | What resource source will pay for recurrent costs? | Budget line, municipal plan, community fund, service fee, donor commitment, or in-kind arrangement. |
| Staffing | Are there enough trained people to continue the function? | Training records, staff assignment, volunteer roster, duty schedule, or supervision plan. |
| Technical quality | Is the output safe, functional, and technically acceptable for transfer? | Completion certificate, inspection record, defect list, technical sign-off, or service test. |
| Risk management | What risks remain unresolved? | Risk register, mitigation plan, referral list, protection notes, or escalation pathway. |
| Accountability | Can residents report problems and receive responses? | Feedback channel, complaint protocol, public contact list, response commitment, or community meeting record. |
| Community communication | Do residents know what will continue, what will stop, and who is responsible? | Community notice, meeting record, translated communication material, or FAQ. |
| Referral | Where will unresolved needs be referred after exit? | Referral directory, case handover notes, partner commitments, or service map. |
| Preparedness | Will early warning, evacuation, emergency roles, or response supplies remain active? | Drill record, emergency contact list, route map, equipment inventory, or contingency plan. |
| Monitoring | How will post-handover performance be checked? | Follow-up schedule, indicators, municipal/community monitoring role, or trigger list. |

## Handover Readiness Scoring

| Score | Meaning | Decision |
|---|---|---|
| 3 - Ready | Responsible actor, budget/resource pathway, maintenance plan, accountability route, and risk mitigation are all clear. | Proceed with handover and schedule follow-up. |
| 2 - Mostly ready | Main owner is clear, but some maintenance, budget, communication, or risk issues need closure. | Proceed only with a short corrective action plan. |
| 1 - Not ready | Ownership or resources are unclear, or critical risks remain unresolved. | Delay handover or redesign the transition pathway. |
| 0 - No pathway | No credible actor, authority, budget, or maintenance route exists. | Do not hand over as-is; escalate to redesign or closure decision. |

## Handover Meeting Agenda

| Agenda item | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Output inventory | Confirm what is being transferred or transitioned. |
| Responsible actor confirmation | Verify who owns, manages, supervises, and maintains each output. |
| Technical status review | Review quality, defects, safety issues, and completion records. |
| Maintenance plan review | Agree tasks, frequency, staff, tools, spare parts, and escalation routes. |
| Budget and resource review | Confirm recurrent cost source and resource gaps. |
| Community communication | Agree what will be communicated to residents and through which channels. |
| Accountability pathway | Confirm how residents report problems and how responses are tracked. |
| Referral and residual caseload | Agree where unresolved cases, needs, or risks will go. |
| Post-handover monitoring | Set check-in dates, indicators, responsible actors, and triggers. |
| Sign-off and unresolved issues | Record what is accepted, what is conditional, and what remains open. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Exit planning | Turns exit into a staged process with readiness gates. |
| Technical design | Forces teams to design infrastructure and services with ownership and maintenance in mind. |
| Budget planning | Identifies recurrent cost gaps before handover. |
| Municipal engagement | Clarifies where municipal departments, utilities, or service providers must accept responsibility. |
| Community engagement | Ensures residents understand what will change after exit. |
| Risk management | Prevents unresolved risks from being hidden inside a handover ceremony. |
| Accountability | Maintains feedback and complaint options after implementation ends. |
| Scaling | Helps distinguish models that are truly transferable from those dependent on external actors. |

## Risk Alerts

**Paper handover risk:** Signed documents do not prove readiness if resources, maintenance, and accountability are missing.

**Unfunded maintenance risk:** Local actors may accept responsibility without having funds, staff, tools, or spare parts.

**Ownership ambiguity risk:** Multiple actors may assume someone else is responsible.

**Community expectation risk:** Residents may expect continued support unless the exit and referral pathway are clearly communicated.

**Residual vulnerability risk:** Unresolved household or protection cases may be abandoned during transition.

**Parallel-system risk:** Humanitarian structures may disappear if they were never connected to existing municipal or community systems.

**Premature exit risk:** Donor timelines may force closure before local systems can realistically maintain gains.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Handover Readiness Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | Neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone covered by the transition. |
| Outputs to hand over | Infrastructure, services, preparedness systems, committees, equipment, tools, or referral arrangements. |
| Responsible actors | Named institutions, departments, service providers, community groups, or individuals. |
| Maintenance plan | Tasks, schedule, tools, staffing, technical support, and supervision arrangements. |
| Budget/source | Funding or resource pathway for recurrent costs and repairs. |
| Unresolved risks | Technical, social, protection, tenure, environmental, financial, or governance risks. |
| Community communication | How residents will be informed about responsibilities, changes, feedback, and referral options. |
| Referral options | Services, partners, government offices, or community structures for unresolved needs. |
| Readiness score | Ready, mostly ready, not ready, or no pathway. |
| Follow-up actions | Deadlines, responsible actors, monitoring checks, and escalation triggers. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework before closing, transferring, or scaling any area-based intervention.

A handover is ready only when **the responsible actor is named, the maintenance pathway is realistic, the resource source is identified, unresolved risks are visible, the community knows what will happen next, and accountability or referral options remain available after exit**.
