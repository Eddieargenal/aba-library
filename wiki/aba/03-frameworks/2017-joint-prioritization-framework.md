# Joint Prioritization Framework

## Atomic question
Across competing risks and needs identified in area diagnosis, what should the ABA programme address first?

## Why this decision matters
Without a joint cross-sector prioritization process, each sector lead defaults to prioritizing their own domain, producing a set of parallel sector programmes rather than an integrated area response. The EFL Basics First concept demonstrates that addressing foundational life-safety issues first is not obvious under sector pressure — it requires an explicit joint process to surface and protect. In multi-actor contexts, issues that no single actor can claim full ownership of are systematically underweighted unless contribution-not-attribution logic is built into the prioritization design.

## Prerequisites
- From stage 2 (Neighbourhood Diagnosis): completed HEVC profile, service functionality map, and stakeholder landscape
- From stage 3 (Area-Based Coordination): functioning area coordination platform with cross-sector actor representation to conduct the joint session
- Minimum evidence standard: at least 2 independent source types per domain before scoring (direct observation, key informant interviews, community consultation, household data, service provider data, secondary data, or 4W/5W coordination data)

## Decision domains

1. **Risk severity** (25%) — what is the magnitude, likelihood, and imminence of the risk or need? Life-safety risks score highest regardless of operational feasibility. A low feasibility score for a life-safety issue triggers resource mobilization, not deprioritization.

2. **Vulnerability concentration** (20%) — which population groups are most exposed and least able to cope? Score based on disaggregated data by gender, age, disability, tenure, legal status, and social exclusion. Hardest-to-reach groups must be explicitly assessed, not assumed absent.

3. **Systems interdependence** (20%) — does addressing this issue unlock or block progress on multiple other sectors simultaneously? Key nodes in the urban system (water supply, drainage, shelter, market infrastructure) that cascade across sectors score higher.

4. **DRR and future-risk relevance** (15%) — does addressing this now reduce future disaster risk, or does it address only present need? Informed by Sendai Priority 3 (invest in DRR for resilience). Issues that both meet present need and reduce future risk score highest.

5. **Operational feasibility** (10%) — can this be addressed within the programme's mandate, budget, and timeframe? Feasibility is a tiebreaker between issues of equal priority, not a gatekeeping criterion. A life-safety issue that scores low on feasibility triggers resource mobilization and escalation, not automatic reassignment to a lower tier.

6. **Actor responsibility and coverage** (10%) — is this issue within the programme's mandate, or is another actor better placed to address it? Use 4W/5W data to distinguish gaps (no actor covering) from duplications (multiple actors covering) and mandate-mismatches (another actor better placed).

## Decision logic

Score each identified risk or need issue 0–4 per domain:
- 4 = severe / highly concentrated / critical system node / strongly reduces future risk / fully within mandate and capacity / clear gap with no actor coverage
- 3 = significant
- 2 = moderate
- 1 = low
- 0 = absent or not applicable to this issue

Multiply each domain score by its weight percentage, sum to produce a weighted total out of 100.

**Priority tiers:**

| Score | Priority tier | Required action |
|---|---|---|
| 80–100 | Critical | Address in phase 1 with dedicated resources; do not defer |
| 60–79 | High | Address in phase 1 with a resource plan; may be phased within phase 1 |
| 40–59 | Medium | Address in phase 2, or formally refer to a more appropriate actor |
| Below 40 | Low | Refer to competent actor and document the referral; do not programme |

**Basics First override (EFL/Twigg):** Any issue scoring ≥3 on Risk Severity AND ≥3 on Vulnerability Concentration must be placed in phase 1 regardless of its weighted total score. This prevents operational feasibility from excluding the most at-risk populations from immediate programming.

**Sendai Priority 1 application:** The joint session must produce a shared written risk narrative — the reasoning behind each tier assignment — not only a ranked list. The narrative makes the prioritization defensible to communities, donors, and partners, and enables accountability when the programme adapts.

**Minimum joint session standard:** The scoring must be conducted in a cross-sector joint session (not by individual sector leads separately then merged). Participants must represent at least three different sector mandates. Community representatives or validated community data must be present or the output is flagged as provisional.

## Red flags / override conditions

- A life-safety issue (domain 1 score ≥3) is assigned to phase 2 or lower solely due to a low operational feasibility score → do not accept this outcome; escalate for resource mobilization and document the resource gap
- Prioritization is conducted by individual sector leads producing separate lists that are then merged rather than through a joint deliberative session → reject the output; the merged list is not a joint prioritization; redo as a cross-sector joint session
- Protection risks from targeting a specific population group were not assessed before finalizing priorities → halt until a protection review is complete; do not finalize any priority that involves targeting a specific group without this check
- The scoring is completed without any community input or validation → flag all outputs as provisional; community validation session required before strategy design can proceed

## Failure modes

- Breaks when the joint session is structured as a reporting meeting — each sector presents its own priority list and the lists are merged rather than jointly examined against shared criteria; the result is sector priorities, not an integrated area prioritization
- Breaks when operational feasibility is applied as a primary criterion rather than a tiebreaker, systematically excluding the highest-risk populations who are hardest to reach and whose needs have the poorest feasibility profile
- Breaks when the Sendai four priorities are applied as a rigid sequential order (first understand risk, then strengthen governance, then invest) rather than as simultaneously active lenses weighted differently depending on context and programming phase
- Breaks when contribution-vs-attribution logic is absent from the evaluation design: in multi-actor contexts, any issue for which no single actor can claim full ownership tends to receive a lower actor responsibility score and is deprioritized — despite being the issue that most requires joint programming
