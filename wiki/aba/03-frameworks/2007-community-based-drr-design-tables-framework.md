# Community-Based DRR Design Tables Framework

**Source:** Twigg, *Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community*  
**Years:** 2007 / 2009  
**Lifecycle stage:** 7. Detailed technical design  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to translate “resilience” into observable community capacities, preparedness actions, early warning systems, evacuation arrangements, local response mechanisms, risk knowledge, and recovery activities.

## Framework Overview

The **Community-Based DRR Design Tables Framework** helps teams design practical disaster risk reduction (DRR) activities at community, neighborhood, settlement, or area level.

It is designed for the detailed technical design stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), when teams need to convert broad resilience goals into concrete capacities, actions, roles, indicators, and implementation steps.

The framework is especially useful because it makes resilience observable. Instead of treating resilience as a vague outcome, it breaks it into capacities that can be assessed, strengthened, monitored, and maintained over time.

## Core Framework Question

**What capacities must the community and local systems have to understand risk, prepare for hazards, receive warnings, evacuate safely, respond locally, and recover after shocks?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Risk knowledge | Community understanding of hazards, exposure, vulnerability, capacities, past events, and future risks. | People and institutions can only reduce risks they understand. |
| Preparedness planning | Local contingency plans, roles, procedures, resources, communication channels, and coordination arrangements. | Converts risk knowledge into organized readiness. |
| Early warning | Monitoring, alerts, communication chains, trusted messengers, warning interpretation, and action triggers. | Helps people act before hazards become disasters. |
| Evacuation and safe access | Evacuation routes, safe spaces, transport, accessibility, support for vulnerable groups, and route safety. | Reduces loss of life and harm during emergencies. |
| Local response capacity | Volunteers, first aid, search and rescue, emergency supplies, referral systems, local leadership, and coordination. | Enables rapid action before external support arrives. |
| Risk reduction actions | Practical measures that reduce exposure and vulnerability before a shock occurs. | Helps communities reduce risk rather than only prepare to respond. |
| Recovery capacity | Local ability to restore services, livelihoods, housing, infrastructure, social networks, and governance after a shock. | Supports faster, safer, and more equitable recovery. |
| Inclusion and accountability | Participation of women, children, older people, people with disabilities, displaced people, renters, informal settlers, and marginalized groups. | Ensures DRR systems work for those most at risk. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a capacity-based DRR design process:

1. **Define the target area and hazards**  
   Identify the neighborhood, settlement, community, or urban area and the main hazards or stresses affecting it.

2. **Assess existing capacities**  
   Review what communities, local authorities, service providers, civil society, and households already do to prepare, respond, and recover.

3. **Identify capacity gaps**  
   Compare current capacities against the capacities needed for preparedness, warning, evacuation, response, risk reduction, and recovery.

4. **Define practical actions**  
   Translate each capacity gap into a concrete activity, such as risk mapping, training, evacuation planning, early warning communication, local stockpiles, or community drills.

5. **Assign roles and responsibilities**  
   Clarify which actions belong to households, community groups, volunteers, local leaders, authorities, service providers, schools, health facilities, and humanitarian actors.

6. **Design inclusive mechanisms**  
   Ensure that people most at risk can participate in planning, receive warnings, evacuate safely, access assistance, and influence recovery decisions.

7. **Link community systems to formal systems**  
   Connect community DRR structures with municipal DRR committees, emergency services, health systems, schools, service providers, and humanitarian coordination.

8. **Define indicators and verification methods**  
   Select observable signs that the capacity exists and can function during a shock.

9. **Review and update regularly**  
   DRR systems should be tested through drills, updated after events, and revised as risks, populations, services, and local leadership change.

## Community DRR Capacity Design Table

| Capacity area | Required capacity | Possible program activities |
|---|---|---|
| Risk knowledge | Community members understand local hazards, exposed areas, vulnerable groups, and available capacities. | Participatory risk mapping, historical hazard timelines, transect walks, seasonal calendars, community risk discussions, school-based risk education. |
| Preparedness planning | The community has clear plans, roles, resources, and communication procedures before a hazard occurs. | Community contingency planning, role assignment, preparedness committees, household preparedness plans, emergency contact lists, resource mapping. |
| Early warning | People receive timely, trusted, understandable warnings and know what action to take. | Warning protocols, community alert chains, SMS or radio alerts, flag or siren systems, trusted messenger networks, action-trigger guidance. |
| Evacuation | People can move safely to appropriate locations, including those with mobility or protection barriers. | Evacuation route mapping, safe site identification, route marking, evacuation drills, transport support, disability-inclusive evacuation planning. |
| Local response | Local actors can take immediate action before external support arrives. | First aid training, light search and rescue, community response teams, emergency supplies, referral pathways, response simulations. |
| Risk reduction | Communities and authorities take action to reduce exposure and vulnerability. | Drainage clearing, slope stabilization, safe access improvements, waste removal, fire prevention, safer shelter guidance, hazard-informed settlement planning. |
| Recovery | The community can restore basic functions and recover safely after a shock. | Recovery planning, damage assessment training, repair guidance, livelihood restart support, psychosocial support, community recovery committees. |
| Inclusion | At-risk groups can participate, receive support, and influence decisions. | Separate consultations, accessible communication, disability inclusion, gender-sensitive planning, child-safe mechanisms, protection referrals. |
| Accountability | Community members can raise concerns, report problems, and help improve DRR systems. | Feedback channels, complaint mechanisms, public action plans, community scorecards, review meetings, transparent decision-making. |

## Early Warning and Preparedness Design Table

| Design question | What to define |
|---|---|
| What hazards require warning? | Flood, fire, landslide, storm, heat, disease outbreak, conflict escalation, eviction, or infrastructure failure. |
| Who monitors the risk? | Community volunteers, local authorities, meteorological services, health workers, schools, service providers, or local observers. |
| What triggers action? | Rainfall threshold, river level, fire signal, public health alert, conflict indicator, service failure, or official warning. |
| Who sends the warning? | Trusted leaders, authorities, radio stations, SMS systems, community committees, schools, faith leaders, or volunteers. |
| Who receives the warning? | All residents, specific high-risk groups, schools, health facilities, landlords, tenants, informal settlers, and service providers. |
| How is the warning communicated? | Door-to-door alerts, SMS, WhatsApp, radio, loudspeaker, flags, sirens, community focal points, or public announcements. |
| What action should people take? | Evacuate, shelter in place, move assets, protect documents, avoid routes, boil water, contact support, or activate local response teams. |
| How are vulnerable people supported? | Buddy systems, transport support, accessible routes, caregiver plans, priority assistance, and inclusive communication. |

## Evacuation and Safe Access Design Table

| Component | Design requirement |
|---|---|
| Routes | Identify safe, accessible, well-known routes that avoid hazard zones and protection risks. |
| Safe locations | Identify safe shelters, public buildings, higher ground, host sites, or temporary assembly points. |
| Accessibility | Include people with disabilities, older people, children, pregnant women, and people with limited mobility. |
| Safety | Assess lighting, security, GBV risks, checkpoints, crowding, family separation, and unsafe travel times. |
| Communication | Make route and safe-site information understandable to all groups. |
| Drills | Test routes, timings, roles, and bottlenecks through practice exercises. |
| Responsibilities | Assign who supports evacuation, manages safe sites, checks high-risk households, and communicates updates. |
| Review | Update plans after drills, population movements, infrastructure changes, or hazard events. |

## Local Response Capacity Design Table

| Response function | Required design element |
|---|---|
| First aid | Trained volunteers, basic supplies, referral links, and safe response protocols. |
| Search and rescue | Light rescue capacity, equipment, safety rules, and coordination with formal responders. |
| Fire response | Fire prevention, basic suppression tools, safe evacuation, and links to fire services. |
| Flood response | Monitoring, evacuation triggers, drainage clearing, rescue support, and safe water practices. |
| Public health response | Hygiene messaging, disease alerts, referral pathways, isolation guidance, and coordination with health actors. |
| Protection response | Safe referral pathways for GBV, child protection, violence, exploitation, eviction, and family separation. |
| Information management | Community reporting channels, situation updates, needs tracking, and coordination with authorities. |
| Relief coordination | Fair distribution, priority support, crowd management, feedback channels, and accountability safeguards. |

## Recovery Capacity Design Table

| Recovery area | Capacity to strengthen |
|---|---|
| Housing recovery | Safer repair guidance, damage assessment, material access, technical support, and tenure safeguards. |
| Livelihood recovery | Market access, tools, cash flow, skills, local employment, business restart, and debt management support. |
| Service recovery | Restoration of water, sanitation, electricity, waste, schools, health, transport, and administrative services. |
| Infrastructure recovery | Repair of access routes, drainage, public spaces, community facilities, and protective works. |
| Social recovery | Mutual aid, psychosocial support, community cohesion, conflict resolution, and inclusive participation. |
| Governance recovery | Local recovery committees, municipal coordination, transparent decisions, public plans, and accountability. |
| Risk-informed recovery | Build-back-safer guidance, hazard-aware planning, future-risk reduction, and preparedness updates. |

## Design Principles

**Make resilience observable.** Each capacity should be translated into visible behaviors, systems, tools, or arrangements that can be checked.

**Start from existing capacities.** Communities already have coping mechanisms, knowledge, networks, and leadership structures that should be recognized and strengthened.

**Connect community and municipal systems.** Community-based DRR is strongest when linked to formal preparedness, early warning, emergency response, and recovery systems.

**Include those most at risk.** DRR systems fail if warnings, evacuation routes, response teams, or recovery mechanisms exclude people with the greatest barriers.

**Test plans in practice.** Drills, simulations, and after-action reviews reveal weaknesses that documents alone cannot show.

**Update regularly.** Risk, population, leadership, infrastructure, and service conditions change over time.

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Preparedness activities | Defines the capacities, roles, tools, and procedures needed before a shock. |
| Early warning design | Clarifies warning sources, triggers, channels, messengers, and expected actions. |
| Evacuation planning | Identifies routes, safe sites, accessibility measures, protection risks, and support roles. |
| Local response strengthening | Defines training, equipment, referral, coordination, and volunteer systems. |
| DRR infrastructure | Links small works to risk reduction, preparedness, and community maintenance. |
| Recovery planning | Identifies capacities needed for safer and faster post-shock recovery. |
| Inclusion | Ensures at-risk groups are included in design, communication, evacuation, response, and recovery. |
| Monitoring | Converts resilience into measurable capacities and indicators. |

## Risk Alerts

**Resilience-vagueness risk:** Do not use “resilience” as a slogan. Define the specific capacities that should exist.

**Training-only trap:** Training alone does not create preparedness unless roles, resources, triggers, and coordination systems are in place.

**Plan-without-practice risk:** Written plans may fail if they are not tested through drills and simulations.

**Exclusion risk:** Warnings, evacuation routes, and response systems may not work for people with disabilities, older people, women, children, renters, informal settlers, undocumented people, or isolated households.

**Local-capacity overload:** Do not assign responsibilities to community volunteers without resources, support, safety measures, or realistic expectations.

**Disconnected-systems risk:** Community DRR mechanisms may fail if they are not linked to municipal authorities, services, and formal emergency systems.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Community-Based DRR Design Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | The neighborhood, settlement, community, or urban zone where DRR capacities will be strengthened. |
| Main hazards and risks | Hazards, exposure, vulnerability, and priority risk scenarios. |
| Existing capacities | Current community, household, institutional, infrastructure, and response capacities. |
| Capacity gaps | Preparedness, warning, evacuation, response, risk reduction, recovery, and inclusion gaps. |
| Proposed actions | Activities to strengthen each required capacity. |
| Roles and responsibilities | Households, volunteers, community groups, authorities, service providers, schools, health facilities, and humanitarian actors. |
| Inclusion measures | How at-risk groups will participate and safely access DRR systems. |
| Linkages to formal systems | Connections to municipal DRR, emergency services, early warning, health, protection, education, and recovery systems. |
| Indicators | Observable signs that capacities exist and can function. |
| Review and update plan | Drills, simulations, after-action reviews, and periodic updates. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the team needs to translate **community resilience** into practical DRR design.

A strong community-based DRR design should show **what hazards matter, what capacities already exist, what gaps must be addressed, what actions will strengthen preparedness and response, who is responsible, how inclusion is ensured, and how capacities will be tested and maintained**.
