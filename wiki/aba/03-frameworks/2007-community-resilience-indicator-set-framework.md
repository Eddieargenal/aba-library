# Community Resilience Indicator Set Framework

**Source:** Twigg, *Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community: A Guidance Note*  
**Years:** 2007 / 2009  
**Lifecycle stage:** 9. Monitoring, learning, and adaptation  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to measure progress against community resilience characteristics, including risk knowledge, governance, preparedness, livelihoods, infrastructure, health, social capacity, and local systems.

## Framework Overview

The **Community Resilience Indicator Set Framework** helps teams monitor whether an area-based intervention is strengthening the capacities that make a community safer, more prepared, more adaptive, and better able to recover from shocks.

It is designed for the monitoring, learning, and adaptation stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), especially when teams need to move beyond counting outputs and assess whether resilience capacities are actually improving across households, communities, services, institutions, and local governance systems.

The framework adapts Twigg’s disaster-resilient community characteristics into a practical indicator set for baseline, midline, endline, participatory review, and adaptive management. It treats resilience as a set of observable characteristics rather than a vague aspiration.

## Core Framework Question

**Is the target area becoming more disaster-resilient, and which capacities still need to be strengthened, adapted, coordinated, or sustained?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it measures | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Governance and participation | Local DRR policies, plans, responsibilities, coordination mechanisms, partnerships, accountability, and community participation. | Resilience depends on clear roles, legitimate local institutions, and meaningful participation. |
| Risk assessment and risk knowledge | Community understanding of hazards, exposure, vulnerabilities, capacities, risk mapping, monitoring, and shared analysis. | Communities and institutions can only reduce risks they understand and monitor. |
| Knowledge and education | Public awareness, education, training, information sharing, local knowledge, and cultures of safety. | Risk information must be understood, trusted, shared, and used in everyday decisions. |
| Risk management and vulnerability reduction | Livelihood protection, social protection, health, ecosystem management, safe shelter, infrastructure, land-use planning, and physical risk reduction. | Preparedness is not enough if underlying risk conditions remain unchanged. |
| Disaster preparedness and response | Early warning, contingency planning, evacuation, emergency resources, response coordination, recovery arrangements, and volunteer systems. | Communities need functional systems before, during, and immediately after a shock. |
| Inclusion and equity | Participation and outcomes for women, children, older people, people with disabilities, displaced people, informal settlers, renters, and marginalized groups. | Resilience is weak if the people most exposed to risk are excluded from planning or cannot use preparedness systems. |
| Enabling environment | Support from local authorities, national systems, legal frameworks, technical agencies, service providers, and financing arrangements. | Community resilience depends on wider institutional, legal, financial, and technical support. |
| Coordination and coherence | Alignment between community, municipal, humanitarian, service-provider, and civil society efforts. | Fragmented DRR actions can create gaps, duplication, and weak sustainability. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a resilience-characteristics monitoring process:

1. **Define the target area and risk profile**  
   Identify the neighborhood, settlement, district, or community being monitored and the main hazards, stresses, exposure patterns, and vulnerability groups.

2. **Select relevant resilience characteristics**  
   Do not use every possible indicator automatically. Select the characteristics most relevant to the local hazard context, intervention scope, community priorities, and institutional responsibilities.

3. **Convert characteristics into practical indicators**  
   Translate each resilience characteristic into a measurable or observable indicator that can be assessed through surveys, focus groups, observation, administrative data, participatory scoring, or service-system review.

4. **Establish a baseline**  
   Assess the current level of resilience before or at the start of implementation. Record existing capacities, gaps, risks, and institutional support.

5. **Use participatory scoring**  
   Combine community views, technical observation, local authority input, service-provider data, and project evidence to assess progress.

6. **Track progress at multiple levels**  
   Monitor households, community structures, local services, infrastructure systems, institutions, and governance arrangements, not only project outputs.

7. **Review progress against milestones**  
   Use a simple staged scale to show movement from low capacity to stronger, coordinated, and sustained resilience.

8. **Identify gaps, bottlenecks, and adaptation needs**  
   Use monitoring findings to adjust activities, strengthen coordination, change targeting, improve inclusion, or address weak implementation.

9. **Use findings for learning and advocacy**  
   Share results with communities, local authorities, humanitarian actors, donors, and service providers to support better planning and investment.

## Community Resilience Indicator Set

| Resilience area | Example indicators | Possible verification methods |
|---|---|---|
| Governance and coordination | Local DRR roles are defined; community DRR structures exist; local authorities participate; responsibilities are documented; coordination meetings produce action points. | Meeting records, role maps, municipal plans, interviews, community validation, coordination minutes. |
| Participation and accountability | Residents influence priorities; feedback channels are used; vulnerable groups are represented; complaints are tracked and addressed. | Focus groups, complaint logs, participant lists, community scorecards, feedback analysis. |
| Risk knowledge | Community members can identify main hazards, exposed zones, vulnerable groups, safe areas, and local capacities. | Participatory risk maps, household surveys, KIIs, transect walks, community exercises. |
| Risk assessment systems | Hazard and vulnerability assessments are updated; findings are shared; risk data informs planning. | Assessment reports, map updates, planning documents, stakeholder interviews. |
| Public awareness and education | Residents know preparedness actions; schools or community groups conduct risk education; public information is understandable and accessible. | Awareness surveys, school records, communication materials, observation, FGDs. |
| Early warning | Warning sources, triggers, messengers, channels, and actions are defined and understood by the community. | Warning protocols, drill reports, interviews, simulation results, communication logs. |
| Preparedness planning | Contingency plans, evacuation routes, emergency contacts, roles, and response procedures exist and are tested. | Plan review, drill reports, route mapping, volunteer lists, after-action reviews. |
| Emergency response capacity | Local volunteers or responders have training, supplies, referral links, and coordination arrangements. | Training records, stock checks, response simulations, referral pathway review. |
| Evacuation and safe access | Safe routes and sites are identified, accessible, inclusive, and known by residents. | Route observation, accessibility checks, community mapping, drills, protection risk review. |
| Livelihood resilience | Households have safer income strategies, diversified livelihoods, savings, market access, or recovery support options. | Household surveys, market assessments, livelihood monitoring, group discussions. |
| Health and WASH resilience | Health risks are monitored; WASH systems reduce disease risk; health referral pathways are known; service continuity is planned. | Health facility data, WASH assessments, disease surveillance, referral records, observation. |
| Shelter and infrastructure safety | Housing, drainage, access routes, public buildings, schools, and critical infrastructure are safer or better maintained. | Technical inspection, infrastructure checklists, community reporting, maintenance records. |
| Social capacity | Mutual aid, community networks, trusted leaders, local organizations, and support mechanisms are active. | Social network mapping, FGDs, volunteer records, community organization review. |
| Recovery capacity | Recovery roles, damage assessment processes, safer repair guidance, service restoration plans, and support mechanisms are defined. | Recovery plans, damage assessment tools, interviews, simulation exercises. |
| Institutional support | Local authorities, service providers, NGOs, and technical agencies provide support to community DRR systems. | Partnership records, budget lines, technical assistance records, coordination review. |

## Suggested Milestone Scale

Use a simple five-level scale to track progress over time. The purpose is not to produce a perfect score, but to support discussion, comparison, learning, and adaptation.

| Level | Description | What it means for the area |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1: Minimal capacity | Little awareness, weak organization, limited information, and no clear preparedness or risk reduction systems. | The area remains highly vulnerable and depends heavily on external action. |
| Level 2: Emerging capacity | Some awareness, initial community structures, partial risk information, and isolated preparedness or DRR actions. | Progress has started, but systems are incomplete and uneven. |
| Level 3: Developing capacity | Community and local actors have defined roles, selected plans, some tested actions, and growing coordination. | Core capacities exist but need stronger coverage, inclusion, and reliability. |
| Level 4: Established capacity | DRR systems are coordinated, tested, inclusive, linked to local institutions, and used for planning and response. | The area has functional resilience systems that can reduce risk and support response. |
| Level 5: Sustained resilience culture | Risk reduction is embedded in local decisions, institutions, services, infrastructure, education, and community behavior. | Resilience is maintained beyond the project and adapted as conditions change. |

## Baseline, Midline, Endline, and Review Use

| Monitoring moment | Purpose | Key questions |
|---|---|---|
| Baseline | Establish the starting level of resilience and identify priority gaps. | What capacities already exist? Which groups face the greatest barriers? Which systems are missing or weak? |
| Midline | Check progress and adapt implementation. | Which indicators are improving? Which activities are not producing capacity change? What needs adjustment? |
| Endline | Assess contribution to resilience outcomes. | What capacities are stronger than at baseline? Which systems are now functional? What remains unresolved? |
| Participatory review | Validate results with communities and stakeholders. | Do residents agree with the findings? Are the indicators meaningful? What changed in daily risk management? |
| Post-event review | Learn from an actual shock, stress, or emergency. | Which systems worked? Which failed? Who was excluded? What should be revised? |
| Sustainability review | Check whether capacities are maintained after project support reduces. | Who owns the systems? Are resources available? Are roles, maintenance, and updates continuing? |

## Indicator Selection Matrix

| Selection criterion | Practical test |
|---|---|
| Relevance | Does the indicator relate to the main hazards, vulnerabilities, and resilience priorities in the target area? |
| Observable change | Can the team and community see or verify progress over time? |
| Multi-level usefulness | Does it help understand household, community, service, infrastructure, or governance change? |
| Participatory validity | Can community members understand, challenge, and interpret the indicator? |
| Inclusion sensitivity | Does it reveal whether high-risk groups are benefiting or being excluded? |
| Actionability | Can the finding lead to a clear adaptation, coordination decision, or investment? |
| Feasibility | Can the data be collected without excessive burden or unrealistic technical requirements? |
| Sustainability | Does the indicator help track whether capacities will continue beyond the project period? |

## Participatory Review Questions

| Review area | Questions for communities and local actors |
|---|---|
| Risk knowledge | Do people know the main risks, warning signs, safe areas, exposed groups, and available support? |
| Preparedness | Are roles, plans, evacuation routes, contacts, and supplies clear and tested? |
| Inclusion | Can people with mobility, language, documentation, gender, age, disability, tenure, or social barriers use the system safely? |
| Coordination | Are community groups, local authorities, service providers, and humanitarian actors working from the same understanding of risk? |
| Infrastructure | Are drainage, access, shelter, public buildings, schools, health facilities, and WASH systems reducing or increasing risk? |
| Response | What would happen in the first 24 to 72 hours after a shock? Who acts first, and who needs support? |
| Recovery | Are there arrangements for damage assessment, safer repair, service restoration, livelihood recovery, and accountability? |
| Learning | What has changed since the last review? What still does not work? What should be adapted now? |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Monitoring plan | Converts resilience into observable capacities, indicators, milestones, and verification methods. |
| Baseline design | Helps teams assess existing capacities and gaps before implementation. |
| Adaptive management | Identifies which activities need revision because they are not improving real resilience. |
| Coordination | Highlights gaps and overlaps across community, municipal, service-provider, and humanitarian actors. |
| Inclusion strategy | Shows whether high-risk groups can participate in and benefit from DRR systems. |
| Infrastructure and service planning | Links resilience monitoring to the safety, functionality, and continuity of local systems. |
| Learning agenda | Supports participatory reflection and comparison over time. |
| Advocacy | Provides evidence for local authorities, donors, and partners on remaining resilience gaps. |

## Risk Alerts

**Indicator-overload risk:** The source contains many characteristics. Do not convert all of them into a heavy monitoring system. Select a manageable set.

**Output-counting risk:** Counting trainings, plans, or meetings does not prove resilience unless capacities, behaviors, systems, and coordination improve.

**False precision risk:** Resilience scores involve judgment. Use scoring to support discussion, not to create a misleading sense of mathematical certainty.

**Participation-tokenism risk:** Community scoring is weak if residents cannot challenge findings, identify missing indicators, or explain what changed in practice.

**Exclusion-blind risk:** Overall progress can hide the fact that people with disabilities, older people, renters, informal settlers, women, children, displaced people, or marginalized groups still face unsafe conditions.

**Project-isolation risk:** A community may appear more resilient inside a project boundary while wider municipal, service, infrastructure, or policy systems remain weak.

**Static-baseline risk:** Hazards, displacement, markets, infrastructure, services, and leadership can change quickly. Update indicators and baselines when the context shifts.

**Attribution-risk:** In area-based work, many actors contribute to change. Use contribution analysis and triangulation rather than claiming that all resilience gains were caused by one project.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Community Resilience Monitoring and Learning Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | The neighborhood, settlement, district, or community being monitored. |
| Risk profile | Main hazards, exposure patterns, vulnerable groups, and priority risk scenarios. |
| Selected resilience indicators | The agreed indicator set organized by governance, risk knowledge, preparedness, livelihoods, infrastructure, health, social capacity, and enabling environment. |
| Baseline status | Starting level for each selected resilience area. |
| Milestone scores | Participatory rating of progress using a simple staged scale. |
| Evidence sources | Surveys, FGDs, KIIs, observation, technical checks, service data, maps, complaints, drill reports, and administrative records. |
| Inclusion findings | How different groups experience risk, preparedness, access, response, and recovery. |
| Coordination findings | Gaps, overlaps, and linkages between community, municipal, service-provider, and humanitarian systems. |
| Adaptation actions | Changes required in implementation, targeting, coordination, resourcing, or technical design. |
| Learning and advocacy points | Findings to share with communities, authorities, donors, and partners. |
| Next review date | When the next baseline, midline, endline, participatory, or post-event review will occur. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the team needs to monitor whether an area-based intervention is actually strengthening community resilience, not merely delivering activities.

A strong community resilience indicator set should be **locally selected, participatory, manageable, multi-level, inclusion-sensitive, and action-oriented**. It should show whether people, institutions, services, infrastructure, and local systems are becoming better able to understand risk, reduce vulnerability, prepare, respond, recover, and adapt.
