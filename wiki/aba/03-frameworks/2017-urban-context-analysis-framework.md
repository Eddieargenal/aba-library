# Urban Context Analysis Framework

**Source:** IRC, *Urban Context Analysis Toolkit*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 2. Area profile and systems diagnosis  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to understand how an urban area works by analyzing power, governance, stakeholders, livelihoods, services, social networks, access, resource distribution, and local institutions.

## Framework Overview

The **Urban Context Analysis Framework** helps teams move beyond a simple needs assessment and develop a deeper understanding of the systems that shape life in a city, neighborhood, settlement, or urban district.

It is designed for the area profile and systems diagnosis stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA). The framework helps teams understand not only **what people need**, but also **why those needs exist**, **who controls access**, **how services and resources are distributed**, and **which institutions or networks shape outcomes**.

## Core Framework Question

**How does this urban area function, who influences it, and what systems shape people’s access to safety, services, livelihoods, resources, and support?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Power and governance | Formal authorities, informal leaders, political dynamics, decision-making structures, rules, and influence. | Shows who has authority, who makes decisions, and who may enable or block assistance. |
| Stakeholders and institutions | Government bodies, community groups, service providers, civil society, private sector, humanitarian actors, and informal institutions. | Identifies the actors that shape access, coordination, service delivery, and accountability. |
| Livelihoods and markets | Employment, income sources, informal work, market access, supply chains, financial services, and economic constraints. | Explains how people survive economically and how shocks affect income, purchasing power, and recovery. |
| Services and infrastructure | Water, sanitation, electricity, health, education, transport, waste, shelter, roads, drainage, and public facilities. | Reveals how essential services function, fail, and interact across the area. |
| Social networks and community systems | Family networks, neighborhood groups, faith networks, mutual aid, identity groups, social cohesion, and support mechanisms. | Helps teams understand how people access help, information, protection, and resources. |
| Access and mobility | Physical access, transport, safety, checkpoints, social barriers, affordability, discrimination, and documentation constraints. | Shows who can reach services and opportunities, and who is excluded. |
| Resource distribution | Distribution of land, housing, services, income, public investment, aid, social support, and political attention. | Identifies inequality, exclusion, and patterns of advantage or disadvantage. |
| Local risks and vulnerabilities | Safety risks, tenure insecurity, environmental hazards, exploitation, violence, service disruption, and institutional neglect. | Connects contextual analysis to practical response priorities and protection concerns. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a systems-diagnosis process:

1. **Define the area of analysis**  
   Identify the neighborhood, settlement, district, market zone, service catchment, or urban area being profiled.

2. **Map key actors and institutions**  
   Identify formal and informal actors that influence services, access, protection, livelihoods, infrastructure, and decision-making.

3. **Analyze governance and power**  
   Examine who controls decisions, resources, information, land, services, permissions, and access.

4. **Assess livelihoods and economic systems**  
   Understand how households earn income, access markets, manage debt, cope with shocks, and connect to formal or informal economies.

5. **Assess services and infrastructure systems**  
   Examine availability, quality, reliability, affordability, coverage, and dependency across key urban services.

6. **Analyze social networks and community relationships**  
   Identify trust relationships, mutual aid systems, community leadership, social cohesion, exclusion, and conflict dynamics.

7. **Examine access and exclusion**  
   Identify barriers faced by different groups, including women, children, older people, people with disabilities, displaced people, renters, informal workers, undocumented residents, and marginalized groups.

8. **Identify system bottlenecks and leverage points**  
   Determine where small changes could improve access, reduce risk, strengthen coordination, or unlock wider benefits.

9. **Translate findings into area profile priorities**  
   Convert the analysis into practical implications for program design, coordination, targeting, partnerships, and risk management.

## Urban Systems Diagnostic Matrix

| Diagnostic question | What to look for |
|---|---|
| Who has power in the area? | Formal authorities, informal leaders, landlords, armed actors, service providers, political brokers, community leaders, business owners, and civil society actors. |
| Who controls access to services and resources? | Gatekeepers, institutions, documentation systems, payment systems, landowners, utilities, committees, or informal networks. |
| How do people make a living? | Employment patterns, informal work, daily labor, small businesses, remittances, market dependency, debt, and coping strategies. |
| Which services are most critical? | Water, sanitation, health, education, electricity, transport, waste, shelter, protection, markets, and administrative services. |
| Where are the main service gaps? | Areas with poor coverage, unreliable systems, unaffordable access, damaged infrastructure, overcrowding, or discrimination. |
| How do people obtain help and information? | Family ties, neighbors, faith groups, community committees, WhatsApp groups, local NGOs, authorities, or informal brokers. |
| Who is excluded or underserved? | Marginalized groups, displaced people, tenants, squatters, informal workers, undocumented people, women-headed households, older people, and people with disabilities. |
| What tensions or risks shape the area? | Competition over services, land disputes, rent pressure, eviction threats, crime, violence, discrimination, political conflict, or environmental hazards. |
| What local capacities already exist? | Community organizations, skilled workers, local businesses, service providers, civil society, municipal systems, and mutual aid networks. |

## Analysis Principles

**Start from how the city actually works.** Do not assume that official systems describe the real distribution of power, services, and resources.

**Look beyond needs.** A context analysis should explain causes, relationships, constraints, and opportunities, not only list problems.

**Analyze formal and informal systems together.** In many urban settings, informal networks, markets, leaders, landlords, and brokers shape access as much as formal institutions.

**Disaggregate the area.** Different groups may experience the same neighborhood very differently depending on gender, age, disability, legal status, displacement status, tenure, income, or social identity.

**Identify leverage points.** The goal is not only to describe complexity but to find practical entry points for better programming.

## Design Implications

If the framework is applied well, it should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Targeting | Identifies who is most excluded, underserved, exposed, or dependent on fragile systems. |
| Partnerships | Shows which local actors are trusted, influential, capable, or necessary for implementation. |
| Coordination | Reveals where agencies, authorities, service providers, and community structures need to align. |
| Risk mitigation | Identifies power dynamics, protection risks, gatekeeping, exclusion, and conflict-sensitive concerns. |
| Service strengthening | Shows which systems need repair, support, referral, coordination, or advocacy. |
| Community engagement | Clarifies how people organize, communicate, make decisions, and access support. |
| Adaptive management | Provides baseline understanding for monitoring changes in power, access, services, and risks. |

## Risk Alerts

**Needs-only trap:** A list of needs is not a context analysis. The framework must explain systems, actors, power, access, and causes.

**Official-data trap:** Municipal or administrative data may miss informal settlements, undocumented residents, marginalized groups, or informal service arrangements.

**Stakeholder-list trap:** Listing actors is not enough. Teams must analyze relationships, influence, incentives, trust, and gatekeeping power.

**Service-map trap:** Mapping facilities does not show whether people can actually access them, afford them, trust them, or use them safely.

**Community-homogeneity trap:** Do not treat “the community” as one unified group. Urban areas often contain competing interests, inequalities, and exclusion patterns.

**Do-no-harm risk:** Poor analysis of power and resource distribution can reinforce gatekeeping, exclusion, conflict, or exploitation.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Urban Context and Systems Diagnosis Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Area profile | Description of the target area, population groups, spatial characteristics, and key urban features. |
| Governance and power map | Formal and informal actors, authority structures, influence relationships, and decision-making patterns. |
| Stakeholder analysis | Key institutions, service providers, community groups, private actors, and humanitarian or development actors. |
| Livelihoods analysis | Main income sources, market dependencies, employment barriers, coping strategies, and economic vulnerabilities. |
| Service and infrastructure analysis | Availability, quality, reliability, affordability, access barriers, and critical service gaps. |
| Social network analysis | Community support systems, information channels, trust relationships, and social cohesion or tension. |
| Access and exclusion analysis | Groups facing barriers to services, resources, protection, mobility, or participation. |
| Key risks | Protection, governance, economic, service, infrastructure, environmental, and conflict-sensitive risks. |
| Leverage points | Practical entry points for improving access, reducing risk, strengthening systems, or supporting local capacities. |
| Program implications | Recommendations for targeting, partnerships, coordination, activities, risk mitigation, and monitoring. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the team needs to understand the **urban system behind the humanitarian problem**.

A strong area profile should explain **who holds power, how people access services and resources, where exclusion occurs, which systems are failing, and what local capacities can be strengthened**.
