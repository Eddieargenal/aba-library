# Transition and Handover Framework

## Atomic question
When and how should the programme transfer area ownership to local or municipal actors?

## Why this decision matters
Handover is not an end-of-programme event — it is a process that must be designed at programme inception and built into every implementation phase. When handover is treated as a close-out activity, ownership never genuinely transfers: local actors receive responsibilities without resources, communities lose continuity of support, and gains achieved during the programme reverse within months. The Barrio Mio and Katye cases document that programmes which failed to shift from implementer to facilitator during implementation left no sustainable local capacity at exit. The IASC TOR standard explicitly requires area-based coordination platforms to have dissolution and succession criteria written into founding documents — the same logic applies to the programme overall.

## Prerequisites
- From stage 8 (Implementation): documented evidence that receiving actors have been managing at least one programme component under humanitarian supervision
- From stage 9 (M&E): outcome monitoring data sufficient to assess whether programme targets have been met
- Minimum evidence standard: a completed receiving-actor capacity assessment (formal or participatory); community consultation on handover plans; a written residual risk register with named owners

## Decision domains

1. **Handover readiness** — have programme outcomes reached a threshold that makes handover sustainable rather than premature exit? Readiness is not binary: partial handover (of components where targets are met) is preferable to full delay or full exit under pressure.

2. **Receiving actor capacity** — does the designated receiving actor (municipal government, community organisation, civil society body) have the mandate, financial resources, technical skills, and community legitimacy to maintain gains without external support? Mandate without resources, or resources without legitimacy, each independently cause handover failure.

3. **Resource transfer completeness** — are financial resources, institutional knowledge, programme data, physical assets, and legal titles being transferred alongside responsibilities? A handover of responsibilities without resources transfers burden, not ownership.

4. **Residual risk management** — what risks remain unresolved at the point of handover? Every unresolved risk must have a named owner who has accepted responsibility in writing, and a specific plan — not a vague expectation that the receiving actor will manage.

5. **Exit timeline and conditions** — was the exit date set at programme design or only when funding ran out? An exit driven purely by funding cycle pressure, without readiness validation, is an abandonment not a handover. Conditions for early exit (readiness achieved ahead of schedule) and for extension (critical gate not met) must also be defined.

## Decision logic

Use a five-gate readiness model. All five gates must pass before full handover proceeds. Partial handover of components where gates are met is permitted — and preferred over delaying all handover until all gates pass simultaneously.

| Gate | Minimum threshold | If not met |
|---|---|---|
| G1 — Handover readiness | ≥70% of programme outcome targets met with no life-safety regression in the area | Delay handover of unmet components; maintain targeted support for gap areas only |
| G2 — Receiving actor capacity | Receiving actor has actively managed ≥1 programme component for ≥3 continuous months without external management intervention | Extend supervised co-management period; do not hand over additional components until met |
| G3 — Resource transfer completeness | All physical assets legally transferred or ownership process formally initiated; programme data accessible to receiving actor; financial transition plan agreed | Block handover of affected components until transfer is documented |
| G4 — Residual risk management | Every identified residual risk has a named accepting party and a written management plan, co-signed by both humanitarian and receiving actor | Do not exit until every unresolved risk has a named owner — risks declared "resolved" under close-out pressure routinely re-emerge |
| G5 — Exit timeline and conditions | Exit date was established at or before programme mid-point; early-exit and extension conditions are documented | Flag as funding-driven exit; require accelerated readiness review before proceeding |

**Partial handover protocol:** If G1 and G2 are met for specific programme components but not others, hand over those components on schedule and maintain time-limited support for remaining components. Set a defined deadline for the remaining components — open-ended continuation creates dependency, not transition.

**Scale-up trigger (alternative pathway):** If the programme has demonstrated a replicable model and a municipal or national actor is ready to scale it across additional areas, the handover framework doubles as a replication gateway. The pilot-demonstrate-replicate logic (replication-scale-up note) requires that the demonstrating area completes all five gates before the model is presented as ready for scale.

## Red flags / override conditions

- **Receiving actor was not identified at programme design** — this is a major structural gap. A receiver named only at programme end has no ownership of outcomes and limited motivation to sustain them. Require a retroactive stakeholder engagement process; accept that this will extend the transition period.
- **Exit is being driven purely by funding end-date with no readiness assessment** — this is abandonment framed as handover. Require a formal readiness review against the five gates before proceeding. If gates cannot be met, escalate to donor for no-cost extension or graceful close-out documentation.
- **Community groups that participated in the programme have not been consulted on handover plans** — halt until community consultation is complete. Communities are not passive beneficiaries of handover decisions; their buy-in to the receiving actor determines whether gains are sustained.
- **Residual risks are being declared "resolved" to meet close-out requirements** — this is the single most common handover failure mode. Require co-signed residual risk registers with external validation before accepting "resolved" status.

## Failure modes

- **Breaks when handover is designed as a close-out event rather than a continuous process.** Handover must begin at programme design — identifying the receiver, building their capacity throughout implementation, and progressively transferring management. Starting the handover process in the final three months guarantees failure.
- **Breaks when the municipal or community partner is designated as receiving actor but was never integrated into implementation.** A partner who observes programming has no ownership of outcomes. Receiving actors must co-manage at least one programme component during implementation before being handed full responsibility.
- **Breaks when residual risks are declared resolved rather than transferred.** Under close-out budget and timeline pressure, risk registers are routinely marked as resolved without genuine resolution. These risks re-emerge within 6–12 months of exit, often without any actor in place to address them.
- **Breaks when scale-up is confused with handover.** Scaling a model to new areas while exiting the original area requires that the original area completes all five handover gates first. Premature scale-up while the original area is still dependent creates a programme that is wide but shallow in all areas simultaneously.
