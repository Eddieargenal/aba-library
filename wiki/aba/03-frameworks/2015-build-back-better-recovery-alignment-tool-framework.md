# Build Back Better / Recovery Alignment Tool Framework

**Source:** Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015–2030  
**Year:** 2015  
**Lifecycle stage:** 10. Transition, handover, and scaling  
**Framework purpose:** Ensure transition links emergency response to recovery, rehabilitation, reconstruction, disaster risk reduction, and resilience.

## Framework Overview

The **Build Back Better / Recovery Alignment Tool Framework** helps teams check whether emergency and early recovery activities are connected to longer-term recovery, rehabilitation, reconstruction, disaster risk reduction (DRR), and resilience.

It is designed for the transition, handover, and scaling stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), especially when teams are preparing recovery proposals, donor strategies, municipal handover plans, or neighborhood recovery roadmaps.

The framework adapts the Sendai Framework’s emphasis on preparedness, risk-informed recovery, and “Build Back Better” into a practical design tool. It prevents emergency activities from restoring pre-crisis risk conditions and instead uses the recovery phase to reduce existing risk, avoid creating new risk, improve preparedness, strengthen inclusion, and connect reconstruction with longer-term resilience.

## Core Framework Question

**Does the transition plan use recovery, rehabilitation, and reconstruction as an opportunity to reduce disaster risk, strengthen preparedness, and build back better rather than simply restore pre-crisis vulnerability?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Risk understanding | Hazards, exposure, vulnerability, capacity, environmental conditions, and future risk scenarios. | Recovery should be based on multi-hazard risk knowledge. |
| Risk governance | Roles, responsibilities, policies, coordination, public decision-making, and stakeholder participation. | Recovery requires clear governance across sectors and levels. |
| Risk-informed investment | Structural and non-structural measures that reduce risk and strengthen resilience. | Recovery financing should avoid recreating risk. |
| Preparedness for response and recovery | Contingency plans, early warning, evacuation, response roles, recovery procedures, and continuity of services. | Recovery should improve readiness for the next shock. |
| Build Back Better | Safer reconstruction, improved standards, resilient infrastructure, service restoration, and risk-informed land-use decisions. | Reconstruction is a critical opportunity to reduce risk. |
| Inclusion and accessibility | Participation and leadership of women, persons with disabilities, older people, children, displaced people, renters, informal settlers, and marginalized groups. | Recovery must be equitable, accessible, and people-centred. |
| Basic service continuity | Water, sanitation, health, education, transport, waste, energy, communications, and social services. | Recovery must restore and strengthen essential functions. |
| Temporary settlement and displacement | Safety, services, tenure, relocation, return, and integration of displaced people. | Temporary arrangements can become long-term risk if not planned. |
| Learning and standards | Lessons from past events, building standards, land-use guidance, operational protocols, and after-action review. | Recovery should institutionalize safer practice. |
| Donor and municipal alignment | How proposals, budgets, public plans, and donor strategies connect to DRR and resilience priorities. | Financing and planning must reinforce the same risk reduction logic. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a recovery-alignment process:

1. **Define the transition or recovery package**  
   List emergency response outputs, early recovery actions, rehabilitation activities, reconstruction plans, preparedness actions, and handover arrangements.

2. **Identify the pre-crisis risk conditions**  
   Document hazards, unsafe locations, weak infrastructure, service gaps, tenure risks, social vulnerabilities, and governance failures that existed before the disaster.

3. **Check whether activities restore or reduce risk**  
   For each activity, ask whether it returns the area to the same risk condition or improves safety, service continuity, preparedness, and resilience.

4. **Align with the four Sendai priorities**  
   Verify links to risk understanding, risk governance, risk reduction investment, and preparedness/Build Back Better.

5. **Identify recovery governance roles**  
   Clarify municipal, national, community, private-sector, humanitarian, and development actor responsibilities.

6. **Integrate preparedness into recovery**  
   Ensure early warning, evacuation, contingency planning, emergency communications, drills, and area-based support systems are strengthened during recovery.

7. **Apply safer reconstruction and land-use principles**  
   Review whether shelter, infrastructure, public facilities, and services are rebuilt in ways that reduce exposure and vulnerability.

8. **Confirm inclusion and accessibility**  
   Ensure women, persons with disabilities, older people, children, displaced people, informal settlers, renters, and marginalized groups influence recovery decisions.

9. **Define financing and implementation pathway**  
   Link recovery actions to donor strategy, municipal budgets, public works schedules, service-provider plans, and community priorities.

10. **Set monitoring triggers**  
   Track whether recovery is reducing risk, restoring services, strengthening preparedness, and avoiding new vulnerabilities.

## Build Back Better Alignment Matrix

| Sendai priority | ABA recovery alignment question | Practical design implication |
|---|---|---|
| Understanding disaster risk | Does the recovery plan use hazard, exposure, vulnerability, capacity, and environmental information? | Use risk maps, community risk analysis, service data, damage data, and future scenarios before deciding what to rebuild. |
| Strengthening disaster risk governance | Are roles, responsibilities, coordination mechanisms, and accountability arrangements clear? | Define municipal, community, service-provider, humanitarian, and donor responsibilities. |
| Investing in DRR for resilience | Do recovery investments reduce existing risk and avoid creating new risk? | Prioritize safer infrastructure, drainage, access, WASH, housing repair, public facilities, and non-structural risk reduction. |
| Enhancing preparedness and Build Back Better | Does recovery strengthen preparedness, response capacity, and safer reconstruction? | Integrate early warning, evacuation, drills, continuity planning, safer building standards, and recovery procedures. |

## Recovery Alignment Checklist

| Checklist area | Key question |
|---|---|
| Risk evidence | What hazard, exposure, vulnerability, capacity, and environmental data informs the recovery plan? |
| Pre-crisis vulnerability | Which unsafe conditions existed before the disaster and should not be recreated? |
| Recovery objective | Is the objective to restore, improve, transform, or relocate the affected function? |
| Build Back Better measure | What specific measure reduces risk compared with the pre-disaster condition? |
| Governance | Which authority, service provider, community structure, or institution owns the recovery decision? |
| Preparedness | Does the action improve early warning, evacuation, contingency planning, response roles, or service continuity? |
| Reconstruction standards | Are safer design, land-use, structural, accessibility, and environmental standards applied? |
| Basic services | Does the action restore and strengthen water, sanitation, health, education, waste, transport, energy, or communications? |
| Inclusion | Are women, persons with disabilities, older people, children, displaced people, renters, informal settlers, and marginalized groups meaningfully included? |
| Temporary arrangements | Could temporary shelters, service points, or relocation sites create long-term risks? |
| Financing | Which donor, municipal, national, private, or community resource pathway supports implementation and maintenance? |
| Monitoring | What indicators will show that recovery is reducing risk and strengthening resilience? |

## Emergency-to-Recovery Transition Table

| Emergency output | Recovery alignment question | Build Back Better adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| Emergency shelter | Will repair or reconstruction reduce future hazard risk and respect tenure/protection concerns? | Add safer repair guidance, technical support, risk-informed siting, and referral for tenure risks. |
| WASH emergency services | Will temporary water/sanitation arrangements connect to durable service restoration? | Link to municipal/utility systems, maintenance plans, water safety, drainage, and public health surveillance. |
| Debris removal | Does debris clearance reduce access, drainage, health, and safety risks? | Prioritize critical routes, drainage channels, public facilities, recycling/reuse options, and environmental safeguards. |
| Cash or market support | Does support strengthen recovery without recreating unsafe housing, debt, or market exclusion? | Align transfer values with safer recovery costs, market capacity, protection safeguards, and livelihoods recovery. |
| Public facility repair | Are schools, clinics, and community facilities safer and more accessible than before? | Apply safer standards, accessibility, continuity planning, and preparedness roles. |
| Drainage and access works | Do works reduce flood risk and improve emergency movement? | Link to municipal maintenance schedules, public works plans, and future land-use controls. |
| Community preparedness | Will local systems remain active after the project? | Institutionalize drills, early warning, emergency contacts, evacuation maps, and volunteer support. |
| Coordination mechanisms | Will recovery coordination continue beyond humanitarian delivery? | Link area coordination to municipal recovery, DRR, and public investment structures. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Recovery proposals | Ensures donor strategies explicitly link emergency response with recovery, reconstruction, DRR, and resilience. |
| Technical design | Prevents rebuilding unsafe infrastructure, housing, services, or public facilities. |
| Municipal planning | Connects area-level recovery to public works, land-use, DRR, and service restoration plans. |
| Preparedness design | Uses recovery to strengthen early warning, evacuation, contingency plans, and response capacity. |
| Inclusion | Ensures recovery decisions are accessible and shaped by people most at risk. |
| Investment prioritization | Directs resources toward measures that reduce risk and strengthen essential systems. |
| Handover | Clarifies who will own, maintain, finance, and monitor recovery outputs. |
| Monitoring | Tracks whether the area is safer and more resilient, not only whether outputs were completed. |

## Risk Alerts

**Restoration-of-risk risk:** Recovery can rebuild the same vulnerability that caused the disaster impact.

**Build Back Better slogan risk:** “Build Back Better” must be translated into concrete standards, decisions, budgets, and monitoring indicators.

**Preparedness disconnect risk:** Recovery may ignore early warning, evacuation, response roles, and contingency planning.

**Infrastructure-only risk:** Safer recovery also requires governance, inclusion, services, livelihoods, and accountability.

**Temporary-to-permanent risk:** Temporary shelters, service points, or relocation arrangements can create long-term risk if not planned.

**Exclusion risk:** Recovery may leave out renters, informal settlers, people without documents, persons with disabilities, women, older people, and marginalized groups.

**Budget realism risk:** Safer recovery requires financing for both construction and long-term maintenance.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Build Back Better / Recovery Alignment Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | Neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone covered by recovery planning. |
| Recovery package | Emergency, recovery, rehabilitation, reconstruction, preparedness, and handover actions. |
| Risk profile | Hazards, exposure, vulnerability, capacity, environmental risks, and pre-crisis risk conditions. |
| Sendai alignment | How the package supports risk understanding, governance, investment, preparedness, and Build Back Better. |
| Build Back Better measures | Specific design, policy, infrastructure, service, preparedness, or governance improvements. |
| Governance roles | Authorities, service providers, communities, humanitarian actors, development actors, and donors. |
| Inclusion measures | How at-risk groups participate and benefit safely. |
| Financing and maintenance | Budget, public investment, donor, service-provider, or community resource pathway. |
| Unresolved risks | Technical, protection, tenure, environmental, financial, or governance risks. |
| Monitoring indicators | Measures showing safer recovery, service restoration, preparedness, and resilience. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when emergency response outputs are transitioning into recovery, rehabilitation, reconstruction, donor proposals, municipal handover, or longer-term resilience planning.

A strong recovery alignment plan should show **how the response reduces existing risk, avoids creating new risk, strengthens preparedness, improves basic services, applies safer reconstruction and land-use decisions, includes people most at risk, and connects area-level recovery to municipal and donor systems**.
