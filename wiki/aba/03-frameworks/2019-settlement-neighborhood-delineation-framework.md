# Settlement / Neighborhood Delineation Framework

**Source:** Global Shelter Cluster / Urban Settlements Working Group, *Area-Based Approaches in Urban Settings: Compendium of Case Studies*  
**Year:** 2019  
**Lifecycle stage:** 1. Area selection and boundary definition  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to define, justify, and validate the geographic boundaries of an Area-Based Approach (ABA).

## Framework Overview

The **Settlement / Neighborhood Delineation Framework** helps teams decide what the “area” is in an Area-Based Approach. It moves beyond a simple mapping exercise and provides a structured way to define a target geography that is operationally manageable, locally meaningful, and analytically useful.

The framework recognizes that urban areas rarely fit neatly into one type of boundary. A neighborhood or settlement may be shaped by formal administrative lines, informal social identities, service networks, hazards, infrastructure, markets, displacement patterns, and agency capacity. Good area selection therefore requires comparing several boundary logics before deciding where the intervention should focus.

## Core Framework Question

**What is the most appropriate geographic unit for understanding needs, coordinating actors, engaging communities, and designing an integrated response?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Administrative geography | Municipal, district, ward, cadastral, or official planning boundaries. | Supports alignment with government systems, official data, and coordination structures. |
| Social geography | Locally recognized neighborhoods, settlements, identity areas, community networks, or informal leadership zones. | Ensures the area reflects how people understand, navigate, and organize their own environment. |
| Service geography | Water, sanitation, electricity, schools, health facilities, markets, roads, transport, and waste systems. | Reveals how people actually access services and where shared service gaps exist. |
| Risk geography | Flood, landslide, fire, eviction, conflict, contamination, disease, protection, or infrastructure risks. | Helps ensure the area captures common exposure, vulnerability, and risk patterns. |
| Infrastructure geography | Roads, rivers, drainage channels, slopes, walls, railways, utility networks, and physical barriers. | Identifies practical boundaries, access constraints, and system dependencies. |
| Operational geography | The area that agencies can realistically assess, coordinate, implement, monitor, and support. | Keeps the response feasible and accountable within available resources and time. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a layered analysis process:

1. **Identify candidate areas**  
   Start with possible neighborhoods, settlements, districts, service catchments, or crisis-affected zones.

2. **Map boundary dimensions**  
   Examine the area through administrative, social, service, risk, infrastructure, and operational lenses.

3. **Compare boundary tensions**  
   Identify where boundaries overlap and where they conflict. For example, a government ward may not match a community-recognized neighborhood, or a flood-risk zone may cut across several administrative areas.

4. **Define the working area**  
   Select the most useful boundary for the response purpose. The boundary should be clear enough for planning but flexible enough to reflect urban complexity.

5. **Validate with local actors**  
   Test the proposed area with residents, local authorities, service providers, civil society, market actors, and operational partners.

6. **Document the rationale**  
   Explain why this area was selected, what is included, what is excluded, and what trade-offs were accepted.

7. **Review and adjust**  
   Revisit the boundary as new information emerges during assessment, implementation, monitoring, or coordination.

## Key Decision Criteria

A strong ABA boundary should meet five criteria:

| Criterion | Guiding question |
|---|---|
| Relevance | Does this area capture the main needs, risks, capacities, and affected populations? |
| Coherence | Does the area function as a recognizable or connected urban unit? |
| Feasibility | Can teams realistically work in this area with available resources and time? |
| Coordination value | Does this boundary help actors reduce duplication, align activities, and engage local systems? |
| Equity | Does the boundary avoid excluding nearby populations with similar or greater needs without justification? |

## Boundary Selection Principles

**Use multiple forms of evidence.** Combine maps, field visits, community input, service data, hazard information, infrastructure analysis, and local authority knowledge.

**Prioritize function over neatness.** The best boundary is not always the cleanest map boundary. It is the geography that best explains how people live, move, access services, experience risk, and connect to institutions.

**Make trade-offs explicit.** Every boundary includes some people, risks, and systems while excluding others. The framework requires teams to document these decisions clearly.

**Keep the area manageable.** The selected area should be large enough to capture relevant systems but small enough to allow meaningful assessment, coordination, community engagement, and implementation.

**Treat boundaries as working hypotheses.** Initial boundaries should be validated and adjusted as the team learns more.

## Risk Alerts

**Administrative boundary trap:** Official boundaries may be useful for coordination but may not reflect social identity, service access, or risk exposure.

**Convenience-area trap:** Selecting an area only because an agency already works there can bias targeting and miss higher-priority needs.

**Over-expansion risk:** Defining the area too broadly can make the ABA too complex, expensive, and difficult to coordinate.

**Over-narrowing risk:** Defining the area too tightly can miss upstream infrastructure, market systems, displacement flows, or hazards that shape conditions inside the target area.

**Equity risk:** Focusing on one area without explaining why nearby areas are excluded can create perceived unfairness and undermine acceptance.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Area Boundary Rationale** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Selected area name | The working name of the settlement, neighborhood, district, or target area. |
| Boundary description | Clear explanation of the geographic limits. |
| Boundary logic | The main reasons the area was selected. |
| Included systems | Key services, infrastructure, risks, institutions, markets, and communities included in the area. |
| Excluded areas | Nearby areas or populations not included, with justification. |
| Validation sources | Community members, authorities, service providers, maps, datasets, or partner inputs used to validate the boundary. |
| Review trigger | Conditions that would require the boundary to be updated. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when an Area-Based Approach requires a defensible geographic focus.

A good area boundary should answer:

1. **Why this area?**
2. **Why these boundaries?**
3. **What systems and stakeholders shape conditions inside this area?**
4. **Who or what is excluded, and why?**
5. **Can the area be assessed, coordinated, implemented, and monitored in practice?**
