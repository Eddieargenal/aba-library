# Participatory Assessment and Planning Tool Framework

**Source:** Sanderson & Sitko, *Urban Area-Based Approaches in Post-Disaster Contexts*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 3. Community and stakeholder engagement platform  
**Framework purpose:** Create a process where residents, local authorities, service providers, and humanitarian actors jointly identify risks, needs, and priorities.

## Framework Overview

The **Participatory Assessment and Planning Tool Framework** helps teams build an area-based engagement platform that brings residents, community groups, local authorities, service providers, private actors, civil society, and humanitarian agencies into the same analysis and planning process.

It is designed for the community and stakeholder engagement stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), when the team needs to move from separate sector assessments toward a shared understanding of the area as a functioning place.

The framework translates urban ABA principles into a practical process for joint assessment, shared prioritization, area planning, implementation review, and monitoring. It is especially useful where needs are complex, services are interdependent, and humanitarian action must work through existing local systems rather than creating parallel structures.

## Core Framework Question

**How can residents, local authorities, service providers, and humanitarian actors jointly identify area-level risks, needs, capacities, and priorities, and convert them into a coordinated area plan?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Area definition | The neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone used as the shared planning unit. | A clear area boundary allows actors to assess the same place and coordinate priorities. |
| Stakeholder mapping | Residents, local leaders, authorities, service providers, civil society, private actors, and humanitarian agencies. | Urban response depends on many actors with overlapping responsibilities and influence. |
| Participation and representation | Who participates, who is excluded, and how different groups influence priorities. | Area plans can reinforce exclusion if only powerful or visible groups are consulted. |
| Multi-sector analysis | Shelter, WASH, health, education, protection, livelihoods, markets, roads, drainage, waste, and public services. | Urban problems are interconnected and rarely fit within a single sector. |
| Local systems and capacities | Existing services, community networks, municipal systems, market actors, and informal coping mechanisms. | ABA should strengthen and coordinate with what already exists. |
| Risk and vulnerability analysis | Hazards, unsafe services, exposure, tenure, social tension, protection risks, and institutional weaknesses. | Joint planning should prioritize risks, not only visible needs. |
| Shared prioritization | How actors agree on urgent, feasible, and high-impact area priorities. | Prioritization reduces fragmented delivery and competition between sectors. |
| Accountability and feedback | How residents receive information, raise concerns, and influence revisions. | Engagement must continue through implementation and monitoring, not stop after assessment. |
| Adaptation and learning | How the plan is reviewed and revised as area conditions change. | Urban contexts change quickly; plans must remain adaptive. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a participatory area-planning cycle:

1. **Define the area and purpose**  
   Agree on the geographic area, planning objective, and main decision the process must support.

2. **Map stakeholders and power relations**  
   Identify residents, groups, authorities, service providers, market actors, local institutions, civil society, landlords, private actors, and humanitarian agencies. Note who has influence, who controls resources, and who may be excluded.

3. **Create an engagement structure**  
   Establish a practical engagement platform, such as an area working group, community planning forum, municipal coordination meeting, or mixed stakeholder committee.

4. **Collect multi-sector evidence**  
   Combine assessment data, service mapping, risk mapping, community discussions, direct observation, secondary data, and provider interviews.

5. **Validate findings with residents and service actors**  
   Test whether the assessment reflects lived experience, service realities, and the priorities of groups at greatest risk.

6. **Prioritize jointly**  
   Use transparent criteria such as severity, scale, urgency, risk reduction value, feasibility, inclusion, system dependency, and local ownership.

7. **Convert priorities into an area plan**  
   Define actions, responsible actors, sequencing, referrals, coordination needs, risk mitigation, and monitoring arrangements.

8. **Communicate decisions**  
   Share what was prioritized, what was not, why, and how residents can provide feedback.

9. **Review and adapt**  
   Repeat engagement during implementation and monitoring to update priorities and correct course.

## Participatory Assessment and Planning Checklist

| Planning area | Key questions |
|---|---|
| Area definition | What is the target area, and why is it the right planning unit? |
| Stakeholders | Who lives, works, governs, provides services, owns assets, or influences decisions in the area? |
| Representation | Which groups are usually absent from public meetings or decision-making? |
| Evidence | What data exists, what is missing, and what must be validated with residents? |
| Multi-sector needs | Which needs cut across shelter, WASH, health, education, livelihoods, protection, markets, and infrastructure? |
| Local capacity | What are residents, local authorities, service providers, civil society, and market actors already doing? |
| Risk | Which hazards, service failures, protection issues, tenure risks, or social tensions could worsen harm? |
| Prioritization | What criteria will be used to decide what comes first? |
| Coordination | Which actors must align their plans, budgets, referrals, or implementation schedules? |
| Accountability | How will residents receive information, challenge decisions, and influence adaptation? |

## Joint Prioritization Table

| Criterion | Question for the group |
|---|---|
| Severity | Which problems create the greatest harm or risk? |
| Scale | How many people or which groups are affected? |
| Urgency | What must be addressed first to prevent deterioration? |
| Inclusion | Which groups face the greatest barriers or exclusion? |
| System dependency | Which issue blocks other interventions or services? |
| Feasibility | What can realistically be addressed with available actors, permissions, time, and resources? |
| Local ownership | Which priorities can be maintained by local actors after project support? |
| Risk reduction | Which actions reduce future risk, not only immediate need? |
| Do-no-harm | Could the proposed action worsen tension, exclusion, market distortion, or dependency? |

## Engagement Platform Design

| Platform element | Design requirement |
|---|---|
| Membership | Include residents, local authorities, service providers, civil society, community groups, private actors, and humanitarian agencies where relevant. |
| Representation safeguards | Ensure women, youth, older people, people with disabilities, displaced people, renters, informal settlers, and marginalized groups have safe ways to participate. |
| Meeting purpose | Use meetings for decisions, validation, problem-solving, and follow-up, not only information sharing. |
| Evidence package | Bring maps, service data, risk findings, community feedback, and sector assessments to the same table. |
| Decision record | Document what was agreed, what was rejected, who is responsible, and what remains unresolved. |
| Feedback loop | Communicate decisions back to residents and provide channels for correction. |
| Adaptation schedule | Set dates or triggers for reassessment and plan revision. |

## Design Implications

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Area selection | Defines why the selected geography is useful for joint planning. |
| Stakeholder engagement | Identifies who must be involved and who may be excluded. |
| Needs analysis | Integrates household, community, service-system, governance, and infrastructure perspectives. |
| Prioritization | Creates a transparent method for agreeing what comes first. |
| Coordination | Reduces fragmented sector delivery by aligning actors around one area plan. |
| Sequencing | Helps identify dependencies across sectors and services. |
| Accountability | Makes residents part of assessment, decision-making, review, and adaptation. |
| Adaptation | Keeps the area plan responsive to changing conditions. |

## Risk Alerts

**Token participation risk:** Meetings may appear participatory while real decisions remain controlled by agencies or authorities.

**Elite capture risk:** Local leaders or visible groups may dominate the process and hide the needs of renters, informal settlers, displaced people, women, youth, people with disabilities, or minorities.

**Consultation fatigue risk:** Repeated discussions without visible decisions or feedback can reduce trust.

**Sector fragmentation risk:** Separate sector consultations can produce conflicting priorities if not integrated into one area process.

**Data-over-dialogue risk:** Technical data should inform, not replace, resident knowledge and lived experience.

**Unclear mandate risk:** Participatory platforms fail if no one has authority to act on decisions.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a **Participatory Area Assessment and Planning Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | Boundary and rationale for the selected area. |
| Stakeholder map | Key residents, groups, authorities, providers, private actors, and humanitarian agencies. |
| Participation approach | How different groups were engaged and how exclusion was mitigated. |
| Shared findings | Main risks, needs, capacities, service gaps, and system issues. |
| Prioritized issues | Agreed priorities with rationale and affected groups. |
| Area actions | Proposed activities, referrals, coordination steps, and enabling works. |
| Roles and responsibilities | Who will act, support, coordinate, decide, maintain, or monitor. |
| Accountability actions | How findings and decisions will be communicated and challenged. |
| Adaptation plan | When and how the plan will be reviewed. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when an area plan needs legitimacy, coordination, local ownership, and cross-sector prioritization.

A strong participatory planning process should show **who was involved, who may still be missing, what evidence was validated, which priorities were agreed, who is responsible, and how residents will continue to influence implementation and adaptation**.
