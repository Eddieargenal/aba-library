# Urban Resilience Action Design Framework

**Source:** IFRC, *Building Urban Resilience: A Guide for Red Cross and Red Crescent Engagement and Contribution*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 7. Detailed technical design  
**Framework purpose:** Design DRR actions involving volunteers, local branches, community preparedness, public awareness, and local institutions.

## Framework Overview

The **Urban Resilience Action Design Framework** helps teams translate urban risk analysis into practical disaster risk reduction (DRR) and resilience actions that can be implemented with local branches, volunteers, communities, local authorities, and partner institutions.

It is designed for the detailed technical design stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), especially where local NGOs, National Societies, community-based organizations, municipal actors, and humanitarian partners need to design actions that strengthen preparedness, risk awareness, local response capacity, and community resilience in dense and complex urban environments.

The framework is grounded in the IFRC emphasis on volunteer-based action, community engagement, local institutions, preparedness, disaster management, and urban risk reduction.

## Core Framework Question

**What practical actions should local actors, volunteers, communities, service providers, and institutions take to reduce urban risk, strengthen preparedness, and build resilience in the target area?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Urban risk profile | Hazards, exposure, vulnerability, urban growth, informal settlements, infrastructure risks, and service gaps. | Urban resilience actions must respond to the specific risks of the area. |
| Local branch and volunteer capacity | Presence, skills, networks, safety, equipment, supervision, and surge capacity. | Volunteers and local branches are often the first and most trusted operational platform. |
| Community preparedness | Household preparedness, contingency plans, early warning, evacuation, drills, and local response roles. | Preparedness reduces harm before external actors arrive. |
| Public awareness and risk communication | Messages, channels, trusted messengers, schools, media, digital tools, and behavioral change. | Urban risk communication must reach diverse and mobile populations. |
| Local institutions and partnerships | Municipal authorities, schools, health facilities, service providers, civil society, private sector, and community structures. | Urban resilience requires coordination with existing institutions and systems. |
| Service and infrastructure resilience | WASH, drainage, waste, roads, electricity, health, education, markets, and public spaces. | Community safety depends on systems, not only households. |
| Social inclusion and protection | Women, children, older people, people with disabilities, migrants, renters, informal settlers, and marginalized groups. | Urban resilience actions must work for those most exposed and least able to access services. |
| Preparedness for response and recovery | Response roles, emergency supplies, referral pathways, recovery planning, and build-back-safer practices. | Resilience links preparedness, response, and recovery. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses an urban resilience action-design process:

1. **Define the target area and priority risks**  
   Identify the neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone and the main hazards, service failures, and vulnerabilities.

2. **Map local actors and institutional entry points**  
   Identify local branches, volunteers, municipal offices, community groups, schools, health facilities, service providers, private actors, and civil society organizations.

3. **Assess volunteer and partner capacity**  
   Review skills, coverage, safety, supervision, equipment, communication channels, and ability to engage different groups.

4. **Identify community preparedness gaps**  
   Examine warning, evacuation, household readiness, contingency plans, drills, local response, and referral pathways.

5. **Design risk awareness and behavior-change actions**  
   Define messages, channels, audiences, trusted messengers, and feedback mechanisms.

6. **Design practical DRR actions**  
   Convert risks into activities such as risk mapping, preparedness planning, public awareness, volunteer training, school safety, first aid, drainage cleaning, fire prevention, or safe shelter guidance.

7. **Link actions to local systems**  
   Ensure activities are connected to municipal plans, service providers, schools, health systems, emergency services, and community structures.

8. **Define roles, resources, and safety measures**  
   Clarify who will implement, supervise, finance, monitor, refer, maintain, and adapt each action.

9. **Test, review, and adapt**  
   Use drills, simulations, feedback, and after-action reviews to improve the design.

## Urban Resilience Action Design Table

| Action area | Possible activities | Key design checks |
|---|---|---|
| Volunteer mobilization | Recruit, train, equip, supervise, and support volunteers for preparedness and risk reduction. | Are volunteers safe, representative, trained, resourced, and linked to formal systems? |
| Community risk mapping | Map hazards, exposed households, service gaps, safe routes, assets, and local capacities. | Are residents and at-risk groups involved in producing and validating the map? |
| Public awareness | Campaigns on fire, flood, heat, disease prevention, waste, safe shelter, evacuation, and preparedness. | Are messages clear, locally relevant, multilingual if needed, and delivered through trusted channels? |
| Household preparedness | Household plans, emergency contacts, document protection, supplies, warning actions, and evacuation planning. | Are measures realistic for low-income households and renters? |
| Early warning | Alert chains, trusted messengers, action triggers, communication protocols, and warning tests. | Do warnings reach people with disabilities, isolated households, migrants, and informal settlers? |
| Evacuation and safe access | Route mapping, safe-site identification, drills, lighting, transport support, and disability inclusion. | Are routes safe, accessible, known, and tested? |
| Local response capacity | First aid, light search and rescue, fire safety, flood response, referral pathways, and emergency communication. | Are local responders linked to municipal and formal response systems? |
| School and youth engagement | School safety, youth volunteerism, risk education, drills, and peer communication. | Are children and youth protected and meaningfully engaged? |
| Infrastructure risk reduction | Drainage clearing, waste management, fire prevention, access improvements, and small mitigation works. | Is there an owner, maintenance plan, and coordination with public works or utilities? |
| Health and WASH preparedness | Hygiene promotion, disease prevention, safe water practices, public health alerts, and referral links. | Are activities linked to health authorities and WASH providers? |
| Social cohesion and inclusion | Mutual aid, inclusive committees, feedback mechanisms, conflict-sensitive engagement, and support networks. | Are tensions, discrimination, and exclusion risks being actively managed? |

## Local Actor Role Matrix

| Actor | Potential role |
|---|---|
| Local branch or local NGO | Coordinate volunteers, organize preparedness activities, support community engagement, and link with authorities. |
| Volunteers | Conduct outreach, risk mapping, preparedness messaging, warning support, first aid, drills, and feedback collection. |
| Community groups | Identify risks, validate priorities, support vulnerable households, maintain local preparedness systems. |
| Municipal authorities | Provide mandate, technical guidance, permits, public works coordination, emergency management links, and sustainability. |
| Schools | Support risk education, preparedness drills, safe access, and communication with children and caregivers. |
| Health facilities | Link public health risks, disease prevention, referrals, and emergency care. |
| Service providers | Address WASH, waste, electricity, transport, drainage, and infrastructure dependencies. |
| Private sector | Support supplies, logistics, market continuity, risk communication, and service continuity. |
| Humanitarian actors | Provide technical support, financing, coordination, monitoring, and surge capacity. |

## Preparedness and Public Awareness Design Checklist

| Design area | Key questions |
|---|---|
| Audience | Who must change behavior or take preparedness action? |
| Message | What exact action should people take before, during, or after a hazard? |
| Messenger | Who is trusted by the target group? |
| Channel | What channel reaches the group reliably: volunteers, radio, WhatsApp, schools, workplaces, faith groups, posters, door-to-door visits? |
| Timing | When should the message be delivered relative to hazard seasons or risk triggers? |
| Inclusion | How will people with disabilities, low literacy, language barriers, renters, migrants, or isolated households be reached? |
| Feedback | How will residents ask questions, report barriers, or correct misinformation? |
| Verification | How will the team know whether people understood and acted on the message? |

## Design Implications

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Volunteer strategy | Defines the training, safety, supervision, and roles needed for urban resilience work. |
| DRR activity package | Converts risk analysis into practical preparedness, awareness, response, and mitigation actions. |
| Partnership model | Clarifies how local branches, community groups, institutions, and service providers work together. |
| Public awareness | Designs messages and channels that reach diverse urban populations. |
| Preparedness planning | Links household, community, and municipal preparedness systems. |
| Infrastructure risk reduction | Connects small mitigation works to public systems and maintenance responsibilities. |
| Inclusion | Ensures resilience actions reach groups most exposed or least served. |
| Monitoring | Identifies observable outputs and capacity improvements from DRR activities. |

## Risk Alerts

**Volunteer overload risk:** Volunteers should not be assigned responsibilities beyond their training, safety, time, or resources.

**Awareness-only risk:** Public messaging does not build resilience unless people have realistic actions, resources, and support systems.

**Parallel-system risk:** Community preparedness structures can fail if they are disconnected from municipal, health, school, and emergency systems.

**Urban complexity risk:** One neighborhood may contain different tenure groups, social networks, service arrangements, and risk profiles. Avoid one-size-fits-all programming.

**Exclusion risk:** Preparedness messages and drills may miss renters, migrants, informal settlers, people with disabilities, older people, and people with limited literacy.

**Maintenance risk:** Small infrastructure or preparedness assets may fail if ownership and maintenance are not agreed.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce an **Urban Resilience Action Design Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | Neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone where actions will be implemented. |
| Priority risks | Main hazards, service failures, exposure, vulnerabilities, and risk scenarios. |
| Local actors | Local branches, volunteers, community groups, municipal actors, service providers, schools, health facilities, and partners. |
| Action package | Preparedness, awareness, volunteer, response, infrastructure, inclusion, and coordination activities. |
| Roles and responsibilities | Who implements, supports, supervises, coordinates, refers, and maintains each action. |
| Volunteer support plan | Training, equipment, supervision, safety, communication, and referral arrangements. |
| Inclusion measures | How at-risk groups will be reached and involved. |
| Linkages to formal systems | Municipal DRR, emergency response, health, WASH, schools, service providers, and public works connections. |
| Monitoring indicators | Observable changes in preparedness, awareness, local response, inclusion, and service coordination. |
| Review and adaptation | Drills, feedback, after-action review, and update schedule. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the detailed design requires community-based and volunteer-supported urban DRR action.

A strong urban resilience action design should explain **which risks will be reduced, which local actors will act, what volunteers and institutions will do, how residents will be reached, how actions connect to formal systems, and how preparedness will be tested and sustained**.
