# Replication / Scale-Up Learning Note Framework

**Source:** Global Shelter Cluster, *Area-Based Approaches in Urban Settings: Compendium of Case Studies*; ALNAP GPR 12 urban ABA guidance  
**Year:** 2019  
**Lifecycle stage:** 10. Transition, handover, and scaling  
**Framework purpose:** Document what worked, what failed, what conditions mattered, and what should be adapted before applying an area-based model to another neighborhood.

## Framework Overview

The **Replication / Scale-Up Learning Note Framework** helps teams decide whether an area-based intervention should be repeated, expanded, adapted, institutionalized, or stopped.

It is designed for the transition, handover, and scaling stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), when a team needs to learn from one neighborhood, settlement, or district before applying the model elsewhere.

The framework treats replication as a learning decision, not a copy-paste exercise. It requires teams to document the conditions that made the model work, the assumptions that failed, the minimum data required, the staffing and coordination burden, the cost and timeline, the stakeholder conditions, and the warnings that should prevent replication in unsuitable contexts.

## Core Framework Question

**What exactly should be replicated, what must be adapted, what conditions must be present, and when should this area-based model not be used in another neighborhood?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Model description | The intervention package, process, target group, geography, delivery approach, and coordination model. | Teams must know what the “model” actually is before scaling it. |
| Results and evidence | Outputs, outcomes, user feedback, service improvements, risk reduction, coordination gains, and unintended effects. | Replication should be based on evidence, not enthusiasm. |
| Context conditions | Hazards, settlement type, governance, markets, tenure, infrastructure, services, social cohesion, and security. | A model that works in one area may fail under different conditions. |
| Cost and resources | Budget, unit costs, staff time, partner inputs, municipal contributions, logistics, and recurrent costs. | Scale-up decisions require realistic resource assumptions. |
| Staffing and skills | Team composition, technical expertise, community facilitation, coordination capacity, and management load. | Replication fails when staffing requirements are underestimated. |
| Timeline | Assessment, design, approvals, implementation, coordination, handover, and monitoring time. | Area-based work often requires longer timeframes than sector delivery. |
| Data requirements | Minimum assessment, mapping, risk, service, stakeholder, market, protection, and infrastructure data needed. | Poor data leads to bad targeting and weak adaptation. |
| Stakeholder conditions | Community buy-in, municipal support, service-provider cooperation, partner alignment, and private-sector roles. | ABA success depends on collaboration beyond one agency. |
| Risks and safeguards | Protection, exclusion, land tenure, conflict, aid capture, environmental harm, market distortion, and governance risks. | Scale-up can reproduce harm if risks are not made explicit. |
| Adaptation requirements | What should be modified for different hazards, settlement types, governance systems, or service conditions. | Replication should preserve logic, not copy activities mechanically. |
| Do-not-replicate warnings | Conditions under which the model should not be used. | Prevents inappropriate expansion. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a structured learning-to-scale process:

1. **Define the model being reviewed**  
   Describe the intervention package, target area, actors, activities, decision process, and intended outcomes.

2. **Separate core logic from local adaptations**  
   Identify what was essential to the model and what was specific to the pilot neighborhood.

3. **Document results and evidence quality**  
   Record what improved, what did not improve, who benefited, who was missed, and what evidence supports the conclusion.

4. **Analyze enabling conditions**  
   Identify the governance, market, community, infrastructure, risk, access, and partner conditions that made implementation possible.

5. **Analyze failure points and constraints**  
   Document delays, coordination failures, weak assumptions, quality problems, unmet needs, community concerns, and unintended consequences.

6. **Calculate resource requirements**  
   Summarize cost, staffing, time, logistics, technical expertise, coordination intensity, and recurrent requirements.

7. **Define minimum data requirements**  
   Specify what teams must know before deciding to replicate in another area.

8. **Create adaptation guidance**  
   Explain how to modify the model for different settlement types, hazards, population groups, service systems, and governance arrangements.

9. **Write do-not-replicate warnings**  
   Identify conditions where the model would be unsafe, inefficient, inequitable, or unsustainable.

10. **Recommend scale-up pathway**  
   Choose whether to replicate, adapt, institutionalize, pause, or stop.

## Replication Learning Table

| Learning area | Questions to answer |
|---|---|
| Model summary | What was the model, where was it implemented, and what problem did it address? |
| Core components | Which activities or processes were essential to the result? |
| Local adaptations | Which elements were specific to the neighborhood and should not be copied automatically? |
| Evidence of success | What changed for households, communities, services, infrastructure, coordination, or risk reduction? |
| Evidence limitations | What data gaps, attribution problems, or weak monitoring findings remain? |
| What worked | Which actions, partnerships, tools, or sequencing decisions were effective? |
| What failed | Which assumptions, activities, coordination mechanisms, or technical choices did not work? |
| Conditions that mattered | What social, institutional, market, hazard, infrastructure, or political factors shaped results? |
| Cost | What was the total cost and approximate cost by output, household, area, or service improvement? |
| Staffing | What roles, skills, and level of effort were required? |
| Timeline | How long did each stage take from assessment to handover? |
| Minimum data | What information must be collected before applying the model elsewhere? |
| Risks | What protection, tenure, market, environmental, financial, or governance risks emerged? |
| Adaptation needs | What must change in a different neighborhood or city? |
| Do-not-replicate warning | Under what conditions should this model not be used? |

## Scale-Up Decision Matrix

| Decision | When appropriate | Required next step |
|---|---|---|
| Replicate with minor adaptation | The context is similar, evidence is strong, costs are realistic, and local actors can support implementation. | Prepare a replication plan and confirm local conditions. |
| Adapt before replication | The model logic is useful, but hazards, governance, markets, infrastructure, or population dynamics differ. | Redesign the model for the new area using minimum data requirements. |
| Institutionalize | Municipal or service-provider systems can absorb the model into regular planning, budgeting, or service delivery. | Prepare policy, budget, staffing, and systems integration plan. |
| Pilot again | Evidence is promising but incomplete, or major assumptions still need testing. | Run a smaller structured pilot with stronger monitoring. |
| Do not replicate | Costs are too high, risks are unacceptable, conditions are absent, or results are weak. | Document reasons and identify alternative approaches. |

## Minimum Data Requirements

| Data area | Minimum information needed |
|---|---|
| Geography | Boundaries, settlement type, access routes, density, public spaces, and physical constraints. |
| Hazards and risks | Main hazards, exposure, vulnerable groups, seasonal risks, and risk hotspots. |
| Population and vulnerability | Household types, displacement status, tenure, renters, informal settlers, people with disabilities, older people, children, and marginalized groups. |
| Infrastructure and services | Water, sanitation, drainage, waste, roads, electricity, schools, health, markets, and emergency access. |
| Governance | Municipal actors, local leaders, committees, service providers, informal power structures, and coordination platforms. |
| Markets and livelihoods | Availability of materials, labor, transport, financial services, vendors, and livelihood constraints. |
| Protection and tenure | Eviction risks, GBV risks, aid capture, discrimination, documentation, land and property concerns. |
| Stakeholders | NGOs, CBOs, private sector, faith groups, utilities, community groups, and government departments. |
| Costs and logistics | Unit costs, procurement routes, staff needs, transport constraints, and implementation timeline. |
| Community priorities | What residents identify as most urgent, feasible, acceptable, and risky. |

## Do-Not-Replicate Warning Table

| Warning condition | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| No credible local owner | The model will likely collapse after external support ends. |
| High unresolved protection or tenure risk | Replication may expose residents to eviction, exclusion, violence, or exploitation. |
| Costs exceed realistic financing | The model may create inequity or become impossible to sustain. |
| Required technical capacity is unavailable | Quality, safety, or maintenance may fail. |
| Municipal or service-provider opposition | Outputs may not be approved, maintained, or connected to systems. |
| Community trust is low | Participation, targeting, feedback, and maintenance may be undermined. |
| Data is too weak | The model may target the wrong area or miss critical risks. |
| Hazards differ significantly | Activities may not reduce the main risks in the new area. |
| Market conditions differ | Material, labor, cash, rental, or service assumptions may fail. |
| Timeframe is too short | Area-based coordination and handover may be unrealistic. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Scale-up planning | Identifies whether replication is justified and under what conditions. |
| Program adaptation | Distinguishes transferable logic from context-specific activities. |
| Budgeting | Provides realistic cost and staffing assumptions. |
| Risk management | Makes scale-up risks and do-not-replicate conditions explicit. |
| Municipal engagement | Clarifies whether local systems can absorb or support the model. |
| Learning | Converts project experience into usable institutional knowledge. |
| Donor strategy | Supports evidence-based proposals for expansion or adaptation. |
| Knowledge management | Creates a reusable note for future neighborhoods, cities, or crises. |

## Risk Alerts

**Copy-paste risk:** Replicating activities without adapting to context can reproduce failure.

**Success-story bias:** Teams may document what worked and ignore what failed.

**Hidden-cost risk:** Coordination, staffing, community engagement, and handover costs are often underestimated.

**Context-blind scale-up risk:** Hazards, tenure, markets, governance, and service systems may differ sharply between neighborhoods.

**Data-minimum risk:** Replication without enough area data can create poor targeting and weak design.

**Equity risk:** Scaling one area model can create perceived unfairness if adjacent areas with similar needs are excluded.

**Institutional-memory risk:** Lessons may be lost if not documented before staff turnover or project closure.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Replication / Scale-Up Learning Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Model summary | What was implemented, where, for whom, and why. |
| Core model logic | Essential components that explain how the model works. |
| Evidence of results | What changed and what evidence supports the finding. |
| What worked | Effective practices, partnerships, sequencing, tools, or design choices. |
| What failed | Activities, assumptions, coordination arrangements, or technical choices that did not work. |
| Conditions that mattered | Governance, market, infrastructure, hazard, social, and institutional conditions affecting success. |
| Cost | Budget, unit cost, recurrent cost, and hidden coordination or staffing costs. |
| Staffing | Roles, skills, level of effort, partner inputs, and management requirements. |
| Timeline | Time required for assessment, design, approvals, implementation, handover, and monitoring. |
| Minimum data requirements | Data that must be collected before replication. |
| Adaptation guidance | What should change in another neighborhood. |
| Do-not-replicate warnings | Conditions under which the model should not be used. |
| Scale-up recommendation | Replicate, adapt, institutionalize, pilot again, or do not replicate. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework before proposing scale-up or replication of any area-based intervention.

A model should only be replicated when **the core logic is clear, evidence of benefit is credible, costs and staffing are realistic, local conditions are understood, risks are manageable, and the team can state both what must be adapted and when not to replicate**.
