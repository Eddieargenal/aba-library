# Area-Based Approach Suitability Framework

**Source:** Sanderson & Sitko, *Urban area-based approaches in post-disaster contexts: Guidance Note for Humanitarian Practitioners*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 0. Appropriateness decision  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to decide whether an Area-Based Approach (ABA) is the right response architecture before moving into design, assessment, or proposal development.

## Framework Overview

The **Area-Based Approach Suitability Framework** helps teams decide whether a crisis should be addressed through a place-based, multi-sector, locally coordinated approach rather than through a household-only, sector-only, or institution-only response.

The framework should be used before committing to an ABA. It tests whether the crisis problem is best understood through a defined geography and whether that geography provides a useful organizing frame for assessment, coordination, community engagement, implementation, and recovery planning.

## Core Framework Question

**Is the response problem best understood and solved through a defined area or neighborhood as an integrated whole?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Geographic concentration | Whether needs, risks, damage, displacement, or service gaps are concentrated in a defined area. | An ABA requires a meaningful place-based focus. If needs are dispersed, an area-based model may not be the right fit. |
| Multi-sector interdependence | Whether shelter, WASH, protection, livelihoods, services, infrastructure, governance, and markets interact in the same area. | ABAs are most useful when sector problems are connected and cannot be solved well in isolation. |
| Local stakeholder relevance | Whether local authorities, community groups, service providers, market actors, and civil society are central to response success. | An ABA depends on local coordination, participation, and use of existing structures. |
| Coordination value | Whether an area-based frame would reduce duplication, improve sequencing, or align actors around shared priorities. | The approach should improve collective action, not simply add another coordination layer. |
| Community engagement need | Whether affected people must help define priorities, risks, capacities, and acceptable solutions. | ABAs rely on participatory analysis and locally grounded decision-making. |
| Recovery and scalability potential | Whether the response can connect immediate relief to recovery, resilience, and possible replication in other areas. | ABAs are strongest when they support a pathway from emergency response to longer-term recovery. |
| Operational feasibility | Whether the team has enough time, access, resources, flexibility, and coordination capacity to work through the complexity of the area. | An ABA should be practical to implement, not only conceptually attractive. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a staged decision process:

1. **Clarify the response problem**  
   Define the crisis issue the team is trying to solve. Identify whether the problem is primarily household-level, sector-specific, institutional, or place-based.

2. **Test geographic relevance**  
   Assess whether the affected population, risks, damage, infrastructure gaps, service failures, or displacement patterns cluster in a meaningful geography.

3. **Assess sector interdependence**  
   Identify whether needs across sectors reinforce one another in the same area. For example, damaged housing may interact with drainage failure, protection risks, tenure insecurity, market disruption, and poor service access.

4. **Map local stakeholder dependency**  
   Determine whether local authorities, community groups, service providers, market actors, informal leaders, or civil society organizations are necessary for analysis, access, implementation, or sustainability.

5. **Evaluate coordination benefit**  
   Ask whether using an area as the organizing frame would improve coordination, reduce duplication, close gaps, sequence interventions, or strengthen accountability.

6. **Check operational feasibility**  
   Test whether the team can realistically conduct participatory assessment, coordinate with multiple actors, adapt programming, and monitor results within the selected area.

7. **Make the suitability decision**  
   Decide whether to proceed with an ABA, adapt the approach, combine it with sector or household targeting, or use another response model.

## Suitability Decision Criteria

| Criterion | Guiding question | Strong ABA signal |
|---|---|---|
| Place-based relevance | Is the crisis meaningfully concentrated in a defined geography? | Needs and risks cluster in a neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban area. |
| Integrated problem structure | Are multiple sectors interacting in the same area? | Shelter, WASH, protection, livelihoods, services, infrastructure, and governance issues reinforce each other. |
| Local systems dependency | Are local actors and systems essential to the response? | Authorities, service providers, communities, markets, or informal structures must be engaged. |
| Coordination advantage | Would an ABA improve collective action? | The area frame helps actors align activities, avoid duplication, and identify shared priorities. |
| Participatory value | Is local knowledge necessary to define the right response? | Community input is essential to understand needs, capacities, risks, and acceptable solutions. |
| Recovery pathway | Can the response link relief to recovery or resilience? | The area can support phased programming beyond immediate emergency delivery. |
| Feasibility | Can the approach be implemented with available time, access, resources, and flexibility? | The team can manage the complexity of assessment, coordination, engagement, and adaptation. |

## Suitability Scoring

Use the framework as a practical judgment tool rather than a rigid formula.

| Score | Interpretation | Recommended decision |
|---|---|---|
| 0–2 strong signals | Weak fit | Do not use a full ABA. Consider household, sector, or institution-focused programming. |
| 3–4 strong signals | Partial fit | Use selected ABA elements, such as area profiling, local coordination, or multi-sector referral pathways. |
| 5–7 strong signals | Strong fit | Proceed with an ABA and move into area selection, boundary definition, and participatory assessment. |

## Design Implications if ABA Is Suitable

If the framework indicates a strong fit, the next design steps should include:

| Design step | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Define the target area | Establish the geographic unit for assessment, coordination, and implementation. |
| Conduct multi-sector area profiling | Understand needs, risks, capacities, services, infrastructure, markets, institutions, and community priorities. |
| Map stakeholders | Identify authorities, community groups, service providers, market actors, civil society, and operational partners. |
| Establish coordination arrangements | Clarify leadership, roles, referral pathways, information-sharing, and decision-making mechanisms. |
| Design integrated interventions | Sequence and combine sector activities around shared area-level outcomes. |
| Build adaptive management into the program | Allow the response to adjust as new information, risks, and stakeholder priorities emerge. |

## Boundary Conditions

An ABA may not be appropriate when:

| Condition | Why it weakens ABA suitability |
|---|---|
| Needs are highly dispersed | There may be no meaningful area around which to organize the response. |
| The response problem is purely individual or household-based | Household targeting may be more efficient and appropriate. |
| The intervention is narrow and technical | A sector-specific intervention may be sufficient. |
| Local stakeholder engagement is not feasible | The approach may become externally driven and poorly grounded. |
| Timeframes are too short for participatory design | The team may not be able to carry out the coordination and analysis required. |
| Agencies lack flexibility | Rigid sector budgets or activities may prevent integrated area-level programming. |

## Risk Alerts

**Urban-label trap:** Do not use an ABA simply because the crisis is urban. Use it only when geography is analytically and operationally meaningful.

**Sector-repackaging risk:** Do not rename a set of sector activities as an ABA without changing the assessment, coordination, participation, and design logic.

**Over-complexity risk:** An ABA can become too heavy if the team lacks time, coordination capacity, or decision-making authority.

**Equity risk:** Focusing on one area without explaining why other nearby areas are excluded can create perceived unfairness and undermine acceptance.

**Local bypass risk:** An ABA that does not engage local authorities, communities, and service systems misses one of the main reasons for using the approach.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **ABA Suitability Decision Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Decision | Proceed with ABA, use partial ABA elements, or do not use ABA. |
| Rationale | Explanation of why the approach is or is not suitable. |
| Strong suitability signals | The main factors supporting an ABA. |
| Weak suitability signals | The main concerns or limitations. |
| Required adaptations | Any conditions needed to make the approach feasible. |
| Next step | Boundary definition, area profiling, alternative targeting, or further analysis. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use an **Area-Based Approach** when the crisis problem is best understood and solved through a **defined geography**, where needs are **multi-sectoral**, local systems matter, coordination is necessary, and affected people must help shape the response.

Do not use a full ABA when the problem is dispersed, narrow, purely household-level, or when the program lacks the time and flexibility required to work through area-level complexity.
