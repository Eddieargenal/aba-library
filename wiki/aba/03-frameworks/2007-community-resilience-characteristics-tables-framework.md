# Community Resilience Characteristics Tables Framework

**Source:** Twigg, *Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community*  
**Years:** 2007 / 2009  
**Lifecycle stage:** 3. Community and stakeholder engagement platform  
**Framework purpose:** Structure discussion around what a resilient community should have: governance, risk knowledge, preparedness, services, livelihoods, infrastructure, and social capacity.

## Framework Overview

The **Community Resilience Characteristics Tables Framework** helps teams facilitate participatory self-assessment and planning discussions with communities and local stakeholders.

It translates the concept of a disaster-resilient community into practical discussion areas that residents, local authorities, service providers, community organizations, and humanitarian actors can use to assess strengths, gaps, priorities, and actions.

The framework is useful during the community and stakeholder engagement stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), especially when teams need a common language for discussing resilience across sectors and actors. It can be used for planning, assessment, monitoring, evaluation, and participatory review.

## Core Framework Question

**What characteristics should this community or area have to be more resilient, and which of those characteristics are strong, weak, missing, or unequal across groups?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it explores | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Governance | Local leadership, participation, accountability, organization, and links to authorities. | Resilience depends on inclusive and functioning local decision-making. |
| Risk knowledge | Understanding of hazards, exposure, vulnerability, capacities, and past events. | Communities cannot reduce risks they do not understand. |
| Knowledge and education | Public awareness, school-based learning, training, local knowledge, and communication. | Risk knowledge must be shared and usable by different groups. |
| Preparedness and response | Early warning, evacuation, contingency plans, local response, supplies, and coordination. | Preparedness reduces harm before external support arrives. |
| Risk management and vulnerability reduction | Actions that reduce exposure and vulnerability in everyday life. | Resilience requires reducing underlying risk, not only responding to disasters. |
| Livelihood resilience | Income, assets, savings, market access, skills, and recovery options. | Households with stronger livelihoods recover faster and rely less on negative coping. |
| Infrastructure and services | WASH, roads, drainage, electricity, waste, health, schools, public spaces, and facilities. | Community resilience depends on functioning services and safe infrastructure. |
| Health and wellbeing | Public health, disease prevention, psychosocial support, nutrition, and access to care. | Health vulnerabilities often determine disaster impact and recovery capacity. |
| Social capacity | Trust, mutual aid, social networks, inclusion, conflict resolution, and collective action. | Social cohesion helps people prepare, respond, and recover together. |

## Facilitation Logic

Use the tables as a participatory self-assessment process:

1. **Define the discussion area**  
   Clarify whether the group is assessing a neighborhood, settlement, village, district, or specific hazard-prone zone.

2. **Explain the resilience characteristics**  
   Present the dimensions in simple language and ask participants to add locally relevant characteristics.

3. **Identify existing strengths**  
   Ask what already works, what people rely on, and which capacities existed before the project.

4. **Identify gaps and unequal access**  
   Ask which characteristics are weak, missing, unreliable, or inaccessible to some groups.

5. **Score or rank characteristics**  
   Use a simple scale, ranking exercise, or traffic-light method to prioritize what needs attention.

6. **Identify actions and responsible actors**  
   Convert gaps into actions for households, community groups, local authorities, service providers, and humanitarian actors.

7. **Validate with excluded groups**  
   Hold separate or adapted discussions with groups who may not speak freely in mixed forums.

8. **Use findings in the area plan**  
   Translate the self-assessment into priorities, activities, indicators, and monitoring questions.

## Community Resilience Characteristics Table

| Characteristic area | What a resilient community should have | Discussion questions |
|---|---|---|
| Governance | Inclusive local leadership, organized groups, clear roles, links to authorities, and accountability. | Who makes decisions? Who is left out? How are authorities engaged? |
| Risk knowledge | Shared understanding of hazards, risk hotspots, vulnerable groups, and local capacities. | What hazards affect this area? Who is most at risk? What has changed? |
| Knowledge and education | Risk information, training, school activities, public awareness, and local knowledge sharing. | Do people know what to do before, during, and after a shock? |
| Preparedness | Early warning, evacuation routes, safe places, household plans, response roles, and supplies. | Can people act quickly and safely when warned? |
| Local response | Volunteers, first aid, referral systems, communication, response tools, and coordination. | Who responds first? Do they have the skills and resources needed? |
| Risk reduction | Drainage, safer shelter, fire prevention, slope protection, waste management, safe access, and environmental management. | What can be done now to reduce future risk? |
| Livelihoods | Diverse income, skills, market access, savings, credit, productive assets, and recovery support. | How do people protect income and recover after shocks? |
| Infrastructure | Safe roads, drainage, water, sanitation, electricity, schools, health facilities, and public spaces. | Which infrastructure failures create the greatest risk? |
| Health and wellbeing | Access to health services, disease prevention, WASH, psychosocial support, and care networks. | What health risks increase during shocks or service disruption? |
| Social capacity | Trust, mutual support, conflict resolution, inclusion, communication, and collective action. | Who helps whom? Where are tensions or divisions increasing? |
| Accountability | Feedback channels, transparent decisions, complaint response, and community monitoring. | Can residents raise problems and see action taken? |

## Participatory Self-Assessment Scale

| Score | Meaning | Facilitation prompt |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Not present | This characteristic is absent or unknown. |
| 1 | Weak | Some elements exist, but they are informal, unreliable, or unequal. |
| 2 | Basic | The characteristic exists but needs support, resources, inclusion, or coordination. |
| 3 | Functional | The characteristic works for most people and has clear roles or systems. |
| 4 | Strong and adaptive | The characteristic has been tested, improved, and is sustained locally. |

## Planning Conversion Table

| If the discussion finds... | Convert into... |
|---|---|
| Low risk knowledge | Risk mapping, awareness sessions, school DRR, household preparedness messaging. |
| Weak governance links | Municipal coordination, committee strengthening, role clarification, public action plans. |
| Excluded groups | Separate consultations, accessibility measures, targeted communication, representation safeguards. |
| Poor early warning | Warning protocols, trusted messengers, action triggers, drill exercises. |
| Unsafe evacuation | Route mapping, safe-site review, lighting, transport support, disability-inclusive plans. |
| Weak services | Service gap analysis, provider coordination, referral pathways, infrastructure support. |
| Livelihood fragility | Market support, savings groups, livelihood recovery, skills support, cash feasibility review. |
| Infrastructure risk | Drainage, access, waste, shelter, WASH, public works, and maintenance planning. |
| Low accountability | Feedback channels, complaint response, community scorecards, public review meetings. |

## Design Implications

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Engagement design | Gives communities a structured way to discuss resilience. |
| Needs prioritization | Identifies which resilience characteristics are weakest or most urgent. |
| Inclusion | Shows whether resilience capacities are shared equally across groups. |
| Activity design | Converts broad resilience gaps into practical actions. |
| Monitoring | Provides baseline and review indicators for resilience capacity. |
| Coordination | Links community priorities with local authorities and service providers. |
| Handover | Tests which capacities can be maintained locally after project exit. |

## Risk Alerts

**Abstract resilience risk:** Avoid asking communities to discuss “resilience” in general terms only. Use concrete characteristics and examples.

**Consensus masking risk:** A group score can hide differences between men, women, renters, informal settlers, displaced people, older people, youth, and people with disabilities.

**Over-assessment risk:** Self-assessment should lead to action. Do not collect perceptions without feeding back decisions.

**Local responsibility overload:** Do not assign complex service or infrastructure responsibilities to community groups without resources and institutional support.

**Static checklist risk:** Resilience characteristics should be adapted to the local hazard, urban system, culture, and governance context.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a **Community Resilience Self-Assessment and Planning Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | Community, neighborhood, settlement, or urban zone assessed. |
| Participants | Groups consulted and any representation gaps. |
| Resilience characteristics reviewed | Governance, risk knowledge, preparedness, livelihoods, services, infrastructure, health, and social capacity. |
| Strengths | Existing capacities and local assets to build on. |
| Gaps | Weak, missing, unequal, or untested characteristics. |
| Priority actions | Practical actions proposed by participants and technical teams. |
| Responsible actors | Community groups, local authorities, service providers, humanitarian actors, or private actors. |
| Inclusion measures | How excluded groups will participate and benefit. |
| Monitoring indicators | Observable signs that resilience characteristics are improving. |
| Follow-up process | How findings will be validated, reviewed, and updated. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when teams need a participatory way to translate resilience into visible capacities and planning priorities.

A strong community resilience self-assessment should show **what capacities already exist, what is missing, who is excluded, what actions are needed, and who will carry them forward**.
