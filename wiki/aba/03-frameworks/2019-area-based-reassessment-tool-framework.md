# Area-Based Reassessment Tool Framework

**Source:** REACH, *Area-Based Assessment with Key Informants: A Practical Guide*; IRC, *Urban Context Analysis Toolkit*  
**Years:** 2019 / 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 9. Monitoring, learning, and adaptation  
**Framework purpose:** Periodically update the area profile, risks, service gaps, population movement, social tensions, and intervention priorities.

## Framework Overview

The **Area-Based Reassessment Tool Framework** helps teams update their understanding of a neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban area during implementation.

Area-based programs operate in places where needs, populations, risks, services, markets, access, social relations, and institutional conditions change over time. A one-time assessment can quickly become outdated. This framework converts area-based assessment and urban context analysis methods into a periodic reassessment process that supports monitoring, learning, adaptation, and coordination.

It is designed for use during implementation, midline review, contingency planning, project redesign, and transition planning. It helps teams decide whether the original area profile and intervention logic remain valid or whether priorities, targeting, sequencing, coordination, and risk mitigation need to change.

## Core Framework Question

**Has the area changed in ways that require the program to update its priorities, targeting, sequencing, coordination, risk mitigation, or handover plan?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it reassesses | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Population movement and composition | Arrivals, departures, returns, displacement patterns, household composition, renters, hosts, informal settlers, and vulnerable groups. | Population change can shift needs, tensions, service pressure, and targeting assumptions. |
| Service access and gaps | WASH, health, education, shelter, roads, drainage, waste, markets, protection, documentation, and public services. | Area priorities should reflect current service functionality, not outdated baseline findings. |
| Risk and hazard profile | Flooding, fire, landslide, heat, disease, conflict, eviction, crime, infrastructure failure, and seasonal risks. | New or increasing risks may require changes in design and sequencing. |
| Infrastructure and environmental conditions | Roads, drainage, access routes, public spaces, utilities, waste systems, buildings, and environmental hazards. | Physical conditions affect safety, access, shelter repair, WASH, mobility, and preparedness. |
| Social cohesion and tensions | Host-displaced relations, landlord-tenant issues, aid-related tensions, discrimination, conflict, safety, and community leadership. | Social tensions can affect access, protection, targeting, acceptance, and accountability. |
| Markets and livelihoods | Market access, prices, employment, supply chains, credit, transport, and livelihood coping strategies. | Market and livelihood changes affect assistance modality, recovery options, and vulnerability. |
| Governance and stakeholders | Local authorities, service providers, informal leaders, civil society, committees, landlords, private actors, and humanitarian actors. | Programs must adapt to changing power, mandates, coordination, and implementation capacity. |
| Protection and inclusion | Safety risks, GBV, child protection, disability access, exclusion, evictions, documentation barriers, and referral pathways. | Adaptation must respond to changing protection risks and access barriers. |
| Program relevance and sequencing | Whether current interventions still match area priorities and dependencies. | Activities may need to be resequenced, redesigned, scaled, paused, or handed over. |
| Accountability and perception | Community feedback, complaints, satisfaction, trust, information gaps, and perceived fairness. | Reassessment should include how people experience the response, not only technical conditions. |

## Reassessment Logic

The framework uses a recurring update cycle:

1. **Define the reassessment trigger**  
   Reassess on a schedule or when a major change occurs, such as population movement, flooding, eviction threats, disease outbreaks, price shocks, insecurity, service collapse, new actors, or implementation delays.

2. **Review the original area profile**  
   Start from the baseline assessment, stakeholder map, risk map, service gap analysis, and intervention logic.

3. **Identify what may have changed**  
   Select the dimensions that need updating, rather than repeating the full original assessment every time.

4. **Collect targeted evidence**  
   Use key informant interviews, direct observation, service-provider checks, community feedback, focus groups, market checks, secondary data, and program monitoring data.

5. **Triangulate findings**  
   Compare information across different informants, groups, locations, and data sources to avoid relying on one perspective.

6. **Update the area profile**  
   Revise the map of needs, capacities, risks, stakeholders, service systems, population groups, and unresolved issues.

7. **Test program assumptions**  
   Check whether targeting, sequencing, modality, partnerships, technical design, and handover assumptions remain valid.

8. **Agree adaptation actions**  
   Convert findings into specific decisions, such as redesign, resequencing, referral, coordination escalation, mitigation, or handover adjustment.

9. **Communicate changes**  
   Explain to communities, local actors, and partners what changed, what the program will adapt, and what cannot be addressed.

10. **Schedule the next review**  
   Set the next reassessment date or trigger.

## Area-Based Reassessment Checklist

| Reassessment area | Key questions |
|---|---|
| Population movement | Have people arrived, left, returned, been evicted, relocated, or changed settlement patterns? |
| Household vulnerability | Have priority vulnerability groups changed? Are new groups being missed? |
| Service gaps | Which services have improved, deteriorated, become inaccessible, or become overstretched? |
| Infrastructure | Have access routes, drainage, roads, WASH systems, public spaces, or buildings changed? |
| Hazard exposure | Are seasonal, environmental, public health, conflict, fire, flood, or eviction risks increasing? |
| Social tensions | Are there new tensions between groups, neighborhoods, landlords, authorities, or aid recipients? |
| Protection | Are safety, GBV, child protection, disability access, documentation, or eviction risks changing? |
| Markets | Have prices, availability, transport, employment, credit, or supply chains changed? |
| Stakeholders | Have relevant authorities, service providers, committees, NGOs, private actors, or informal leaders changed? |
| Coordination | Are there new actors, duplication risks, referral gaps, or coordination failures? |
| Program relevance | Do planned activities still match current priorities and dependencies? |
| Sequencing | Do any activities need to happen earlier or later because of new constraints or risks? |
| Accountability | What are communities saying through feedback, complaints, informal channels, and review meetings? |
| Handover | Are local actors still able and willing to maintain services, infrastructure, or preparedness systems? |

## Key Informant Reassessment Guide

| Informant type | What to ask |
|---|---|
| Local authorities | Changes in population, service pressure, public works, risk management, permits, budgets, and coordination priorities. |
| Service providers | Functionality, coverage, access barriers, maintenance issues, demand changes, and capacity gaps. |
| Community leaders | Priority concerns, social tensions, vulnerable groups, safety risks, local initiatives, and trust issues. |
| Women’s groups and protection actors | Safety risks, access barriers, exclusion, GBV concerns, referral gaps, and accountability issues. |
| Youth groups | Mobility, employment, safety, social tension, communication channels, and public space use. |
| People with disabilities and caregivers | Accessibility, evacuation barriers, communication gaps, service access, and support needs. |
| Market actors | Price changes, stock availability, transport issues, credit, demand shifts, and supply constraints. |
| Schools and health facilities | Service continuity, demand, safety, WASH, referral needs, disease risks, and preparedness. |
| Humanitarian partners | Duplication, coverage gaps, referral pathways, standards, unresolved risks, and coordination issues. |

## Reassessment Trigger Matrix

| Trigger | What to reassess immediately |
|---|---|
| Major population movement | Targeting, service pressure, social tensions, shelter needs, WASH demand, protection risks. |
| Flooding or seasonal hazard | Drainage, access, shelter damage, WASH contamination, early warning, evacuation, disease risk. |
| Fire or infrastructure failure | Shelter safety, public space, utilities, access, emergency response capacity, risk reduction measures. |
| Eviction threat or tenure change | Protection, legal support, shelter strategy, relocation risk, landlord relations, referral pathways. |
| Disease outbreak | WASH, health access, public communication, household vulnerability, service continuity. |
| Market price shock | Cash feasibility, procurement, livelihoods, affordability, supply chains, targeting. |
| Insecurity or social tension | Access, protection mitigation, acceptance, referral pathways, community communication, activity sequencing. |
| New public works or municipal plan | Coordination, technical design, sequencing, permits, infrastructure integration, handover. |
| New major actor or donor activity | Duplication, complementarity, referral, coordination, geographic coverage, harmonization. |
| High complaint volume | Accountability, targeting, communication, exclusion, quality, protection, trust. |

## Adaptation Decision Table

| Finding | Possible adaptation |
|---|---|
| Service gap has worsened | Reprioritize activities, coordinate with service provider, adjust budget, escalate advocacy, add temporary support. |
| Population has shifted | Update targeting, revise maps, adjust community engagement, review inclusion safeguards. |
| Hazard risk has increased | Resequence activities, add mitigation, update contingency plans, strengthen early warning and evacuation. |
| Social tensions are rising | Review targeting, improve communication, strengthen conflict sensitivity, adjust implementation approach. |
| Market conditions changed | Reassess cash/in-kind modality, update transfer values, change procurement or supplier strategy. |
| Infrastructure dependency identified | Delay dependent activities until enabling works occur, coordinate public works, revise sequencing map. |
| Protection risk identified | Add referral pathways, adapt delivery modality, change site selection, involve protection specialists. |
| Local actor capacity decreased | Adjust handover timeline, add capacity support, revise maintenance plan, identify alternative responsible actor. |
| Community feedback shows low trust | Improve information sharing, explain decisions, adjust complaint response, involve community representatives. |
| Duplication detected | Coordinate with actors, retarget, hand over activities, or shift to uncovered gaps. |

## Minimum Reassessment Dataset

| Data category | Minimum information to update |
|---|---|
| Area profile | Boundaries, population groups, key locations, service points, risk hotspots, access constraints. |
| Population | Estimated changes, movement patterns, new vulnerable groups, tenure status, household composition. |
| Services | Current functionality, access barriers, coverage gaps, responsible actors, referral routes. |
| Risks | Current hazards, seasonal risks, conflict or protection issues, environmental risks, infrastructure risks. |
| Stakeholders | Authorities, service providers, community structures, NGOs, private actors, informal leaders. |
| Program status | Activities completed, delayed, blocked, duplicated, no longer relevant, or needing redesign. |
| Community perception | Priority concerns, satisfaction, complaints, trust, information needs, perceived fairness. |
| Adaptation actions | Decisions, responsible actors, deadlines, unresolved issues, and communication plan. |

## Design Implications

| Design decision | How reassessment supports it |
|---|---|
| Targeting | Updates who should be prioritized and why. |
| Sequencing | Identifies dependencies and constraints that affect implementation order. |
| Technical design | Revises designs based on current infrastructure, hazard, and service conditions. |
| Coordination | Detects duplication, gaps, new actors, and referral needs. |
| Protection mitigation | Captures emerging safety, exclusion, eviction, and access risks. |
| Modality choice | Updates cash, voucher, in-kind, service, or infrastructure decisions based on markets and access. |
| Handover | Checks whether responsible actors can still maintain outputs after exit. |
| Learning | Creates evidence for adaptation, not only reporting. |

## Risk Alerts

**Baseline fixation risk:** Do not keep implementing based on old baseline data when the area has changed.

**Key informant bias:** Key informants may represent only certain groups or interests. Triangulate with direct observation, community feedback, and diverse informants.

**Assessment fatigue risk:** Reassessment should be targeted and useful, not a full repeated assessment unless necessary.

**Invisible group risk:** Population movement, renters, undocumented people, informal settlers, people with disabilities, and isolated households may be missed.

**Adaptation gap risk:** Reassessment has little value if findings do not lead to decisions, budget changes, sequencing changes, or coordination action.

**Do-no-harm risk:** Updating priorities can create tensions if changes are not communicated transparently.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Area-Based Reassessment and Adaptation Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Reassessment date and trigger | Scheduled review or event that triggered reassessment. |
| Target area | Neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone reviewed. |
| What changed | Updated findings on population, services, risks, infrastructure, markets, social tensions, and stakeholders. |
| What remains valid | Original assumptions and priorities that still hold. |
| What no longer holds | Outdated assumptions, obsolete priorities, changed risks, or invalid design choices. |
| Adaptation decisions | Changes to targeting, sequencing, technical design, modality, staffing, coordination, risk mitigation, or handover. |
| Responsible actors | Who will act on each adaptation decision. |
| Community communication | What will be communicated to residents and how. |
| Unresolved issues | Questions or risks needing further assessment, referral, or escalation. |
| Next review | Date or trigger for the next reassessment. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework whenever the team suspects that the area has changed enough to affect program relevance, safety, fairness, coordination, or sustainability.

A strong area-based reassessment should explain **what changed, what evidence supports the update, what assumptions must be revised, what decisions will change, who is responsible, and how communities will be informed**.
