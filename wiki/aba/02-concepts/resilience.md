> **Abstract.** Resilience is the capacity of individuals, communities, systems, or institutions to absorb, adapt to, and recover from shocks while maintaining essential functions and reducing future risk. Five sources in this wiki define the term, producing four distinct formulations that are not interchangeable. The definitional divergence is not terminological imprecision — it reflects genuinely different theories of what resilience is for: community capacity, engineering efficiency, structural preservation, or long-term trajectory protection. Practitioners who conflate these definitions risk designing programmes that are internally incoherent and that cannot be meaningfully evaluated.

## 1. Definition

No single authoritative definition of resilience commands consensus across the sources in this wiki. Four formulations are in use, each from a distinct institutional or disciplinary tradition:

**Twigg (2009):** the capacity of "community/system to anticipate and absorb shocks, maintain core functions during events, and recover after them" (p. 8). This formulation emphasizes *community* as the primary unit of resilience and foregrounds the temporal sequence: anticipation before, maintenance during, recovery after.

**World Bank/GFDRR (2013):** the "capacity of systems/communities to resist, absorb, adapt, and recover from hazards efficiently" (p. 9). The emphasis on *efficiency* reflects an engineering systems orientation — resilience is a performance characteristic measured against an optimization standard.

**UNISDR (cited in IFRC, 2017):** the "ability of systems/communities to resist, absorb, accommodate, and recover while preserving essential structures/functions" (p. 17). The addition of *accommodation* and the focus on preserving structure distinguishes this formulation — resilience is not about returning to an original state but about maintaining core functions while adapting.

**IFRC (2017):** the "ability of individuals/communities/organizations/countries to anticipate, reduce impact, cope, and recover without undermining long-term prospects" (p. 17). The phrase "without undermining long-term prospects" is the distinguishing feature — resilience here includes a future-oriented condition that excludes coping strategies that trade short-term survival for long-term capacity loss.

**UNDRR (2017):** the "capacity of systems/communities/societies exposed to hazards to resist, absorb, adapt, recover, and transform in a timely way" (p. 8). The addition of *transform* marks this as the most ambitious formulation — resilience is not only recovery but structural change toward reduced risk.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Twigg (2009)** grounds the concept in community-level disaster risk reduction practice, using a five-thematic-area, 167-characteristic framework to operationalize resilience as a multidimensional target state (pp. 13-14). The framework is explicitly normative — the "disaster-resilient community" is described as an idealized benchmark, with milestones tracking movement toward it rather than arrival (p. 20). This makes resilience a direction of travel, not a threshold condition.

**World Bank/GFDRR (2013)** applies resilience logic to urban systems with an emphasis on engineering design, arguing that cities must account for unknown and uncertain futures and avoid over-reliance on single "optimal" designs (p. 10). The concept of *residual risk* — risk that remains after mitigation measures and must be managed through preparedness and response systems — is introduced here as a practical complement to resilience (pp. 10-11). The corollary principle of *redundancy and graceful failure* — that critical systems should continue core functions and fail safely under extreme shocks — is specific to this source and not present in other definitions (p. 11).

**IFRC (2017)** presents two parallel definitions in the same document: the UNISDR formulation and the IFRC formulation (p. 17). This dual presentation signals that even within a single institutional framework, resilience is understood differently depending on whether the referent is systems and communities or individuals and organizations.

**UNDRR (2017)** links resilience to the Making Cities Resilient campaign's Ten Essentials framework, embedding the concept within a six-phase city-level implementation cycle (pp. 82, 84-99). This operationalization is the most institutionally elaborated in the source set and the most directly linked to local government planning processes.

**Campbell, L. (2019)** demonstrates resilience as an outcome in practice: the Barrio Mio programme in Guatemala City explicitly aimed to "reduce risk, improve response, and build safer, healthier, more resilient neighbourhoods" through a neighbourhood approach that integrated preparedness, mitigation, and response rather than treating them separately (p. 17).

## 3. Contested dimensions

The four definitional formulations are not interchangeable, and selecting among them has consequences for programme design and evaluation:

- **Community vs. systems as primary unit:** Twigg (2009) centres the community; World Bank (2013) centres systems. A programme targeting community social capital and one targeting infrastructure redundancy may both claim to build resilience but are measuring different things.
- **Efficiency vs. transformation:** World Bank (2013) frames resilience as optimized performance; UNDRR (2017) includes transformation. These are different standards — a system can recover efficiently to a pre-disaster state without transforming underlying risk conditions.
- **Short-term coping vs. long-term trajectory:** IFRC (2017) explicitly excludes coping strategies that "undermine long-term prospects." This rules out a range of adaptive behaviours (asset depletion, migration, indebtedness) that other definitions might count as evidence of resilience.
- **Timing:** Twigg (2009) structures resilience as anticipation → maintenance → recovery; UNDRR (2017) adds transformation as a fourth phase. These temporal models imply different monitoring frameworks.

None of the sources in this wiki resolves which definition should be used in area-based urban humanitarian programming. Practitioners should select a definition explicitly, state it in programme documents, and design monitoring accordingly.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Resilience must be defined and operationalized before it can be measured — programmes that list resilience as an outcome without specifying which definition they are using cannot be meaningfully evaluated (IFRC, 2017, p. 17; UNDRR, 2017, pp. 82-99).
- Monitoring systems should distinguish between recovery (return to pre-shock state), adaptation (functional maintenance under changed conditions), and transformation (structural change toward reduced future risk), as these require different indicators (UNDRR, 2017, p. 8; Twigg, 2009, p. 20).
- The concept of residual risk requires that resilience-building programmes include preparedness, early warning, and response system investment alongside risk reduction — not as add-ons but as core components (World Bank/GFDRR, 2013, pp. 10-11).
- Programmes working through the neighbourhood approach can generate resilience outcomes by integrating preparedness, mitigation, and response rather than treating them as sequential phases (Campbell, L., 2019, pp. 17, 46).
- ABA programmes should not conflate community participation in response with community resilience — participation is a means; resilience requires demonstrated capacity that persists beyond the programme (Twigg, 2009, pp. 7-8).

## 5. Open questions

- Which definition of resilience is most appropriate for area-based urban humanitarian programming, and on what basis should that selection be made?
- How should resilience be measured at the area level given that the concept operates simultaneously at individual, community, system, and institutional scales?
- To what extent can 18-to-24-month humanitarian programme cycles make a measurable contribution to resilience outcomes, as opposed to recovery outcomes?
