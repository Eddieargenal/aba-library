<a id="definition"></a>
## Definition

Resilience is the capacity of a community to **anticipate, minimize and absorb** potential stresses or destructive forces through adaptation or resistance; to **maintain basic functions and structures** during a disaster; and to **recover or "bounce back"** afterwards (Twigg 2009, p. 8).

Resilience is a set of *capacities*, not the absence of vulnerability or hazard exposure. No community is ever completely safe — resilience describes a direction of travel rather than a fixed end state (Twigg 2009, p. 7–8).

The World Bank's urban-resilience guidance corroborates these core capacities, adopting the UNISDR definition: resilience is "the ability of a system, community, or society exposed to hazards to resist, absorb, accommodate to and recover from the effects of a hazard in a timely and efficient manner, including through the preservation and restoration of its essential basic structures and functions" (World Bank 2013, p. 9). It adds the **residual-risk** principle: even with the best mitigation, some risk always remains, so cities must plan for it through early warning, preparedness, and rapid recovery (World Bank 2013, pp. 10–11).

<a id="field-use"></a>
## Field Use

- Resilience is made operational through the **Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community** — five thematic areas aligned to the Hyogo Framework (governance; risk assessment; knowledge and education; risk management and vulnerability reduction; disaster preparedness and response), subdivided into components and characteristics. See [[community-resilience-characteristics]] (framework).
- The characteristics describe an **ideal**, used to frame baselines, vulnerability and capacity analysis (VCA), project design, and M&E — *not* as a pass/fail checklist (Twigg 2009, p. 7).
- Because the ideal is rarely reachable directly, teams track progress with a **milestones model** — intermediate, locally-agreed steps between the current state and the ideal (Twigg 2009, p. 19).
- The framework is generic and must be **adapted to local hazards, culture, and capacities** before use (Twigg 2009, p. 18).
- Resilience is not only physical: "technical solutions alone are insufficient; resilience depends on institutions, governance quality, social equity, and community capacity" (World Bank 2013, pp. 11, 24). Treat **social resilience and inclusive governance** as co-equal with infrastructure.

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

- **Consensus assumption.** The concept assumes a community with a shared vision; in practice communities are dynamic and often not united, and conflict can undermine resilience. There is no tested field experience applying the characteristics in conflict-affected settings (Twigg 2009, p. 9, 15). See [[resilience-consensus]] (known tension).
- **Urban adaptation gap.** The Twigg source is written for generic/rural communities. Applying it to dense informal settlements — with insecure tenure, fragmented governance, and heterogeneous populations — requires substantial modification; neither community consensus nor accessible local government can be assumed (Twigg 2009, urban-applicability assessment). The World Bank (2013) supplies urban-specific resilience guidance — residual-risk planning, cross-sectoral coordination, ecosystem-based approaches — that partially narrows this gap, though it remains pitched at city infrastructure/investment governance rather than neighbourhood scale (World Bank 2013, pp. 6, 10–11).
- **Checklist misuse.** Treating the 167 characteristics as a checklist rather than an adaptable ideal is a recognised failure mode. See [[checklist-misuse]] (risk).
