> **Abstract.** Multi-sector response analysis is the systematic examination of interrelated urban needs across sectors to identify the most appropriate combination of interventions, modalities, and target groups. Six sources in this wiki ground the concept, which is operationalized most fully in the Urban Response Analysis Framework (Mohiddin et al., 2017). A central unresolved tension in the literature is whether integrated multi-sector programming constitutes a genuinely different planning model or simply the coordination of parallel sector outputs within the same geographic area.

## 1. Definition

Multi-sector response analysis is "the link between situation analysis and programme planning/implementation, used to select response options, modalities, and target groups based on appropriateness and feasibility while minimizing harm" (Mohiddin et al., 2017, p. 8). Its defining characteristic is that response selection is driven by the interdependence of urban needs rather than by sector boundaries — a consequence of the recognition that urban system failures compound each other and cannot be adequately addressed through single-sector interventions alone (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, p. 10; Sanderson, 2019, p. 113).

The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) specifies that multi-sectoral support in area-based programming does not require that all needs be met, but that "support is structured across relevant sectors with relative-needs logic for all people in the area" (p. 9). This formulation distinguishes multi-sector response analysis from comprehensive service delivery: the standard is structured needs interdependence analysis, not full sector coverage.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**The Urban Response Analysis Framework (URAF)** provides the most operationalized account of multi-sector response analysis in this source set (Mohiddin et al., 2017). URAF defines a six-step process — from multi-sector needs assessment through response recommendation and monitoring design — designed for programme manager workshops of approximately four to six hours when assessment data are available (p. 18). Response selection is explicitly linked to needs, context, feasibility, and risk minimisation rather than to modality preference alone (pp. 8-10, 12-13). Multipurpose cash grants are promoted as a primary modality alongside complementary sector-specific and governance interventions where appropriate (pp. 3, 9, 13).

**Parker & Maynard (2015)** established multi-sectorality as one of three defining criteria for area-based approaches, drawing on Turok's argument that effective urban programmes "typically embrace a range of social, economic and physical development objectives cutting across fields such as education, housing, transport and economic development" (p. 11). Multi-sector programming is explicitly distinguished from single-sector programmes co-located in the same area: the former requires integrated planning logic linking needs across sectors; the latter does not constitute an area-based approach (pp. 10-11).

**Sanderson & Sitko (2017)** argue from post-disaster case evidence that "needs across shelter, WASH, health, and livelihoods are interrelated and should be assessed and addressed jointly" (p. 10), and that existing cluster coordination structures are "insufficient for urban multi-sector territorial needs without stronger inter-cluster city-level linkage" (p. 19). This frames multi-sector response analysis as a coordination reform, not only a programme design tool.

**Sanderson (2019)**, drawing on the Good Practice Review 12 synthesis, confirms that "multi-sector assessment is recommended in urban crises because needs are interrelated and cannot be reliably addressed in isolation" (p. 113), identifying area-based and multi-sector approaches as the primary operational response to this interdependence (pp. 79, 113, 128).

## 3. Contested dimensions

The central unresolved tension concerns the distinction between **genuine sectoral integration** and **parallel sector delivery in the same geographic area**. Parker & Maynard (2015) specify that multi-sector programming only qualifies as area-based when combined with geographic targeting and participatory delivery — co-location without integrated planning logic does not meet the standard (pp. 10-11). This distinction matters operationally: programmes can claim multi-sector status while retaining sector-siloed planning, budgeting, and reporting with geographic co-location as the only integration point.

A secondary tension concerns scope. The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) frames multi-sectoral ABA as operating with "relative-needs logic" — not comprehensive coverage — (p. 9). URAF (Mohiddin et al., 2017) operationalizes this through cross-sector needs severity scoring (pp. 29, 49), while GPR 12 (Sanderson, 2019) recommends multi-sector profiling as a baseline (p. 113). The gap between relative-needs analysis and full multi-sector profiling is not resolved in the current source set and has practical implications for assessment design and resource allocation.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Response options should be selected on the basis of cross-sector needs interdependence analysis, not sector availability or agency mandate (Mohiddin et al., 2017, pp. 8-10).
- Multi-sector assessment must precede response option selection; retrospectively combining independently collected sector datasets does not satisfy the integration standard (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, p. 10; Sanderson, 2019, p. 113).
- Parallel sector delivery co-located in one area does not satisfy the multi-sector criterion for ABA unless it involves shared planning logic and integrated outcome targets (Parker & Maynard, 2015, pp. 10-11).
- Cash grants and complementary sector responses should be analysed together, not as mutually exclusive alternatives (Mohiddin et al., 2017, pp. 3, 9, 13).
- Monitoring should track multi-agency contribution to area-level change, not only individual sector outputs (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, p. 27; Sanderson, 2019, pp. 138-143).

## 5. Open questions

- At what threshold of needs interdependence does multi-sector analysis add sufficient value over parallel sector assessment to justify the additional coordination cost?
- How should multi-sector response analysis be adapted in acute-onset emergencies where data scarcity and time pressure preclude full URAF-type processes?
- What comparative evidence exists that integrated multi-sector ABA produces better outcomes than well-coordinated parallel sector programmes operating in the same area?
