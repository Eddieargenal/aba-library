> **Abstract.** Multi-stakeholder engagement is the structured, active involvement of government, civil society, affected communities, the private sector, and humanitarian actors in the design, implementation, coordination, and evaluation of area-based programming. Eight sources in this wiki ground the concept, which is defined consistently as a requirement rather than a preference. The central tension is not whether to engage multiple stakeholders but how to distribute authority among them — and whether the humanitarian agency functions as implementer, facilitator, or convenor. This role distinction has measurable consequences for scale, sustainability, and local ownership of outcomes.

## 1. Definition

Multi-stakeholder engagement in ABA contexts refers to the active collaboration of a defined set of actor types — government at national and local levels, civil society organizations, affected communities, private sector actors, and humanitarian or development agencies — across the full programme cycle, not only at consultation stages (Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, pp. 9-10, 13; IFRC, 2017, pp. 31, 40-41).

The IASC (2010) specifies that effective urban humanitarian response "depends on coordinated engagement among national/municipal authorities, humanitarian actors, communities, and non-traditional partners" (pp. 9-10), and establishes the host-government leadership principle: national and local public institutions should lead or at minimum coordinate response architecture wherever possible (p. 9). This positions multi-stakeholder engagement not as a participatory add-on but as the structural architecture within which programming takes place.

The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) defines multi-stakeholder participation as one of four core ABA characteristics, requiring "active engagement of government, civil society, affected communities, private sector, and external actors as context requires" (pp. 9-10). The phrase "as context requires" is significant: actor composition is a design variable, not a fixed list.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**The IASC (2010)** strategy establishes the institutional rationale for multi-stakeholder engagement at the system level: urban response is structurally dependent on the authority, knowledge, and legitimacy of non-humanitarian actors — particularly municipal authorities — in ways that rural or camp-based response is not (pp. 9-10). The strategy also introduces the principle that humanitarian response should strengthen rather than bypass existing governance systems (p. 9).

**Parker & Maynard (2015)** document multi-stakeholder engagement as an operational requirement emerging from case evidence: ABA requires "developing effective partnerships and capacity development with a larger range of actors at both strategic and operational levels such as mayors, municipalities, police forces and residents" (p. 12, citing Global CCCM Cluster, 2014). The case study of NRC Baghdad demonstrates multi-level actor coordination: NRC engaged at community and district level while UN-Habitat engaged at provincial and national policy level, using neighbourhood-level results to influence national policy (pp. 12-13). The humanitarian agency's primary operational role is framed as facilitator rather than service deliverer — "fostering cooperation between local stakeholders" (p. 18).

**IRC/IIED (2017)** operationalize multi-stakeholder engagement through an explicit stakeholder-centred analytical approach: "explicit focus on actor interests, influence, incentives, and relationships for realistic engagement and coordination" (pp. 8, 29, 31). This frames stakeholder analysis as a prerequisite for engagement design, not an output of it.

**Campbell (2019)** documents the most advanced expression of the concept in this source set: PCI's shift in the Barrio Mio programme from direct implementer to *convenor* — "facilitator of municipal/community/private-sector replication" (p. 37). This shift enabled indirect reach and partner-driven implementation at a scale unachievable through direct delivery. The case is the most fully developed illustration of what stakeholder engagement looks like when pursued beyond the project level.

**Sanderson & Sitko (2017)** state the structural rationale directly: "collaboration is not optional in humanitarian response because no single agency can cover all needs" (p. 19). This framing locates multi-stakeholder engagement not as a design preference but as a structural consequence of the scale and complexity of urban crises.

**IASC (2026)** refine the concept at the coordination architecture level: area-based coordination "emphasizes local actor engagement, community participation, and accountability to affected people" as constitutive features, not optional enhancements (p. 2). The shift from engagement as a programme activity to engagement as a coordination design requirement is the most recent formulation in the source set.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Facilitator vs. implementer role:** The most substantive tension in the literature concerns whether humanitarian agencies should function as direct implementers of services or as facilitators of local actor networks. Parker & Maynard (2015) frame facilitation as the ideal ABA role (pp. 12-13, 18); Campbell (2019) documents PCI's operational shift to convenor role as a key enabler of scale (p. 37). However, the conditions under which this shift is feasible — adequate local capacity, functioning governance, access — are not always present in acute emergency contexts. No source in this wiki resolves the conditions that determine which role is appropriate.

**Who leads:** The IASC (2010) establishes host-government leadership as a principle (p. 9). In practice, multiple sources note that municipal authority capacity, legitimacy, and political alignment vary substantially, making government leadership a context-dependent goal rather than a universal starting point. The IASC (2026) tempers this by framing local actor engagement as a coordination feature without specifying who has decision authority (p. 2).

**Depth of engagement across actor types:** The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) and IFRC (2017) define multi-stakeholder engagement at the level of presence and active involvement (pp. 9-10; pp. 31, 40-41). Campbell (2019) documents that genuine engagement requires relationship-building, alignment of timelines and methodologies, and shared learning across partners (p. 42) — conditions that take time to establish and that are frequently absent in short-cycle humanitarian programming.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Stakeholder mapping and actor relationship analysis must precede programme design — who is engaged determines programme legitimacy, feasibility, and sustainability (IRC/IIED, 2017, pp. 8, 29, 31; Parker & Maynard, 2015, p. 18).
- The humanitarian agency's role — implementer, facilitator, or convenor — should be made explicit at programme inception and aligned with local actor capacity and governance conditions (Campbell, 2019, p. 37; Parker & Maynard, 2015, pp. 12-13).
- Engagement must extend across programme phases, not only to consultation stages — IASC (2026) frames local actor engagement as a feature of coordination architecture, not a one-time event (p. 2).
- Government engagement should aim to strengthen rather than bypass existing governance systems; parallel humanitarian structures that replicate municipal functions create dependency and undermine sustainability (IASC, 2010, p. 9).
- Private sector and non-traditional partners should be included in stakeholder mapping — their roles in markets, land, service delivery, and community networks are frequently decisive for programme feasibility (IRC/IIED, 2017, pp. 29, 31; Campbell, 2019, p. 17).

## 5. Open questions

- Under what conditions should a humanitarian agency operate as convenor rather than implementer, and what minimum local governance capacity does that require?
- How should multi-stakeholder engagement be structured in contexts where local government is absent, contested, or complicit in the crisis?
- What does meaningful accountability to affected people look like within a multi-stakeholder coordination architecture, and how is it distinguished from accountability to government partners?
