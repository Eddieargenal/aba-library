<a id="definition"></a>
## Definition

**Neighbourhood scales** is the Young Foundation's observation that neighbourhoods
"can and do vary in size" and that large and small conceptions "can be considered
as complementary" — so a neighbourhood is best understood not as one unit but as
two nested scales serving different functions (Young Foundation 2010, p. 16):

- **Proximity neighbourhood (~100–1,500 people).** Streets or estate areas "where
  neighbourliness is personal, people know one another as individuals." This is the
  scale of social coordination and mutual aid. The source grounds the upper bound in
  anthropological evidence that "a human group finds it hard to coordinate to reach
  decisions if its population is above a range from 500 to 1,500," and notes that
  neighbourhood management "seems to work well when grounded in smaller-scale
  neighbourhoods" (p. 16).
- **Strategic neighbourhood (~5,000–10,000 people).** A unit "large enough to
  include a primary school, park and playground space, a doctor's surgery, library
  or leisure centre, and a number of shops and offices" — the scale at which a range
  of services sits "within walking distance of home" and where common issues
  "require a more strategic response" (p. 16).

The two are layered rather than rival: the source describes "a layered understanding
of neighbourhood, under which neighbourhoods of several thousand people bring
together several smaller areas," and positions the typology along an axis from
individual action and association (proximity) toward citizen empowerment and
governance (strategic) (pp. 15–16).

<a id="field-use"></a>
## Field Use

Use the dual-scale distinction to **match the unit to the function** rather than
forcing one boundary to do everything (Young Foundation 2010, pp. 15–16):

- **Pick proximity scale for coordination and mutual aid.** Functions that depend on
  people knowing each other — neighbourliness, informal mutual aid, day-to-day
  neighbourhood management, early collective action — should be designed at the
  100–1,500 band, below the 500–1,500 threshold where group decision-making starts
  to break down (p. 16).
- **Pick strategic scale for service infrastructure.** Where the question is shared
  services — a school, GP surgery, park, library, leisure centre — and the issues
  that span them, work at the 5,000–10,000 band, which the source ties to walking
  distance and to facility catchments. The source's "counting neighbourhood
  structures" box (p. 16) anchors this empirically: roughly one electoral ward per
  5,590 people, one GP practice per 5,580, one primary school per 3,300.
- **Run both scales in parallel.** Residents "may find themselves simultaneously
  located within a network of neighbourhoods of different scales and types" — a block
  committee, a neighbourhood-watch area, a school-gate network, a leisure-centre
  catchment (pp. 16–17). Design proximity and strategic units together rather than
  choosing one, nesting the smaller inside the larger.
- **Apply at area selection and coordination design.** The distinction is most useful
  when fixing the operating geography (`area-selection`) and when deciding which tier
  a given activity or governance body should sit at (`coordination-design`).

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

- **Collapsing both functions into one boundary.** Choosing a single scale forces a
  trade-off the source warns against: a proximity-sized unit cannot carry service
  infrastructure, and a strategic-sized unit is too large for the personal
  acquaintance that mutual aid relies on (p. 16).
- **Operating governance above the coordination threshold.** Building decision-making
  bodies at a population well above 500–1,500 risks the very coordination difficulty
  the anthropological evidence predicts; neighbourhood management is noted to work
  best at the smaller scale (p. 16).
- **Complexity of layered, overlapping units.** The source presents overlapping
  neighbourhoods of different scales as truer to lived reality, but elsewhere notes
  that governance, service delivery and data collection "require fixed, non-overlapping
  boundaries" (cf. source's known tensions, pp. 10–11, 16–17). Running two scales in
  parallel adds governance and data overhead that must be planned for, not assumed away.
- **Treating the figures as fixed thresholds.** The population bands are written with
  question marks in the source diagram (p. 15) and the wider document frames such
  proposals as hypotheses for testing; treat 100–1,500 and 5,000–10,000 as indicative
  ranges, not validated cut-offs.
