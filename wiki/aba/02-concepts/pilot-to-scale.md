> **Abstract.** Pilot-to-scale is the programme strategy of beginning with a small-scale demonstration intervention, generating evidence of effectiveness, and using that evidence to replicate or expand through local partners rather than through direct agency delivery. Two sources ground the concept: Campbell (2019) provides the most developed operational account in PCI's Barrio Mio programme; the Global Shelter Cluster (2019) documents the pilot-demonstrate-replicate pathway as a standard scale-up option in ABA practice. The concept is operationalizable — it implies specific programme design choices, partnership structures, and monitoring requirements — but the evidence that pilot-to-scale actually produces city-level change rather than a series of independent neighbourhood projects remains thin. This gap is identified by the Global Shelter Cluster (2019) as an open research question.

## 1. Definition

Pilot-to-scale is a programme strategy in which an intervention is first implemented at small scale in one or a few demonstration communities, evaluated for effectiveness and replicability, and then expanded through partner-driven replication rather than continued direct agency implementation. Campbell (2019) describes it as "start small, test, adapt with evidence, then replicate through local partners for wider reach" (pp. 42, 59).

The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) articulates the pathway as: "start with demonstrated neighbourhood-level results, then use those results as evidence to influence city-level policy and resource allocation" (p. 13). The distinction from simple programme expansion is that replication is designed to be partner-driven and policy-linked — the goal is institutional change at city or district level, not continued agency-managed geographic rollout.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Campbell (2019)** provides the most operationally developed case evidence in this source set. PCI's Barrio Mio programme in Guatemala City was explicitly designed as a strategy, not a project — the purpose was to generate demonstration evidence that could influence municipal policy and enable replication across other informal settlements (p. 17). PCI's "demonstrate-and-replicate operational model across phased implementation" (p. 17) involved: selecting demonstration communities, implementing integrated multi-sector interventions, documenting results, and transitioning the facilitation role to municipal and community partners for wider replication. The shift from direct implementer to convenor was the operational mechanism that made replication achievable at scale (p. 37).

The case also identifies the key enabling conditions: donor-permitted flexibility, an organizational culture willing to tolerate iterative adaptation (pp. 42, 56), and relationship-building with municipal authorities sufficient to support institutional uptake (p. 17). Monitoring limitations are documented honestly: "monitoring frameworks struggled to capture full integrated effects where outcomes extended beyond pre-set indicators" (p. 60).

**Global Shelter Cluster (2019)** positions the pilot-demonstrate-replicate model as one of the primary pathways for addressing the multi-scale linkage challenge — the persistent gap between neighbourhood-level ABA results and city-level policy or resource impact (p. 13). The paper also calls for more research on this pathway: "what evidence exists that pilot-demonstrate-replicate pathways actually produce city-level change, as opposed to a series of independent neighbourhood projects?" is treated as an open empirical question.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Replication vs. scaling from inception.** The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) frames pilot-demonstrate-replicate as a scale-up pathway after demonstration (p. 13). Sanderson & Sitko (2017) argue, by contrast, that scale-up planning should be built in from programme inception rather than added as a post-demonstration phase (pp. 25-27). These are not mutually exclusive — scale-up design from inception is compatible with beginning implementation at pilot scale — but they imply different programme design logics, funding instruments, and organizational capabilities. Pilot-to-scale with ex post replication planning is the documented practice; scale-from-inception is the normative aspiration.

**Whether replication produces city-level change.** The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) explicitly identifies this as an unresolved empirical question (p. 13). Campbell (2019) provides one positive case — Barrio Mio's influence on municipal policy in Guatemala City (p. 17) — but also acknowledges the limits of its generalizability, particularly to settings with weak or non-functioning governance (p. 58).

**Measurement.** Campbell (2019) identifies that standard output-based monitoring frameworks are poorly designed to capture the systemic and relational outcomes that define successful pilot-to-scale implementation — partner adoption, policy change, institutional capacity — rather than direct service delivery (p. 60).

## 4. Implications for practice

- Scale-up strategy — including which partners will replicate, what institutional change is required, and what evidence will trigger replication — should be specified at programme design, not as a post-implementation addition (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, pp. 25-27; Campbell, 2019, p. 17).
- The transition from direct implementer to facilitator or convenor role is the operational mechanism for pilot-to-scale; agencies that maintain direct delivery throughout cannot achieve replication scale (Campbell, 2019, p. 37).
- Monitoring frameworks must be designed to capture partner adoption, policy influence, and institutional change alongside output metrics; standard humanitarian logframes will systematically under-represent the outcomes that define successful scaling (Campbell, 2019, p. 60).
- Municipal and institutional relationships must be established during the pilot phase, not built after demonstration — the policy influence pathway requires sustained engagement before evidence is available (Campbell, 2019, p. 17).

## 5. Open questions

- What evidence exists that pilot-to-scale pathways produce city-level policy or resource change, rather than a collection of independent replication projects?
- What minimum municipal governance capacity is required for neighbourhood-level ABA demonstration results to translate into city-level institutional change?
- How should replication fidelity be defined — what must be preserved and what can be adapted by partners without invalidating the demonstration logic?
