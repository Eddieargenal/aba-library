# Definition

The deliberate selection of specific geographic areas for humanitarian response based on need, vulnerability, concentration, and feasibility criteria, as distinct from population-based or institution-based targeting approaches.

---

# Why It Matters for Urban DRR and Response

Appropriate geographic targeting ensures resources reach the most vulnerable areas while avoiding duplication and maintaining operational coherence. Poor targeting wastes resources and excludes the most affected populations. In urban contexts, geographic targeting must be based on evidence — not operational convenience, administrative assignment, or historical presence.

---

# Evidence Base

## Why Geography Is the Entry Point in ABA (Parker & Maynard 2015, pp.8-10)

> "The single most defining characteristic was that an area-based approach 'defines an area, rather than a sector or target group, as the main entry point'." — Parker & Maynard (2015, p.8), citing IRC (2015)

Geographic targeting in ABA means:
- The area's problems (not a sector or beneficiary category) define the programme scope
- All residents in the target area are potential stakeholders, not just those meeting individual vulnerability criteria
- This inclusive approach reduces tensions between displaced and host populations — NRC Baghdad: "support for all the residents in the settlements reduced tensions and increased acceptance of NRC's approach"

## Two Boundary Types (Parker & Maynard 2015, p.9)

**Administrative boundaries** — defined by existing government administrative units (barangays, districts, sub-districts):
- Preferred when it creates greatest opportunity for partnering with local government
- Strengthens community-government links and supports exit planning
- Risk: administrative boundaries may not reflect actual vulnerability distribution

**Physical boundaries** — defined by natural or built features that act as barriers (main roads, rivers, steep terrain):
- More appropriate when physical features also reinforce social identity and community cohesion
- Example: Katye programme in Ravine Pintade bounded by three main roads and steep ravine slope
- Risk: may split communities or create awkward operational areas

**In practice:** boundary definition must combine administrative or physical boundaries with social analysis to support participatory processes.

## Scale Question (Parker & Maynard 2015, p.10)

- Area need not mean "neighbourhood" — scale should be defined by the problem being targeted
- Range: a small cluster of households to a city-wide intervention
- In practice: case studies reviewed operated at neighbourhood/district level with ~1,400–6,000+ households
- City-wide ABA is possible but raises additional considerations not well-evidenced in the literature

## Inclusive vs. Selective Targeting Within the Area (Parker & Maynard 2015, p.9)

Geographic targeting can be inclusive (all residents in the area) or selective (identified households within the area). Both are documented:
- NRC Baghdad: inclusive — targeted the whole settlement regardless of IDP status
- KASS Kabul, CRS Tacloban: selective household identification within defined geographic zones

Inclusive approach is recommended in conflict/displacement settings to avoid reinforcing inequalities between displaced and host populations.

# Practical Implications for Field Teams

## Area Selection Criteria

Before drawing a boundary, confirm:
1. Needs are geographically concentrated (>40% of most-affected population in one area)
2. The area's problems are addressable within its boundaries
3. Boundary logic is documented (administrative, physical, or social — and why)
4. The area is not selected because it is operationally convenient or already covered by the programme

## Boundary Drawing Process

From Parker & Maynard (2015) case studies:
1. Start with available administrative boundaries as a baseline
2. Check for physical features that reinforce or cut across those boundaries
3. Conduct social mapping to identify community identity and cohesion patterns
4. Test draft boundaries with community leaders before finalising
5. Document the rationale — this will be needed for coordination, donor reporting, and exit planning

## Cross-Boundary Risks

- Targeting one area creates perceived inequity in adjacent areas with similar needs
- "Bandwagon effect": agencies cluster in targeted area, leaving adjacent areas unserved
- Mitigation: "blur the line" by extending some projects just beyond the formal boundary; locate shared facilities (health centres) on the border to encourage cross-usage

# Common Mistakes

1. **Selecting area by operational convenience** — "We already have a field office there"
2. **Using administrative boundaries without vulnerability analysis** — administrative zones rarely align with actual need concentration
3. **Ignoring physical features** — boundaries that cut through natural community spaces create implementation problems
4. **Skipping social boundary validation** — drawing lines without community input leads to misidentification of community identities and exclusion of sub-groups
5. **Not documenting boundary rationale** — undocumented boundaries cannot be defended in coordination, audits, or exit planning

## Contested dimensions

**Administrative vs. social boundary legitimacy:** Administrative boundaries are operationally convenient and support government partnership, but Allen (2018) documents that "resident-defined neighbourhood boundaries frequently diverge from administrative boundaries" (pp. 37, 53). Boundaries drawn on administrative logic without social validation risk misidentifying community identities and excluding sub-groups whose social geography crosses or is subdivided by administrative lines.

**Inclusive vs. selective targeting within the area:** Parker & Maynard (2015) document both approaches — NRC Baghdad used inclusive whole-settlement targeting regardless of displacement status (p. 9), while KASS Kabul and CRS Tacloban used selective household identification within defined geographic zones (p. 9). Neither is inherently superior; the choice depends on whether the primary risk is exclusion-driven tension or resource misallocation.

**Scale ambiguity:** The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) notes that ABA scale can range from neighbourhood to city level, each with different staffing, process, and coordination implications (p. 11). The appropriate scale is not derivable from the concept alone and requires context-specific decision-making.
