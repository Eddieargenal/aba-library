> **Abstract.** Disaster risk reduction (DRR) is the systematic approach to identifying, assessing, and reducing the risks of disaster. Three sources in this wiki ground the concept, representing two consecutive global policy frameworks and a community-level operational guide. Twigg (2009) defines DRR in relation to the Hyogo Framework for Action (HFA 2005-2015) and develops its most operationally detailed community-level application. UNDRR (2015) supersedes HFA with the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030, which broadens DRR scope, formalizes four priorities and seven global targets, and embeds Build Back Better as a core post-disaster function. UNDRR (2017) operationalizes the Sendai Framework at city level through the Ten Essentials and a resilience-building cycle for local government. The definitions are compatible and sequential rather than contradictory; the evolution from Hyogo to Sendai shifted DRR's emphasis from disaster management toward proactive risk governance and integrated resilience investment.

## 1. Definition

Disaster risk reduction is "a systematic approach to identifying, assessing, and reducing disaster risk across development and relief work" that "should be integrated, not treated as an add-on" to wider development and humanitarian programming (Twigg, 2009, p. 8).

The Sendai Framework (UNDRR, 2015) frames the DRR goal as: "prevent new and reduce existing disaster risk through integrated and inclusive measures that reduce exposure and vulnerability and strengthen preparedness and recovery capacity" (p. 11). The scope encompasses natural and human-made hazards — including environmental, technological, and biological risks — and applies across small, large, frequent, infrequent, sudden, and slow-onset events (UNDRR, 2015, p. 10). This broadened scope distinguishes Sendai from Hyogo, which was more narrowly oriented toward natural hazard disasters.

UNDRR (2017) operationalizes DRR at city scale: "DRR is framed as a core municipal development function, not a stand-alone emergency task" (pp. 7-8, 15). This framing positions DRR within urban governance rather than as a parallel humanitarian system.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Twigg (2009)** provides the most operationally detailed account of DRR at community level in this source set. The framework organizes DRR into five thematic areas — Governance; Risk Assessment; Knowledge and Education; Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction; and Disaster Preparedness and Response — with 28 components and 167 characteristics aligned to HFA priorities (pp. 6, 9, 28). The key operational insight is that DRR cannot be reduced to any single thematic area or component: "no single group or organization can address every aspect of DRR" (p. 8), and characteristics must be applied selectively and contextually rather than as a uniform checklist (pp. 7, 16, 18). A five-level milestone scale is proposed for tracking DRR maturity over time, from low awareness through to "a culture of safety" (p. 20).

**UNDRR (2015)** establishes the current global normative framework, superseding HFA. Four priorities structure implementation: (1) understanding disaster risk; (2) strengthening disaster risk governance; (3) investing in disaster risk reduction for resilience; and (4) enhancing disaster preparedness and applying Build Back Better in recovery, rehabilitation, and reconstruction (p. 13). Seven global targets provide measurable accountability benchmarks through 2030, including targets on mortality, affected people, economic losses, critical infrastructure damage, strategy coverage, international cooperation, and early warning access (p. 11). The guiding governance principle is shared responsibility: "States hold primary responsibility, with shared responsibilities across sectors/stakeholders and all-of-society engagement" (p. 12).

**UNDRR (2017)** translates Sendai into a local government action framework. The Ten Essentials for Making Cities Resilient span governance, finance, risk assessment, land use, infrastructure, ecosystems, and preparedness — functioning as an organizing principle for reporting and monitoring progress at city scale (pp. 2, 5, 30). The resilience-building cycle (stakeholder mobilization → risk assessment → baseline/scorecard → action planning → financing/implementation → monitoring) provides an end-to-end operational sequence for municipal DRR (pp. 82-99).

## 3. Contested dimensions

**HFA to Sendai transition.** Twigg (2009) is explicitly aligned to HFA priorities and uses HFA's thematic structure as its organizing framework. The Sendai Framework (UNDRR, 2015) supersedes HFA with a broader scope and a more explicit shift from disaster management toward proactive risk governance. The two frameworks are broadly compatible but not identical in emphasis — Sendai formalizes Build Back Better and broadens hazard scope in ways not present in HFA. Programmes designed using Twigg's framework are HFA-aligned but may require updating to reflect Sendai's expanded scope and governance emphasis.

**Scale of application.** Twigg (2009) operates at community level; UNDRR (2017) at city/local government level; UNDRR (2015) at national and global level. None of the three sources specifies how DRR frameworks should be articulated across these scales simultaneously — which is precisely the challenge that multi-scale programming addresses. The frameworks are complementary rather than integrated.

**Implementation dependence on governance capacity.** UNDRR (2015) conditions implementation on "national circumstances, legal systems, capacities and available international support" (pp. 12-13). UNDRR (2017) makes local government capacity an explicit enabling condition (p. 84). Twigg (2009) documents that the enabling environment — wider institutional, policy, and socio-economic conditions — "shapes community-level resilience outcomes" (pp. 12-13). In settings where governance is fragmented, contested, or absent, the operationalization of DRR frameworks is constrained in ways that none of the three sources fully resolves.

## 4. Implications for practice

- DRR should be integrated into programme design from inception rather than added as a separate workstream or exit-phase activity; treating it as an add-on is the most common failure mode identified by Twigg (2009, p. 8).
- Programme design should reference the Sendai Framework's four priorities as the current normative baseline; HFA-aligned frameworks (including Twigg 2009) remain operationally useful but should be read in relation to Sendai's updated scope (UNDRR, 2015, p. 13).
- At city level, the Ten Essentials (UNDRR, 2017, pp. 5, 30) provide a structured municipal DRR architecture that can anchor ABA programme design within local government priorities rather than operating in parallel to them.
- DRR assessment should be multi-dimensional: hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and capacity must all be assessed; single-factor assessments produce systematically incomplete risk pictures (UNDRR, 2015, p. 13; Twigg, 2009, pp. 31-32).
- Build Back Better should be incorporated in programme design before disasters occur, not only after; UNDRR (2015) formalizes this as Priority 4, and UNDRR (2017) embeds it in the resilience-building cycle (pp. 20-22; 14-15).

## 5. Open questions

- How should DRR frameworks designed for government-led implementation be adapted for humanitarian ABA programmes operating in settings where municipal governance is absent or contested?
- Which Sendai Framework targets are most relevant for ABA monitoring frameworks, and how should neighbourhood-level contributions to city-level and national-level targets be tracked?
- How should Twigg's (2009) community-level characteristics framework be updated to reflect Sendai's broader scope, including human-made hazards and climate-linked risks?
