> **Abstract.** Urban context analysis is the systematic examination of the political, economic, social, service/infrastructure, and spatial dynamics within an urban area that shape humanitarian programming effectiveness. Two sources ground the concept: IRC/IIED (2017) provides the primary operationalizable account through a structured 10-step toolkit; Sanderson (2019) confirms context analysis as a programme-cycle prerequisite for urban response. Both sources distinguish context analysis from needs assessment: it examines *why* conditions exist (system dynamics, actor interests, power relationships) rather than *what* needs exist. The concept is directly operationalizable — IRC/IIED (2017) provides an end-to-end methodology — but the toolkit's resource and time assumptions may not align with acute emergency deployment conditions.

## 1. Definition

Urban context analysis is the structured investigation of political, governance, social, cultural, economic, service/infrastructure, and spatial/settlement dynamics that influence the feasibility, effectiveness, and risks of humanitarian programming in a specific urban area (IRC/IIED, 2017, pp. 8, 17-19).

IRC/IIED (2017) explicitly distinguish it from needs assessment: "context analysis focuses on 'why' and systems dynamics, while needs assessment focuses on 'what' needs and gaps" (pp. 8-9). Context analysis is a complement to, not a replacement for, sector or vulnerability assessments — it addresses "the kind of contextual factors which can enable or hinder response effectiveness" (p. 8).

Sanderson (2019) confirms this framing: context analysis is part of the programme-cycle toolchain for urban response, positioned before design to inform targeting, partnership, and risk assessment (pp. 107-110).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**IRC/IIED (2017)** provides the most operationally developed account in this source set. The toolkit is structured as a 10-step, 3-phase process: preparation (scoping, desk review, team), data collection (stakeholder analysis, KIIs/FGDs, debrief), and analysis/documentation (key findings, programme implications, validation, report) (pp. 10-11). The analytical framework uses a five-domain thematic model — politics/governance, social/cultural, economic, service/infrastructure, and space/settlements — with gender and Do No Harm as cross-cutting lenses (pp. 17-19). This domain structure aligns with Campbell's (2016) urban systems typology, reinforcing a multi-domain analytical requirement.

The toolkit's key claim: analysis is "intentionally user friendly, relatively quick to use, and adaptable" for field deployment (pp. 3, 8-9), designed for completion in approximately two weeks of in-country data collection. It also specifies a shelf life: "findings should be revisited and may need updating within months" in dynamic crisis contexts (p. 33).

The stakeholder analysis component is particularly operationalized: Tool 3 maps actor interests, incentives, influence, and relationships — not just actor presence — to inform realistic engagement and coordination strategy (pp. 8, 29, 31).

**Sanderson (2019)** organizes a typology of context analysis approaches for urban settings, including stakeholder, market, governance, and conflict analysis (pp. 107-110), and confirms that urban M&E systems must account for complexity and rapidly changing assumptions (pp. 138-143) — which depends on having an adequate initial context analysis to establish what assumptions are being tracked.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Scope and depth vs. field constraints.** IRC/IIED (2017) positions the toolkit as "relatively quick" but estimates two weeks of in-country data collection as a baseline (p. 9). In acute onset emergencies, this timeline may not be feasible. The toolkit itself acknowledges it "does not provide comprehensive step-by-step programme design guidance" (p. 25) — meaning field teams must supplement it with contextual judgment rather than treat it as a complete operational manual.

**Periodic updating.** IRC/IIED (2017) specifies that context analysis findings have a limited shelf life and should be updated "if major shifts occur or if analysis is older than ~6-9 months" (p. 33). In practice, programme cycles and resource constraints rarely allow for systematic reassessment at this frequency. No source specifies what minimum updating is adequate to avoid materially inaccurate programming assumptions.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Context analysis should be conducted before programme design, not derived from it — the purpose is to surface system dynamics and actor relationships that would otherwise remain invisible until mid-implementation (IRC/IIED, 2017, pp. 8-9).
- The five-domain thematic framework (politics/governance, social/cultural, economic, service/infrastructure, space/settlements) should structure data collection and findings, not serve as a checklist — the goal is to understand interactions across domains, not to produce one finding per domain (IRC/IIED, 2017, pp. 17-19).
- Stakeholder analysis must address interests, incentives, and influence, not only actor presence; actor mapping without these dimensions produces coordination plans that fail on implementation (IRC/IIED, 2017, pp. 8, 29, 31).
- Context analysis findings should be dated and the currency explicitly tracked; assumptions based on outdated context analysis should be flagged in programme reviews (IRC/IIED, 2017, p. 33).
- In acute emergency conditions where two weeks is not feasible, a scoped rapid context analysis covering the highest-stakes domains (governance and stakeholders) is preferable to skipping context analysis entirely.

## 5. Open questions

- What is the minimum viable context analysis process for an acute onset emergency where standard two-week timelines are unavailable?
- How should context analysis findings be formally linked to adaptive management triggers — when does updated context information warrant programme revision?
- What analytical depth is required for the stakeholder component before coordination strategies built on it are reliable?
