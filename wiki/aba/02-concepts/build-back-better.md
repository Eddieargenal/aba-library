> **Abstract.** Build Back Better (BBB) is the principle that post-disaster recovery, rehabilitation, and reconstruction should reduce future disaster risk rather than restore pre-disaster conditions of vulnerability. Three sources ground the concept: UNDRR (2015) formalizes BBB as Sendai Framework Priority 4 and establishes it as a normative requirement; UNDRR (2017) operationalizes it at city level through resilience-building cycles for local government; World Bank/GFDRR (2013) frames it as a multi-pillar disaster risk management requirement. The concept is consistent across all three sources. The contested dimension is timing: UNDRR (2015) specifies that BBB planning should be integrated *before* disasters occur, not only afterward — a requirement that contradicts the common practice of treating BBB as a post-disaster policy aspiration.

## 1. Definition

Build Back Better is the practice of integrating disaster risk reduction into recovery, rehabilitation, and reconstruction to reduce future vulnerability rather than restoring pre-disaster conditions. UNDRR (2015) formalizes it as Sendai Framework Priority 4: "enhance disaster preparedness for effective response and to 'Build Back Better' in recovery, rehabilitation and reconstruction" (p. 13). The associated operational requirement is that BBB should "integrate DRR into recovery, rehabilitation and reconstruction planning *before* disasters and throughout post-disaster action" (pp. 20-22; emphasis added).

World Bank/GFDRR (2013) frames the same principle as a multi-pillar DRM requirement: recovery and reconstruction should use "rebuilding approaches that reduce future risk rather than restoring pre-disaster vulnerability" (p. 27).

UNDRR (2017) embeds BBB as a defining characteristic of a disaster-resilient city: "explicit build-back-better orientation within long-term urban development" is listed among the core characteristics of resilient city systems (pp. 14-15).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**UNDRR (2015)** establishes BBB as the most fully elaborated of the four Sendai priorities, specifying a range of national and local actions: integrating DRR into post-disaster recovery plans; using recovery to improve land-use and construction standards; building the resilience of health and education facilities; and linking recovery planning to long-term sustainable development (pp. 20-22). The normative innovation in Sendai is the pre-disaster planning requirement: BBB should not be improvised in the chaos of post-disaster response but planned and resourced in advance.

**World Bank/GFDRR (2013)** provides the most theoretically grounded account of why pre-disaster BBB planning matters. The paper argues that "traditional cost-benefit analysis does not work well when dealing with catastrophic tail risk" (p. 11), which means that post-disaster reconstruction decisions made under time and resource pressure are systematically biased toward restoring what existed rather than building what would reduce risk. Pre-arranged recovery frameworks — including land-use standards, construction guidelines, and risk transfer mechanisms — reduce this bias by establishing decision rules before the post-disaster window opens (p. 27).

**UNDRR (2017)** translates BBB into local government practice through the resilience-building cycle. Phase 6 (monitoring and follow-up) explicitly tracks whether BBB principles are embedded in post-event reconstruction (p. 99). The City Disaster Resilience Scorecard includes BBB as one of ten Essential areas for city-level assessment (p. 91).

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Pre-disaster vs. post-disaster application.** All three sources endorse BBB as a pre-disaster planning requirement. In practice, most agencies treat BBB as a post-disaster design principle applied during reconstruction. This gap between normative requirement and operational practice is not resolved in the source set — sources assert the requirement without specifying how to resource or institutionalize pre-disaster BBB planning within programme cycles funded event-by-event.

**Scope ambiguity.** The Sendai Framework applies BBB to all four recovery phases — rehabilitation, recovery, reconstruction, and post-disaster development (UNDRR, 2015, pp. 20-22). In ABA practice, the concept is most commonly applied to physical reconstruction. Its logical extension covers livelihood restoration, governance strengthening, and social recovery — all of which can either restore or reduce pre-disaster vulnerability patterns. No source specifies where physical reconstruction ends and the broader BBB agenda begins.

## 4. Implications for practice

- BBB provisions — standards, land-use commitments, reconstruction protocols — should be agreed before disasters occur and included in preparedness planning, not designed during the post-disaster response period (UNDRR, 2015, pp. 20-22; World Bank/GFDRR, 2013, p. 27).
- Recovery and reconstruction programmes should explicitly assess whether proposed interventions restore pre-disaster vulnerability or reduce it; rebuilding damaged but hazard-exposed infrastructure to its previous location and standard is not BBB (UNDRR, 2015, pp. 20-22).
- BBB applies to governance and livelihood systems as well as physical infrastructure — reconstruction of market systems, community institutions, or local governance capacity can either entrench or reduce pre-existing vulnerability (World Bank/GFDRR, 2013, p. 27; UNDRR, 2017, pp. 14-15).
- City-level BBB performance should be tracked through the Scorecard or equivalent mechanism, not evaluated only through project-level reconstruction outputs (UNDRR, 2017, p. 91).

## 5. Open questions

- How should BBB planning be resourced and institutionalized within programme cycles that are funded event-by-event and therefore cannot plan for pre-disaster contingencies?
- In informal settlement contexts where pre-disaster land use and construction were themselves hazardous, what constitutes BBB when restoration of pre-disaster conditions would perpetuate risk?
- How should BBB trade-offs between speed and standards be negotiated when affected communities prioritize rapid return to shelter over relocation to safer ground?
