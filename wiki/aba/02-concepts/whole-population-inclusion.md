> **Abstract.** Whole-population inclusion is the design principle that area-based programming should target all people residing within a defined geographic area — regardless of displacement status, legal category, or administrative registration — rather than selecting beneficiaries by individual criteria within the area. Two sources ground the concept consistently. The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) defines it as a core ABA characteristic; Parker & Maynard (2015) document its operational effects in case studies. The concept is directly operationalizable: whole-population inclusion is a binary programme design decision with documented consequences for social cohesion, targeting efficiency, and programme legitimacy.

## 1. Definition

Whole-population inclusion is the practice of targeting all residents within a defined program area as potential stakeholders and service recipients, without applying additional individual or household selection criteria based on displacement status, legal category, or vulnerability tier (Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, p. 9; Parker & Maynard, 2015, p. 9).

The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) defines this as an explicit ABA characteristic: the approach "considers the whole population in an affected area, especially where needs are complex and interrelated... including all affected groups in target areas regardless of legal status or category, while also recognizing long-term residents" (p. 9). This encompasses both displaced and host populations, and both formal and informal residents.

Parker & Maynard (2015) document the NRC Baghdad programme as the primary case illustration: NRC "targeted the whole settlement regardless of IDP status," with the result that "support for all the residents in the settlements reduced tensions and increased acceptance of NRC's approach" (p. 9).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Global Shelter Cluster (2019)** grounds whole-population inclusion as one of four core ABA definitional characteristics, alongside geographic targeting, multi-sectorality, and multi-stakeholder participation (p. 9). The rationale is that in urban settings with complex, interrelated needs, needs are spatially concentrated in ways that align with area residence rather than individual registration status. Applying individual or categorical selection criteria within an area-based programme contradicts the geographic-entry-point logic that defines ABA.

**Parker & Maynard (2015)** distinguish between inclusive targeting (all residents in area, regardless of status) and selective household identification within defined geographic zones — documenting both as valid ABA variants (p. 9). KASS Kabul and CRS Tacloban used selective household identification within the area; NRC Baghdad used whole-settlement inclusive targeting. The paper does not recommend one approach universally, but the NRC Baghdad finding — that inclusive targeting reduced IDP/host tensions — is the most operationally documented consequence of the choice in the source set.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Inclusive vs. selective targeting within an area.** Parker & Maynard (2015) explicitly document both approaches as observed in ABA practice (p. 9). Neither is inherently superior: inclusive targeting reduces exclusion-driven tensions between displaced and host populations but may allocate resources to people with lower need; selective targeting concentrates resources on the most vulnerable but risks generating tensions and undermining the area-based logic if selection criteria create visible in-group/out-group divisions within the programme area. The choice is a context-dependent design decision.

**Whole-population inclusion vs. resource constraints.** The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) positions inclusive targeting as a characteristic but also notes that ABA is best suited to "areas with concentrated, complex multi-sector needs" (p. 10) and requires "sufficient time, funding, and urban-specific expertise" (p. 13). In practice, coverage of an entire settlement population requires commensurate resource allocation that may not align with programme budgets.

## 4. Implications for practice

- The decision to use inclusive whole-population targeting vs. selective household identification within the area should be made explicitly at programme design and documented with contextual rationale; the choice has measurable consequences for social cohesion and programme legitimacy (Parker & Maynard, 2015, p. 9).
- In conflict and displacement settings where IDP/host tensions are present or anticipated, inclusive targeting is the documented risk-mitigation approach (Parker & Maynard, 2015, p. 9).
- Programme budgets must be sized to cover the intended target population; committing to whole-population inclusion without corresponding resource allocation creates gaps that erode trust and legitimacy (Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, p. 13).

## 5. Open questions

- Under what conditions does selective household targeting within an area produce the same social cohesion outcomes as whole-population inclusive targeting?
- How should programmes handle eligibility tensions when whole-population inclusion implies services for residents with substantially different need levels?
