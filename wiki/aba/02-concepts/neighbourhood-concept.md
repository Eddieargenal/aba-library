> **Abstract.** The neighbourhood concept is contested at its definitional core. Three sources dedicated to defining and reviewing the concept arrive at three different typologies, none of which supersedes the others. The Young Foundation (2010) contrasts administrative-geographic and socially experienced neighbourhoods, introducing "local public realm" as a bridging construct. Allen (2018) organizes the literature into spatial, social, and socio-spatial categories, finding that resident-defined boundaries systematically diverge from administrative ones. Baffoe (2019) frames neighbourhood as variably construed as place, community, and policy unit, each with distinct analytical implications. The debate is the content: no single definition can be imported wholesale into area-based programming. The concept page's job is to present the typologies clearly so practitioners can make explicit definitional choices rather than defaulting to operationally convenient boundaries.

## 1. Definition

Neighbourhood has no single agreed definition across urban planning, social science, or humanitarian practice. The term designates a sub-city geographic and social unit, but the criteria used to delineate it — physical features, administrative boundaries, social networks, residents' cognitive maps — produce different and often conflicting units (Young Foundation, 2010, p. 9; Allen, 2018, p. 59; Baffoe, 2019, pp. 1-2).

Three typological frameworks structure the debate in this source set:

**Young Foundation (2010)** contrasts two dominant definitional models: (1) *administrative geography* — wards, census zones, statistical output areas — and (2) *personal identification* — socially experienced neighbourhoods produced through mental maps, social ties, and patterned local interaction (pp. 10-11). The paper proposes a bridging concept, the *local public realm* — the ensemble of local spaces, services, and civic meeting points that anchor neighbourhood experience regardless of administrative designation (pp. 13-14, 17). It also notes that neighbourhoods are *layered and overlapping*: residents participate in multiple neighbourhood scales simultaneously, not a single fixed unit (pp. 16-17).

**Allen (2018)** organizes the broader research literature into three streams: *spatial* neighbourhoods (defined by physical boundaries, land use, and proximity), *social* neighbourhoods (defined by social networks, interaction patterns, and community identity), and *socio-spatial* combinations of both (p. 59). The key empirical finding is that resident-defined neighbourhood boundaries frequently diverge from administrative boundaries (pp. 37, 53) — a divergence that is not noise but reflects genuine differences in how populations organize their social and economic lives.

**Baffoe (2019)** traces the conceptual evolution from planning and Chicago School roots and proposes that neighbourhood is variably useful as: (1) *place* — locale, lived experience, identity formation; (2) *community* — a more formalized social unit used for governance or administration; and (3) *policy unit* — operational scale for decentralization, participation, resource targeting, and local interventions (pp. 4-5). He argues that definitions should reflect research or policy purpose rather than being imported from a single theoretical tradition (pp. 2, 7).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Young Foundation (2010)** provides the most operationally developed account in this source set. The paper is explicit that "there is no consensus answer to the question, 'What is a neighbourhood?'" (p. 9) and that administrative statistical geographies may have "not a direct" relationship to lived neighbourhood experience (p. 11). The local public realm concept — shared spaces, service nodes, civic meeting points — is proposed as an empirically identifiable feature that bridges the administrative/experienced divide and can be mapped in field settings (pp. 14, 17). The paper also documents that neighbourhood scales exist simultaneously at *proximity* level (small-scale social coordination) and *strategic* level (larger-scale services and governance), requiring layered-scale design rather than a single unit (pp. 15-16).

**Allen (2018)** synthesizes the research literature from urban studies, planning, and policy without privileging any single definition. The three-stream typology — spatial, social, socio-spatial — reflects how different disciplines have approached the concept (p. 59). The finding on boundary divergence is the most directly consequential for ABA practice: attempting to operationalize neighbourhood through administrative boundaries alone risks systematically misidentifying the social units within which residents actually coordinate, share resources, and mobilize (pp. 37, 53). Mixed socio-spatial methods combining subjective (survey, interview) and objective (GIS, mapping) data are described as the methodologically most defensible approach (p. 59).

**Baffoe (2019)** grounds the concept in planning theory and Chicago School scholarship, tracing how neighbourhood was first theorized as a planning ideal (Howard, Perry) and how sociologists subsequently expanded it to include social process, collective action, and spatial identity (pp. 2-3). The policy unit dimension is particularly relevant for ABA: neighbourhood-as-policy-unit supports decentralized governance and local participation, but it is also susceptible to reflecting administrative or political priorities rather than resident realities (p. 5). Baffoe argues that negotiated, context-sensitive definitions — developed with residents and planners together — improve both objectivity and social acceptability (p. 7).

## 3. Contested dimensions

**The three typologies are not interchangeable.** Young Foundation's administrative vs. experienced binary, Allen's spatial/social/socio-spatial distinction, and Baffoe's place/community/policy unit triad each foreground different aspects of the same problem and are suited to different analytical purposes. Using one framework's categories to answer another's questions produces confusion, not synthesis.

**Resident-defined vs. administrative boundaries.** Allen (2018) documents the divergence empirically; Baffoe (2019) and Young Foundation (2010) both acknowledge it conceptually. The practical consequence for ABA is that administrative boundaries — which are operationally convenient and support government partnership — may not align with the social geography within which residents organize, share risk, and mobilize collective action. The Young Foundation (2010) proposes the local public realm as a partial resolution, but this construct itself requires field-mapping and cannot be assumed from secondary data.

**Global north dominance in the theoretical base.** Baffoe (2019) explicitly flags that the seminal scholarship on neighbourhood is drawn from global north contexts, particularly the Chicago School's urban ecology tradition (p. 7). Data limitations, weak research infrastructure, and different urbanization trajectories in global south contexts make direct transfer of these typologies uncertain. Allen (2018) acknowledges this implicitly through New Zealand-focused policy application. Neither source provides a validated neighbourhood typology specifically developed for informal settlement contexts.

**Scale ambiguity.** Young Foundation (2010) notes that "neighbourhoods can and do vary in size" (p. 16) — from small clusters to district-level units — and that the appropriate scale depends on the problem being addressed. Allen (2018) and Baffoe (2019) do not resolve the scale question either. For ABA, this means neighbourhood scale is a design variable, not a given, and must be specified and justified per context.

## 4. Implications for practice

- The word "neighbourhood" should not be used in programme design or boundary documentation without specifying which definitional frame is being applied — administrative, lived/experiential, or policy unit — and why (Allen, 2018, p. 59; Baffoe, 2019, pp. 4-5).
- Boundary definition should combine administrative or physical baseline data with social mapping and resident validation before finalizing. Administrative convenience is not a sufficient justification (Young Foundation, 2010, pp. 10-11; Allen, 2018, pp. 37, 53).
- The local public realm — shared spaces, service nodes, civic meeting points — provides an empirically identifiable bridging construct that can be mapped in field settings and used to anchor boundary discussions across administrative and social frames (Young Foundation, 2010, pp. 14, 17).
- In global south informal settlement contexts, standard typologies from global north scholarship should be adapted rather than applied wholesale. Context-specific empirical investigation is preferable to importing a fixed definition (Baffoe, 2019, p. 7).
- Scale should be explicitly chosen and documented: proximity scale for social coordination functions, strategic scale for service and governance functions (Young Foundation, 2010, pp. 15-16).

## 5. Open questions

- Which neighbourhood typology (place, community, or policy unit) produces the most operationally valid unit for ABA geographic targeting in informal urban settlements in crisis contexts?
- How should boundary definition be conducted when administrative records are absent or inaccurate, resident self-organization is informal, and field access is constrained?
- Does the local public realm concept translate to contexts where public space is contested, controlled by armed actors, or physically destroyed?
