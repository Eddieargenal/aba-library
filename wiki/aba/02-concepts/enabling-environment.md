<a id="definition"></a>
## Definition

The **enabling environment** is the wider set of institutional, policy and socio-economic factors that support community-level resilience (Twigg 2009, p. 12). Twigg's framework specifies enabling-environment characteristics in parallel with each Component of Resilience, across local, national, and international levels (Twigg 2009, Section 6, pp. 28–44).

Communities are **dependent on external duty bearers**: many features of an ideal enabling environment are missing in practice, and the absence of key components can create a *"disabling"* environment for local initiatives (Twigg 2009, p. 12).

Related concept: [[resilience]].

<a id="field-use"></a>
## Field Use

- Assess the enabling-environment characteristics **alongside** the resilience characteristics in baselines, VCA, and advocacy, to identify which external policy and institutional supports are present or missing (Twigg 2009, Section 6, pp. 28–44).
- Treat the enabling environment as **integral to community work, not a separate or optional add-on** — "the Enabling Environment is not separate from community work" (Twigg 2009, p. 12). It links community-level effort to higher (municipal/national) levels.

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

- **"Separate column" misreading.** Because the framework presents the enabling environment in a separate column from the resilience characteristics, users may treat it as secondary or optional when it is integral (Twigg 2009, p. 12). See [[enabling-environment-not-separate]] (known tension).
- **Single-source + urban gap.** This page rests on one source (Twigg 2009), written for generic/rural communities; applying the enabling-environment lens to dense, informally-governed urban settings requires adaptation, and external duty bearers cannot be assumed present or accessible (Twigg 2009, urban-applicability assessment).
