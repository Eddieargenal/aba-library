> **Abstract.** Enabling environment refers to the wider institutional, policy, and socio-economic conditions that shape what is achievable at community or programme level — the structural context within which resilience outcomes and DRR gains can or cannot be sustained. A single source grounds the concept in this wiki: Twigg (2009) introduces the enabling environment as a distinct analytical category within the disaster-resilient community framework, operating alongside community-level characteristics rather than as a sum of them. This is a seed-level concept page. It should be enriched when additional sources with explicit enabling environment analysis — particularly governance and policy frameworks — are ingested.

## 1. Definition

The enabling environment comprises the "wider institutional, policy, and socio-economic conditions that shape community-level resilience outcomes" (Twigg, 2009, pp. 12-13). It is analytically distinct from community characteristics — the internal capacities, knowledge, structures, and practices of a given community — because it refers to conditions that communities cannot change through their own action alone and that primarily depend on external institutional actors (governments, donors, legal systems, markets).

In Twigg's (2009) framework, enabling environment factors appear alongside each of the five thematic areas — Governance; Risk Assessment; Knowledge and Education; Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction; and Disaster Preparedness and Response — as the structural conditions that either support or constrain community progress within each area (p. 11). The implication is that community-level characteristics can be improved through programme action, but enabling environment conditions require policy, governance, or institutional change that is frequently outside programme scope.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Twigg (2009)** is the sole source in this wiki that explicitly frames enabling environment as a distinct analytical category. The framework's structure makes the enabling environment analytically separable from community characteristics: for each of the 28 components within the five thematic areas, the framework identifies both community-level characteristics and their corresponding enabling environment conditions (p. 11). This separation has practical consequences for programme design: interventions that address community characteristics without adequate enabling environment conditions will produce fragile, reversible gains. Conversely, enabling environment improvements that are not matched by community-level capacity development will remain underutilized.

The framework cautions that "resilience is an ideal rather than a fully attainable state" partly because enabling environment conditions are often beyond community or programme control (p. 7). The five-level milestone scale is designed to track progress without assuming that full enabling environment conditions are achievable within a programme cycle (p. 20).

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Programme scope vs. enabling environment change.** The enabling environment concept implicitly raises a scope question that Twigg (2009) does not resolve: when should ABA or DRR programmes attempt to influence enabling environment conditions (through advocacy, policy engagement, or government capacity development) versus treat them as fixed constraints? The answer depends on programme mandate, timeline, and the leverage available through existing government relationships — none of which Twigg specifies.

**Thin evidence base.** With a single source grounding this concept, the current wiki cannot adequately characterize enabling environment conditions across different governance contexts, crisis phases, or programme types. The concept appears in multiple ABA and DRR sources without being named — Parker & Maynard's (2015) reference to "vertical integration to city/regional plans" and Campbell's (2016) urban systems analysis both engage with enabling environment dynamics without using the term. Future source development should surface these implicit enabling environment analyses.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Programme feasibility assessment should include an enabling environment analysis: what institutional, policy, and socio-economic conditions are required for programme outcomes to be sustainable, and are those conditions present, absent, or improvable within programme scope? (Twigg, 2009, pp. 12-13).
- Programmes designed without enabling environment analysis may achieve community-level changes that are reversed when programme support ends; exit planning must address whether the enabling environment will sustain gains (Twigg, 2009, pp. 12-13).
- Where enabling environment conditions are inadequate, policy advocacy or government capacity development should be part of the programme strategy, not treated as out of scope (Twigg, 2009, p. 26).

## 5. Open questions

- Which enabling environment conditions are most consistently decisive for ABA programme sustainability across different governance and crisis contexts?
- How should enabling environment analysis be structured within ABA feasibility assessments, and what evidence is required to assess it adequately in the field?
- How should programmes balance investment in community-level characteristics vs. enabling environment change when both are inadequate?

> **Source development note.** The enabling environment concept is implicitly present in multiple ABA and DRR sources beyond Twigg (2009) but is not consistently named or analyzed as such. Priority enrichment: Campbell (2016) urban systems analysis; Parker & Maynard (2015) vertical integration; IASC (2010) governance objective.
