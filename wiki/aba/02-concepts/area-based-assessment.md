> **Abstract.** Area-based assessment is the methodological approach to data preparation, collection, analysis, and interpretation that supports area-based programming. A single primary source grounds the concept operationally: REACH/UNHCR (2019) provides a structured four-section toolkit for conducting area-based assessments in out-of-camp displacement contexts using key informants, with optional social network analysis for informant selection. This concept page is seed-level — one dedicated methodological source — and should be enriched as additional assessment methodology sources are ingested. The central operational insight from REACH/UNHCR (2019) is that "proper area definition is a prerequisite for KI data quality; poor boundary definition can produce misleading findings" (p. 32).

## 1. Definition

Area-based assessment is "the data preparation, collection, analysis, and interpretation components that support area-based programming" (REACH/UNHCR, 2019, p. 5 glossary). It is distinguished from household needs assessment by its geographic unit of analysis: the area — defined by boundary, community, and service geography — is the organizing frame for data collection and interpretation, rather than individual household eligibility criteria.

REACH/UNHCR (2019) operationalize it through a cumulative four-section process: (1) area identification using Mapping Focus Group Discussions (MFGDs) for participatory boundary definition and validation; (2) key informant (KI) selection by topic-specific profile; (3) optional social network analysis to improve KI selection; and (4) multisectoral KI data collection and interpretation (pp. 7, 13-14, 49-50).

The approach is designed for out-of-camp urban and peri-urban displacement contexts where populations are dispersed, mobile, and not captured in camp registration systems (pp. 7-9).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**REACH/UNHCR (2019)** is the dedicated methodological source for this concept. Three design principles structure the toolkit:

First, *area definition precedes data collection*: "proper area definition is a prerequisite for KI data quality; poor boundary definition can produce misleading findings" (p. 32). The MFGD process for participatory boundary definition and validation is the operational mechanism for this — community participants map both *community areas* (social geography) and *service areas* (administrative or functional geography) as distinct inputs to boundary decisions (pp. 15-17).

Second, *KIs are topic-specific, not general-purpose proxies*: "KI accuracy improves when KIs are selected for topic-specific expertise, not as general multisector proxies" (pp. 33-34). This means that a single well-connected community leader is not an adequate key informant for multisectoral data collection — different informants should be recruited for different sectors.

Third, *the assessment is cumulative*: "each step builds on prior outputs... not isolated rounds" (pp. 49-50). KI data is triangulated with secondary data, community data, and other assessment sources to build a layered, defensible picture of area conditions.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**KI methods vs. household surveys.** REACH/UNHCR (2019) is explicit that "KI data should complement, not replace, household needs assessments for many information types" (p. 46). Some question types — particularly normative and perception-trend questions — are poorly suited to KI methods (p. 46). The toolkit is designed for settings where full household surveys are not feasible; it does not claim equivalence with household-level data.

**Social network analysis complexity.** The optional SNA-informed KI selection workflow "adds complexity and requires additional technical capacity and resources" (pp. 38-39, 50). Field teams without SNA training should use the standard KI selection process rather than attempting SNA-informed selection without appropriate capacity.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Area boundary definition using participatory methods (MFGDs) should precede KI selection and data collection, not follow it; the boundary determines what the data describes (REACH/UNHCR, 2019, p. 32).
- KIs should be selected by topic, not as general community representatives; a multisectoral assessment requires different KIs for different sectors (REACH/UNHCR, 2019, pp. 33-34).
- Community areas and service areas should be mapped separately — they frequently diverge, and conflating them produces assessment findings that misrepresent either social or service geography (REACH/UNHCR, 2019, pp. 15-17).
- Triangulation across data sources is required for defensible findings; KI data alone is insufficient for programme design decisions (REACH/UNHCR, 2019, pp. 49-50).

## 5. Open questions

- How should area-based assessment methodology be adapted when MFGDs are not feasible due to access, security, or population dispersal constraints?
- What data quality thresholds distinguish adequate KI-based area-level findings from findings that require household survey verification before programme design decisions?
