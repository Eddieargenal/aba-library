> **Abstract.** Risk calculation is the analytical process of estimating disaster risk as a function of hazard, exposure, and vulnerability interactions. Three sources in this wiki ground the concept: the World Bank/GFDRR (2013) provides the most explicit formulation, defining risk as "the uncertainty of loss" reducible through location, structural, operational, and fiscal measures; UNDRR (2015) establishes multi-dimensional risk assessment as a Sendai Framework requirement; UNDRR (2017) operationalizes it through a city-level risk assessment workflow and scorecard. The formula itself is consistent across sources; the contested dimension is how to operationalize vulnerability in informal urban settings where data is absent, populations are mobile, and informal tenure arrangements are not captured in administrative records.

## 1. Definition

Risk in disaster contexts is a function of the interaction of hazard, exposure, and vulnerability: "Risk derives from hazard, exposure, and vulnerability interactions" (World Bank/GFDRR, 2013, p. 30). Hazard refers to the probability of occurrence of a potentially damaging physical event; exposure to the location of people, assets, and systems within the hazard zone; vulnerability to the conditions that determine susceptibility to harm and capacity to recover (UNDRR, 2015, p. 13).

World Bank/GFDRR (2013) extends this to residual risk — the portion that remains after mitigation and requires preparedness, warning, evacuation, and recovery systems to manage — and to the structural challenge of decision-making under uncertainty: "cities ought to adopt a robust approach to uncertainty and unknown risks" rather than over-relying on single optimal designs (p. 10).

UNDRR (2015) frames risk assessment as requiring multi-dimensional baseline and periodic measurement: "vulnerability, capacity, exposure, hazard characteristics, and sequential effects" should all be assessed (p. 13). The emphasis on *sequential effects* — cascading failure across systems — is a specifically urban application of the formula, reflecting the interdependence of urban infrastructure.

UNDRR (2017) operationalizes risk assessment at city scale as one of six phases in the resilience-building cycle: "collect and systematize risk information, diagnose baseline, assess current/future threats, update assessments regularly" (p. 88).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**World Bank/GFDRR (2013)** provides the most theoretically developed account of risk in this source set. The core argument is that traditional cost-benefit analysis does not work well for catastrophic tail risk: "traditional cost-benefit analysis does not work well when dealing with catastrophic tail risk" (p. 11). This has direct methodological implications for urban DRR investment decisions — single-point risk estimates that ignore tail risk systematically under-invest in resilience. The paper proposes a portfolio approach — combining locational, structural, operational, and fiscal risk reduction measures — and argues for building critical infrastructure with *redundancy and graceful failure* properties: systems should continue core functions and fail safely during extreme shocks (p. 11).

The multi-pillar disaster risk management (DRM) framework — identification/assessment, prevention/mitigation, financing/insurance, preparedness, and resilient recovery/reconstruction (p. 27) — integrates risk calculation into the full programme cycle. Risk is not assessed once at programme design but revisited at each phase.

**UNDRR (2015)** embeds risk assessment in Sendai Priority 1 (understanding disaster risk). The requirement to assess vulnerability, capacity, exposure, hazard characteristics, and sequential effects (p. 13) — including disaggregation by sex, age, and disability dimensions (p. 14) — establishes risk assessment as a normative baseline requirement for all DRR programming, not a specialized technical exercise. Location-based risk mapping is specified as a standard planning input: "develop/update/disseminate risk maps and location-specific risk information for decision-makers and at-risk communities" (p. 14).

**UNDRR (2017)** translates risk assessment into a practical municipal workflow: compile historical disaster data, update with current hazard/vulnerability assessments, include community-level participation, and produce a baseline using the City Disaster Resilience Scorecard (p. 88, 91). The Scorecard enables comparison of performance across Ten Essentials and identification of gaps and priorities. The handbook explicitly notes that urban risk management "has no universal one-size-fits-all approach" (p. 8).

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Vulnerability operationalization in informal urban settings.** The hazard-exposure-vulnerability formula is consistent across all three sources. The contested dimension is how to measure vulnerability in settings where populations are informal, mobile, and absent from administrative records. World Bank/GFDRR (2013) and UNDRR (2017) both acknowledge that risk data systems require strong interoperability and institutional sharing protocols (pp. 13, 29; p. 88) — but neither specifies methods for generating vulnerability data where those systems do not exist. UNDRR (2015) specifies disaggregated data as a normative requirement (p. 14) without resolving the methodological challenge of collecting it in informal settlement contexts.

**Uncertainty and tail risk.** World Bank/GFDRR (2013) is the only source in this set to explicitly engage with decision-making under deep uncertainty and catastrophic tail risk (pp. 10-11). UNDRR (2015) and UNDRR (2017) treat risk as measurable and reducible through standard assessment workflows, without acknowledging the limits of point estimates for tail events. This is not a contradiction — Sendai and the city handbook are normative policy frameworks, not epistemological arguments — but practitioners applying them should recognize that standard risk assessments may systematically underestimate extreme-event probability.

**Static vs. dynamic risk assessment.** All three sources endorse periodic reassessment — UNDRR (2017) explicitly recommends regular updates (p. 88). In practice, urban humanitarian programmes typically conduct risk assessment at programme inception and update infrequently. The dynamic character of urban risk — driven by rapid population movement, construction activity, and changing governance — means point-in-time assessments may be outdated by programme midpoint.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Risk assessment must cover all three components of the formula — hazard, exposure, and vulnerability — at the area scale; assessments that cover only one or two components produce systematically incomplete risk pictures (UNDRR, 2015, p. 13; World Bank/GFDRR, 2013, p. 30).
- Sequential effects — cascading failure across urban systems — should be explicitly assessed in urban contexts, not only individual sector risks (UNDRR, 2015, p. 13; World Bank/GFDRR, 2013, pp. 10-11).
- Vulnerability data should be disaggregated by age, sex, disability, tenure status, and displacement status at a minimum; administrative data should be supplemented with participatory methods where formal records are absent (UNDRR, 2015, p. 14).
- Risk assessments should be designed for periodic updating, not treated as a one-time baseline product; urban conditions change rapidly enough that a programme-inception assessment may be materially inaccurate by implementation midpoint (UNDRR, 2017, p. 88).
- Investment decisions should account for residual risk and tail risk, not only expected average losses; no-regret and portfolio approaches are more appropriate than single optimal solutions in high-uncertainty urban contexts (World Bank/GFDRR, 2013, pp. 10-11).

## 5. Open questions

- How should the hazard-exposure-vulnerability formula be operationalized in informal settlement contexts where both exposure and vulnerability are under-documented, administrative boundaries do not align with social geography, and rapid change makes any baseline outdated quickly?
- How should sequential (cascade) effects across urban system interdependencies be assessed in field conditions with limited data and time?
- What is the minimum viable risk assessment process for a programme operating under acute emergency constraints — and what risks are introduced by using it?
