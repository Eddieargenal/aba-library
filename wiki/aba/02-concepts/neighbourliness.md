<a id="definition"></a>
## Definition

**Neighbourliness** is a dimension of neighbourhood life that the Young Foundation
defines, in the British context, as "friendliness, helpfulness, and respect for
privacy" — a way of relating that holds "the tension between cooperation and
privacy, helpfulness and non-interference, between friendliness and distance"
(Young Foundation 2010, p. 15). The source draws an explicit contrast with the
American emphasis on visiting neighbours' homes: the British version is **private
interaction that takes place largely in shared public spaces**, and so it
"relies crucially on the local public realm" rather than on access to each
other's homes (p. 15). The source treats neighbourliness as more than a pleasant
texture: it is **a stepping-stone to local collective action** and "a valuable
indicator for measuring the success, failure or progress of neighbourhood
initiatives" (p. 15) — a proxy for social cohesion and a neighbourhood's
willingness to engage in reciprocity.

<a id="field-use"></a>
## Field Use

Field query this should answer: *"How do I read social cohesion progress in a
neighbourhood intervention without running a formal survey?"*

- **Read it as a progress indicator.** Because the source positions neighbourliness
  as "a valuable indicator for measuring the success, failure or progress of
  neighbourhood initiatives" (p. 15), observable friendliness, low-key helping
  behaviour, and reciprocity between residents can stand in as a soft signal of
  whether an intervention is building or eroding cohesion.
- **Watch the public realm, not the doorstep.** Since the British form of
  neighbourliness lives in shared public space, look for it where the source says
  it occurs — in streets, parks, and shared facilities — rather than expecting
  home-visiting (p. 15). This ties the indicator directly to the condition of the
  `local-public-realm`.
- **Use resident–surroundings interactions as a diagnostic.** The source notes
  that unsatisfactory interactions between residents and their surroundings (for
  example, frustration at a derelict park or feeling out of touch with a
  regeneration project) can drive down neighbourliness — or, conversely, trigger
  a cohesive neighbourhood response (p. 15). Either reaction is informative.
- **Apply at diagnosis and monitoring.** The concept is most useful during
  `neighbourhood-diagnosis` (to read baseline cohesion) and `monitoring-learning`
  (to track whether an intervention is moving it).

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

- **Do not equate deprivation with a "bad" neighbourhood.** The source is explicit
  that "deprivation and poverty do not necessarily make a neighbourhood into a
  'bad' one so long as social cohesion and feelings of 'neighbourliness' prevail"
  (p. 15). Using material deprivation as a proxy for low cohesion would
  contradict the source and risk misreading resilient communities.
- **Cultural specificity.** The definition is explicitly *British* and contrasted
  with American home-visiting norms (p. 15); the behavioural markers of
  neighbourliness will differ in other cultural settings, so the indicator should
  not be transplanted without adaptation.
- **Soft, not measured.** The source presents neighbourliness as a valuable
  indicator but offers no measurement instrument or threshold; it is a qualitative
  reading, vulnerable to observer bias, and not a substitute for systematic
  assessment.

> **Evidence note.** Single source (Young Foundation 2010, p. 15), so this sits at
> `limited` trust. Every claim above is grounded in the source's "experience of
> neighbourliness" passage; the source itself frames much of its material as
> hypotheses requiring testing, and corroboration from a second source would be
> needed to promote to `usable`.
