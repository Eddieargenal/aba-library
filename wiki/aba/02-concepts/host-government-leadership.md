> **Abstract.** Host-government leadership is the principle that national and municipal governments should lead — or at minimum coordinate — humanitarian response in urban areas, with humanitarian actors positioned as support rather than as primary decision-makers. Two source documents ground the concept: IASC (2010) establishes it as the foundational principle for urban humanitarian strategy; IASC (2026) embeds it structurally in area-based coordination architecture. Both are IASC documents, and the source base is thin — this page is seed-level and should be enriched as additional sources documenting the operational conditions and failure modes of government leadership are ingested.

## 1. Definition

Host-government leadership is the principle that "the host government must lead, or at a minimum coordinate, an emergency response" (IASC, 2010, p. 8). In urban contexts specifically, this reflects the structural reality that municipal authorities hold the legal mandate, institutional infrastructure, and long-term accountability for service delivery and urban governance — functions that humanitarian actors lack by definition and would undermine by replicating.

IASC (2010) frames the principle in terms of what humanitarian actors should avoid: "parallel humanitarian structures that replicate municipal functions create dependency and undermine sustainability" (p. 8). The preferred relationship is one in which humanitarian response strengthens existing governance systems rather than bypassing them.

IASC (2026) embeds the principle at the coordination architecture level: area-based coordination "emphasizes local actor engagement, community participation, and accountability to affected people" (p. 2), with local actor engagement constitutive of the coordination design rather than an optional enhancement.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**IASC (2010)** establishes the principle at strategic level. The six strategic objectives for urban humanitarian response include as their first action priority: establish a "multi-stakeholder partnership mechanism" with host government leadership — formalizing partnerships before crises occur where possible (pp. 8-9). The rationale is structural: in urban settings, humanitarian actors depend on municipal authority, knowledge, and institutional infrastructure in ways that are not present in rural or camp-based settings. The strategy acknowledges that "no one assistance strategy fits all crises — adaptation to each crisis is required" (p. 5), which implicitly recognizes that host-government leadership is a contextually conditioned goal, not a uniform starting point.

**IASC (2026)** translates the principle into coordination architecture: local actor engagement is framed as a constitutive feature of area-based coordination design (p. 2), not a stakeholder consultation activity. This is the most recent formulation in the source set.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Governance capacity and legitimacy.** Both sources treat host-government leadership as a principle without specifying the governance capacity or political legitimacy required for it to be operationally meaningful. In practice, municipal governance capacity, political alignment, and accountability to affected populations vary substantially across urban crisis contexts. The IASC (2010) acknowledgment that "adaptation to each crisis is required" (p. 5) implicitly recognizes this — but neither source specifies the threshold below which government leadership is not feasible and alternative coordination arrangements are required.

**Government complicity in crisis.** Neither source addresses the operational and ethical challenge of host-government leadership when the government is complicit in, or a party to, the conditions generating humanitarian need. This is an acknowledged gap in the source set.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Programme design should assess host government capacity, mandate, and political alignment as inputs to determining the appropriate humanitarian actor role — implementer, facilitator, or convenor — from inception (IASC, 2010, pp. 8-9).
- Parallel service delivery structures that replicate existing municipal functions should be avoided; where government systems are weak, humanitarian action should aim to strengthen them rather than substitute for them (IASC, 2010, p. 8).
- Government engagement should be established before crises where possible — pre-crisis partnership formalization reduces coordination friction during response (IASC, 2010, pp. 8-9).
- Transition and handover planning should include a documented pathway for transferring programme functions to government or local actors, not just an intention to do so (IASC, 2026, p. 2).

## 5. Open questions

- At what level of municipal governance capacity and political legitimacy does host-government leadership become operationally viable rather than aspirational?
- How should humanitarian programming be designed when the host government is a party to the crisis generating humanitarian need?
- How should the host-government leadership principle be reconciled with accountability-to-affected-populations standards when the two are in tension?
