> **Abstract.** Multi-scale programming is the deliberate design of neighbourhood-level area-based interventions to connect upward and outward to district, city, and regional systems, policies, and actors. Six sources in this wiki identify the failure to link across scales as one of the most persistent and consequential weaknesses in urban ABA practice. The concept is defined consistently across sources; the tension is not whether scale linkage is necessary — all sources agree it is — but how to achieve it given the structural mismatch between humanitarian programme cycles, local implementation logic, and city-level planning and governance processes.

## 1. Definition

Multi-scale programming is the practice of designing, implementing, and monitoring area-based interventions at the neighbourhood level in explicit connection to the systems, actors, plans, and policies that operate at district, city, and regional scales (Campbell, 2016, pp. 36-37; Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, pp. 10, 13). The concept rests on the recognition that neighbourhood-level interventions embedded in urban systems cannot achieve durable outcomes if they are designed and evaluated in isolation from the wider system dynamics within which they operate.

Parker & Maynard (2015) identify multi-scale linkage as a defining operational challenge: "neighbourhood interventions risk weak linkage to city/regional systems unless explicit vertical integration is built in" (pp. 18, 22). The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) specifies this as a quality criterion: ABA plans at local scales "should connect with wider city/regional plans and policies" (pp. 10, 13).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Campbell (2016)** provides the most theoretically grounded account of why multi-scale analysis is a structural requirement rather than a design preference. Urban systems analysis "requires analysis from household to neighbourhood to city and beyond" (pp. 36-37), because the causal mechanisms driving neighbourhood-level conditions frequently originate at city or regional scale — in land use policy, market dynamics, infrastructure investment decisions, or governance structures. Campbell explicitly cautions that "useful area focus should not ignore wider system linkages across boundaries" (p. 40), framing this as an analytical discipline, not only a coordination aspiration.

**DFID/IRC/ALNAP (2015)** establish the operational principle: "neighbourhood-level interventions should be connected to surrounding areas and city-wide systems for sustainability" (pp. 6-7). The paper frames multi-scale programming not as an add-on to neighbourhood work but as a condition for its sustainability — interventions that improve conditions at the neighbourhood level without addressing the city-level drivers of those conditions are likely to produce temporary gains.

**Parker & Maynard (2015)** document multi-scale linkage as a recurring finding across four city case studies (Kabul, Port-au-Prince, Baghdad, Tacloban): "linking [ABA] to wider city/regional plans [is] costly but necessary for sustainability" (p. 22). The cost of vertical integration — in time, coordination effort, and organizational capacity — is acknowledged as a real barrier, but the case studies show that ABA programmes that bypassed this linkage produced more fragile outcomes.

**The Global Shelter Cluster (2019)** identifies cross-scale synchronization as a "recurrent challenge, especially linking neighbourhood initiatives with city/national planning frameworks" (p. 13), and proposes the pilot-demonstrate-replicate model as one pathway: start with demonstrated neighbourhood-level results, then use those results as evidence to influence city-level policy and resource allocation (p. 13).

**Sanderson & Sitko (2017)** embed multi-scale programming in the evaluation framework: scale-up planning should begin at programme inception rather than being added as a final phase, and evaluation should track how neighbourhood-level interventions contribute to wider city-level recovery ecosystems rather than measuring only within-area outputs (pp. 25-27). The WHO nine-step scaling framework is cited as a structured process for this design requirement (p. 26).

**Campbell (2019)** documents the most operationally developed expression of multi-scale programming in this source set: the Barrio Mio programme in Guatemala City was explicitly designed as a *strategy*, not a project — using neighbourhood-level demonstration to generate evidence that would influence municipal policy and enable replication across other settlements (p. 17). PCI's shift to a convenor role was the operational mechanism enabling this: by facilitating municipal and community partner replication rather than direct delivery, PCI accessed a scale of reach impossible through single-agency implementation (p. 37).

## 3. Contested dimensions

The central tension is not conceptual — all six sources agree that multi-scale linkage is necessary — but **structural**: humanitarian programme cycles, funding instruments, and reporting requirements are predominantly designed around the project or programme unit, not around the city-level planning horizon. This creates a systematic misalignment between what multi-scale programming requires (sustained cross-scale engagement, influence on municipal policy, transfer of functions to local actors) and what humanitarian organizational systems are built to deliver (time-bound, geographically bounded, output-measured programmes).

DFID/IRC/ALNAP (2015) and Parker & Maynard (2015) acknowledge this structural constraint without resolving it. Parker & Maynard note that vertical integration is "costly" (p. 22) without specifying who bears those costs or how they should be allocated across programme budgets and timelines.

A second tension concerns **replication vs. scaling**. The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) treats pilot-demonstrate-replicate as the primary scale pathway (p. 13), while Sanderson & Sitko (2017) argue that scale-up planning should be built in from inception rather than added after demonstration (pp. 25-27). These are not incompatible — but they imply different programme design logics, funding instruments, and organizational capabilities.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Programme design should explicitly map the city-level systems, plans, and actors to which neighbourhood-level interventions connect — and specify how those connections will be maintained (Campbell, 2016, pp. 36-37; Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, pp. 10, 13).
- Scale-up planning should be included in the initial programme design, not added as a post-implementation afterthought; Sanderson & Sitko (2017) specify this as Principle 9 of effective post-disaster ABA (pp. 25-27).
- Monitoring and evaluation frameworks should track contribution to city-level change, not only neighbourhood-level outputs — this requires establishing baseline conditions at both scales (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, pp. 27-28; Campbell, 2019, pp. 42, 59).
- Pilot-to-scale pathways should be documented explicitly: what was demonstrated, by whom, with what evidence, and what institutional change at city or district level is required before replication is feasible (Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, p. 13; Campbell, 2019, p. 17).
- The humanitarian agency's role should shift toward facilitation and policy influence as scale-up advances — direct delivery that does not progressively transfer to local actors does not constitute multi-scale programming (Campbell, 2019, p. 37; Parker & Maynard, 2015, p. 18).

## 5. Open questions

- What minimum municipal governance capacity and alignment is required for neighbourhood-level ABA results to translate into city-level policy change?
- How should multi-scale programming objectives be reflected in donor reporting frameworks that are designed around project-level outputs?
- What evidence exists that pilot-demonstrate-replicate pathways actually produce city-level change, as opposed to a series of independent neighbourhood projects?
