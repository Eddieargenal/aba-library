<a id="definition"></a>
## Definition

**Ecological empowerment** is the principle that neighbourhood outcomes emerge
from many interacting factors — neighbourhood leadership, citizen engagement,
local public services, investment, community safety, and the local public realm —
rather than from any single intervention. It frames neighbourhood transformation
as a **systems problem**: the factors reinforce one another, so a programme that
moves only one of them tends to be absorbed by the others left unchanged. The
Young Foundation positions empowerment as something to pursue "on an integrated
rather than piecemeal basis," bringing together local government, neighbourhood
leaders, and voluntary organisations (Young Foundation 2010, p. 35; cf. pp. 6–8).

<a id="field-use"></a>
## Field Use

Use ecological empowerment as a **design and diagnostic lens**, not a deliverable:

- **Map the whole factor set before intervening.** Across a target neighbourhood,
  read the interacting domains together — geographic and social structure, the
  local public realm (spaces, services, civic nodes), community safety,
  leadership, citizen engagement, investment, and governance (Young Foundation
  2010, p. 35; the source's domain list, p. ~12). A gap in one domain usually
  explains why gains in another do not hold.
- **Coordinate across service silos.** Because the factors are coupled, sequence
  interventions across departments and organisations rather than running them in
  parallel silos; the source ties ecological empowerment directly to
  "coordination across service silos" and cross-neighbourhood cooperation.
- **Treat the public realm as the connective tissue.** Many empowerment levers —
  from parks to policing — operate by improving elements of the local public
  realm, so it is often the highest-leverage entry point into the wider system
  (Young Foundation 2010, pp. 25, 35).
- **Apply at the strategy stage.** This concept is most useful during
  `coordination-design` and `integrated-area-strategy`, when the question is how
  the pieces fit together rather than which single activity to fund.

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

- **Single-sector capture.** The most common failure is funding one visible
  intervention (a new building, a single programme) and expecting neighbourhood
  change; ecological empowerment predicts this will be diluted by the unaddressed
  factors around it.
- **Treating symptoms, not the system.** Indicators can improve locally while the
  underlying interaction structure is untouched, producing gains that reverse once
  the intervention ends.
- **Coordination cost and fragmentation.** The source notes that bottom-up
  empowerment is often "complex, overlapping and fragmented" (p. ~17); pursuing
  every factor at once without prioritisation can stall delivery. Pair this
  concept with an explicit scope/sequencing decision rather than treating
  "integrated" as "do everything."
