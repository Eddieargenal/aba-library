<a id="definition"></a>
## Definition

The **local public realm** is "the ensemble of neighbourhood spaces open to
people" (Young Foundation 2010, p. 18). The source breaks it into four
components, not all of them physical:

- **Local public spaces** — the network of open spaces people use: streets,
  parks, squares, estate areas (p. 18).
- **Local public services and civic centres** — primary schools, community
  centres, health centres, libraries, leisure centres, transport stops, and
  faith organisations (churches and mosques), where "people cross paths and the
  personal can become public" (pp. 18, 20).
- **Third spaces** — informal settings such as cafés, pubs, and shopping
  centres that host informal public life (pp. 18, 20–21).
- **The local public sphere** — the communicative layer of civic media:
  newsletters, noticeboards, public neighbourhood maps, community radio, civic
  websites, and email lists (pp. 18, 21).

Together these "provide the frame for our lived experiences of neighbourhood…
where neighbours meet and gather, and… where neighbourhood challenges are
confronted" (p. 18). The Young Foundation offers the public realm as a **third
lens** on neighbourhood definition, alongside administrative geography and
personal identifications, and argues it can "build a bridge" between them because
mental maps of neighbourhood cluster around exactly these common landmarks — the
doctor's surgery, post office, and primary school (pp. 13–14).

<a id="field-use"></a>
## Field Use

The source frames the public realm as both a **diagnostic** and a
**prescriptive** instrument: it "describes the environments where living
neighbourhoods form, and — where that environment is derelict or lacking — has
potential as a diagnostic and prescriptive framework" (p. 14).

- **Anchor the neighbourhood unit on centres, not borders.** Because a key
  centre such as a park or school "may fall outside [a neighbourhood's] nominal
  boundaries," the source recommends "basing neighbourhoods around centres rather
  than on borders" and combining top-down (administrative/ONS) with bottom-up
  (resident mental-map) definitions to map "neighbourhood gaps" (pp. 14, 17).
- **Read the realm as the system's connective tissue.** The source observes that
  policy areas where empowerment is contemplated — "from parks to policing" —
  "revolve around improving elements of the local public realm" (p. 15), and that
  divisions between public space, safety, youth, and leisure are "largely
  artificial" on the ground (p. 25).
- **Enrich, don't just inventory.** The realm "is not static: enterprise,
  intervention and collective action can enrich and transform it, healing scars
  and filling gaps" (p. 18) — for example, community involvement in renovating
  parks or green space reinforces local networks (p. 25).
- **Treat it as a personal public realm.** It should be understood as
  "organically connected to local people's identity and practices," not as
  anonymous physical stock (p. 18).

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

- **Absence or negative centres.** Some neighbourhoods are "characterised by the
  near-total absence of a local public realm, or blighted by negative centres
  (e.g. a fortress-like benefits office)" (p. 18); the framework presumes there is
  a realm to map.
- **Degradation and closure.** The source documents homogenised high streets and
  derelict, unsafe parks (p. 19); weak or non-existent connections between civic
  centres, many "substantially closed to the community" (p. 20); and the loss of
  third spaces — "30,000 local economic outlets" between 1995 and 2000 (p. 20).
  A realm can exist on paper yet be functionally unavailable.
- **Thin local public sphere.** UK local media quality is "very variable and
  often bad — and it almost never reaches down to neighbourhood level" (p. 21),
  so the communicative component is often the weakest.
- **Provisional evidence.** The source's own working definition — combining
  geographic, social, and public-realm characteristics — is offered explicitly as
  a "hypothesis," not a tested instrument (p. 17).
