> **Abstract.** Adaptive management is the structured practice of using evidence from implementation to revise programme assumptions, activities, and design as conditions change. Three sources in this wiki ground the concept consistently: Sanderson & Sitko (2017) define it as "structured flexibility and iterative adjustments" required by dynamic urban conditions; Campbell (2019) documents it as the operational norm in PCI's Barrio Mio and Katye programmes, enabled by flexible funding and organizational culture; Sanderson (2019) frames it as a monitoring and evaluation design requirement for programmes operating under urban complexity. The concept is defined consistently across sources; the central challenge is not whether to adapt but how to document and account for adaptations within logframe-driven programme systems that were designed for output delivery rather than iterative learning.

## 1. Definition

Adaptive management in urban ABA contexts is the practice of building structured flexibility into programme design, implementation, and monitoring so that teams can revise plans in response to evidence from the field without abandoning programme logic or accountability frameworks (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, p. 21; Sanderson, 2019, pp. 132-134).

Sanderson & Sitko (2017) define it operationally: "structured flexibility and iterative adjustments are needed due to dynamic urban conditions" (p. 21). This distinguishes adaptive management from ad hoc programme changes — the word "structured" implies a defined process for identifying when and how adjustments are triggered, documented, and approved.

Campbell (2019) documents its empirical expression in PCI's programmes: "iterative, evidence-based, and community-led processes" (p. 42) where assumptions were revised as field realities changed. The Barrio Mio programme required "flexible design/funding and tolerance for iterative adaptation under uncertainty" as foundational operating conditions (pp. 37, 42).

Sanderson (2019) frames adaptive management as a monitoring and evaluation design requirement: urban M&E systems must handle "uncertainty, changing assumptions, and complex causality" (pp. 138-143) and should prioritize "need to know" over comprehensive data collection (p. 142).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Sanderson & Sitko (2017)** provide the most explicit operational account in this source set. Adaptive management appears as Principle 7 of ten: programmes in post-disaster urban contexts require an "action planning/adaptive management approach: problem/opportunity-driven, incremental, locally informed, and flexible under uncertainty" (pp. 21-23). The paper argues that standard humanitarian logframes are structurally incompatible with adaptive management because they fix activities and outputs at programme inception — creating accountability structures that reward delivery of predetermined outputs over evidence-based revision. The recommended response is logframes redesigned to "avoid single-sector purposes and output-only metrics" and "enable flexibility and multi-sector outcomes" (pp. 21-23).

**Campbell (2019)** documents adaptive management as the de facto operating mode for programmes navigating urban complexity, rather than as an explicit methodology. PCI's Barrio Mio programme used iterative pilot-and-scale logic — "start small, test, adapt with evidence, then replicate through local partners for wider reach" (pp. 42, 59) — with adaptations informed by field evidence and enabled by donor-permitted flexibility (p. 42). The paper also identifies a key measurement gap: "monitoring frameworks struggled to capture full integrated effects where outcomes extended beyond pre-set indicators" (p. 60). This gap is not incidental — it reflects a structural incompatibility between output-based accountability and the systemic, relational outcomes that adaptive management typically pursues.

**Sanderson (2019)** frames adaptive management at the M&E design level. Urban monitoring systems must be built for complexity from the start: they must "handle uncertainty, changing assumptions, and complex causality" (pp. 138-143) rather than being designed for stable, linear causal pathways. The recommendation to "prioritize 'need to know' over data-heavy collection" (p. 142) reflects the same logic — adaptive management requires rapid feedback loops, not comprehensive measurement systems that take months to produce findings.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Adaptive management vs. accountability.** The core tension in the literature is between the organizational flexibility that adaptive management requires and the fixed-output accountability demanded by donor logframes and humanitarian reporting systems. Sanderson & Sitko (2017) identify this as a persistent structural constraint: "inflexible tools, funding, and internal systems can undermine ABAs if unaddressed" (pp. 21-25). Campbell (2019) documents that donor-enabled flexibility was a precondition for PCI's adaptive practice in Barrio Mio (p. 42) — but treats this as an exceptional enabling condition, not the norm. No source in this wiki specifies how agencies operating within standard donor accountability frameworks should formally document and justify mid-programme adaptations.

**How to measure adaptation.** Sanderson (2019) identifies urban M&E design as requiring complexity awareness (pp. 138-143) but does not specify how to track adaptations as distinct from outputs or outcomes. Campbell (2019) notes that monitoring frameworks under-represent systemic outcomes in adaptive, multi-actor programmes (p. 60) without resolving this. The gap between the aspiration to adaptive management and the capacity to document it rigorously remains unresolved in the current source set.

**Organizational culture as a prerequisite.** Campbell (2019) identifies "organisational culture (risk-taking, staff autonomy, innovation mindset)" as "a decisive enabling factor" for adaptive practice (p. 56). Sanderson & Sitko (2017) make a related point about "nimble" internal systems (pp. 24-25). Neither source specifies what organisational change is required to enable adaptive management in agencies whose internal systems were not designed for it, or how rapidly such change is achievable.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Programme logframes should be designed to enable flexibility in activities and outputs while maintaining accountability for intermediate and final outcomes; single-sector, output-only metrics actively undermine adaptive management (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, pp. 21-23).
- Adaptive management requires an explicit trigger-and-documentation process: what evidence prompts review, who decides, and how changes are recorded and communicated to donors and partners (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, p. 21; Sanderson, 2019, pp. 132-134).
- Urban M&E systems should be designed for complexity from programme inception — "need to know" data rather than comprehensive measurement; rapid feedback cycles rather than periodic reporting (Sanderson, 2019, pp. 138-143).
- Monitoring frameworks must capture systemic and relational outcomes, not only physical and service delivery outputs; where programmes work through replication partners, indirect and systems-level change needs dedicated measurement approaches (Campbell, 2019, p. 60).
- Donor negotiations should explicitly address flexibility provisions as a programme design requirement, not an exception to be negotiated mid-implementation (Campbell, 2019, p. 42).

## 5. Open questions

- How should mid-programme adaptations be formally documented and justified within standard humanitarian accountability frameworks without triggering scope-creep or audit risk?
- What minimum organizational capabilities — staff autonomy, flexible budget lines, internal decision processes — are required before adaptive management is practically achievable in a given agency context?
- How can contribution-based evaluation (as defined by Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, p. 27) be designed to track adaptations as evidence-generating events rather than programme failures?
