> **Abstract.** The convenor role is the operational stance in which a humanitarian agency facilitates coordination, replication, and policy influence among government, community, and private-sector actors rather than directly implementing services. Two sources ground the concept: Campbell (2019) provides the only fully documented case — PCI's shift from direct implementer to convenor in the Barrio Mio programme — and Parker & Maynard (2015) identify facilitation as the aspirational ABA role for humanitarian agencies. The concept is seed-level: one primary case study and one normative framing constitute the current evidence base. Generalizability to other governance contexts remains unresolved. The concept's importance for the wiki is significant — it is the operational mechanism that distinguishes pilot-to-scale from simple programme expansion, and multi-stakeholder engagement from multi-stakeholder presence.

## 1. Definition

The convenor role is the operational stance in which a humanitarian or development agency shifts from direct implementation — designing and delivering services using agency staff and resources — to facilitation and convening: building the conditions under which government, community, and private-sector actors can design, fund, and deliver at scale without continued agency presence (Campbell, 2019, p. 37).

Campbell (2019) describes PCI's shift as moving "from direct implementer toward facilitator of municipal/community/private-sector replication" (p. 37). The consequence: "working as facilitator/convenor increased indirect reach and partner-driven implementation at scale" (p. 37) — a reach "impossible through single-agency implementation" (p. 37).

Parker & Maynard (2015) frame facilitation as the ideal ABA role more generally: the primary humanitarian role in urban area-based settings is "fostering cooperation between local stakeholders" (p. 18) rather than service delivery.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Campbell (2019)** is the only source in this wiki that fully documents the convenor role in operation. PCI's Barrio Mio programme made the transition from direct implementer to convenor as an explicit strategic decision tied to the pilot-to-scale logic of the programme (pp. 17, 37). The operational mechanism: PCI used a "stakeholder convening model — engagement across government, academia, private sector, and communities with incentives-based coordination" (pp. 17, 32, 37). By facilitating replication through municipal and partner channels rather than continuing direct delivery, PCI accessed a scale of reach unachievable through its own staffing and budget.

Three enabling conditions are documented: (1) organizational culture — "risk-taking, staff autonomy, innovation mindset" (p. 56); (2) flexible design/funding that allowed programme architecture to shift (pp. 37, 42); and (3) sustained relationship-building with municipal and community partners during the pilot phase (p. 17). The case also notes persistent coordination friction "with actors using conventional sectoral methods, especially in emergency settings" (p. 49) — suggesting that the convenor role is more achievable in stabilization and recovery contexts than in acute emergency phases.

**Parker & Maynard (2015)** frame facilitation as normatively correct for ABA without documenting it in operational detail: "humanitarian agencies should foster cooperation between local stakeholders" (p. 18), complementing rather than replacing existing governance structures. The NRC Baghdad case is the closest case illustration in Parker & Maynard — NRC engaged at community and district level while UN-Habitat engaged at provincial and national level — but the convenor framing is not made explicit.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Conditions for feasibility.** Campbell (2019) explicitly notes that the convenor role is not universally applicable: "transferability is unresolved for settings with weak/non-functioning governance and different crisis phases" (p. 58). Acute emergency contexts that demand direct service delivery, settings without adequate local actor capacity, and situations where government partners are contested or absent all challenge the convenor model. No source specifies the minimum governance and institutional conditions required for a convenor transition to be operationally viable.

**Role clarity and accountability.** The convenor role changes what the agency is accountable for — from its own service delivery outputs to the conditions it creates for others to act. This raises accountability questions that neither source resolves: how are convenor agencies evaluated, how do they demonstrate value to donors, and how are partner-driven outcomes tracked and attributed?

## 4. Implications for practice

- The convenor role should be planned as a deliberate programme design choice at inception, not adopted reactively when direct implementation reaches its limits; the transition from implementer to convenor requires relationship investment that must begin early (Campbell, 2019, pp. 17, 37).
- Organizational prerequisites — flexible funding, staff autonomy, leadership tolerance for indirect impact — must be in place before the convenor role is operationally achievable (Campbell, 2019, p. 56).
- The convenor role is more feasible in stabilization and recovery phases than in acute emergency phases; programme designers should specify when the transition from implementation to facilitation is planned (Campbell, 2019, p. 49).
- Monitoring frameworks must be redesigned for the convenor role: output metrics from direct delivery are replaced by indicators of partner capacity, institutional change, and collective impact (Campbell, 2019, p. 60).
- The role distinction — implementer, facilitator, convenor — should be made explicit in programme design documents and communicated to donors and partners as a programme evolution rather than a programme failure (Parker & Maynard, 2015, p. 18; Campbell, 2019, p. 37).

## 5. Open questions

- What minimum local governance capacity and institutional legitimacy is required before a humanitarian agency can responsibly transition to a convenor role rather than maintaining direct delivery?
- How should convenor agencies be evaluated by donors, and what evidence standards apply to indirect, partner-mediated impact?
- Does the convenor model transfer to acute emergency contexts, or is it structurally limited to recovery and development phases?
