> **Abstract.** Participation in urban response is the structured inclusion of affected populations and local stakeholders in assessment, prioritization, design, implementation, and adaptation decisions. Three core sources in this wiki ground the concept: Parker & Maynard (2015) define participation as a foundational ABA criterion and distinguish community-driven from community-informed practice; Sanderson & Sitko (2017) integrate participatory assessment into post-disaster ABA principles; and Sanderson (2019) links participation to adaptive learning in complex urban systems. The concept is consistently valued across sources, but strongly contested in implementation because emergency timelines and institutional constraints often reduce participation to consultation without shared decision power.

## 1. Definition

Participation in urban response means affected residents and relevant local actors shape substantive programme decisions across the cycle, not merely receive information or provide one-off feedback (Parker & Maynard, 2015, pp. 10, 19).

In ABA terms, participation is not optional accompaniment to technical planning. It is one of the core criteria that determines whether a programme's design logic is genuinely area-based.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Parker & Maynard (2015)** provide the clearest empirical and normative grounding. Their case synthesis shows that ABA outcomes depend on active stakeholder involvement, while many programmes remain community-informed rather than community-driven in practice (pp. 10, 19). They also document practical constraints, including time demands, trust-building requirements, and consultation fatigue.

**Sanderson & Sitko (2017)** strengthen the argument by positioning participatory assessment as a core post-disaster principle. Their framing links participation to programme legitimacy, contextual accuracy, and adaptive problem-solving under uncertainty.

**Sanderson (2019)** supports participation from an MEL perspective: in complex urban systems, local knowledge and feedback are necessary for iterative adaptation and realistic interpretation of change pathways.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Community-driven vs. community-informed practice.** Sources consistently endorse community-driven intent, yet field practice frequently limits communities to consultation roles. The unresolved issue is the threshold at which participation can credibly be considered decision-sharing.

**Participation vs. emergency speed.** Rapid-response timelines, access constraints, and funding cycles often conflict with the time needed for representative engagement and trust formation. Sources acknowledge the tension but do not provide a universally accepted minimum participation standard for acute phases.

**Representation and inclusion quality.** Participation structures often over-represent formal leaders and under-represent women, youth, renters, people with disabilities, minorities, and mobile populations. Who is present in engagement spaces is often not equivalent to who is affected.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Define participation scope at programme start, including which decisions are shared, advisory, or agency-led.
- Use inclusion safeguards so engagement mechanisms do not default to the most visible or powerful community actors.
- Sequence quick visible actions with longer participatory planning to balance trust-building and emergency delivery pressure.
- Link feedback systems to explicit decision logs to show where community input changed priorities, design, or implementation.
- Coordinate engagement across sectors to reduce repeated consultation burden and avoid community fatigue.

## 5. Open questions

- What minimum decision-sharing threshold should define credible participation in acute urban emergencies?
- Which participation designs most reliably include socially and legally marginalized groups in mixed urban populations?
- How should agencies evaluate participation quality beyond attendance counts and consultation frequency?
