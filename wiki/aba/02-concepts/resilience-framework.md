<a id="definition"></a>
## Definition

The **Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community** is a structured framework describing what an ideal disaster-resilient community looks like. It is presented as "a resource, not a manual" and "not a checklist to be ticked off" (Twigg 2009, pp. 7, 18) — an aspirational reference model rather than an attainable standard. The community it portrays is explicitly an ideal, "for in reality no community can be free of risk"; one is better thought of as "the safest possible community that we have the knowledge to design and build" (Twigg 2009, pp. 7, 10).

The framework has a nested, three-level structure, organised (following the Hyogo Framework for Action) into five **Thematic Areas** (Twigg 2009, pp. 9–10):

1. Governance
2. Risk Assessment
3. Knowledge and Education
4. Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction
5. Disaster Preparedness and Response

Each Thematic Area is subdivided into **Components of Resilience** — broad sub-themes (28 in total) — and each Component is detailed as **Characteristics of a Disaster-Resilient Community**, the specific, ground-level descriptors (167 in total) (Twigg 2009, pp. 10–11). A parallel column of **Characteristics of an Enabling Environment** captures the external institutional, policy and socio-economic conditions that support each Component (Twigg 2009, p. 12). Governance (Thematic Area 1) is treated as cross-cutting, relevant whichever area a user focuses on (Twigg 2009, p. 11).

<a id="field-use"></a>
## Field Use

The framework is meant to "stimulate and facilitate discussion," and how to start "is up to you, the user, to decide"; a practitioner selects the elements most appropriate to their context rather than working through all 167 (Twigg 2009, pp. 7, 18). Practical uses the source describes include:

- **Scope ambition to mandate and capacity.** "No single group or organization can address every aspect of DRR" (Twigg 2009, p. 7); the intended response is partnership — addressing a deliberate subset while partners and duty bearers cover the rest.
- **Map coverage, then look for connections.** Use the Components of Resilience to see which areas are covered and where gaps lie, and look for reinforcing links across Thematic Areas — e.g. disaster preparedness (Area 5) is more effective when based on risk assessment (Area 2) and high participation and accountability (Area 1) (Twigg 2009, p. 21).
- **Adapt before applying.** The Characteristics are general descriptors, but "every project, location and community is unique"; users debate the resource's usefulness and select, reword, or add Characteristics — even drafting new Thematic Areas (e.g. Plan's child-centred area) (Twigg 2009, pp. 7, 18).
- **Treat the Enabling Environment as connected, not optional.** It "is not separate from community-level work and should not be viewed in isolation," given the "web of relationships" between community and external actors (Twigg 2009, p. 12).

<a id="risks"></a>
## Risks

The source raises its own cautions against misuse:

- **Compliance/checklist drift.** The most-stated risk is treating an aspirational ideal as a checklist to tick off. The framework "must be adapted to the context"; mechanical application overlooks local conditions and is "not a model for every situation" (Twigg 2009, pp. 7, 18).
- **Imbalance and artificial separation.** Thematic Area 4 (Risk Management and Vulnerability Reduction) is "very broad" and bundles issues that "do not necessarily fit together easily," creating imbalance; users are advised to take "particular care" that its Components are investigated thoroughly (Twigg 2009, pp. 13–14). Likewise, putting each Characteristic in just one place understates real overlap — connections across themes "must be kept in mind" (Twigg 2009, p. 13).
- **Context limits.** The ideal state is "far removed from the condition of a community that has just suffered a disaster," so a reduced, recovery-focused set is more realistic post-disaster (Twigg 2009, p. 14). The framework also assumes a spatial community with some consensus and an accessible enabling environment — assumptions that may break down; see proposed risk **R-resilience-conflict-gap** for the conflict-setting limitation, where the source notes no field experience exists (Twigg 2009, p. 15).
