> **Abstract.** Area-based coordination (ABC) is a geographically bounded, multi-actor coordination modality that complements rather than replaces sectoral cluster coordination in humanitarian response. Four sources in this wiki ground the concept, with the IASC (2026) Terms of Reference providing the most authoritative operational definition. The literature consistently identifies a structural gap between area-level and sector-level coordination as a primary obstacle to effective urban ABA, but differs on whether the solution is a formal coordination mechanism, strengthened inter-cluster linkage, or flexible multi-stakeholder convening.

## 1. Definition

Area-based coordination is defined by the IASC (2026) as "a light operational coordination structure focused on a defined subnational geography, linked to wider IASC structures" (p. 2). Its core function is to coordinate integrated area-level delivery across sectors and actors operating within a defined geographic area, while clusters retain responsibility for sectoral standards, analysis, and technical guidance (pp. 1, 5, 8). ABC is explicitly designed as a complement to the cluster system, not a replacement: it fills the coordination gap created by sector-siloed coordination structures operating in complex urban settings where needs and actors are geographically concentrated but cross-sectoral.

A key operational characteristic is that ABC is time-bound: it "is not intended to be indefinite and should transition or disband when criteria are met" (IASC, 2026, pp. 7-8), distinguishing it from standing coordination platforms and aligning it with the humanitarian programme cycle.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**IASC (2026)** provides the most formally developed operational definition and terms of reference for area-based coordination in this source set. ABC is characterized by four features: a defined geographic focus; linkage to wider IASC coordination structures; emphasis on local actor engagement, community participation, and accountability to affected people (p. 2); and a protection mainstreaming mandate — ABC supports "protection risk understanding and area-level protection outcomes aligned with national Centrality of Protection efforts" (p. 5). The time-bound nature of ABC is treated as a design requirement, not an administrative default, to ensure exit planning and local ownership are built in from the start (pp. 7-8).

**Sanderson & Sitko (2017)** identify the gap that ABC is designed to address: "current cluster coordination can be insufficient for urban multi-sector territorial needs without stronger inter-cluster city-level linkage" (p. 19). The argument is structural — the cluster system's sectoral architecture is poorly fitted to urban contexts where the relevant unit of planning is the neighbourhood or district, not the sector. This framing positions area-based coordination not as a technical add-on but as a response to a design limitation in the existing coordination system.

**The Global Shelter Cluster (2019)** documents three operational coordination models used across ABA case studies: city-level working groups providing coordination across sectors at the city scale; municipal or sub-city task forces focused on specific geographic areas; and one-agency area coordination linked to cluster and community structures (p. 12). This typology illustrates that area-based coordination operates across a spectrum of formality and scale, with no single model appropriate to all contexts.

**DFID/IRC/ALNAP (2015)** argue that "urban coordination should move beyond one-size-fits-all sector-only structures and include stronger inter-sector and municipal mechanisms" (pp. 3, 7-8). This recommendation predates the IASC (2026) ToR and frames the same coordination problem from a programme rather than system architecture perspective.

## 3. Contested dimensions

The primary tension in the literature concerns **the relationship between area-based and sector coordination**: whether they are genuinely complementary or structurally in tension. The IASC (2026) ToR asserts complementarity — clusters retain sectoral authority while ABC coordinates geographic delivery — but does not fully resolve how conflicts between area-level priorities and sector-level standards should be adjudicated (pp. 1, 5, 8). In practice, agencies operating across both systems may face competing reporting demands, resource allocation conflicts, and unclear decision authority.

A secondary tension concerns **the appropriate level of formality**. IASC (2026) defines a formal coordination structure with specified roles, ToR, and linkage to IASC systems (p. 2). The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) documents informal working groups operating at city and sub-city level that function as area coordination without formal designation (p. 12). DFID/IRC/ALNAP (2015) advocate for flexible rather than standardized coordination architecture (pp. 3, 7-8). The appropriate level of formality appears context-dependent, but the conditions that determine that choice are not specified in the current source set.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Area-based coordination should be established with explicit terms of reference specifying its geographic scope, relationship to cluster coordination, decision authority, and transition criteria (IASC, 2026, pp. 1-2, 7-8).
- ABC should include local actors — community structures, municipal authorities, service providers — as genuine coordination participants, not only as consultation targets (IASC, 2026, p. 2; DFID/IRC/ALNAP, 2015, pp. 7-8).
- Coordination at the area level is most effective when structured to match the scale at which interventions are integrated — neighbourhood-level ABC cannot substitute for city-level coordination, and vice versa (Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, p. 12).
- Protection must be treated as a core coordination function, not an add-on: area-level protection risk mapping and outcome monitoring should be embedded in the ABC mandate (IASC, 2026, p. 5).
- Exit planning should be built into the ABC design from the outset — the time-bound nature of ABC requires that coordination functions be progressively transferred to local or standing mechanisms (IASC, 2026, pp. 7-8).

## 5. Open questions

- How should conflicts between area-level priorities and sector-level technical standards be resolved when the two coordination systems produce incompatible guidance?
- Under what conditions is a formal IASC-aligned ABC mechanism more appropriate than a flexible working group or convening arrangement?
- What indicators distinguish effective area-based coordination from coordination that exists nominally but does not reduce duplication or fill gaps?
