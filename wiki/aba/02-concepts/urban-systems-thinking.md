> **Abstract.** Urban systems thinking is an analytical approach that treats the city as a set of interdependent subsystems whose interactions — not individual components — drive crisis dynamics and recovery trajectories. Five sources in this wiki ground the concept, which converges on a five-domain typology (economy/livelihoods, governance, social/cultural, infrastructure/services, and space/settlements). The concept is well-established within the source set; the primary tension is not in the framework itself but in how to operationalize it under field conditions with limited time and data.

## 1. Definition

Urban systems thinking treats cities as "interconnected elements bounded in structures," where analysis must focus on interrelationships as well as components (Campbell, 2016, pp. 22-23). The central insight is that shocks do not affect isolated systems — they propagate through interdependencies, generating cascading effects that cannot be understood by examining any single sector or subsystem in isolation. Sanderson (2019) frames this operationally: "humanitarian response depends on engaging municipal authorities, communities, and other local actors embedded in urban systems" (pp. viii, ix), locating the response actor within the system rather than external to it.

The analytical approach is distinct from sectoral planning in that it looks for feedback loops, leverage points, and system-level consequences of intervention, rather than mapping inputs to sectoral outputs (Campbell, 2016, pp. 24-25).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Campbell (2016)** provides the most theoretically developed account in this source set. The guide defines systems as "interconnected elements bounded in structures" and argues that meaningful urban analysis must focus on stocks (accumulated resources), flows (rates of change), and feedback loops (self-reinforcing or self-correcting dynamics) to avoid premature conclusions (pp. 22-25). Campbell organizes urban systems into five domains: economy and livelihoods, governance, social and cultural, infrastructure and services, and space and settlements (p. 25). Multi-scale analysis — from household to neighbourhood to city and beyond — is presented as a requirement, not an option (pp. 36-37). The guide also cautions that geographic focus should not be allowed to create artificial boundary restrictions on systems analysis: "useful area focus should not ignore wider system linkages across boundaries" (p. 40).

**The IRC/IIED Urban Context Analysis Toolkit** (2017) operationalizes systems thinking as a five-theme analytical framework — politics and governance, social and cultural, economic, service and infrastructure, and space and settlements — with gender and Do No Harm as cross-cutting dimensions (pp. 17-19). This thematic model maps directly onto Campbell's five-domain typology, providing consistent vocabulary across both theoretical and practitioner-facing sources.

**URAF** (Mohiddin et al., 2017) applies the same five-domain framework to response option analysis, treating it as the required analytical lens for linking situational context to programme choices (pp. 13, 17). This confirms the framework's utility not only at the diagnostic stage but throughout the programme cycle.

**DFID/IRC/ALNAP (2015)** foreground systems-based response as a core complement to area-based programming, arguing that restoring and strengthening urban networks — water, sanitation, transport, power, markets, health, and education service channels — is essential alongside area-level interventions (p. 7). This positions systems thinking not as a purely analytical activity but as a design principle for complementary response strategies.

**Sanderson (2019)** characterizes the broader challenge as engaging an "actor ecology" — the complex web of municipal authorities, communities, service providers, and others embedded in urban systems — whose roles, incentives, and interdependencies shape what is feasible in any given intervention (pp. viii, ix).

## 3. Contested dimensions

The five-domain typology is consistent across sources and does not appear contested within this source set. The primary tension concerns **operationalization under field constraints**. Campbell (2016) develops the analytical framework at length but does not specify how to conduct rapid systems mapping when access, time, and data are limited. The IRC/IIED toolkit (2017) provides a structured 10-step process estimated at approximately two weeks of in-country data collection (p. 9) — a standard that may not be achievable in acute emergency contexts.

A second tension concerns the relationship between systems analysis at the area level and the broader city or regional systems within which the target area is embedded. Campbell (2016) explicitly warns against treating geographic focus as a reason to limit systems analysis (p. 40), while practical field guidance does not resolve how teams should manage this boundary analytically.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Systems analysis should precede programme design: understanding which subsystems are disrupted, how they interact, and what leverage points exist determines what kinds of interventions are likely to be effective (Campbell, 2016, pp. 22-25; IRC/IIED, 2017, pp. 8-9).
- All five domains — economy/livelihoods, governance, social/cultural, infrastructure/services, space/settlements — should be considered even when an intervention targets only one; cross-system effects are often where unintended harms originate (Campbell, 2016, p. 25; IRC/IIED, 2017, pp. 17-19).
- Area-level systems analysis must not be isolated from city-level systems: neighbourhood-scale interventions that ignore wider market, governance, or infrastructure dynamics risk producing unsustainable outcomes (Campbell, 2016, p. 40; DFID/IRC/ALNAP, 2015, pp. 6-7).
- Actor mapping is a systems task, not only a stakeholder management exercise: understanding the roles, incentives, and interdependencies of the actor ecology is required for realistic programme design (Sanderson, 2019, pp. viii-ix).
- Monitoring systems should track feedback effects — unintended consequences of intervention on adjacent systems — not only programme outputs within the targeted domain (Campbell, 2016, pp. 24-25).

## 5. Open questions

- What is the minimum viable systems analysis that can be conducted in acute emergency phases without compromising programme quality?
- How should teams manage the analytical boundary between area-level and city-level systems when operating in one defined neighbourhood?
- How can feedback loops and second-order effects be monitored without the complexity adding unmanageable cost to programme MEL systems?
