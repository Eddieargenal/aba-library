> **Abstract.** Protection in urban settings addresses the specific risks of violence, exploitation, discrimination, and legal precarity that urban displacement and poverty generate for affected populations. A single source grounds this concept in the current wiki: IASC (2010) identifies protection as a strategic objective for urban humanitarian response, documenting that "pressure on services and livelihoods can exacerbate poverty and discrimination leading to violence and exploitation" (p. 13) and that individual targeting approaches in urban settings can increase protection risk by making beneficiaries visible. This concept page is seed-level and should be significantly enriched when sources dedicated to urban protection — including UNHCR urban refugee guidance, ICRC urban operations doctrine, and protection mainstreaming frameworks — are ingested.

## 1. Definition

Protection in urban settings refers to the measures, design choices, and programming adaptations required to prevent, mitigate, and respond to violence, exploitation, discrimination, and rights violations that urban crises generate and humanitarian response can inadvertently exacerbate (IASC, 2010, pp. 12-13).

IASC (2010) frames urban protection as distinct from camp-based protection in several respects: affected populations are dispersed and anonymous, making identification and targeting difficult; legal precarity (undocumented status, informal tenure) is common and increases vulnerability; urban violence and exploitation patterns differ from conflict-zone dynamics; and individual targeting approaches that create visible beneficiary lists introduce risks not present in camp registration systems (p. 13).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**IASC (2010)** identifies protection as one of six strategic objectives for improved urban humanitarian response. The operational problem is articulated as follows: "the pressure on services and livelihoods in urban settings can exacerbate poverty and discrimination leading to... violence and exploitation" (p. 13). The recommended design response is two-fold: (1) use "community-based approaches where individual targeting may increase protection risk or social tension" (p. 13) — the area-based logic of whole-population inclusion directly reduces the visibility of individual beneficiaries; and (2) adapt standard protection tools to urban constraints, including the RPAT (Rapid Protection Assessment Tool) expansion to address urban-specific concerns (pp. 12, 18).

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Evidence base gap.** The current source set contains only one dedicated protection reference (IASC, 2010), and this document addresses protection as one of six objectives rather than as the primary focus. The operational specificity required for field teams designing protection-sensitive ABA programmes — community-based protection mechanisms, referral pathway design, Do No Harm analysis in urban settings, data protection standards — is not adequately grounded in the current source set. This page should not be used as the primary reference for protection programming design until additional sources are ingested.

**Individual vs. area-based targeting and protection risk.** IASC (2010) notes that individual targeting in urban settings can create visibility risks for beneficiaries (p. 13). This is one documented reason to prefer area-based, whole-population approaches. However, area-based targeting does not eliminate protection risks — it changes their character. Specific vulnerable sub-groups (women, unaccompanied minors, LGBTQ+ individuals, persons with disabilities) may remain invisible within an area-based approach without dedicated inclusion and protection safeguards.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Individual targeting approaches in urban settings should be assessed for protection risk before deployment; visible beneficiary lists create exposure in contexts with security threats, discrimination, or exploitation risks (IASC, 2010, p. 13).
- Area-based, whole-population approaches partially mitigate individual targeting protection risks — but dedicated inclusion mechanisms are still required to reach the most vulnerable within the area (IASC, 2010, p. 13).
- Protection analysis should be integrated into urban context analysis from programme inception, not added as a Do No Harm afterthought (IASC, 2010, pp. 12-13).
- Data collection instruments — KII guides, community mapping, household surveys — must incorporate data protection standards appropriate to the specific protection risk profile of the urban context (IASC, 2010, p. 13).

## 5. Open questions

- What community-based protection mechanisms are most effective in informal urban settlement contexts with mixed displaced and host populations?
- How should area-based approaches incorporate dedicated protection mechanisms for sub-groups who remain invisible within whole-population targeting?
- What data protection standards apply to area-level vulnerability mapping in contexts where information about community composition creates risks for residents?
