> **Abstract.** Neighbourhood boundary definition is the process of delineating the spatial perimeter of an area-based intervention or assessment unit. Six sources in this wiki ground the concept, drawing from both humanitarian operational practice and urban planning and neighbourhood theory. Three distinct theoretical frameworks for defining boundaries — administrative, physical, and socially negotiated — are documented, and the literature consistently finds that no single approach is adequate without triangulation. A structurally important finding is that resident-defined boundaries frequently diverge from administrative boundaries, with significant consequences for who is included in or excluded from programming and assessment.

## 1. Definition

Neighbourhood boundaries in the context of ABA programming are the delineated perimeters of operational areas that determine the scope of assessment, intervention, and coordination. Boundaries may be based on administrative units, physical features, social identity, service catchment areas, hazard zones, or combinations of these criteria (Parker & Maynard, 2015, p. 9; Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, p. 11).

The concept of "neighbourhood" underlying boundary definition is itself contested. Baffoe (2019) identifies three analytically distinct framings: neighbourhood as *place* — emphasizing locale, lived experience, and social interaction (pp. 4-5); neighbourhood as *community* — a formalized social unit used for governance or administrative purposes (pp. 4-5); and neighbourhood as *policy unit* — an operational scale for resource targeting, decentralization, and participation (pp. 5-6). These framings imply different boundary-drawing logics and are not interchangeable. Allen (2018) produces a compatible typology — spatial, social, and socio-spatial neighbourhood — organized around method rather than function (p. 59). The Young Foundation (2010) frames the same distinction as administrative geography versus personal identification, arguing that each carries trade-offs for legitimacy, inclusiveness, and manageability (pp. 10-11).

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Parker & Maynard (2015)** document two primary boundary types in ABA case studies. *Administrative boundaries* — defined by existing government administrative units such as barangays, districts, or sub-districts — are preferred when they create the greatest opportunity for partnership with local government and support exit planning (p. 9). *Physical boundaries* — defined by natural or built features such as main roads, rivers, or steep terrain — are appropriate when physical features also reinforce social identity and community cohesion, as in the Katye programme in Ravine Pintade, bounded by three main roads and a steep ravine slope (p. 9). The authors conclude that boundary definition in practice must combine administrative or physical boundaries with social analysis to support participatory processes (p. 10).

**REACH/UNHCR (2019)** introduce an operational distinction between *community areas* (defined by residents' social organization and identity) and *service areas* (defined by the catchment logic of formal services) as parallel boundary types used to structure key informant selection and data interpretation (pp. 15-17, 34). The guide identifies area definition as a prerequisite for assessment quality, warning that "poor boundary definition can produce misleading findings" (p. 32).

**Young Foundation (2010)** argue that *layered and overlapping neighbourhoods* are the normal condition — residents simultaneously participate in multiple neighbourhood scales and social structures (pp. 16-17). This challenges the assumption that a neighbourhood has a single definable boundary and suggests that different purposes require different boundary definitions drawn from different scales of social organization.

**Allen (2018)** documents the empirical finding that "resident-defined neighbourhood boundaries frequently diverge from administrative boundaries" (pp. 37, 53), and identifies mixed socio-spatial methods — combining subjective resident perceptions with objective spatial data — as increasingly necessary for policy-relevant boundary analysis (p. 59). The implication for DRR and ABA programming is that administrative unit boundaries systematically risk excluding populations whose neighbourhood identity extends beyond or is subdivided by those units.

**Baffoe (2019)** recommends *context-driven, negotiated definitions* as the appropriate approach for both research and policy: "definitions should reflect research/policy purpose and local realities" (pp. 2, 7). The review explicitly cautions against applying global-north neighbourhood typologies in settings with different spatial organization and data availability (p. 7).

## 3. Contested dimensions

The central tension in the literature is between **administrative efficiency** and **social legitimacy** in boundary definition. Administrative boundaries are operationally convenient and align with local government partnership opportunities (Parker & Maynard, 2015, p. 9), but systematically diverge from how residents understand and organize neighbourhood space (Allen, 2018, pp. 37, 53; Young Foundation, 2010, pp. 10-11). Boundaries drawn on administrative logic without social validation risk misidentifying community identities, excluding sub-groups, and producing assessment or programme areas that do not correspond to the social units within which trust, collective action, and local governance actually operate.

A second tension concerns **scale**. The Young Foundation (2010) document that residents participate in overlapping neighbourhood scales simultaneously (pp. 16-17), and Parker & Maynard (2015) note that ABA areas can range from a small cluster of households to a city-wide intervention (p. 10). The concept of "neighbourhood boundary" presupposes a single resolvable perimeter, which the literature suggests is a methodological simplification rather than a social fact.

A third tension concerns **purpose dependency**: Baffoe (2019) argues that the right boundary definition depends on what the boundary is for — a hazard mapping boundary, a service delivery boundary, and a social mobilization boundary may legitimately differ (pp. 2, 7). Programmes that use a single boundary definition for all purposes risk analytical errors at each stage.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Boundary selection should be documented with an explicit rationale: which boundary logic was applied (administrative, physical, social, or combined), why, and what trade-offs were accepted (Parker & Maynard, 2015, p. 10; Baffoe, 2019, pp. 2, 7).
- Draft boundaries should be validated with community members before finalization — resident-defined boundaries frequently diverge from administrative ones and may signal important inclusion or exclusion risks (Allen, 2018, pp. 37, 53; Young Foundation, 2010, pp. 10-11).
- Area definition should be treated as a prerequisite for assessment quality, not an administrative formality: "poor boundary definition can produce misleading findings" (REACH/UNHCR, 2019, p. 32).
- Different purposes — assessment, service delivery, community mobilization, coordination — may justify different boundaries; programmes should specify which boundary definition applies to which function (Baffoe, 2019, p. 7; REACH/UNHCR, 2019, pp. 15-17).
- Where targeting one area creates perceived inequity with adjacent areas, mitigation strategies such as shared facilities on area borders or blurred boundary zones should be considered (Parker & Maynard, 2015, pp. 9-10).

## 5. Open questions

- Under what conditions should resident-defined boundaries take precedence over administrative boundaries, and how should conflicts between them be resolved?
- How should boundary definitions be managed when the target area is internally heterogeneous — for example, containing both displaced and long-term host populations with different neighbourhood identities?
- What is the minimum viable boundary validation process in acute emergency phases when participatory mapping is not feasible?
