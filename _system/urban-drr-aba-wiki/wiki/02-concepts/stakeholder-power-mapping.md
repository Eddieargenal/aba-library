# Definition
The systematic identification and analysis of all actors (government, NGOs, CBOs, private sector, community) with roles, interests, resources, and power relationships relevant to an area-based program.

# Why it matters for urban DRR and response
Understanding stakeholder power dynamics is essential for avoiding duplication, leveraging local capacity, aligning with authorities, and managing coordination risks — ignoring power dynamics leads to programs that reproduce existing inequalities.

# Evidence base

## Multi-Actor Coordination Requirement (Parker & Maynard 2015, pp.12-13)

Area-based approaches require "developing effective partnerships and capacity development with a larger range of actors at both strategic and operational levels such as mayors, municipalities, police forces and residents" (Global CCCM Cluster 2014, cited in Parker & Maynard). The humanitarian agency's primary role is as **facilitator** — not service deliverer — fostering cooperation between local stakeholders.

The NRC Baghdad programme demonstrates multi-level stakeholder engagement: NRC worked at community and district level, while UN-Habitat worked at provincial and national policy level. This division allowed them to tackle the same problem at different scales simultaneously, using neighbourhood-level results to influence national policy.

Parker & Maynard also note that inter-sector coordination within the cluster system is chronically weak in urban settings, making stakeholder power mapping essential for understanding who actually makes decisions vs. who nominally coordinates.

## Humanitarian Facilitator Role (Parker & Maynard 2015, p.18)

The KASS Kabul programme identified that "the success of the project rested on bringing in key stakeholders such as the Kabul municipality and community members into all aspects of the project, from beneficiary selection, choice of project sites and also regular project discussions."

Key implication: stakeholder mapping must precede programme design, not follow it — who is mapped determines who is engaged, which determines programme legitimacy and sustainability.
