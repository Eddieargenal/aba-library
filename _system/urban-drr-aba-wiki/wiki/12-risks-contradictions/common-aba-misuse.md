# Common ABA Misuse Patterns

## Purpose

Documents recurring patterns where area-based approaches are applied incorrectly, superficially, or in ways that harm target populations. Enables agents to flag risk when a proposed programme design matches a known misuse pattern.

> **Coverage note:** Patterns 1–4 are sourced from Parker & Maynard (2015) only. Additional patterns from ALNAP GPR 12 (2019) and GSC/USWG Compendium (2019) are pending ingestion of those sources. Do not treat this registry as complete.

---

## Misuse Pattern Registry

### Pattern 1: ABA Label Without Criteria

**How it manifests:** A programme is described as area-based without meeting all three defining criteria — geographically targeted (by need/vulnerability), multi-sector (integrated design, not co-located parallel activities), and participatory (community input changes design decisions). Common variants:
- Working in a neighbourhood but targeting households sector-by-sector
- Running parallel sector activities in the same area with no shared entry point or coordination
- Holding one community meeting and recording it as participatory design

**Why it happens:** "Area-based" satisfies donor or cluster requirements without requiring programme redesign. The term is used as a label, not a design standard.

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015, p.21) — all three criteria must be present simultaneously. "A programme can be multi-sectoral, but not area-based (if it does not take a participatory or geographically targeted approach)."

**Detection:** Ask three questions:
1. Is the entry point an area (not a sector or beneficiary group)?
2. Are 3+ sectors designed jointly around a shared area profile?
3. Has community input demonstrably changed a design decision?

If any answer is no → not ABA.

**Corrective action:** Either restructure to meet all three criteria or relabel accurately (geographically-located sector programme, multi-sector programme, etc.).

### Pattern 2: Area Selection Without Vulnerability Analysis

**How it manifests:** The target area is selected because it is operationally convenient — existing field office, pre-existing relationships, cluster assignment, or political negotiation — rather than because it has a documented concentration of need, vulnerability, or hazard.

**Why it happens:** Area selection is treated as an administrative starting point rather than a substantive analytical decision. Teams skip Tool #01 and proceed directly to programme design.

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015, pp.8-10) — the geographic targeting criterion requires that the area defines the entry point based on concentration of problems, not operational convenience.

**Detection:** Ask: "What vulnerability or risk data was used to select this area over adjacent areas? Show the criteria." If the answer is "it's our operational zone" or "the cluster assigned it" → misuse.

**Corrective action:** Run area selection through [[wiki/04-tools/02-area-selection-matrix]] with documented vulnerability criteria, or acknowledge the programme is geographically located but not area-targeted.

---

### Pattern 3: Coordination Theatre

**How it manifests:** Area-based coordination meetings are established and meeting minutes are produced, but no decisions are made, no activities are adjusted, and duplication persists. Meeting attendance is logged as evidence of coordination.

**Why it happens:** Coordination is treated as a process obligation rather than a tool for improving outcomes. Agencies attend to be seen as cooperative without changing their programmes.

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015, pp.12-13) — ABA coordination overhead is only justified when it produces better outcomes. OCHA is "more successful at information sharing, but much weaker in identifying multidisciplinary issues, strengthening coordination between clusters, and following-up on identified issues."

**Detection:** Ask: "Name one decision made in the last three coordination meetings that changed a programme activity." If no specific decisions can be named → coordination theatre.

**Corrective action:** Restructure coordination around explicit decision points (duplication resolution, joint targeting, shared data) with assigned accountability and follow-up logs.

---

### Pattern 4: Premature Exit

**How it manifests:** Programme exits before target outcomes are achieved, local capacity is confirmed, or sustainability mechanisms are in place. Exit is driven by funding cycle end, not by readiness criteria.

**Why it happens:** Humanitarian funding cycles (typically 12–18 months) are mismatched with ABA timeframes (5+ years for genuine community-driven programming).

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015, p.16) — "short timelines mean that not all relevant issues can be dealt with." BRC Haiti and NRC Baghdad both found that trust-building and participation required much longer timelines than initial funding allowed.

**Detection:** Check against [[wiki/10-transition-scale/handover-readiness-checklist]] — if minimum handover conditions are not met, exit is premature.

**Corrective action:** Document handover readiness criteria at programme design stage; build exit conditions into funding agreements from the start.

## Detection Checklist

Before accepting any programme's ABA claim, verify:

- [ ] Area selected based on documented vulnerability/need analysis (not operational convenience)
- [ ] Three+ interdependent sectors addressed through a shared area entry point (not co-located parallel programmes)
- [ ] Community engagement mechanism exists with documented instances where input changed design
- [ ] Coordination structure produces decisions with accountability, not just information sharing
- [ ] Timeline matches scope — participation requirements are feasible in the available time
- [ ] Exit/handover criteria matched to readiness, not to funding end dates
