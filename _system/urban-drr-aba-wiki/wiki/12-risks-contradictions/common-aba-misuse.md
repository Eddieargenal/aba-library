# Common ABA Misuse Patterns

## Purpose

Documents recurring patterns where area-based approaches are applied incorrectly, superficially, or in ways that harm target populations. Enables agents to flag risk when a proposed programme design matches a known misuse pattern.

---

## Misuse Pattern Registry

### Pattern 1: Geographic Placement ≠ Area-Based

**How it manifests:** A programme operating in a defined neighbourhood or district is described as area-based without meeting the multi-sector or participatory criteria. The programme's entry point is a sector or beneficiary group — geographic location is incidental.

**Why it happens:** "Area-based" is used as a label to satisfy donors or cluster requirements without actually changing programme design.

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015) — the paper's central finding is that this is the most widespread misuse. The three defining criteria (geographic, multi-sector, participatory) must all be present. "A programme can be multi-sectoral, but not area-based (if it does not take a participatory or geographically targeted approach)." (p.21)

**Detection:** Ask: "What is the entry point — area, sector, or beneficiary group?" If the answer is sector or beneficiary group, it is not ABA regardless of where it is located.

**Corrective action:** Reframe as a geographically-located sector programme, or restructure to meet all three criteria.

---

### Pattern 2: Multi-Sector Label on Parallel Single-Sector Programming

**How it manifests:** Multiple sector activities operate in the same area but are designed, implemented, and monitored independently. There is no shared area profile, no joint planning, and no integrated coordination mechanism. The programme is called "multi-sector" or "integrated."

**Why it happens:** Funding streams are sector-specific; coordination overhead is underestimated; agencies lack cross-sector technical capacity.

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015), Box 2 — "Multi-sector or integrated programmes are not always participatory, nor are they necessarily geographically targeted." The multi-sector criterion for ABA requires integrated design, not parallel activities.

**Detection:** Ask: "Are sectors designed jointly with a shared area entry point, or designed separately and co-located?" If separately designed → not ABA.

**Corrective action:** Establish a shared area coordination mechanism with joint planning and accountability.

---

### Pattern 3: Participation Washing

**How it manifests:** One or two community meetings are held at the start of a programme. The community is informed of the programme design. This is recorded as "participatory engagement." Programme design does not change based on community input.

**Why it happens:** Genuine participation takes time and technical skill; informing is faster and less disruptive to pre-approved programme plans.

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015, p.10) — participation requires active involvement in planning and decision-making, not just "formal inclusion." The paper notes that case studies typically have residents "contributing to processes established by humanitarian agencies, rather than humanitarian agencies supporting local residents to drive their own process."

**Detection:** Ask: "At what point did community input change the design? What was proposed, what was changed, and who made that decision?" If the answer is vague or describes informing → participation washing.

**Corrective action:** Establish ongoing community engagement mechanisms with documented decision points where community input changes programme decisions.

---

### Pattern 4: Area Selection Without Vulnerability Analysis

**How it manifests:** The target area is selected because it is operationally convenient (existing field office, pre-existing relationships, cluster assignment) rather than because it has a documented concentration of need, vulnerability, or hazard.

**Why it happens:** Area selection is treated as an administrative starting point rather than a substantive analytical decision.

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015, pp.8-10) — geographic targeting requires that the area "defines the entry point" based on concentration of problems. Administrative boundary assignment without vulnerability analysis does not meet this criterion.

**Detection:** Ask: "What vulnerability or risk data was used to select this area over adjacent areas? Show the criteria." If the answer is "it's our operational zone" or "the cluster assigned it" → misuse.

**Corrective action:** Run area selection through Tool #02 with documented vulnerability criteria, or acknowledge the programme is geographically-located but not area-targeted.

---

### Pattern 5: Coordination Theatre

**How it manifests:** Area-based coordination meetings are established, meeting minutes are produced, but no decisions are made, no activities are adjusted, and duplication persists. Meeting attendance is logged as evidence of coordination.

**Why it happens:** Coordination is treated as a process obligation rather than a tool for improving programme outcomes.

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015, p.12-13) — ABA creates coordination overhead that is only justified when it produces better outcomes. OCHA is "more successful at information sharing, but much weaker in identifying multidisciplinary issues, strengthening coordination between clusters, and following-up on identified issues."

**Detection:** Ask: "Name one decision made in the last three coordination meetings that changed a programme activity." If no specific decisions can be named → coordination theatre.

**Corrective action:** Restructure coordination around decision points (duplication resolution, joint targeting, shared data) with assigned accountability and follow-up.

---

### Pattern 6: Premature Exit

**How it manifests:** Programme exits before target outcomes are achieved, local capacity is confirmed, or sustainability mechanisms are in place. Exit is driven by funding cycle end, not by readiness criteria.

**Why it happens:** Humanitarian funding cycles (typically 12-18 months) are mismatched to ABA timeframes (5+ years for genuine community-driven programming). Parker & Maynard: "short timelines mean that not all relevant issues can be dealt with."

**Source:** Parker & Maynard (2015, p.16) — programme duration must match scope. BRC Haiti and NRC Baghdad both found that trust-building and participation required much longer timelines than initial funding allowed.

**Detection:** Check against [[wiki/10-transition-scale/handover-readiness-checklist]] — if minimum handover conditions are not met, exit is premature.

**Corrective action:** Document handover conditions at programme design stage; build exit criteria into funding agreements.

---

## Detection Checklist

Before accepting any programme's ABA claim, verify:

- [ ] Area was selected based on documented vulnerability/need analysis (not operational convenience)
- [ ] Three+ interdependent sectors are addressed through a shared area entry point (not co-located parallel programmes)
- [ ] Community engagement mechanism exists with documented instances where community input changed programme design
- [ ] Coordination structure produces decisions with accountability, not just information sharing
- [ ] Timeline matches scope (participation requirements are feasible in the available time)
- [ ] Exit/handover criteria are documented and matched to readiness, not to funding end dates
