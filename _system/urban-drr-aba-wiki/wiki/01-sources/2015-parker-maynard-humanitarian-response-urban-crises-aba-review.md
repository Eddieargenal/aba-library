# Summary

Parker and Maynard's 2015 IIED working paper (commissioned by DFID) reviews how area-based approaches have been used in humanitarian response to urban crises. Drawing on case studies from Kabul, Port-au-Prince, Baghdad, and Tacloban, plus policy literature, the paper proposes three defining characteristics of ABA: geographically targeted, multi-sectoral, and participatory — **all three must be present simultaneously**. It is the primary definitional reference for what distinguishes an area-based approach from location-based or multi-sector-only programming. The paper also catalogues why ABA is being adopted, its positive and negative consequences, and eight good-practice principles for implementation.

---

# Key Concepts

## The Three ABA Criteria (p.3, p.8, p.21)

The paper's central and most-cited claim: area-based approaches in urban contexts share three defining characteristics — all three are required.

**a) Geographically targeted** (pp.8-10)
- Defines an area, rather than a sector or target group, as the main entry point
- Area selection combines administrative and/or physical boundaries with social analysis
- Programme scope is determined by the spatial concentration of problems
- Typical scale in the case studies: neighbourhood/district level (~1,400–6,000+ households)
- Two boundary types identified: administrative (aligned to government units) and physical (natural features — roads, rivers, topography)

**b) Participatory** (p.10)
- Strong emphasis on community and wider stakeholder engagement
- Not merely informing residents but active participation in planning, assessment, and decision-making
- Local authorities and civil society engagement is critical
- USAID/OFDA: "success is dependent on the active involvement of community stakeholders in a highly consultative planning process"
- Case studies used tools like PASSA (Participatory Approach for Safe Shelter Awareness)

**c) Multi-sectoral** (pp.10-11)
- Addresses multiple interdependent sector needs — shelter, WASH, cash, DRR, livelihoods, health, governance
- Multi-sector integration must be design-level, not just referral-level
- NRC/Baghdad: multi-sector area-based teams with ad-hoc specialist support

## Box 2: How ABA Differs from Neighbourhood/Integrated/Multi-Sector Approaches (p.11)

- Neighbourhood/settlement approaches: can be described as ABA if they meet all three criteria — geographic, participatory, multi-sector
- Integrated/multi-sectoral: not always participatory, and not necessarily geographically targeted as a primary entry point
- Holistic: from the community's perspective — sectors are artificial distinctions

## Box 3: Targeting Approaches Compared (p.13)

Parker and Maynard situate ABA within a taxonomy of urban targeting strategies:
- Area-based: target high-concentration locations
- Market-based: re-establish supply/distribution/market mechanisms
- Systems-based: rehabilitate critical infrastructure (water, sanitation, roads, health, education)
- Institution-based: target by affiliation (school, clinic, workplace) or support local authorities
- Individual/household: means testing, proxy targeting, self-targeting

"Deciding which approach is most appropriate depends on: proportion of population needing assistance, type of programme, trade-offs between targeting cost and accuracy, and feasibility of options" (p.13).

# Why ABA Is Being Adopted (Section 2.2, pp.11-13)

Five urban challenges driving interest in ABA:

1. **Beneficiary identification is problematic**: Urban vulnerability is often tied to infrastructure and services shared across areas — individual/household targeting misses this
2. **Different entry points are required**: Cities are complex systems where a household or sector entry point is insufficient — "paradigm shift" to larger-scale intervention called for by IASC
3. **Engaging/advocating approach needed**: IRC: "far more engaging with existing services, advocating for access and supporting local governments than direct service delivery"
4. **Gaps in inter-cluster coordination**: OCHA is weaker on identifying multidisciplinary issues across clusters in urban settings
5. **Greater non-humanitarian partnership needed**: Municipal government, civil society, private sector engagement is essential and exceeds what cluster coordination provides

---

# Positive Consequences of ABA (Section 2.3, pp.14-15)

**Relationships and equality:**
- Act as catalyst for local change — strengthens community-government links
- Prevent creation or reinforcement of tensions between displaced and host populations (inclusive approach)

**Resource efficiency:**
- Prevent consultation fatigue — area focus vs. sector-by-sector community consultation
- Focus resources effectively when problems are geographically concentrated
- Prevent over-stretching mainstream programmes
- Increase impact through tight-boundary resource concentration and duplication avoidance

**Economic:**
- Attract inward investment; boost image of deprived areas

---

# Negative Consequences of ABA (Section 2.3, pp.15-16)

**Cross-neighbourhood:**
- Create inequalities outside defined area — Katye programme reported "tension with surrounding neighbourhoods not included" (p.15)
- Negatively affect surrounding areas — "bandwagon effect" drawing agencies to existing initiatives rather than underserved areas

**Governance:**
- Disconnect between local plans and wider city or regional plans
- Distraction from underlying structural/economic causes
- Shift responsibility onto local actors who have responsibility but no control over structural drivers

**Implementation:**
- Costly — "costs of integration rise with scale and complexity" (Vrbensky 2009)
- Slow — participatory processes require 5+ years; humanitarian funding typically 18 months
- Difficult to monitor and evaluate — urban mobility complicates attribution; BRC Haiti experienced this directly

---

# Eight Good-Practice Principles (Section 2.4, pp.16-20)

a. **Adopt ABA selectively** — compare advantages/disadvantages with systems, market, institution-based approaches first
b. **Define and test scope, outcomes, intended impacts** — manage expectations; communicate clearly
c. **Match timeline to scope** — 18-month humanitarian cycles often insufficient; NRC Baghdad required 12-month trust-building phase
d. **Link to wider city/regional plans** — vertical integration of plans is costly but necessary for sustainability
e. **Early and ongoing engagement with local government, civil society, other stakeholders** — humanitarian actor as facilitator, not service deliverer
f. **Early and ongoing engagement with residents** — community-driven, not merely community-informed
g. **Design for flexibility** — programme structures, staff, funding must adapt to rapidly changing post-crisis contexts
h. **Demonstrate results early** — small visible interventions build trust and maintain participation momentum (NRC Baghdad, Katye programme)

# Case Studies Documented

| Programme | Context | Sectors | Key lesson |
|---|---|---|---|
| CARE KASS, Kabul (2006-2007) | Post-conflict, unplanned settlements | Shelter, WASH, roads, governance | Community Council engagement; MoU with municipality for land tenure security |

# Coordination Implications

- Area-based approaches represent a coordination mechanism; in urban contexts, "multi-sector geographical coordination could replace the 'compartmentalised' cluster system" (Groupe URD, cited p.13)
- ABA requires humanitarian actors to take a **facilitating** role (not service delivery) — supporting local government, civil society, private sector
- Inter-cluster coordination gaps in urban settings are a structural driver of ABA adoption
- Vertical integration (neighbourhood → district → city → national) is essential but requires sustained investment

---

# DRR Implications

- DRR content is **limited** in this paper — it focuses on post-disaster recovery and post-conflict response, not hazard-risk reduction programming
- Indirectly relevant: geographic targeting in ABA should be informed by hazard/exposure/vulnerability data, not just service need
- Case studies include DRR activities (KASS: hazard mitigation training; BRC: PASSA methodology includes local hazard assessment)
- Limitation: 2015 paper predates Sendai Framework integration into ABA practice

---

# Limitations and Cautions

1. **Date**: 2015 — field has evolved significantly with GSC Compendium (2019), IASC ToR (2026), and practice in Syria, South Sudan, Yemen since publication
2. **Geographic gaps in evidence base**: Limited documentation from Latin America, New Zealand, Japan, Africa
3. **Language limitation**: Only English-language materials included
4. **Scale**: Mainly neighbourhood/district scale ABA — city-wide ABA consequences not well-evidenced
5. **M&E challenge**: Paper notes difficulty of attribution in urban ABA contexts; evidence base remains thin
6. **Practitioner knowledge gap**: "The majority of 'good practice' rests within the experiences of practitioners" — not captured in published literature at time of writing
