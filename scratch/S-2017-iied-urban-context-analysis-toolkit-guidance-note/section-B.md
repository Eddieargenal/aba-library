## Known Tensions

### TNS-001: Assumption of representative sampling vs. structural bias risk

**Tension type:** Methodological

**First position (ASM-002):** The toolkit assumes that adequate diversity of participants — including women, marginalized groups, ethnic minorities, and displaced populations — can be achieved through deliberate sampling design and careful team composition. Guidance across Steps 5–6 and the validation workshop treats representation as an achievable standard if proper steps are followed.

**Conflicting position (RISK-001):** The toolkit simultaneously acknowledges that relying on key powerholders to organize participation is a structural bias risk that deliberate design may not fully overcome. Teams operating under time pressure default to accessible participant channels (community leaders, gatekeepers), which systematically exclude marginalized voices.

**Why it matters:** If the assumption holds, the toolkit's guidance on deliberate sampling constitutes sufficient mitigation and practitioners can follow it with confidence. If RISK-001 dominates, the toolkit overstates its ability to produce representative data in access-constrained urban settings, and practitioners may need to supplement with additional bias-mitigation methods not described in this document. The source does not resolve this tension — it is an honest design limitation.

**Related objects:** ASM-002, RISK-001, METHOD-004, SAFE-002

---

### Internal Contradiction: Primary use case vs. rapid-response adaptability (low confidence)

The document states the toolkit "is generally designed with the assumption of approximately two weeks in country of data collection" and assumes prior country presence (p. 9), while simultaneously noting in footnote 3 that it can be adapted for rapid-response by referencing alternative tools (GECARR, IRC conflict sensitivity toolkit). This creates mild tension between the explicit primary use case (established in-country organisations conducting structured analysis) and the implied adaptability of the approach. RULE-009 partially addresses this by pointing practitioners to alternative tools when the toolkit's full structure is not appropriate.

### Partner and Team Composition

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-003`** — Restrict or exclude new local partners when significant conflict or protection dynamics are present

- **Condition:** When considering including new local or international partners in context analysis execution, and a significant conflict or protection dynamic is active.
- **Action:** Assess three factors before inclusion: (1) protection concerns for partner staff in data collection; (2) risks of bias from partner that cannot be mitigated through team composition; (3) risks to participants from public sharing of findings. Exercise caution — do not include by default.
- **Exception:** Partners with established conflict-sensitivity protocols, strong data protection practices, and pre-existing trusted community relationships may be included with documented safeguards.
- **Cross-references:** IMPL-003 (joint analysis design), RISK-003 (partner bias risk), TOOL-003
- **⚠ human_review_required:** The source does not define criteria for what constitutes a "significant" conflict or protection dynamic — requires contextual judgment.

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-013`** — Recruit facilitators and interviewers from the same social groups as target participants

- **Condition:** When composing the data collection team for any analysis involving specific displaced or minority population groups.
- **Action:** Recruit interviewers and facilitators from the same country, city, or social group as participants (same refugee community, same ethnic minority, same gender). Use counterparts trained in question nuances rather than generic translators. Where same-group recruitment is not possible, document the gap and take mitigating steps.
- **Rationale:** Source states this as "essential" — strongest normative language used for any team composition guidance. Ties to LESSON-002 (Dar es Salaam identity masking case).
- **Cross-references:** IMPL-001 (team composition), METHOD-003 (KII), METHOD-004 (FGD), RULE-001, SAFE-002

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-010`** — Conduct participatory planning workshop when in partnership or when staff have limited local knowledge

- **Condition:** When analysis is conducted in formal partnership with another organisation, OR when team staff have limited knowledge of local communities in target areas.
- **Action:** Conduct an optional participatory data collection planning workshop (half-day to one-day) with locally-knowledgeable stakeholders before fieldwork begins. Use Optional Tool: City overview workshop.
- **Exception:** Not required when the team has strong existing local knowledge and is not in formal partnership.
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-004, METHOD-006, RULE-003

### Data Collection Design

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-001`** — Separate FGDs by gender and age with same-sex facilitators

- **Condition:** When planning FGDs in urban humanitarian context analysis, particularly when adolescents are included or gender/protection sensitivities are present.
- **Action:** Plan separate FGDs for men and women / adolescent boys and girls; assign same-sex facilitators to each group.
- **Rationale:** "Adolescents in particular may be less likely to speak in a mixed-gender group" (p. 25); essential for capturing women's and girls' experience of barriers to service access.
- **Cross-references:** METHOD-004, TOOL-005, PRINCIPLE-002, RULE-011 (extends to validation workshop)

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-007`** — Complete desk review 2–3 weeks before primary data collection

- **Condition:** When planning the timeline for urban context analysis, prior to initiating KIIs and FGDs.
- **Action:** Complete desk review 2–3 weeks before primary data collection begins; use the window to revise the data collection plan (Step 5) based on gaps identified.
- **Exception:** In rapid response contexts, timeline may be compressed, but desk review must still precede fieldwork.
- **Cross-references:** METHOD-002, TOOL-002, TOOL-004

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-008`** — Adapt question guides to each context — never use default templates without adaptation

- **Condition:** When preparing KII and FGD question guides for any new urban context analysis.
- **Action:** (1) Eliminate irrelevant questions or those already answered by desk review; (2) add context-specific questions; (3) validate with country staff; (4) brief the team on protection risks around sensitive questions (GBV, child exploitation). Note: questions on legal protection are less relevant for IDP contexts than refugee contexts.
- **Rationale:** "Just as no context is the same, no context analysis will be the same" (p. 25) — un-adapted guides risk collecting irrelevant data and missing critical local dynamics.
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-005, FRAMEWORK-001, IMPL-007

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-009`** — Use a dedicated gender analysis tool when gender equality is the sole analytical interest

- **Condition:** When the organisation's analytical interest is limited to gender equality as the primary or sole focus.
- **Action:** Conduct an in-depth gender analysis (e.g. IRC Gender Analysis and Program Design Toolkit) instead of this toolkit. The toolkit's gender lens is cross-cutting, not a substitute for dedicated gender analysis.
- **Exception:** When gender is one of multiple analytical interests — in that case, the toolkit's cross-cutting gender theme is appropriate.
- **Cross-references:** PRINCIPLE-002, FRAMEWORK-001

### Protection and Data Security

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-004`** — Apply Do No Harm throughout all data collection and analysis activities

- **Condition:** Applies at all stages — questionnaire design, sampling, data collection, analysis, and findings communication.
- **Action:** Apply Do No Harm lens at each stage: tailor questionnaires to avoid exacerbating tensions; apply geographic and social sensitivity in sampling; ensure findings communication does not expose participants to harm; assess how programmes might inadvertently increase tensions or undermine local systems.
- **Cross-references:** PRINCIPLE-001, FRAMEWORK-001, TOOL-005, RULE-005, RULE-012

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-005`** — Protect participant data — anonymize, limit access, destroy raw notes after analysis

- **Condition:** After primary data collection is complete and debrief notes have been consolidated.
- **Action:** Store interview notes securely with access limited to few persons; notes must not include identifiable information (names, specific locations); destroy raw notes after analysis completes unless retention is specifically planned and resourced (with full transcription and anonymization).
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-006, TOOL-005, SAFE-001, RISK-002

### Analysis and Validation

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-006`** — Triangulate findings from multiple sources before concluding analysis

- **Condition:** During Step 7 data analysis and interpretation.
- **Action:** Triangulate information across multiple sources, methods (desk review + KII + FGD), and team perspectives; ensure team members and key stakeholders have opportunity to challenge findings before conclusions are drawn.
- **Cross-references:** METHOD-006, TOOL-007, IMPL-005

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-011`** — Designate women-only working groups in validation workshop when women face obstacles to mixed participation

- **Condition:** When women face obstacles to participating in mixed group discussions during the validation workshop.
- **Action:** Designate women-only working groups with documented products; ensure facilitators include these contributions in synthesis; use targeted invitations rather than blanket organisational invitations to ensure gender diversity.
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-009, METHOD-006, RULE-001, PRINCIPLE-002

### Communication and Reporting

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-012`** — Produce internal-only report when findings contain sensitive or confidential information

- **Condition:** When context analysis findings contain sensitive contextual, political, or protection information, or commercially confidential content; assessed before beginning the write-up process.
- **Action:** Produce internal-only report (Tool 10 assumes this by default); clarify before writing whether an external summary is also needed; when sharing externally, anonymize all sources and respect informed consent commitments.
- **Exception:** If analysis did not involve sensitive dynamics, a non-sensitive external brief may be produced alongside the internal report (Maiduguri case lesson, LESSON-003).
- **Cross-references:** TOOL-010, IMPL-006, RULE-005, LESSON-003

### Context Analysis Currency

**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-RULE-002`** — Reassess context analysis after 6–9 months or significant context change

- **Condition:** When any of these triggers apply: (a) findings are more than 6–9 months old before use for programming; (b) significant crisis change occurred (policy change, major incident, resolution); (c) new data collection is planned; (d) new proposal requires deeper context information.
- **Action:** Revise or redo the context analysis; partial update is acceptable when changes are limited to specific themes or sub-themes.
- **Limitations:** The 6–9 month threshold is a practitioner heuristic, not an evidence-based figure; highly volatile contexts may require more frequent updates.
- **Cross-references:** METHOD-001, TOOL-010, RISK-004
