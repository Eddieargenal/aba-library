## Key Concepts

### Urban Context Analysis Thematic Framework
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-FRAMEWORK-001`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A systems-thinking framework organising urban context analysis around five interconnected thematic areas: (1) politics and governance (stakeholder power, governance structure, policy/legal frameworks); (2) social and cultural (social cohesion, identities, protection risks); (3) economic (labour market, livelihoods, constraints); (4) service delivery and infrastructure (access, supply, coordination); and (5) space and settlements (urban plans, environmental risks, housing/land, public space). Do No Harm and gender equality serve as cross-cutting themes integrated throughout all five areas. The framework is applied across all phases: framing questions (Step 3), desk review (Tool 2), question guides (Tools 5A–5G), debrief synthesis (Tool 6), and final findings analysis (Tool 7). Based on Campbell (2016) ALNAP typology of urban systems; explicitly systems-thinking in design — aims to understand both individual thematic areas and their inter-relations.

*Limitations:* Qualitative only; designed primarily for displacement crises; assumes sufficient time and access to explore all five themes; may require selective focus under resource constraints.

---

### Urban Displacement Crisis Typology
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TYPOLOGY-001`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A four-category classification used to frame the scope and focus of the context analysis, differentiating both the origin and location of crisis:

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| I | Conflict that causes internal displacement TO a city (IDPs) |
| II | Conflict that causes refugee displacement TO a city |
| III | Violent conflict WITHIN a city (in-situ urban population affected) |
| IV | Naturally triggered hazards WITHIN a city or displacement TO a city |

Applied in Step 2 to determine which populations of interest to prioritise, which sub-areas to select, and which thematic questions are most salient. Types are not mutually exclusive; multiple types may co-occur in the same city.

---

### Urban Context Analysis
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-CONCEPT-001`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A structured inquiry into the political, economic, social, service delivery, and spatial factors that enable or hinder effective crisis responses in urban settings. Answers "why" questions — why things are the way they are, how they are connected, what influences decision-makers — rather than "what" needs and gaps exist. Enables identification of entry points, stakeholder interests, power dynamics, governance structures, and systemic drivers of vulnerability. Distinct from needs assessment (which identifies gaps), vulnerability assessment (household-level), and macro-level political economy analysis (which is narrower in scope).

*Limitations:* Primarily designed for urban displacement crises; qualitative in nature; requires existing country presence; findings become outdated in dynamic contexts (see RULE-002 on 6–9 month reassessment).

---

### Sub-Area as Unit of Analysis
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-CONCEPT-002`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A geographically-bounded community within a city used as the primary spatial unit for primary data collection. May be defined by formally designated administrative boundaries (municipality or ward) or by physical landscape features (roads, rivers) combined with social analysis considerations, citing Parker and Maynard (2015). Approximately one to two days of data collection per sub-area is typical. The toolkit operates at two scales: city-wide level (governance, systems, stakeholders) and sub-area level (community dynamics, local service access).

*Limitations:* Sub-area boundaries are socially constructed and may not match administrative boundaries; urban heterogeneity may mean even a single sub-area contains multiple distinct communities.

---

### Context Analysis vs Needs Assessment Distinction
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-CONCEPT-003`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

A fundamental distinction between two complementary but non-substitutable analytical approaches. Context analysis focuses on "why" questions — systemic and structural factors, power dynamics, institutional capacities, and actor incentives. Needs assessment focuses on "what" questions — visible effects, current service gaps, and quantifiable needs. The source states explicitly: "A context analysis is not focused on needs and, therefore, must be understood as quite distinct from a needs assessment." Both are required; context analysis must complement, not replace, needs or vulnerability assessments.

---

### Entry Point Identification
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-CONCEPT-004`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual

The identification of practical and realistic opportunities where external humanitarian actors can engage productively with existing urban systems, actors, and processes to support crisis response. Entry points are grounded in analysis of power relations, stakeholder interests, institutional capacities, and governance structures — they represent what is contextually feasible, not only technically ideal. Positioned as the terminal output purpose of the entire context analysis process. Documented in Tool 8 (Programme implications). Urban entry points are more constrained than rural: more actors, more institutional layers, limited humanitarian leverage.

---

### Do No Harm
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-PRINCIPLE-001`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual | cross-cutting principle

Programmes must not increase tensions or undermine existing local systems — including existing service providers and local government support structures. Integrated as a cross-cutting analytical theme throughout all 10 steps and all 5 thematic areas. Operationalised through: Do No Harm guiding questions in Table 3; protection-sensitive data collection protocols (RULE-005, IMPL-004); programme implications analysis (Tool 8); and partner selection risk assessment (RULE-003). See also decision rule RULE-004 for operational application.

---

### Gender Equality Analytical Lens
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-PRINCIPLE-002`** | knowledge_layer: conceptual | cross-cutting principle

Every step of the context analysis must systematically examine gender disparities — differences between women and men in assigned responsibilities, activities, resource access, service access, and decision-making opportunities. Implemented through: gender-specific guiding questions (Table 3); gender-separated FGDs with same-sex facilitators (RULE-001); gender comparison column in Tool 7 findings analysis; and women-only working groups in validation workshops when contextually needed (RULE-011). Note: if gender equality is the primary focus, a dedicated in-depth gender analysis tool is recommended instead (RULE-009).

## Methods and Tools

### Methods

#### Urban Context Analysis Process (3-Phase, 10-Step Method)
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-001`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

A 3-phase, 10-step iterative workflow for conducting urban humanitarian context analysis, designed to be adaptable to varied urban crisis settings. Total elapsed time: typically 4–6 weeks (1–2 weeks preparation, 2 weeks data collection, 1–2 weeks analysis/documentation).

| Phase | Steps |
|---|---|
| Phase 1 — Preparation | Step 1: Launch (workplan, budget, team); Step 2: Frame analysis (scope, sub-areas, crisis type); Step 3: Select initial questions (adapt thematic framework) |
| Phase 2 — Data Collection | Step 4: Collate secondary data (desk review, stakeholder mapping); Step 5: Prepare primary data collection (plan, adapt guides, train team); Step 6: Carry out primary data collection (KIIs, FGDs, daily debriefs, mid-collection review) |
| Phase 3 — Analysis and Documentation | Step 7: Analyse data (key findings, programme implications); Step 8: Validation workshop; Step 9: Write final report; Step 10: Communicate findings |

The process is iterative — desk review shapes stakeholder mapping; early data collection findings refine question guides and sampling; mid-collection review adjusts focus; validation workshop challenges preliminary findings before final documentation.

*field_query_trigger: How do I structure a context analysis in an urban displacement crisis to understand systemic power, service, and governance dynamics before programme design?*

*Limitations:* Not prescriptive for programme design; primarily qualitative; assumes ~2-week in-country data collection baseline and existing country presence; not suitable for immediate response contexts.

---

#### Desk Review Method
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-002`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Secondary data synthesis conducted 2–3 weeks before primary data collection to map existing knowledge, identify gaps, and conceptualise efficient fieldwork. Synthesizes crisis background, demographic data, governance structures, service delivery systems, policies, and prior assessments. Duration: 2 days to 1 week. Led by team lead; in-country staff involvement required. Outputs: completed desk review (Tool 2), initial stakeholder analysis (partial Tool 3), revised thematic guiding questions, input to data collection plan (Tool 4).

*field_query_trigger: How do I conduct a desk review of secondary data to map existing knowledge and identify gaps before primary data collection in an urban humanitarian context analysis?*

---

#### Key Informant Interview (KII) Method
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-003`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Semi-structured interviews with influential stakeholders at city-wide and sub-area levels, informed by prior stakeholder analysis. Sampling uses purposive selection from Tool 3 expanded via snowball/referral (METHOD-008). Uses adapted Tools 5C–5G. Requires: team lead oversight of notetaking quality and probing; daily debriefs and mid-collection review; deliberate inclusion of "difficult stakeholders." KII notes must exclude identifiable information.

*field_query_trigger: How do I plan and conduct key informant interviews with government, civil society, and community actors to map power dynamics and service delivery in an urban displacement context?*

---

#### Focus Group Discussion (FGD) Method
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-004`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Structured group discussions with displaced and host community populations in selected urban sub-areas, separated by gender and age with same-sex facilitators (RULE-001). Uses adapted Tools 5A–5B. Requires: informed consent; protection risk briefing for all team members including translators; deliberate inclusion of marginalized groups; daily debrief and mid-collection gap review. FGD notes must exclude identifiable information.

*field_query_trigger: How do I facilitate focus group discussions with displaced and host community populations in urban sub-areas while managing protection risks and ensuring gender-disaggregated data?*

---

#### Stakeholder Analysis / Actor Mapping
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-005`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Iterative identification and analysis of influential stakeholders — type, level, role, interests, incentives, influence, and relationships. Updated progressively from desk review (Step 4, initial mapping) through data collection to final analysis (Step 7, full update). Explicit requirement to include "difficult stakeholders" — armed groups, gatekeepers, corrupt officials. Outputs: stakeholder analysis matrix (Tool 3), KII candidate list, coordination/partnership strategy inputs.

*field_query_trigger: How do I map and analyze key stakeholders to understand actor interests, influence, and relationships for programming and coordination in an urban humanitarian response?*

---

#### Participatory Validation Workshop
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-006`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

One- to two-facilitator workshop with 15–20 diverse participants (analysis team, consulted stakeholders, internal staff, external local partners) to challenge and refine preliminary findings before final documentation. Inputs: preliminary drafts of Tools 7, 8, and 3. Step is optional under time or resource constraints. Requires: gender diversity of participants; women-only working groups if contextually needed (RULE-011); structured facilitation (Tool 9); confidentiality of data sources maintained.

*field_query_trigger: How do I run a validation workshop to challenge and refine urban context analysis findings with diverse stakeholders before finalizing the report?*

---

#### Systems-Thinking Analytical Approach
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-007`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

Analytical approach that examines interconnections and relations between urban thematic areas rather than analysing each theme in isolation, based on Campbell (2016) ALNAP typology of urban systems. Requires covering all 5 themes; Do No Harm and gender equality applied cross-cuttingly, not separately; team debriefs specifically structured to surface cross-thematic patterns. Outputs: cross-thematic analytical findings, system-level entry points, structural risk drivers.

*field_query_trigger: How do I apply systems-thinking analysis to understand how political, economic, social, and spatial factors interact to shape urban crisis response effectiveness?*

---

#### Snowball / Referral Sampling
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-METHOD-008`** | knowledge_layer: diagnostic

During KIIs and FGDs, continuously solicit referrals from participants for additional informants, particularly to reach marginalized, displaced, or inaccessible groups. Key mechanism for reaching actors invisible in formal stakeholder analyses. Monitor for over-representation of particular networks; monitor daily for representation gaps in vulnerable groups.

*field_query_trigger: How do I use referral sampling to identify and reach marginalized or hard-to-access groups during primary data collection for urban context analysis?*

---

### Tools

The toolkit comprises 10 named instruments. All tools are available at the URLs listed in the document annexes (pp. 38–39).

#### Tool 1 — Urban Context Analysis Workplan and Budget Template
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-001`** | diagnostic

Illustrative workplan and budget template calibrated to a 14-day on-site analysis. Columns for activities, timeline, team time allocation, and budget estimation by line item. Used in Step 1 to plan, sequence, and cost the analysis. Must be adapted to context; does not cover contingency costs for security incidents.

*field_query_trigger: How do I plan and budget a 2–4 week urban humanitarian context analysis for a displacement crisis, including team composition and logistics costs?*

---

#### Tool 2 — Desk Review Summary Template
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-002`** | diagnostic

Template for reviewing secondary data by sub-theme of the thematic framework. Includes optional desk review memo format and guidance on secondary data sources per sub-theme. Must be completed 2–3 weeks before primary data collection. Bridges desk review findings to Tool 3 (stakeholder mapping), revised guiding questions, and Tool 4 (data collection planning).

*field_query_trigger: What secondary data should I collect, document, and analyse before conducting field interviews for an urban humanitarian context analysis in a displacement setting?*

---

#### Tool 3 — Stakeholder Analysis Matrix
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-003`** | diagnostic

Multi-column spreadsheet matrix for listing, categorizing, and analyzing stakeholders. Columns B–E (populated Step 4): stakeholder name/position, type, level, role. Columns F–J (populated Step 7): importance to programme, engagement strategy, actor incentives. Cross-phase tool — used in Steps 4, 5–6, and 7. Must include "difficult stakeholders" explicitly.

*field_query_trigger: How do I map and analyze key stakeholders — government, civil society, private sector, and difficult actors — for a humanitarian programme in an urban displacement context?*

---

#### Tool 4 — Data Collection Plan Template
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-004`** | diagnostic

Two-tab template: Tab 4A for sub-area sampling (FGDs + sub-area KIIs); Tab 4B for city-wide sampling (institutional and governance KIIs). Covers who to interview, with what tools, with what sampling approach, with what logistics. Updated continuously throughout data collection as snowball referrals expand the sample.

*field_query_trigger: How do I plan data collection logistics — who to interview, with what sampling approach — across multiple sub-areas and city-wide for an urban humanitarian context analysis?*

---

#### Tools 5A–5G — KII and FGD Question Guide Set
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-005`** | diagnostic

Set of 7 sample semi-structured guides: Tool 5A (FGD, displaced populations), Tool 5B (FGD, host communities), Tool 5C (KII, influential stakeholders), Tool 5D (KII, service providers), Tool 5E (KII, labour/business climate), Tool 5F (KII, local government), Tool 5G (KII, NGO service providers). All aligned with the 5-theme + Do No Harm + gender framework. Must be adapted for every context (RULE-008): remove irrelevant questions, add context-specific questions, validate with country staff, pilot in target sub-areas. Protection briefing for all team members required before use.

*field_query_trigger: What interview and focus group questions should I use with displaced populations, host communities, local government, and service providers during an urban context analysis field assessment?*

---

#### Tool 6 — KII and FGD Debrief Template
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-006`** | diagnostic

Two-sheet template for structured daily and mid-collection team synthesis. Sheet 1 maps preliminary findings per sub-area to the thematic framework; Column G identifies which question guide modules populate each sub-theme. Sheet 2 lists local service providers identified during data collection. Used in Steps 6 and 7; bridges data collection to formal analysis. Mid-collection review requires cleaning notes in Tool 6 and sharing with team.

*field_query_trigger: How do I structure team debriefs and mid-collection reviews to identify data gaps and adjust sampling during an urban context analysis fieldwork phase?*

---

#### Tool 7 — Key Findings Analysis Template
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-007`** | diagnostic

Two-tab template. Tab 7A: organises all context analysis findings by sub-area and theme, with columns for cross-area comparison (Column I), gender comparison (Column J), population comparison (Column K), age comparison (Column L), identified risks (Column M), and programming implications (Column N). Tab 7B (optional): dividers and connectors analysis to further investigate social cohesion factors. Used in Step 7 to consolidate desk review and primary data.

*field_query_trigger: How do I consolidate and analyse urban context analysis findings across sub-areas, themes, gender, and population groups to identify programming entry points?*

---

#### Tool 8 — Programme Implications Template
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-008`** | diagnostic

Five-table template translating key findings and stakeholder analysis into programming action. Tables cover: (1) programming opportunities, geographic priorities, sub-population needs, constraints; (2) potential partners by sector and geography; (3) advocacy issues and partners; (4) risk mitigation strategies; (5) strategies to harness connectors. Used in Step 7; inputs to validation workshop (Step 8). Sourced from columns F–O of Tool 7 and Tool 3.

*field_query_trigger: How do I translate urban context analysis findings into programming opportunities, partnership options, risk mitigation strategies, and advocacy priorities?*

---

#### Tool 9 — Urban Analysis Validation Workshop
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-009`** | diagnostic

One-day urban analysis workshop tool containing: (a) draft workshop agenda for 15–20 diverse participants; (b) participant list template; (c) facilitators' agenda with group activities, session structure, and notetaking arrangements. Input tools: preliminary drafts of Tools 7, 8, and 3. Outputs: refined versions of Tools 7, 8, and 3 plus participant list.

*field_query_trigger: How do I facilitate a one-day stakeholder validation workshop to challenge and refine preliminary urban context analysis findings before finalizing the report?*

---

#### Tool 10 — Urban Context Analysis Final Report Outline
**`S-2017-iied-urban-context-analysis-toolkit-guidance-note-TOOL-010`** | diagnostic

Table of contents and summary methodological tables for an internal context analysis report. Provides structure for synthesizing all analysis outputs into a final document. Assumes internal audience; sensitive and commercially confidential findings should be reviewed before external sharing (RULE-012). Note: a final written report is optional — validation workshop outputs may suffice (ASM-003).

*field_query_trigger: How do I structure an internal urban context analysis report to document findings on governance, social dynamics, services, and programming implications for a displacement setting?*
