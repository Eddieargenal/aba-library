## Summary

This guidance note introduces a practical urban context analysis toolkit for humanitarian practitioners working in urban crises, especially displacement settings. Developed by the International Rescue Committee (IRC) Stronger Cities Initiative and published by IIED under the Urban Crises Learning Fund (funded by DFID), the toolkit was piloted in five urban crisis contexts: Maiduguri (Nigeria), Juba (South Sudan), Amman (Jordan), Dar es Salaam (Tanzania), and Bangkok (Thailand).

The toolkit provides a user-friendly, relatively quick, and adaptable mechanism for understanding complex urban dynamics before programme design and implementation. It is structured as a 3-phase, 10-step process supported by 10 practical instruments (workplan, desk review summary, stakeholder analysis matrix, data collection plan, 7 KII/FGD question guides, debrief template, key findings analysis, programme implications template, validation workshop tool, and final report outline). The toolkit enables users to identify relevant stakeholders, existing power relations, resource distribution, governance and legal frameworks, sources of livelihoods, social networks, and access to services — information that helps determine suitable entry points and improve the effectiveness and responsiveness of humanitarian programmes.

A critical scope boundary is explicitly stated: context analysis is not a substitute for needs or vulnerability assessments. Context analysis answers "why" questions (structural and systemic factors shaping crisis dynamics), while needs assessment answers "what" questions (visible effects and service gaps). Both are required. The document also notes the toolkit is primarily designed for organisations already established in-country; teams needing rapid response context analysis are directed to alternative tools (GECARR, IRC conflict sensitivity toolkit).

The analysis is organized around five interconnected thematic areas — politics and governance, social and cultural, economic, service delivery and infrastructure, and space and settlements — with Do No Harm and gender equality as mandatory cross-cutting lenses. A systems-thinking approach examines interconnections between thematic areas rather than analysing each in isolation. The toolkit has been tested and refined through field implementation, though the evidence base remains practitioner-generated rather than peer-reviewed, giving it mixed composite authority.
