# Protection and Do-No-Harm Risk Screen Framework

**Sources:** Urban ABA guidance; IRC, *Urban Context Analysis Toolkit*; ALNAP, *Good Practice Review 12: Humanitarian Protection*  
**Years:** 2017 / 2019  
**Lifecycle stage:** 4. Joint risk and needs prioritization  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to screen proposed area-based priorities before finalization to ensure they do not increase eviction, exclusion, violence, social tension, aid capture, GBV risks, or unsafe access.

## Framework Overview

The **Protection and Do-No-Harm Risk Screen Framework** helps teams review proposed priorities in an Area-Based Approach (ABA) before decisions are finalized.

It is designed for the joint risk and needs prioritization stage, when teams have already identified needs, risks, services, stakeholders, and possible interventions. The framework tests whether proposed priorities could unintentionally create harm, reinforce exclusion, increase conflict, expose people to violence, enable gatekeeping, or worsen protection risks.

This framework is especially important in mixed resident/displaced neighborhoods, informal settlements, contested tenure areas, areas affected by armed actors, and contexts where assistance may shift power, access, or resource distribution.

## Core Framework Question

**Could the proposed area priorities, targeting decisions, activities, or delivery mechanisms increase protection risks or cause unintended harm?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Eviction and tenure risk | Whether assistance, repairs, infrastructure, visibility, or registration could increase eviction, rent increases, land disputes, or landlord pressure. | Area-based work can change the perceived value of land, housing, or services and expose insecure residents to harm. |
| Exclusion risk | Whether some groups may be left out because of documentation, status, gender, age, disability, tenure, displacement, ethnicity, income, language, or social position. | Prioritization can unintentionally reinforce existing inequalities. |
| Violence and coercion risk | Whether activities could expose people to threats, intimidation, armed actors, domestic violence, exploitation, or retaliation. | Assistance can shift local power and create new safety risks. |
| Social tension risk | Whether aid decisions could increase tension between residents, displaced people, host communities, renters, landlords, ethnic groups, political groups, or neighborhoods. | Area-based targeting can create perceptions of unfairness if not clearly justified and communicated. |
| Aid capture and gatekeeping risk | Whether powerful actors could control beneficiary lists, information, access, permissions, complaints, or resource distribution. | Gatekeepers can distort targeting, exclude vulnerable groups, or demand favors. |
| GBV and safeguarding risk | Whether women, girls, boys, men, people with disabilities, LGBTQI+ people, or other groups could face harassment, exploitation, unsafe access, or abuse linked to assistance. | Activities must be designed to reduce exposure to abuse and ensure safe reporting and referral. |
| Unsafe access risk | Whether people must travel through unsafe areas, wait in public, disclose sensitive information, or interact with harmful actors to receive assistance. | Delivery mechanisms can create risks even when the assistance itself is appropriate. |
| Data and confidentiality risk | Whether information collection, maps, lists, photos, or referrals could expose people to targeting, stigma, eviction, retaliation, or exploitation. | Poor data handling can create direct protection harm. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a structured protection screening process:

1. **List proposed priorities**  
   Identify the activities, areas, groups, services, infrastructure, and delivery mechanisms being considered for prioritization.

2. **Identify affected groups**  
   Clarify who may benefit, who may be excluded, who may be indirectly affected, and who may face increased risk.

3. **Screen for protection risks**  
   Review each proposed priority against eviction, exclusion, violence, social tension, aid capture, GBV, unsafe access, and confidentiality risks.

4. **Analyze power and incentives**  
   Identify actors who may gain, lose, control access, block assistance, manipulate decisions, or retaliate.

5. **Assess likelihood and severity**  
   Rate each risk based on how likely it is and how serious the consequences would be.

6. **Identify mitigation measures**  
   Adjust targeting, delivery, communication, referral pathways, community engagement, monitoring, or safeguards.

7. **Decide whether to proceed, adapt, or stop**  
   Priorities with manageable risks may proceed with mitigation. Priorities with severe or unmanageable risks should be redesigned or rejected.

8. **Monitor for emerging harm**  
   Track complaints, exclusion patterns, safety incidents, tension, rumors, rent increases, access barriers, and unintended consequences during implementation.

## Protection Risk Screening Matrix

| Screening question | What to check |
|---|---|
| Could this priority increase eviction or rent pressure? | Housing repair, infrastructure upgrades, registration, public visibility, land-value changes, or landlord incentives. |
| Could this priority exclude vulnerable groups? | Documentation requirements, registration methods, communication channels, language, mobility, disability access, tenure status, or social stigma. |
| Could this priority increase violence or intimidation? | Armed actor interference, household tension, retaliation, conflict over resources, coercion, or unsafe distribution arrangements. |
| Could this priority increase social tension? | Unequal coverage, unclear targeting, perceived favoritism, resident/displaced tensions, political divisions, or neighborhood competition. |
| Could powerful actors capture the assistance? | Control over lists, permits, access, distribution points, community representation, complaint channels, or information flows. |
| Could this priority increase GBV or safeguarding risks? | Unsafe travel, public registration, lack of privacy, exploitative intermediaries, untrained staff, or absence of referral pathways. |
| Could access to assistance be unsafe? | Long travel distances, unsafe routes, checkpoints, queues, night travel, physical barriers, cost, or fear of authorities. |
| Could data collection create harm? | Sensitive personal data, household lists, photos, tenure information, protection disclosures, insecure storage, or unauthorized sharing. |

## Risk Rating

Use a simple risk rating to guide decisions.

| Rating | Meaning | Recommended action |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Risk is limited and manageable with standard safeguards. | Proceed with basic mitigation and monitoring. |
| Medium | Risk is plausible and could affect specific groups or locations. | Adapt design and add targeted mitigation before proceeding. |
| High | Risk is likely or consequences could be serious. | Redesign the priority, add strong safeguards, and seek specialist review. |
| Critical | Risk could cause severe harm or cannot be managed safely. | Do not proceed unless the priority is fundamentally redesigned. |

## Mitigation Options

| Risk type | Possible mitigation |
|---|---|
| Eviction and tenure risk | Conduct tenure analysis, avoid publicizing insecure households, engage landlords carefully, use occupancy agreements, monitor rent increases, and avoid upgrades that increase eviction incentives without safeguards. |
| Exclusion risk | Use multiple registration channels, mobile outreach, accessible communication, disability inclusion, language support, and targeted consultation with underrepresented groups. |
| Violence and coercion risk | Conduct conflict-sensitive analysis, avoid empowering harmful actors, use discreet delivery where needed, coordinate with protection actors, and monitor threats or intimidation. |
| Social tension risk | Communicate targeting criteria clearly, use transparent prioritization, include host and displaced populations where appropriate, and create feedback channels. |
| Aid capture risk | Verify beneficiary lists through multiple sources, separate information collection from gatekeepers, use confidential complaints, and rotate or cross-check community representatives. |
| GBV and safeguarding risk | Ensure safe access, privacy, trained staff, referral pathways, gender-sensitive scheduling, safeguarding protocols, and survivor-centered response. |
| Unsafe access risk | Select safe locations and times, reduce travel burden, use mobile delivery where needed, consider accompaniment, and avoid exposing people to checkpoints or public stigma. |
| Data risk | Minimize sensitive data, secure storage, restrict access, anonymize where possible, obtain informed consent, and avoid unnecessary sharing. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Priority selection | Filters out or redesigns priorities likely to create serious harm. |
| Targeting | Helps identify who may be excluded, exposed, or harmed by eligibility criteria. |
| Delivery modality | Determines whether activities should be public, discreet, mobile, decentralized, conditional, or referral-based. |
| Community engagement | Clarifies who must be consulted and how to avoid elite capture or tokenistic participation. |
| Referral pathways | Identifies when protection, GBV, legal aid, shelter, health, or psychosocial referrals are required. |
| Complaints and feedback | Ensures affected people can safely report exclusion, abuse, threats, or manipulation. |
| Monitoring | Tracks emerging harm, tension, access barriers, rent changes, and protection incidents. |
| Coordination | Identifies when protection, shelter, WASH, CVA, legal, municipal, or security actors need to coordinate. |

## Special Use Cases

This framework is especially important when working in:

| Context | Why screening is critical |
|---|---|
| Mixed resident/displaced neighborhoods | Assistance may create tension over fairness, eligibility, or perceived favoritism. |
| Informal settlements | Residents may face eviction, service exclusion, documentation barriers, or lack of legal protection. |
| Areas with contested tenure | Repairs, infrastructure, or registration may affect land claims, landlord behavior, or eviction risk. |
| Areas with armed actors | Assistance may be diverted, politicized, taxed, blocked, or used for coercion. |
| High-density urban areas | Public distributions, queues, or visible assistance may create crowding, safety, stigma, or targeting risks. |
| Areas with weak governance | Gatekeepers, brokers, or informal authorities may control access and distort assistance. |

## Risk Alerts

**Priority-is-not-neutral risk:** Choosing one area, group, service, or infrastructure asset over another can shift power and resource access.

**Landlord and tenure risk:** Shelter or infrastructure support can unintentionally increase rents, evictions, or land-value disputes.

**Elite-capture risk:** Community leaders or authorities may not represent the most vulnerable people.

**Visibility risk:** Public lists, distributions, photos, or maps can expose people to stigma, retaliation, eviction, or exploitation.

**Referral-without-service risk:** Identifying protection or GBV risks without safe referral options can create expectations or expose people to further harm.

**Do-no-harm failure:** A technically sound activity can still be harmful if it ignores power, access, safety, exclusion, or conflict dynamics.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Protection and Do-No-Harm Risk Screening Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Proposed priority | Activity, area, group, service, infrastructure, or delivery mechanism being screened. |
| Affected groups | Beneficiaries, excluded groups, indirectly affected groups, and groups at heightened risk. |
| Key protection risks | Eviction, exclusion, violence, tension, aid capture, GBV, unsafe access, or data risks. |
| Risk rating | Low, medium, high, or critical, with explanation. |
| Power and gatekeeping concerns | Actors who may control, distort, block, or manipulate the intervention. |
| Mitigation measures | Design changes, safeguards, referrals, communication, monitoring, or accountability steps. |
| Residual risk | Risk remaining after mitigation. |
| Decision | Proceed, proceed with adaptations, redesign, seek specialist review, or do not proceed. |
| Monitoring triggers | Warning signs that would require review or suspension. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework before finalizing area priorities.

A priority should only proceed when the team can explain **who may be harmed, how harm could occur, what safeguards are in place, what residual risk remains, and how the team will monitor and respond if harm emerges**.
