# Stakeholder and Power Mapping Framework

**Source:** IRC, *Urban Context Analysis Toolkit*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 2. Area profile and systems diagnosis  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to identify, analyze, and act on the formal and informal actors, relationships, power dynamics, incentives, and exclusion patterns that shape an urban area.

## Framework Overview

The **Stakeholder and Power Mapping Framework** helps teams understand who influences life in a target urban area and how power affects access to services, resources, safety, information, land, livelihoods, and assistance.

It is designed for the area profile and systems diagnosis stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA). The framework goes beyond listing stakeholders. It helps teams analyze relationships, authority, trust, influence, gatekeeping, conflict, incentives, and exclusion.

This framework is especially useful in urban contexts where multiple formal and informal actors operate at the same time, including authorities, landlords, service providers, private businesses, community leaders, civil society, armed actors, informal brokers, and excluded groups.

## Core Framework Question

**Who has influence in the target area, how do they use that influence, and how does power shape people’s access to services, resources, protection, and participation?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Formal authority | Municipal departments, local government, line ministries, elected officials, police, public utilities, and official committees. | Identifies mandated actors responsible for services, regulation, planning, protection, and response. |
| Informal authority | Community leaders, elders, religious leaders, neighborhood representatives, brokers, landlords, influential families, and informal committees. | Reveals who people actually turn to for decisions, mediation, access, information, and support. |
| Service providers | Public, private, NGO, faith-based, community-based, and informal providers of water, sanitation, health, education, transport, waste, shelter, markets, and protection services. | Shows who controls or enables access to essential services. |
| Economic actors | Employers, market actors, shop owners, transport operators, traders, financial service providers, moneylenders, and informal labor intermediaries. | Explains how livelihoods, credit, markets, and economic dependency shape vulnerability and coping. |
| Land and housing actors | Landlords, property owners, developers, informal land brokers, municipal land offices, tenant groups, and settlement committees. | Helps analyze tenure insecurity, eviction risk, rent pressure, housing access, and land disputes. |
| Security and armed actors | Police, armed groups, gangs, militias, security committees, private security, or other actors controlling movement or safety. | Identifies protection risks, access constraints, and actors that may influence safety or humanitarian operations. |
| Community and civil society actors | Local NGOs, CBOs, women’s groups, youth groups, disability groups, faith groups, mutual aid networks, and advocacy organizations. | Identifies local capacity, representation, trust networks, and channels for participation. |
| Excluded or less visible groups | Renters, squatters, displaced people, undocumented residents, women-headed households, older people, people with disabilities, informal workers, minorities, or socially marginalized groups. | Ensures the analysis captures those affected by power but often absent from decision-making. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a structured mapping and analysis process:

1. **Define the area and purpose of the mapping**  
   Clarify the target geography and why the team is mapping stakeholders and power. The purpose may be assessment, coordination, access negotiation, targeting, risk mitigation, partnership selection, or accountability.

2. **Identify all relevant actors**  
   List formal and informal actors that influence services, land, livelihoods, security, information, mobility, aid access, and community decisions.

3. **Classify actors by role and influence**  
   Group actors by function, mandate, level of influence, legitimacy, proximity to affected people, and relationship to the program.

4. **Analyze power relationships**  
   Examine who controls resources, who depends on whom, who can block or enable action, who is trusted, and who is excluded.

5. **Map interests and incentives**  
   Identify what each actor wants, what they may gain or lose, and how they may respond to humanitarian or recovery programming.

6. **Identify gatekeepers and access barriers**  
   Determine whether any actor controls access to services, land, information, permits, beneficiary lists, communities, or physical areas.

7. **Assess representation and exclusion**  
   Compare who speaks for the area with who is actually affected. Identify groups missing from decision-making or consultation.

8. **Identify risks and opportunities**  
   Analyze how stakeholder dynamics could support or undermine assistance, protection, social cohesion, coordination, and accountability.

9. **Translate findings into program decisions**  
   Use the analysis to shape engagement strategy, partnerships, targeting, communication, risk mitigation, and coordination.

## Stakeholder and Power Mapping Matrix

| Diagnostic question | What to look for |
|---|---|
| Who has formal authority in the area? | Municipal officials, departments, public service providers, police, line ministries, and official committees. |
| Who has informal influence? | Community leaders, landlords, religious leaders, elders, brokers, influential families, market leaders, or informal committees. |
| Who controls access to services or resources? | Service providers, landlords, utility actors, local committees, brokers, political actors, armed actors, or private companies. |
| Who is trusted by residents? | Local organizations, respected individuals, faith groups, health workers, teachers, community volunteers, or mutual aid networks. |
| Who can block or distort assistance? | Gatekeepers, corrupt actors, armed groups, political brokers, landlords, service monopolies, or exclusionary committees. |
| Who benefits from the current situation? | Actors gaining from rent, service control, informal fees, political influence, aid access, labor exploitation, or market dominance. |
| Who is excluded from decisions? | Women, youth, older people, people with disabilities, renters, squatters, displaced people, undocumented people, minorities, or informal workers. |
| Where are there tensions or conflicts? | Land disputes, rent conflicts, service competition, political divisions, social discrimination, violence, or resource scarcity. |
| Which actors could support solutions? | Trusted leaders, capable service providers, municipal champions, CBOs, local businesses, technical actors, and community networks. |

## Power Analysis Lens

Use the following categories to interpret stakeholder influence.

| Power type | Meaning | Program relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Decision power | Ability to make or approve formal decisions. | Needed for permits, coordination, public systems, and policy alignment. |
| Resource power | Control over money, land, services, infrastructure, staff, data, or supplies. | Shapes who can support implementation or restrict access. |
| Gatekeeping power | Ability to decide who receives access, information, services, land, or assistance. | Critical for targeting, accountability, and do-no-harm. |
| Social power | Influence based on trust, identity, status, reputation, kinship, religion, or community role. | Shapes participation, acceptance, communication, and legitimacy. |
| Coercive power | Ability to use force, intimidation, threat, or violence. | Creates protection risks and operational access constraints. |
| Knowledge power | Control over information, data, technical expertise, or local knowledge. | Shapes assessment quality, decision-making, and transparency. |
| Network power | Ability to connect actors, mobilize people, or bridge institutions and communities. | Useful for coordination, referral, advocacy, and problem-solving. |

## Actor Positioning Matrix

| Actor type | High influence / supportive | High influence / resistant | Low influence / supportive | Low influence / at risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Can help move the response forward. | Can block, distort, or politicize the response. | Can support implementation but may need backing. | May be affected but lack voice or protection. |
| Engagement strategy | Involve early, clarify roles, build accountability. | Manage carefully, reduce harm, avoid capture, seek safeguards. | Strengthen capacity, include in planning, connect to networks. | Prioritize consultation, protection, access, and representation. |

## Analysis Principles

**Map relationships, not just names.** A stakeholder list is not enough. The framework should show how actors relate to each other and to affected people.

**Include informal power.** In urban areas, landlords, brokers, market actors, informal leaders, or armed actors may shape access more than official institutions.

**Look for who is missing.** The most affected groups may have the least visibility in public meetings, official lists, or leadership structures.

**Separate legitimacy from influence.** Some actors may be influential but not trusted. Others may be trusted but lack formal authority.

**Treat power as dynamic.** Influence can change as aid arrives, resources shift, displacement patterns evolve, or political tensions increase.

**Use the analysis for do-no-harm.** Poorly managed engagement can reinforce exclusion, empower harmful gatekeepers, or increase conflict.

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Engagement strategy | Determines who to consult, inform, involve, monitor, or avoid empowering. |
| Partnership selection | Identifies trusted, capable, legitimate, and locally grounded partners. |
| Targeting and registration | Helps prevent gatekeeping, elite capture, exclusion, or biased beneficiary selection. |
| Communication and accountability | Identifies trusted information channels and feedback mechanisms. |
| Access negotiation | Clarifies who controls physical, social, administrative, or political access. |
| Protection mainstreaming | Identifies coercion, exploitation, discrimination, exclusion, and safety risks. |
| Conflict sensitivity | Reveals tensions that programming could worsen or reduce. |
| Coordination | Shows where formal and informal systems need to be connected. |

## Risk Alerts

**Stakeholder-list trap:** Listing actors without analyzing influence, incentives, relationships, and exclusion will not support good programming.

**Gatekeeper capture risk:** Relying on one leader, committee, landlord, or authority can distort targeting and silence excluded groups.

**Authority-only trap:** Formal authorities matter, but they may not represent all residents or control all relevant systems.

**Community-homogeneity trap:** Do not assume “the community” has one voice. Urban communities often contain competing interests and unequal power.

**Visibility bias:** The most visible actors may not be the most trusted or representative.

**Do-no-harm risk:** Engaging powerful actors without safeguards can reinforce exploitation, discrimination, corruption, or conflict.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Stakeholder and Power Mapping Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | The neighborhood, settlement, district, market zone, or service catchment being analyzed. |
| Actor map | Formal and informal actors influencing the area. |
| Power analysis | Key actors’ influence, authority, incentives, resources, legitimacy, and relationships. |
| Gatekeepers | Actors who control access to services, information, land, assistance, permits, or communities. |
| Excluded groups | Groups affected by decisions but underrepresented in consultation or leadership. |
| Risks | Protection, conflict, corruption, elite capture, coercion, discrimination, or access risks. |
| Opportunities | Trusted actors, connectors, local capacities, service providers, or institutional entry points. |
| Engagement strategy | How the team should consult, coordinate, partner, communicate, or apply safeguards. |
| Program implications | Adjustments needed for targeting, accountability, coordination, access, and risk mitigation. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the team needs to understand **who shapes the urban system and how power affects access, protection, participation, and program outcomes**.

A strong stakeholder and power analysis should identify **formal actors, informal actors, gatekeepers, trusted networks, excluded groups, risks, and practical engagement strategies**.
