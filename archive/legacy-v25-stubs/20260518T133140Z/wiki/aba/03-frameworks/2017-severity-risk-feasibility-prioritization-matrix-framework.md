# Severity–Risk–Feasibility Prioritization Matrix Framework

**Source:** Sanderson & Sitko, *Urban Area-Based Approaches in Post-Disaster Contexts*; REACH area-based assessment guidance; IRC urban context analysis logic  
**Years:** 2017 / 2019 / 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 4. Joint risk and needs prioritization  
**Framework purpose:** Rank neighborhood problems by life-saving urgency, population affected, risk reduction value, feasibility, equity, and do-no-harm risk.

## Framework Overview

The **Severity–Risk–Feasibility Prioritization Matrix Framework** helps area-based teams decide which problems should be addressed first when many needs compete for attention.

It is designed for the joint risk and needs prioritization stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), after assessment findings, community priorities, service gap analysis, risk mapping, stakeholder input, and technical observations have been collected.

The framework prevents prioritization from being driven only by the loudest voices, most visible damage, easiest activities, donor preference, or single-sector logic. Instead, it combines community ranking with technical, protection, feasibility, equity, and do-no-harm analysis.

It is especially useful in dense urban areas where shelter, WASH, drainage, roads, markets, protection risks, public services, social tensions, and governance constraints interact in the same territory.

## Core Framework Question

**Which neighborhood problems should be addressed first because they are severe, affect many people, reduce risk, are feasible, promote equity, and avoid doing harm?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Life-saving urgency | Whether the issue creates immediate threats to life, health, safety, dignity, or protection. | Urgent risks should not be delayed because they are politically difficult or less visible. |
| Population affected | Number and proportion of people affected, including whether affected groups are concentrated or dispersed. | Prioritization should consider scale while not ignoring small groups at extreme risk. |
| Severity of consequences | Depth of harm caused by the problem, including health, protection, shelter, livelihood, service, and social impacts. | Some problems affect fewer people but cause catastrophic harm. |
| Risk reduction value | Whether solving the problem reduces future disaster, disease, protection, conflict, or infrastructure risk. | ABA priorities should reduce risk, not only respond to current need. |
| Feasibility | Whether the action is technically, operationally, financially, legally, politically, and socially achievable. | Unrealistic priorities can stall implementation and reduce trust. |
| Equity and inclusion | Whether the issue disproportionately affects marginalized, excluded, or high-risk groups. | Prioritization should not reinforce existing inequality. |
| Do-no-harm risk | Whether action or inaction could worsen tensions, exclusion, dependency, market distortion, tenure risk, or protection risk. | Good technical choices can still create harm if social and political effects are ignored. |
| System dependency | Whether the issue blocks other interventions or services. | Some enabling actions must happen first for other sectors to work. |
| Community priority | Whether residents identify the problem as important and legitimate. | Technical priorities require local validation and accountability. |
| Institutional ownership | Whether a responsible actor exists to implement, approve, maintain, or sustain the response. | Priorities need a path to action and longer-term ownership. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a structured prioritization process:

1. **Compile candidate problems**  
   List all major problems identified through assessment, community engagement, service mapping, protection analysis, technical visits, and coordination discussions.

2. **Group problems by area and system**  
   Organize problems by neighborhood zone, affected population, service system, hazard, infrastructure dependency, or sector.

3. **Define scoring criteria**  
   Use a shared scoring scale for severity, urgency, population affected, risk reduction value, feasibility, equity, do-no-harm risk, system dependency, and community priority.

4. **Score jointly but transparently**  
   Combine community input, technical analysis, service-provider information, local authority perspectives, protection review, and field observations.

5. **Discuss disagreements**  
   Record where communities, technical staff, authorities, or protection actors rank the same problem differently.

6. **Identify dependencies**  
   Mark actions that must happen before others, such as debris clearance before drainage repair, drainage before shelter repair, permits before public works, or referral pathways before case identification.

7. **Separate urgent action from strategic action**  
   Identify what must happen immediately, what should be sequenced later, and what requires advocacy or long-term investment.

8. **Check do-no-harm and equity safeguards**  
   Review whether proposed priorities could exclude groups, intensify conflict, raise rents, increase eviction risk, distort markets, or create unsafe access.

9. **Validate with stakeholders**  
   Present the proposed priorities to residents, local actors, service providers, municipal actors, and humanitarian partners.

10. **Convert priorities into an action plan**  
   Assign responsible actors, timelines, dependencies, unresolved issues, and monitoring indicators.

## Prioritization Scoring Scale

| Score | Meaning | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Low | Limited harm, low urgency, limited risk reduction value, or weak justification. |
| 2 | Moderate | Important issue, but not immediately life-threatening or not affecting many people. |
| 3 | High | Serious issue affecting many people or creating significant risk. |
| 4 | Very high | Urgent issue with major safety, health, protection, or system consequences. |
| 5 | Critical | Immediate life-saving, protection-critical, or system-blocking issue requiring priority action. |

## Severity–Risk–Feasibility Matrix

| Problem | Life-saving urgency | Population affected | Severity | Risk reduction value | Equity concern | Feasibility | Do-no-harm risk | System dependency | Community priority | Overall priority |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Example: blocked drainage in flood-prone zone | 4 | 4 | 4 | 5 | 3 | 4 | 2 | 5 | 4 | Priority 1 |
| Example: shelter repair in same flood-prone zone | 3 | 4 | 4 | 3 | 4 | 3 | 3 | 2 | 5 | Priority 2 after drainage |
| Example: market access support | 2 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 4 | 2 | 2 | 3 | Priority 3 |
| Example: public awareness campaign only | 2 | 4 | 2 | 2 | 2 | 5 | 1 | 1 | 3 | Lower priority unless linked to action |

## Criterion Definitions

| Criterion | Guiding question | Evidence to use |
|---|---|---|
| Life-saving urgency | Could delay cause death, serious injury, disease outbreak, violence, or acute protection harm? | Health data, protection reports, hazard maps, WASH risk, safety observations, community reports. |
| Population affected | How many people are affected, and which groups are affected? | Household data, KI interviews, population estimates, service coverage, mapping. |
| Severity | How serious is the harm for affected people? | Needs assessment, technical inspection, protection analysis, service quality data, community ranking. |
| Risk reduction value | Would addressing this reduce future hazards, exposure, vulnerability, or cascading failures? | DRR analysis, flood/fire/disease risk, infrastructure assessment, preparedness review. |
| Equity | Does the issue disproportionately affect excluded or high-risk groups? | Disaggregated data, focus groups, protection analysis, disability access review. |
| Feasibility | Can this realistically be done with available resources, permissions, access, time, capacity, and partners? | Budget, permits, technical feasibility, security, procurement, partner capacity. |
| Do-no-harm risk | Could the action worsen tensions, exclusion, aid capture, evictions, dependency, or market distortion? | Conflict sensitivity review, protection review, community feedback, market analysis. |
| System dependency | Must this issue be solved before other activities can work? | Sequencing map, infrastructure analysis, service-provider input, technical design review. |
| Community priority | Do residents identify this as important, legitimate, and urgent? | Community ranking, participatory planning, complaints, feedback, focus groups. |
| Institutional ownership | Is there a responsible actor who can approve, implement, maintain, or sustain the response? | Stakeholder map, municipal meeting, provider interviews, handover planning. |

## Priority Categories

| Category | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Priority 1: Immediate action | High urgency, high severity, high risk reduction value, or system-blocking dependency. | Act first; assign owner, resources, timeline, and safeguards. |
| Priority 2: Sequenced action | Important but dependent on enabling works, permissions, coordination, or prior mitigation. | Sequence after required prerequisite actions. |
| Priority 3: Planned action | Relevant and feasible but less urgent or lower risk reduction value. | Include in workplan if resources allow. |
| Referral / coordination priority | Important but better addressed by another actor, service provider, authority, or specialized agency. | Refer, coordinate, advocate, and monitor. |
| Watchlist | Not currently a priority but could worsen if conditions change. | Monitor through reassessment triggers. |
| Do not proceed | High harm risk, low feasibility, unclear ownership, or likely to worsen exclusion/tension. | Redesign, mitigate, or reject. |

## Community Ranking and Technical Review Process

| Step | Community input | Technical/protection input | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Problem listing | Residents identify priority concerns and lived consequences. | Teams add observed service, infrastructure, hazard, and protection gaps. | Longlist of candidate problems. |
| Community ranking | Groups rank what feels most urgent and important. | Teams review whether any invisible or less vocal group is missing. | Community-ranked priorities. |
| Technical scoring | Residents explain why issues matter. | Specialists score severity, risk, feasibility, dependencies, and safeguards. | Scored matrix. |
| Equity check | Separate groups validate whether priorities reflect their experience. | Protection and inclusion review identifies exclusion or harm risks. | Adjusted priority list. |
| Coordination check | Local actors identify mandates, constraints, and resources. | Technical teams identify implementation and maintenance requirements. | Assigned responsibilities and sequencing. |
| Feedback | Priorities are communicated back to residents. | Complaints and corrections are reviewed. | Final priority action plan. |

## Do-No-Harm Review

| Risk area | Questions before selecting the priority |
|---|---|
| Social tension | Could the activity intensify tensions between host/displaced groups, renters/owners, neighborhoods, ethnic groups, or aid recipients/non-recipients? |
| Protection | Could the activity increase GBV, child protection risks, unsafe travel, exploitation, aid capture, or retaliation? |
| Tenure and eviction | Could improvements increase rent, eviction pressure, land disputes, or landlord control? |
| Market impact | Could the activity distort prices, undermine local providers, or create unfair competition? |
| Governance | Could the activity bypass local systems, strengthen harmful power holders, or create parallel structures? |
| Exclusion | Could selection criteria miss people with disabilities, older people, undocumented people, informal settlers, minorities, or isolated households? |
| Environment and hazard | Could the activity worsen drainage, waste, fire, slope, flood, disease, or environmental risks? |
| Dependency and maintenance | Could the activity create assets or services that no one can maintain? |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Priority selection | Identifies which problems should be addressed first and why. |
| Sequencing | Shows which activities depend on others. |
| Targeting | Highlights who is most affected and which groups may otherwise be missed. |
| Resource allocation | Directs limited funds, staff, and time toward the highest-value priorities. |
| Risk mitigation | Integrates protection, equity, conflict sensitivity, and do-no-harm safeguards. |
| Coordination | Identifies which priorities require municipal, service-provider, or partner action. |
| Advocacy | Shows which high-severity issues cannot be solved by the project alone and need escalation. |
| Monitoring | Provides baseline priority scores that can be updated during reassessment. |

## Risk Alerts

**Loudest-voice risk:** Community meetings may prioritize the most vocal groups unless separate consultations and equity checks are used.

**Visible-damage bias:** Highly visible damage may be selected over less visible but more severe protection, health, or service risks.

**Feasibility trap:** Teams may choose only easy activities and avoid severe problems that require coordination or advocacy.

**Technical-dominance risk:** Technical scoring can override lived experience if community ranking is treated as symbolic.

**Equity-blind scoring risk:** A problem affecting a smaller excluded group may receive a low population score but still require priority action.

**Do-no-harm blind spot:** A high-priority intervention can worsen tensions, rent, eviction risk, or exclusion if safeguards are not applied.

**Static priority risk:** Priorities must be updated when population, hazards, services, markets, or security conditions change.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a **Joint Priority Ranking and Action Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | Neighborhood, settlement, district, or urban zone covered by the prioritization. |
| Candidate problems | Longlist of risks, needs, service gaps, and infrastructure issues considered. |
| Scoring criteria | Criteria and scale used for ranking. |
| Ranked priorities | Problems ranked by urgency, severity, population affected, risk reduction value, feasibility, equity, and do-no-harm risk. |
| Community validation | How residents and different groups ranked and validated priorities. |
| Technical and protection review | Specialist review of severity, feasibility, dependencies, and safeguards. |
| Sequencing dependencies | Activities that must occur before others. |
| Priority actions | Immediate, sequenced, planned, referral, watchlist, and rejected actions. |
| Responsible actors | Actors responsible for action, coordination, approval, referral, or monitoring. |
| Risk mitigation | Do-no-harm, protection, inclusion, conflict sensitivity, and accountability measures. |
| Reassessment trigger | Conditions that require the priority ranking to be updated. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when multiple area-level needs compete for attention and the team must justify what comes first.

A strong prioritization matrix should show **which problems were considered, how they were scored, whose priorities were included, which safeguards were applied, what will be done first, what must wait, what requires referral or advocacy, and what should not proceed because the risk of harm is too high**.
