# City Resilience Investment Logic Framework

**Source:** World Bank / GFDRR, *Building Urban Resilience*  
**Year:** 2013  
**Lifecycle stage:** 5. Integrated area strategy  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to connect area-level humanitarian and recovery interventions to longer-term investment in infrastructure, water, energy, transport, public services, and risk-informed urban planning.

## Framework Overview

The **City Resilience Investment Logic Framework** helps teams design area-level interventions in a way that can connect to municipal, donor, or development-bank investment pipelines.

It is designed for the integrated area strategy stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), when teams need to move from immediate humanitarian or recovery priorities toward investments that reduce future risk and strengthen urban systems.

The framework is especially useful when an area-based response identifies problems that cannot be solved through short-term assistance alone, such as drainage failure, unsafe settlement growth, weak water systems, damaged roads, poor electricity access, overloaded public services, or lack of risk-informed urban planning.

## Core Framework Question

**How can area-level humanitarian and recovery interventions be designed so they reduce immediate needs while also contributing to longer-term city resilience investment?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Risk-informed investment need | Whether the area faces recurring hazards, infrastructure failure, service disruption, or unsafe growth that requires investment. | Ensures investment priorities are driven by risk, not only visible damage or political preference. |
| Infrastructure systems | Roads, drainage, bridges, water, sanitation, electricity, transport, waste, public buildings, and protective infrastructure. | Identifies physical systems that shape safety, access, service continuity, and recovery. |
| Public service continuity | Health, education, water, sanitation, transport, waste, electricity, administrative services, and emergency services. | Connects infrastructure investment to people’s actual access to essential services. |
| Urban planning and land use | Settlement expansion, zoning, land tenure, building safety, density, environmental risk, and future growth. | Helps reduce future exposure and avoid rebuilding risk. |
| Municipal and donor financing | Public budgets, donor funding, development-bank investment, private-sector finance, and community contributions. | Links area-level priorities to realistic financing pathways. |
| Institutional ownership | Municipal departments, utilities, service providers, community structures, and technical agencies responsible for implementation and maintenance. | Ensures investments have owners and do not become isolated project assets. |
| Phasing and sequencing | Immediate recovery, medium-term rehabilitation, and long-term resilience investment. | Helps connect emergency action with development planning over time. |
| Maintenance and sustainability | Operations, maintenance, tariffs, staffing, governance, and community stewardship. | Protects investments from rapid deterioration or failure. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a resilience investment pathway:

1. **Identify area-level problems that require investment**  
   Start from the integrated area strategy and identify needs that are linked to infrastructure, services, risk, planning, or governance.

2. **Classify the problem by system**  
   Determine whether the issue relates to water, sanitation, drainage, transport, energy, public services, land use, housing, waste, or protective infrastructure.

3. **Assess risk and future exposure**  
   Analyze whether the problem is likely to worsen due to hazards, climate change, displacement, urban growth, informal settlement expansion, or service overload.

4. **Define short-, medium-, and long-term actions**  
   Separate actions that can be done immediately from those that require technical design, municipal approval, financing, procurement, or policy change.

5. **Identify investment owners and financing routes**  
   Map municipal departments, utilities, donors, development banks, private-sector actors, or community structures that could finance, implement, or maintain the investment.

6. **Package area-level evidence for investment decisions**  
   Translate humanitarian assessment findings into a clear investment rationale using risk, population impact, service continuity, cost, feasibility, and resilience benefits.

7. **Integrate with municipal planning**  
   Link proposed investments to city plans, DRR strategies, infrastructure plans, land-use plans, recovery plans, or public investment programs.

8. **Define maintenance and accountability arrangements**  
   Clarify who will operate, maintain, monitor, and finance the asset or service after project completion.

9. **Track whether investments reduce risk**  
   Monitor whether the intervention improves service continuity, reduces exposure, lowers vulnerability, and strengthens capacity.

## Investment Screening Matrix

| Screening question | Strong investment signal |
|---|---|
| Does the area face recurring or increasing risk? | Flooding, landslides, fire, heat, infrastructure failure, unsafe growth, or repeated service disruption affects the area. |
| Is critical infrastructure weak, damaged, absent, or overloaded? | Roads, drainage, WASH systems, electricity, bridges, transport, public buildings, or protective systems are failing. |
| Does the issue affect many people or vulnerable groups? | The problem affects dense populations, displaced people, low-income households, informal settlers, or groups with limited alternatives. |
| Does one system failure create wider consequences? | Drainage failure blocks roads, damages homes, disrupts markets, increases disease risk, or limits emergency access. |
| Can intervention reduce future risk? | Investment would reduce exposure, improve service reliability, protect assets, support safer growth, or strengthen preparedness. |
| Is there a realistic institutional owner? | A municipality, utility, service provider, community structure, or technical agency can manage the asset or service. |
| Is there a possible financing pathway? | The priority can be linked to municipal budgets, donor funds, development-bank pipelines, recovery funds, or private/community investment. |
| Can the investment be maintained? | Operations, maintenance, staffing, financing, and accountability arrangements can be defined. |

## Investment Pathway

| Phase | Purpose | Example actions |
|---|---|---|
| Immediate recovery | Address urgent needs and reduce immediate harm. | Temporary drainage clearing, emergency water access, access-route repair, debris removal, service restoration, safety works. |
| Transitional rehabilitation | Stabilize systems and prepare for larger investment. | Technical assessments, design studies, community validation, risk mapping, minor infrastructure upgrades, service-provider support. |
| Long-term resilience investment | Reduce future risk and strengthen urban systems. | Drainage networks, resilient water systems, transport upgrades, public service facilities, slope stabilization, safer land-use planning, energy improvements. |
| Institutionalization | Ensure sustainability and integration into city systems. | Maintenance plans, budget lines, service agreements, municipal adoption, community monitoring, risk-informed planning updates. |

## Investment Package Types

| Package type | What it includes | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Infrastructure resilience package | Repair, upgrade, or protect physical systems. | Drainage improvement, road stabilization, bridge repair, slope protection, flood mitigation. |
| Service continuity package | Strengthen systems that keep essential services functioning. | Water supply reliability, waste management, health facility access, school rehabilitation, transport links. |
| Risk-informed planning package | Improve planning decisions that reduce future exposure. | Hazard-informed zoning, safer settlement planning, building guidance, land-use controls. |
| Community infrastructure package | Improve shared assets used by residents. | Pathways, lighting, water points, communal sanitation, safe public spaces, local evacuation points. |
| Governance and financing package | Strengthen institutional arrangements for investment and maintenance. | Municipal coordination, budget advocacy, donor pipeline development, utility agreements, maintenance protocols. |
| Preparedness and response package | Improve the area’s ability to anticipate and respond to shocks. | Early warning links, evacuation routes, emergency access, contingency planning, community response capacity. |

## Area-to-Investment Translation

Use the framework to convert area-level findings into investment language.

| Area-based finding | Investment framing |
|---|---|
| Households report repeated flooding. | Drainage and flood-risk management investment is needed to reduce recurrent losses and service disruption. |
| Roads are blocked after heavy rain. | Access and transport resilience investment is needed to maintain service delivery, markets, and emergency response. |
| Water service is unreliable. | Water-system rehabilitation and service-provider support are needed for public health and service continuity. |
| Informal settlement is expanding on unsafe land. | Risk-informed land-use planning and safer settlement support are needed to reduce future exposure. |
| Waste blocks drainage and increases disease risk. | Solid waste and drainage systems require integrated investment and maintenance planning. |
| Public services are overloaded. | Service capacity investment is needed to support residents, displaced populations, and recovery. |
| Community assets deteriorate after small repairs. | Maintenance financing and institutional ownership are needed to sustain improvements. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Area strategy | Connects household and community priorities to larger urban systems and investment needs. |
| Infrastructure prioritization | Identifies which assets or systems reduce the most risk and unlock wider benefits. |
| Sequencing | Distinguishes emergency works from rehabilitation and long-term investment. |
| Municipal engagement | Identifies which departments, utilities, or public institutions must own or support the investment. |
| Donor engagement | Converts assessment findings into investment-ready arguments. |
| Technical assessment | Identifies where engineering, financial, environmental, or planning analysis is required. |
| Sustainability planning | Builds maintenance, financing, and governance into design from the start. |
| DRR integration | Ensures recovery investments reduce future disaster risk rather than rebuilding vulnerability. |

## Risk Alerts

**Project-asset trap:** Do not build or repair assets without clear ownership, maintenance, and financing arrangements.

**Short-termism risk:** Emergency repairs may restore access temporarily but fail to address underlying risk or system weakness.

**Infrastructure-only trap:** Physical works alone may not improve resilience without governance, service management, financing, and community accountability.

**Investment-pipeline disconnect:** Area-level priorities may be ignored if they are not translated into the language and evidence required by municipal or donor planning systems.

**Elite-capture risk:** Investment decisions can favor visible or politically connected areas unless equity and vulnerability are explicitly considered.

**Rebuilding-risk risk:** Recovery investments can reproduce exposure if they ignore hazard zones, land-use patterns, climate risk, and informal settlement dynamics.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **City Resilience Investment Logic Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Target area | The neighborhood, settlement, district, corridor, or urban zone under consideration. |
| Priority investment problem | The infrastructure, service, planning, or resilience issue identified through area analysis. |
| Risk rationale | Hazards, exposure, vulnerability, infrastructure weakness, service dependency, or urban growth factors that justify investment. |
| Affected population and systems | People, groups, services, assets, markets, or institutions affected by the problem. |
| Proposed investment pathway | Immediate, transitional, and long-term actions. |
| Institutional owner | Municipality, utility, service provider, community structure, or technical agency responsible for implementation or maintenance. |
| Financing pathway | Municipal budget, donor funding, development-bank pipeline, recovery fund, private-sector finance, or community contribution. |
| Maintenance approach | Operations, maintenance, staffing, cost recovery, community monitoring, or service agreement. |
| Resilience benefit | How the investment reduces risk, improves service continuity, strengthens capacity, or supports safer growth. |
| Next step | Technical assessment, municipal coordination, donor proposal, design study, feasibility review, or implementation. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when area-level priorities point to problems that require **infrastructure, public service, planning, or resilience investment** beyond short-term humanitarian assistance.

A strong city resilience investment logic should explain **what system needs investment, why it matters for risk reduction, who benefits, who owns it, how it can be financed, how it will be maintained, and how it connects humanitarian recovery to long-term urban resilience**.
