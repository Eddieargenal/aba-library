# Sector Technical Design Checklist Framework

**Sources:** ALNAP, *Good Practice Review 12: Humanitarian Protection*; Global Shelter Cluster, *Area-Based Approaches in Urban Settings: Compendium of Case Studies*; relevant sector standards  
**Year:** 2019  
**Lifecycle stage:** 7. Detailed technical design  
**Framework purpose:** Guide technical specialists to convert area-level priorities into technically sound sector designs for shelter, WASH, drainage, roads, solid waste, protection, CVA, livelihoods, and DRR.

## Framework Overview

The **Sector Technical Design Checklist Framework** helps teams translate an integrated Area-Based Approach (ABA) strategy into detailed, sector-specific technical designs.

The framework recognizes that an ABA coordinates action geographically, but it does not replace sector quality. Each sector still requires appropriate technical standards, design calculations, safeguards, quality controls, and specialist review.

This framework is used after area priorities, response packages, and modality choices have been agreed. It ensures that each sector intervention is technically feasible, safe, context-appropriate, protection-sensitive, and aligned with the broader area strategy.

## Core Framework Question

**How should each sector intervention be technically designed so that it meets sector standards while contributing to the integrated area-level strategy?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Sector objective | The specific technical outcome the intervention should achieve. | Keeps technical design linked to the agreed area-level priorities. |
| Standards and guidance | Relevant humanitarian, national, municipal, engineering, protection, and sector standards. | Ensures quality, safety, compliance, and professional accountability. |
| Technical feasibility | Site conditions, materials, labor, access, permits, climate, hazards, and operational capacity. | Prevents designs that cannot be implemented or maintained. |
| Protection and do-no-harm | Risks related to eviction, exclusion, GBV, unsafe access, violence, social tension, aid capture, and data protection. | Ensures technical works do not create or worsen harm. |
| Environmental and DRR integration | Hazard exposure, drainage, waste, climate risk, environmental impact, and safer reconstruction. | Ensures sector interventions reduce future risk rather than rebuild vulnerability. |
| Community use and acceptance | User preferences, accessibility, gender, disability, cultural appropriateness, maintenance capacity, and feedback. | Improves uptake, dignity, inclusion, and sustainability. |
| Operations and maintenance | Ownership, maintenance roles, spare parts, budgets, skills, management systems, and accountability. | Prevents assets or services from failing after implementation. |
| Monitoring and quality control | Technical indicators, supervision, inspection, verification, handover, and defect correction. | Ensures the final output meets design intent and sector standards. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a technical design process:

1. **Confirm the area-level priority**  
   Start with the agreed priority from the integrated area strategy, risk analysis, and response option comparison.

2. **Define the sector-specific objective**  
   Translate the area priority into a clear technical objective for shelter, WASH, roads, drainage, solid waste, protection, CVA, livelihoods, or DRR.

3. **Identify applicable standards**  
   Review relevant sector standards, national regulations, municipal requirements, engineering guidance, humanitarian standards, and donor requirements.

4. **Assess site and system conditions**  
   Conduct the technical analysis needed for the sector, including site visits, measurements, infrastructure checks, service mapping, hazard review, market analysis, or user consultation.

5. **Develop the technical design**  
   Define specifications, quantities, materials, methods, delivery modality, quality requirements, safeguards, and implementation sequence.

6. **Screen for protection and do-no-harm risks**  
   Check whether the design could increase exclusion, eviction, unsafe access, GBV risks, social tension, aid capture, or other harms.

7. **Check integration with other sectors**  
   Confirm how the design interacts with other area-level interventions, such as shelter with drainage, WASH with solid waste, roads with access, or livelihoods with markets.

8. **Define implementation controls**  
   Establish supervision, technical inspection, procurement standards, contractor requirements, community feedback, and corrective action mechanisms.

9. **Plan handover and maintenance**  
   Identify who will own, operate, maintain, finance, and monitor the asset, service, or process after completion.

10. **Approve and document the design**  
   Record the technical rationale, assumptions, drawings, specifications, BoQs, standards, risks, safeguards, and monitoring indicators.

## Sector Design Checklist

| Sector | Technical design questions |
|---|---|
| Shelter and settlements | Are shelter repairs, upgrades, or settlement improvements safe, tenure-sensitive, accessible, locally appropriate, and hazard-informed? |
| WASH | Are water, sanitation, hygiene, drainage, and public health measures technically safe, inclusive, maintainable, and linked to service systems? |
| Drainage and flood risk | Does the design account for water flow, upstream and downstream effects, blockage, maintenance, flood pathways, and future rainfall risk? |
| Roads and access | Do road, pathway, bridge, or access improvements support safe mobility, service access, emergency response, and maintenance? |
| Solid waste | Does the design address collection, storage, transport, disposal, community behavior, worker safety, and municipal service links? |
| Protection | Does the design reduce risks related to violence, eviction, exclusion, exploitation, GBV, unsafe access, and lack of referrals? |
| CVA | Are cash or voucher values, delivery mechanisms, markets, financial access, safeguarding, and monitoring appropriate for the objective? |
| Livelihoods | Are livelihood activities market-informed, safe, inclusive, feasible, and linked to realistic income opportunities? |
| DRR and preparedness | Does the intervention reduce exposure, vulnerability, or future loss, and does it strengthen local preparedness capacity? |

## Cross-Sector Quality Checks

| Quality check | Guiding question |
|---|---|
| Technical appropriateness | Does the design solve the identified problem using technically sound methods? |
| Standards compliance | Does the design meet relevant humanitarian, national, municipal, and sector standards? |
| Safety | Could the design create physical, environmental, social, or protection risks? |
| Inclusion | Can women, men, children, older people, people with disabilities, displaced people, renters, informal settlers, and marginalized groups use or benefit from the design safely? |
| Feasibility | Can the design be implemented with available time, budget, materials, labor, access, permits, and supervision capacity? |
| Integration | Does the design complement other sector interventions in the same area? |
| Sustainability | Is there a realistic plan for ownership, operations, maintenance, and financing? |
| Accountability | Can users provide feedback, report problems, and influence corrections? |
| Monitoring | Are there clear indicators and verification methods for quality, use, safety, and outcomes? |

## Technical Design Outputs

Depending on the sector, the design package may include:

| Output | Description |
|---|---|
| Technical rationale | Explanation of why this design was selected and how it addresses the area priority. |
| Drawings or maps | Site plans, layouts, service maps, hazard maps, route maps, or infrastructure drawings. |
| Specifications | Materials, workmanship, standards, dimensions, performance requirements, and quality criteria. |
| Bill of quantities | Quantities, unit costs, labor, materials, equipment, and budget assumptions. |
| Implementation method | Contractor, community-led, direct works, service contract, cash, voucher, grant, public works, or mixed modality. |
| Safeguards | Protection, environmental, safety, accessibility, tenure, GBV, and do-no-harm measures. |
| Supervision plan | Inspection schedule, quality checks, responsibilities, approval steps, and defect correction process. |
| Handover plan | Ownership, operations, maintenance, training, spare parts, financing, and accountability arrangements. |
| Monitoring indicators | Output, outcome, quality, safety, inclusion, use, maintenance, and risk-reduction indicators. |

## Integration Rules

The ABA platform coordinates sector quality geographically. It should not dilute technical standards.

| Rule | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Sector standards remain mandatory | Area-based coordination does not justify weaker shelter, WASH, protection, CVA, livelihoods, infrastructure, or DRR standards. |
| Technical specialists must review sector designs | Integrated planning must be supported by qualified technical review. |
| Geographic coordination should improve quality | The area frame should help sectors sequence and align interventions, not replace technical rigor. |
| Protection must be mainstreamed in every sector | Every technical design must be checked for safety, dignity, access, participation, and do-no-harm. |
| Maintenance must be designed from the start | Infrastructure and service interventions should not proceed without realistic maintenance arrangements. |

## Risk Alerts

**ABA-substitution risk:** An Area-Based Approach does not replace sector expertise, technical standards, engineering design, or protection analysis.

**Coordination-without-quality risk:** A well-coordinated response can still fail if technical designs are weak.

**Infrastructure-without-maintenance risk:** Roads, drainage, WASH, and solid waste improvements may fail quickly without clear ownership and maintenance.

**Protection-blind design risk:** A technically correct intervention can still increase eviction, unsafe access, exclusion, GBV risk, or social tension.

**One-sector fix risk:** Area problems often require linked sector designs. Drainage, shelter, roads, WASH, solid waste, and protection may need to be designed together.

**Over-standardization risk:** Applying generic standards without adapting to local use, climate, materials, culture, disability access, and maintenance capacity can reduce effectiveness.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **Sector Technical Design Note** for each intervention.

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Area priority | The area-level problem or priority being addressed. |
| Sector objective | The specific sector outcome the design aims to achieve. |
| Technical standards used | Sector, national, municipal, donor, engineering, humanitarian, or protection standards applied. |
| Site and system analysis | Key findings from site visits, measurements, hazard analysis, service mapping, market analysis, or user consultation. |
| Proposed design | Technical solution, specifications, quantities, method, and implementation approach. |
| Integration points | Links with other sectors, services, infrastructure, governance, or community systems. |
| Risk and safeguard analysis | Protection, environmental, safety, tenure, accessibility, and do-no-harm risks with mitigation. |
| Quality assurance plan | Supervision, inspections, verification, approvals, and corrective actions. |
| Handover and maintenance plan | Owner, operator, maintenance responsibilities, financing, and user accountability. |
| Indicators | Technical quality, output, outcome, safety, inclusion, maintenance, and risk-reduction indicators. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when converting area priorities into detailed sector designs.

A strong technical design should show **what standard applies, what technical problem is being solved, how the design fits the area strategy, what risks are mitigated, who will maintain the result, and how quality will be verified**.
