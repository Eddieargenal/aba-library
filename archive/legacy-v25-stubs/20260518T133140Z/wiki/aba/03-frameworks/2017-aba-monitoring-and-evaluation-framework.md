# ABA Monitoring and Evaluation Framework

**Source:** Sanderson & Sitko, *Urban area-based approaches in post-disaster contexts: Guidance Note for Humanitarian Practitioners*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Lifecycle stage:** 9. Monitoring, learning, and adaptation  
**Framework purpose:** Guide teams to track whether an area-based approach is making the target area safer, more functional, more resilient, and better coordinated—not only whether planned outputs were delivered.

## Framework Overview

The **ABA Monitoring and Evaluation Framework** helps teams monitor and learn from area-based humanitarian, recovery, and disaster risk reduction programming in complex urban environments.

It is designed for the monitoring, learning, and adaptation stage of an Area-Based Approach (ABA), when teams need to understand whether integrated action across sectors, communities, local institutions, and service systems is actually improving life in the target area.

The framework adapts Sanderson and Sitko’s guidance on urban ABAs, especially the principles of people-centred action, adaptive management, planning for scale, and measuring **contribution rather than attribution**. It shifts monitoring away from narrow output tracking—such as the number of shelters repaired, drains cleared, trainings delivered, or items distributed—and toward evidence about how the area is changing as a lived system.

The central monitoring question is not only **“Did we deliver?”** but **“Is the area becoming safer, more functional, more resilient, more inclusive, and better coordinated?”**

## Core Framework Question

**How can the team monitor whether the ABA is contributing to safer, more functional, more resilient, and better coordinated area-level recovery, while learning and adapting as conditions change?**

## Framework Dimensions

| Dimension | What it examines | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Household outcomes | Safety, shelter quality, access to services, livelihoods, protection risks, preparedness, satisfaction, and perceived recovery. | ABA success must be visible in people’s everyday lives, not only in project activity records. |
| Community and social capacity | Community organisation, participation, mutual aid, trust, inclusion, local leadership, and collective problem solving. | Urban recovery depends on social systems and local capacity, not only physical repairs. |
| Service-system functionality | Water, sanitation, drainage, waste, roads, schools, health, markets, electricity, transport, and public spaces. | Area-based programming should improve how systems function together in the target area. |
| Governance and coordination | Local authorities, community structures, service providers, humanitarian actors, roles, accountability, and coordination quality. | ABAs rely on collaboration across sectors and actors, especially existing local structures. |
| Risk reduction and resilience | Exposure reduction, preparedness, early warning, safe access, risk-informed infrastructure, mitigation, and recovery readiness. | The area should become safer and better able to manage future shocks. |
| Inclusion and accountability | Participation of women, children, older people, people with disabilities, renters, informal settlers, displaced people, and marginalized groups. | Area-level progress can hide exclusion unless disaggregated and participatory monitoring is used. |
| Adaptive management | Whether monitoring data is used to revise activities, budgets, sequencing, partnerships, and implementation methods. | Urban contexts change quickly; monitoring must support decision-making, not only reporting. |
| Contribution to wider change | How the ABA contributed to recovery alongside other actors, community action, local government, markets, and external conditions. | In complex urban systems, no single project can claim full attribution for area-level change. |
| Scale and replication | Whether learning, methods, partnerships, and systems can be adapted in other areas or absorbed into wider city-level strategies. | ABAs should generate learning and models that can support broader recovery and resilience. |

## Framework Logic

The framework uses a contribution-oriented and adaptive monitoring process:

1. **Define the area-level change ambition**  
   Clarify what “safer, more functional, more resilient, and better coordinated” means in the specific target area.

2. **Map the area as a system**  
   Identify the households, community groups, local institutions, service providers, markets, infrastructure systems, authorities, and humanitarian actors that shape outcomes.

3. **Define outcome domains beyond sector outputs**  
   Translate the ABA goal into household, community, service-system, governance, DRR, and inclusion outcomes.

4. **Develop a contribution logic**  
   Explain how the ABA is expected to contribute to area-level change alongside other actors and external factors.

5. **Select mixed indicators**  
   Combine quantitative indicators, qualitative evidence, community feedback, observation, service functionality checks, and stakeholder reflection.

6. **Establish a practical baseline**  
   Document initial area conditions, including visible risks, service gaps, community priorities, coordination arrangements, and household perceptions.

7. **Monitor outputs and outcomes together**  
   Track whether activities were delivered, but also whether they improved safety, functionality, resilience, access, coordination, and satisfaction.

8. **Use participatory monitoring**  
   Involve affected people and local stakeholders in defining progress, validating evidence, and identifying unintended effects.

9. **Hold regular learning and adaptation reviews**  
   Use monitoring data to adjust sequencing, targeting, technical design, coordination, budgets, and partnership arrangements.

10. **Assess contribution, not isolated attribution**  
   At evaluation stage, examine how the ABA plausibly contributed to observed changes, including positive and negative effects, rather than claiming sole responsibility.

11. **Share learning for scale and institutional uptake**  
   Document what worked, what did not, what can be replicated, and what should be adopted by local institutions or other area teams.

## ABA Outcome Monitoring Matrix

| Outcome level | What to monitor | Example indicators or evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Household | Whether people feel safer, have better access to services, improved shelter conditions, reduced risk, and greater ability to recover. | Household perception surveys, satisfaction scores, shelter habitability checks, access-to-service measures, reduced reported coping stress, preparedness actions taken. |
| Community | Whether residents are more organized, connected, informed, and able to act collectively. | Active community groups, meeting records, participation diversity, mutual aid networks, community action plans, evidence of local problem solving. |
| Service-system | Whether essential systems are more reliable, accessible, connected, and maintained. | Drainage functioning, waste collection frequency, water access, sanitation usability, road/passability, school/health facility access, market access. |
| Governance | Whether roles, responsibilities, coordination, transparency, and accountability have improved. | Coordination meeting minutes, resolved action points, public information sharing, municipal participation, complaint response time, joint planning decisions. |
| DRR and safety | Whether exposure, vulnerability, and preparedness gaps have decreased. | Safer routes, cleared drainage, risk maps used, early warning coverage, evacuation arrangements, risk mitigation works, household preparedness levels. |
| Protection and inclusion | Whether the most at-risk groups can access benefits, participate, and report concerns safely. | Disaggregated participation, safe referral pathways, accessibility checks, inclusion of renters/informal settlers, GBV risk mitigation, feedback from marginalized groups. |
| Coordination quality | Whether actors are reducing duplication, filling gaps, and sequencing activities logically. | 4W/5W area maps, joint workplans, referral pathways, sequencing updates, reduced overlapping meetings or beneficiary fatigue, shared problem tracking. |
| Adaptation | Whether decisions change based on evidence and feedback. | Adaptation log, revised workplans, budget changes, re-sequenced activities, changed targeting, updated risk controls. |
| Sustainability | Whether improvements have owners, resources, and maintenance arrangements after project closure. | Maintenance agreements, municipal budget links, community management plans, service-provider commitments, handover records. |
| Scale and learning | Whether lessons can inform other areas or wider city-level systems. | Replication notes, documented methods, peer learning sessions, municipal uptake, donor or coordination-body adoption. |

## Contribution-Oriented Evaluation Table

| Evaluation question | What to examine |
|---|---|
| What changed in the area? | Household wellbeing, safety, services, governance, social capacity, infrastructure functionality, preparedness, and recovery conditions. |
| Who experienced the change? | Differences by gender, age, disability, tenure status, displacement status, livelihood group, location, and vulnerability. |
| What did the ABA contribute? | Activities, coordination, facilitation, technical support, funding, community engagement, referrals, planning, and systems strengthening. |
| What did other actors contribute? | Municipal authorities, communities, service providers, markets, other humanitarian actors, donors, civil society, and private sector actors. |
| What external factors shaped results? | New shocks, market changes, political decisions, population movement, weather, conflict, funding changes, legal constraints, or service disruptions. |
| What negative or unintended effects occurred? | Exclusion, aid capture, dependency, conflict, displacement pressure, market distortion, beneficiary fatigue, coordination burden, or infrastructure risks. |
| What evidence supports the judgement? | Mixed-method evidence, community feedback, observation, service data, monitoring records, comparison over time, and stakeholder validation. |
| What should change next? | Adapted priorities, revised sequencing, changed partnerships, additional mitigation, scale-up decisions, or exit and handover actions. |

## Indicator Design Guidance

Use a balanced indicator set. Do not rely only on activity counts.

| Indicator type | Purpose | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Output indicators | Confirm what was delivered. | Number of drains cleared, shelters repaired, meetings held, volunteers trained, referrals completed. |
| Quality indicators | Check whether outputs were useful, safe, and appropriate. | User satisfaction, technical quality checks, accessibility, safety, cultural appropriateness, complaint resolution. |
| Outcome indicators | Track whether conditions improved. | Reduced flooding in target streets, improved access to WASH, safer shelter conditions, improved mobility, reduced protection risks. |
| Functionality indicators | Track whether area systems are working. | Drainage flow, waste collection continuity, market reopening, school access, health referral functioning, public lighting. |
| Governance indicators | Track coordination and accountability. | Action points closed, municipal participation, public information shared, joint decisions made, maintenance responsibilities assigned. |
| Inclusion indicators | Track whether progress reaches at-risk groups. | Disaggregated access, participation of marginalized groups, accessibility measures, safe feedback from excluded populations. |
| Adaptation indicators | Track whether learning changes practice. | Number and quality of decisions changed based on feedback, updated sequencing, revised budgets, new mitigation measures. |
| Sustainability indicators | Track whether gains can continue. | Maintenance plans, local budget commitments, trained local actors, handover agreements, service-provider ownership. |

## Participatory Monitoring Methods

| Method | How to use it in an ABA |
|---|---|
| Community scorecards | Ask residents to score safety, services, coordination, inclusion, and trust over time. |
| Spider diagrams | Use visual self-assessment to track perceived change across multiple resilience dimensions. |
| Neighbourhood walks | Observe drainage, access, public spaces, waste, shelter risks, protection risks, and infrastructure conditions with residents. |
| Before-and-after mapping | Compare spatial patterns of risk, access, services, and activity coverage over time. |
| Focus group reflection | Explore how different groups experience changes, including women, renters, displaced people, youth, older people, and people with disabilities. |
| Service functionality checks | Periodically verify whether systems such as WASH, drainage, waste, roads, schools, health, and markets are functioning. |
| Coordination review meetings | Review area-level action points, duplication, gaps, sequencing problems, and unresolved responsibilities. |
| Feedback and complaints analysis | Track recurring issues, response time, unresolved complaints, exclusion patterns, and trust in the response. |
| Adaptation logs | Record what changed in the program, why it changed, who decided, and what evidence supported the decision. |
| Outcome harvesting | Identify significant changes and work backward to examine how the ABA may have contributed. |

## Learning and Adaptation Cycle

| Step | Practice |
|---|---|
| 1. Collect evidence | Gather monitoring data from households, communities, field observation, service providers, coordination meetings, and feedback channels. |
| 2. Interpret with stakeholders | Discuss findings with community representatives, local authorities, service providers, technical teams, and partner organizations. |
| 3. Identify adaptation needs | Determine whether targeting, sequencing, technical standards, communication, referrals, coordination, or budgets need adjustment. |
| 4. Decide and document | Record the decision, rationale, evidence used, responsible actor, and deadline. |
| 5. Implement changes | Update workplans, procurement, partner roles, field schedules, community engagement, or technical designs. |
| 6. Monitor effects | Check whether the adaptation improved results or created new risks. |
| 7. Share learning | Communicate lessons with communities, coordination bodies, donors, municipal actors, and other area teams. |

| Dashboard area | Suggested measures |
|---|---|
| Area safety | Hazard exposure, safety perception, protection incidents, safe access, lighting, evacuation readiness. |
| Basic service functionality | WASH, drainage, waste, roads, schools, health, electricity, markets, and transport status. |
| Household recovery | Shelter conditions, livelihoods, access to assistance, coping capacity, satisfaction, preparedness. |
| Community capacity | Local leadership, participation, mutual aid, collective action, community DRR activities. |
| Coordination | Actor coverage, duplication, gap tracking, action-point completion, municipal engagement, referral functioning. |
| Inclusion | Participation and access by gender, age, disability, displacement, tenure, and marginalized status. |

## Design Implications

The framework should inform:

| Design decision | How the framework supports it |
|---|---|
| Indicator selection | Moves indicators beyond sector outputs toward area-level change and lived outcomes. |
| Monitoring system design | Combines quantitative data, qualitative evidence, participatory tools, service checks, and coordination review. |
| Adaptive management | Creates a regular mechanism to adjust programming as urban conditions change. |
| Accountability | Ensures affected people define and validate whether the program is useful, safe, and relevant. |
| Coordination | Tracks whether actors are reducing duplication, sequencing activities, and solving area-level problems together. |
| DRR integration | Measures whether the area is becoming safer and more prepared, not only repaired. |
| Evaluation approach | Supports contribution analysis rather than unrealistic claims of single-agency attribution. |
| Scale-up planning | Captures learning that can be replicated, adapted, or institutionalized in other areas. |

## Risk Alerts

**Output-counting risk:** Counting activities delivered does not show whether the area is safer, more functional, or more resilient.

**Attribution risk:** In complex urban recovery, claiming that one agency caused area-wide change can be misleading. Use contribution analysis instead.

**Single-sector indicator risk:** Sector-specific indicators can miss how shelter, WASH, protection, markets, services, governance, and DRR interact.

**Inflexible logframe risk:** Fixed indicators and rigid targets can prevent teams from adapting to changing urban conditions.

**Community-blind monitoring risk:** Monitoring designed only for donor reporting may miss the priorities, perceptions, and lived realities of affected people.

**Soft-change invisibility risk:** Improvements in trust, coordination, management capacity, community organization, and governance may be overlooked unless intentionally measured.

**Equity masking risk:** Area averages can hide exclusion of renters, informal settlers, displaced people, women, people with disabilities, or isolated households.

**Learning-without-decision risk:** Collecting data without changing plans wastes time and weakens accountability.

## Framework Output

The framework should produce a short **ABA Monitoring, Learning, and Adaptation Note** containing:

| Output element | Description |
|---|---|
| Area change statement | Definition of what safer, more functional, more resilient, and better coordinated means in the target area. |
| Contribution logic | Explanation of how the ABA is expected to contribute to area-level change alongside other actors. |
| Indicator matrix | Balanced output, quality, outcome, functionality, governance, inclusion, DRR, and sustainability indicators. |
| Baseline summary | Initial area conditions, risks, service gaps, community priorities, and coordination arrangements. |
| Monitoring methods | Household surveys, community scorecards, observation, service checks, focus groups, feedback analysis, and coordination reviews. |
| Adaptation process | Schedule and responsibilities for reviewing evidence and changing implementation decisions. |
| Inclusion plan | How monitoring captures different experiences across vulnerable and marginalized groups. |
| Contribution evidence | Evidence of how ABA activities, coordination, and partnerships contributed to observed changes. |
| Learning products | Short notes, maps, dashboards, case examples, and lessons for scale-up or replication. |
| Handover and sustainability evidence | Maintenance arrangements, local ownership, budget links, and institutional uptake. |

## Practical Decision Rule

Use this framework when the team needs to monitor whether an Area-Based Approach is producing meaningful area-level change.

A strong ABA monitoring and evaluation system should explain **what changed in the area, who experienced the change, how the ABA contributed, what other actors and external factors shaped results, what risks or exclusions remain, what needs to adapt, and what learning can be used for scale-up or institutional uptake**.
