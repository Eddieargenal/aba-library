> **Abstract.** Contribution vs. attribution is the evaluative distinction between assessing how an intervention contributed to outcomes within a wider multi-actor causal system (contribution) versus measuring outcomes that can be directly attributed to a single agency's actions alone (attribution). Two sources ground the concept: Sanderson & Sitko (2017) provide the most explicit operational account, arguing that ABA outcomes must be evaluated through contribution rather than attribution logic; the Global Shelter Cluster (2019) applies the same principle to multi-agency M&E design. Both sources are consistent. The concept is directly actionable for MEL design: it determines what evidence is collected, how causal claims are framed, and what questions evaluators can defensibly answer about a multi-actor urban programme.

## 1. Definition

Attribution evaluation asks: what outcomes were produced by this agency's intervention, holding other factors constant? Contribution evaluation asks: how did this agency's intervention contribute to outcomes within a wider causal system, alongside other actors, conditions, and dynamics? In complex, multi-actor urban recovery contexts, the first question is frequently unanswerable without implausible assumptions; the second is the evaluatively appropriate frame.

Sanderson & Sitko (2017) state this directly: "ABA outcomes should assess how interventions contribute within wider multi-actor recovery ecosystems, not only single-project attribution" (p. 27). They describe a specific methodology — the "contribution to change" approach, structured as an 11-step process — for generating defensible contribution evidence from qualitative and quantitative data (p. 27).

The Global Shelter Cluster (2019) applies the same principle at the coordination level: "multi-agency M&E should track collective contribution to change rather than only single-agency attribution" (pp. 10, 13), framing contribution-based evaluation as a coordination design feature, not only an individual agency choice.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Sanderson & Sitko (2017)** ground the concept in a structural argument: in ABA programmes, "collaboration is not optional because no single agency can cover all needs" (p. 19). If attribution requires holding other actors' contributions constant to isolate a single agency's effect, the attribution approach is structurally impossible in a programme that was designed for multi-actor delivery. Contribution evaluation sidesteps this problem by framing the evaluation question at the level of the causal system — asking how the intervention interacted with other factors to produce outcomes — rather than attempting to isolate a counterfactual that cannot be observed.

The paper also specifies an asset-based monitoring logic as a companion to contribution evaluation: tracking "physical, financial, political, social assets to track vulnerability reduction beyond sectoral outputs" (p. 28). This extends monitoring beyond delivery outputs to the vulnerability conditions that ABA is designed to change — a necessary expansion if contribution to city-level recovery, not single-agency outputs, is the evaluative aim.

**Global Shelter Cluster (2019)** applies contribution-based evaluation at the coordination level. The practical implication is that ABA coordination bodies should design shared M&E frameworks that track collective change rather than requiring each agency to independently document and defend its own contribution in isolation (pp. 10, 13).

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Methodological rigor vs. field feasibility.** Sanderson & Sitko (2017) cite an 11-step contribution-to-change method (p. 27) as the structured approach. Rigorous contribution analysis requires systematic evidence collection across the causal system — including documentation of other actors' interventions, baseline conditions, and confounding factors — that may not be feasible within standard humanitarian M&E budgets and timelines. No source specifies what minimum evidence standard distinguishes defensible contribution claims from unverifiable assertions.

**Accountability implications.** Attribution-based evaluation is often required by donors for accountability purposes, even when it is evaluatively inappropriate. The shift to contribution-based evaluation requires donor agreement, not only internal methodology changes. Neither source addresses how to negotiate contribution-based reporting within standard donor accountability frameworks that are designed around single-agency output attribution.

## 4. Implications for practice

- MEL frameworks for ABA programmes should be designed around contribution questions from programme inception, not adapted from single-agency attribution frameworks at evaluation time (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, p. 27; Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, pp. 10, 13).
- Evidence collection should span the causal system — including other actors' interventions, baseline conditions, and contextual factors — not only the implementing agency's activities and outputs (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, pp. 27-28).
- Asset-based monitoring (physical, financial, political, social) should supplement output tracking to capture vulnerability reduction rather than only delivery volume (Sanderson & Sitko, 2017, p. 28).
- Contribution-based evaluation should be negotiated with donors at programme design; retrofitting it to an attribution-based logframe at evaluation time produces neither good attribution nor good contribution evidence (Global Shelter Cluster, 2019, pp. 10, 13).

## 5. Open questions

- What minimum evidence standard distinguishes a defensible contribution claim from an assertion? How should ABA evaluation teams determine when their evidence meets that standard?
- How should contribution-based evaluation be negotiated within donor accountability frameworks that require single-agency attribution as a standard reporting requirement?
- How should contribution framing be designed when the multi-actor causal system includes actors whose interventions were not coordinated with the evaluating programme?
