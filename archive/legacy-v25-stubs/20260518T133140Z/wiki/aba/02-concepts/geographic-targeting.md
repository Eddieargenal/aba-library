> **Abstract.** Geographic targeting is the evidence-based process of selecting and delineating intervention areas so response design is anchored to spatial patterns of risk, need, and system failure. Three sources in this wiki ground the concept operationally: Parker & Maynard (2015) establish geography as the defining entry point of ABA and document boundary typologies; Sanderson & Sitko (2017) frame location-first problem analysis as a core post-disaster ABA principle; and the Global Shelter Cluster (2019) shows that scale and boundary choices shape staffing, coordination, and inclusion outcomes. The concept is consistent across sources, but its implementation is contested where administrative convenience, social identity, and operational feasibility do not align.

## 1. Definition

Geographic targeting is the deliberate selection of an intervention area based on demonstrable concentration of risk, need, and opportunity, with boundaries justified through transparent criteria rather than inherited operational zones or agency convenience (Parker & Maynard, 2015, pp. 8-10).

In ABA, geography is the primary entry point for programme logic. This means the area's conditions define analysis and action, rather than sector mandates or preselected beneficiary categories.

## 2. Evidence base and theoretical grounding

**Parker & Maynard (2015)** provide the strongest conceptual grounding in this source set. They describe geographic targeting as the foundational ABA characteristic and distinguish major boundary logics: administrative boundaries that support formal governance linkage, and physical boundaries that may better reflect lived settlement dynamics (p. 9). They also show that boundary decisions influence social inclusion and inter-neighbourhood tension.

**Sanderson & Sitko (2017)** reinforce area-first analysis by positioning local problem diagnosis and iterative action planning as essential to effective post-disaster ABA. Their framing implies that boundary definition is not a one-time cartographic task but part of a wider adaptive programme process.

**Global Shelter Cluster (2019)** extends the concept with comparative case evidence across scales. It highlights that neighbourhood, district, and broader urban targeting each produce different coordination demands and implementation constraints, making scale choice a strategic decision rather than a neutral technical step.

## 3. Contested dimensions

**Administrative boundaries vs. socially experienced boundaries.** Administrative units support government alignment and reporting clarity, but they can miss social geographies that shape access, exclusion, and legitimacy. Relying on one boundary logic alone can produce distorted programme targeting.

**Inclusive area targeting vs. selective within-area targeting.** Sources document both whole-population area targeting and selective household targeting within the area. The choice affects social cohesion, perceived fairness, and protection risk; no universal rule resolves this tradeoff.

**Scale ambiguity.** Sources agree ABA can operate at multiple scales, but most evidence is from neighbourhood or district practice. City-scale targeting remains comparatively under-specified in terms of participation, operational sequencing, and coordination burden.

## 4. Implications for practice

- Start with a documented area-selection rationale that combines need concentration, feasibility, and expected system leverage.
- Triangulate boundary definition using administrative, physical, and social evidence instead of treating any single map as authoritative.
- Validate draft boundaries with local actors before operational lock-in, then record inclusion and exclusion consequences explicitly.
- Reassess boundaries when context shifts; geographic targeting should support adaptive management, not freeze early assumptions.
- Plan mitigation for cross-boundary effects, including service spillover, perceived inequity, and actor clustering in one favoured zone.

## 5. Open questions

- What minimum evidence threshold is sufficient to justify selecting one area over similarly affected neighbouring areas?
- How should teams decide between whole-area inclusion and selective within-area targeting under severe resource constraints?
- Which practical governance arrangements best support city-scale geographic targeting without losing local legitimacy?
